================================================================
REGULATION OVERVIEW
================================================================

The European Union adopted Regulation (EU) 2023/1115 on deforestation-free products, commonly
referred to as the EU Deforestation Regulation (EUDR). This landmark legislation aims to ensure
that specific commodities and products sold in the EU have not contributed to deforestation or
forest degradation anywhere in the world.

CORE PURPOSE:
"Guarantee that the products EU citizens consume do not contribute to deforestation or forest
degradation globally."

The regulation repeals and replaces the earlier EU Timber Regulation (EUTR), significantly
expanding its scope from wood products alone to seven major commodity groups.

================================================================
WHY THE EUDR WAS NEEDED: THE PROBLEM CONTEXT
================================================================

GLOBAL DEFORESTATION CRISIS:
- Approximately 10% of the world's forests (an area exceeding the total land area of the EU)
  have been lost through deforestation over the past 30 years
- About 10% of global forests are severely fragmented with minimal connectivity
- Agricultural expansion is the primary driver, accounting for roughly 80% of deforestation

EU CONSUMPTION FOOTPRINT:
- The EU is one of the world's largest importers of commodities linked to deforestation
- EU consumption is estimated to drive approximately 10% of global deforestation
- The EU imports significant quantities of soy, palm oil, cocoa, coffee, cattle products, and timber

PREVIOUS REGULATORY GAPS:
The EU Timber Regulation (EUTR), in force since 2013, covered only timber products and proved
insufficient. The EUDR addresses these gaps by:
- Expanding to six additional commodity groups beyond wood
- Requiring geolocation data to trace products to their exact production plots
- Establishing a binding due diligence system with mandatory third-country risk classification
- Harmonizing enforcement across all EU Member States

================================================================
KEY OBJECTIVES
================================================================

1. ENVIRONMENTAL PROTECTION:
   - Reduce EU-driven deforestation and forest degradation globally
   - Protect biodiversity and ecosystem services
   - Preserve primary forests and carbon sinks

2. CLIMATE ACTION:
   - Reduce greenhouse gas emissions from deforestation by at least 32 million tonnes CO2e/year
   - Support the EU's climate neutrality goals under the European Green Deal
   - Align with Paris Agreement commitments

3. SUPPLY CHAIN ACCOUNTABILITY:
   - Ensure supply chain transparency from forest to consumer
   - Hold operators responsible for due diligence
   - Create market-based incentives for sustainable production

4. HUMAN RIGHTS:
   - Protect indigenous peoples' rights including free, prior and informed consent (FPIC)
   - Support local communities dependent on forests
   - Ensure fair treatment of smallholder farmers

================================================================
COVERED COMMODITIES AND PRODUCTS
================================================================

The seven relevant commodities and their key derived products:

CATTLE:
- Live cattle
- Fresh, chilled, or frozen bovine meat
- Leather and tanned leather products
- Leather footwear
- Collagen and gelatine from bovine hides

COCOA:
- Cocoa beans and husks
- Cocoa paste, butter, powder
- Chocolate and chocolate products

COFFEE:
- Raw (green) coffee beans
- Roasted coffee
- Coffee extracts and essences

OIL PALM:
- Palm oil and palm kernel oil
- Palm fatty acids and crude glycerol
- Margarine and similar products

RUBBER:
- Natural rubber and latex
- Rubber tyres and inner tubes
- Vulcanised rubber products

SOYA:
- Soya beans
- Soya bean oil and meal
- Animal feed containing soya

WOOD:
- Sawn timber, veneer, plywood
- Pulp and paper products
- Furniture containing wood
- Charcoal (excluded from some provisions; to be reviewed)
- Printed books and newspapers

================================================================
WHO IS AFFECTED BY THE EUDR
================================================================

OPERATORS:
Any natural or legal person who places relevant products on the EU market or exports them.
Includes: manufacturers, producers, importers, processors.

LARGE AND MEDIUM OPERATORS (non-SMEs):
- Full due diligence obligations
- Must submit due diligence statements before each placement/export
- Must publish annual reports on due diligence systems
- Application date: 30 December 2026

SMALL AND MICRO OPERATORS (SMEs):
- Simplified obligations for SME traders (not SME operators)
- SME operators still carry full due diligence obligations
- Application date: 30 June 2027

NON-SME TRADERS:
- Exercise due diligence equivalent to operators
- Publish annual due diligence reports

SME TRADERS:
- Collect and keep reference numbers from supplier due diligence statements
- Maintain 5-year records of supply chain information

================================================================
DUE DILIGENCE: STEP-BY-STEP PROCESS
================================================================

STEP 1: INFORMATION COLLECTION
Operators must collect and document for each relevant product:
- Product description (trade name, type, HS codes)
- Quantity (volume and weight)
- Country of production
- Geolocation of all plots of land where produced (polygon or point coordinates)
- Date or time range of production
- Supplier and customer names and contact details
- Evidence that commodities are deforestation-free (satellite imagery, certification)

STEP 2: RISK ASSESSMENT
Evaluate the risk of non-compliance by considering:
- Country risk classification (low/standard/high)
- Prevalence of deforestation in the country/region
- Presence and quality of forests
- Governance indicators (corruption, rule of law)
- Indigenous peoples' rights situation
- Complexity of the supply chain (number of intermediaries)
- Third-party information and complaints
- Certification and third-party audits already in place

STEP 3: RISK MITIGATION (if risk is non-negligible)
- Request additional documentation from suppliers
- Commission independent audits
- Require certified products
- Reject non-compliant suppliers

STEP 4: DUE DILIGENCE STATEMENT SUBMISSION
Before placing products on the market, submit a due diligence statement via the EUDR Information
System. The statement must declare:
- Due diligence has been exercised
- No or negligible risk of non-compliance was found
- Reference to supporting information

STEP 5: RECORD KEEPING
Maintain all due diligence documentation for at least 5 years.

================================================================
COUNTRY BENCHMARKING AND RISK CLASSIFICATION
================================================================

The Commission assigns each country (or regions within countries) to one of three risk tiers:

LOW RISK:
Countries with strong forest governance, low deforestation rates, robust monitoring systems.
Operators may apply simplified due diligence; no detailed risk assessment required if information
collection confirms low-risk origin.

STANDARD RISK (DEFAULT):
Countries with moderate or unknown deforestation risk. Full due diligence required.

HIGH RISK:
Countries with high deforestation rates, weak governance, or known compliance issues.
- Enhanced checks by competent authorities
- Operators may need additional verification
- Increased scrutiny at customs

The benchmarking methodology considers:
- Deforestation rates and trends
- Forest governance quality
- Proportion of forests protected or managed sustainably
- Compliance with international environmental agreements
- Ongoing reform processes

================================================================
ENFORCEMENT STRUCTURE
================================================================

COMPETENT AUTHORITIES (appointed by each Member State):
- Conduct physical checks on operators and their supply chains
- Review due diligence statements and supporting documentation
- Apply proportionate enforcement including fines
- Coordinate with customs authorities at borders

CHECK RATES (minimum):
- High-risk countries: 9% of operators and relevant products
- Standard-risk countries: 3% of operators and relevant products
- Low-risk countries: 1% of operators and relevant products

CUSTOMS INTEGRATION:
- Electronic interface links EU customs systems with the EUDR information system
- Customs authorities can verify due diligence statements at point of entry
- Coordinated checks between customs and competent authorities

================================================================
EUDR INFORMATION SYSTEM
================================================================

Launched: 4 December 2024

Functions:
- Online portal for submitting due diligence statements
- Unique reference numbers issued for each valid statement
- Database accessible to competent authorities across all Member States
- Integration with EU customs IT systems
- Public transparency layer for verified statements

================================================================
RECENT AMENDMENTS AND SIMPLIFICATION
================================================================

Following stakeholder consultations in late 2024, amendments were introduced to:
- Reduce administrative burden for operators in low-risk countries
- Clarify requirements for smallholder supply chains
- Simplify the due diligence statement submission process
- Introduce a partnership approach for countries demonstrating strong governance

The December 2024 amendment extended the application date for large operators from
30 June 2025 to 30 December 2026, providing additional preparation time.

================================================================
KEY POLICY CONTEXT: EU FOREST STRATEGY AND GREEN DEAL
================================================================

The EUDR is a flagship measure under the European Green Deal, specifically:
- EU Biodiversity Strategy 2030: target to protect 30% of land and sea
- EU Forest Strategy 2030: support sustainable forest management globally
- Farm to Fork Strategy: promote sustainable food systems
- 2030 Climate Target Plan: reduce net GHG emissions by at least 55%

EU COMMITMENTS AT GLASGOW (COP26, 2021):
The EU signed the Glasgow Leaders' Declaration on Forests and Land Use, committing to:
- Halt and reverse forest loss and land degradation by 2030
- Deliver sustainable land use, forestry, and agriculture
- Mobilize finance for forest protection

The EUDR is the EU's main legislative instrument to translate these commitments into binding action.

================================================================
FREQUENTLY ASKED QUESTIONS (SELECTED)
================================================================

Q: Does the EUDR apply to products consumed within the EU or only to imports?
A: The regulation applies to both products placed on the EU market (including domestically produced
goods) and products exported from the EU. Any operator, regardless of whether they produce
domestically or import, must comply.

Q: What is the cut-off date and why was December 31, 2020 chosen?
A: The cut-off date is 31 December 2020. Products produced on land that was deforested or
forest-degraded after this date cannot be sold in the EU. This date was chosen as it aligns with
the Glasgow Declaration and the EU's biodiversity strategy timeline.

Q: Does the EUDR cover recycled materials?
A: No. Commodities and products that have completed their lifecycle and would otherwise be disposed
of as waste are excluded. Requiring due diligence on recycled materials would impose disproportionate
burdens without environmental benefit, since the original production has already occurred.

Q: What counts as a "forest" under the regulation?
A: The regulation uses the FAO definition: land spanning more than 0.5 hectares with trees higher
than 5 metres and a canopy cover of more than 10%, or trees able to reach these thresholds in
situ, excluding land that is predominantly under agricultural or urban land use.

Q: Are certification schemes (e.g., FSC, RSPO) sufficient for compliance?
A: No certification scheme alone provides automatic compliance. However, certified products and
supply chains may support due diligence as one element of evidence. Operators must still collect
geolocation data and conduct risk assessments. Certification can reduce risk assessment workload
but does not replace due diligence obligations.

Q: How does geolocation work for smallholder supply chains?
A: For smallholders, geolocation data can be collected as GPS polygon coordinates of plots.
Special guidance exists for aggregated smallholder supply chains where individual plot coordinates
may be pooled. The Commission has worked with producer countries on digital tools to support
smallholder data collection.

Q: What penalties apply for non-compliance?
A: Penalties are determined by Member States but the regulation sets minimum standards:
- Maximum fine of at least 4% of annual turnover in the relevant Member State
- Confiscation of products and revenues
- Temporary exclusion from public procurement
- Temporary bans from placing products on the market

Q: Do NGOs or the public have a role in enforcement?
A: Yes. Third parties, including NGOs, can submit substantiated concerns to competent authorities.
Competent authorities must investigate substantiated concerns within 30 days and inform the
reporter of the outcome.
