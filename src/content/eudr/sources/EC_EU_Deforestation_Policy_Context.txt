================================================================
EU FOREST POLICY CONTEXT
================================================================

FIVE MAIN EU FOREST POLICY PRIORITIES (from 2019 Communication):
The European Commission's 2019 Communication on "Stepping Up EU Action to Protect and
Restore the World's Forests" established five strategic pillars:

1. CONSUMPTION FOOTPRINT:
   - Reduce EU consumption's land impact and promote deforestation-free supply chains
   - The EUDR is the primary legislative instrument implementing this pillar

2. PARTNER COOPERATION:
   - Collaborate with producer countries to alleviate forest pressures
   - Bilateral dialogues with Brazil, Indonesia, Cameroon, and other major producer nations
   - Support for governance reforms and sustainable forest management

3. INTERNATIONAL FRAMEWORK:
   - Strengthen global cooperation on halting deforestation and forest degradation
   - Support implementation of the Glasgow Forest Declaration (2021)
   - Engagement in CBD (Convention on Biological Diversity) processes
   - Advocacy for ambitious NDCs (Nationally Determined Contributions) under Paris Agreement

4. FINANCIAL REDIRECTION:
   - Channel EU development finance and private investment toward sustainable land use
   - Integrate deforestation risk into financial regulation
   - Support REDD+ mechanisms in producer countries

5. INFORMATION ACCESS:
   - Enhance data availability on forests and commodity supply chains
   - EU Observatory on Deforestation and Forest Degradation
   - Cooperation with Global Forest Watch and other monitoring initiatives

================================================================
CHRONOLOGICAL DEVELOPMENT OF EU DEFORESTATION LEGISLATION
================================================================

2010: EU TIMBER REGULATION (EUTR) - Regulation (EU) No 995/2010
- First EU legislation prohibiting placement of illegally harvested timber on the market
- Introduced due diligence obligations for timber operators
- Limitation: Covered only timber and wood products; did not address agricultural deforestation

2013: EUTR APPLICATION
- EUTR came into force March 2013
- Established baseline for supply chain due diligence in EU timber trade

2019: EU DEFORESTATION COMMUNICATION
- European Commission published "Stepping Up EU Action to Protect and Restore the World's Forests"
- Recognized EUTR's limitations and committed to stronger measures

2020: BIODIVERSITY STRATEGY 2030
- EU Biodiversity Strategy adopted, targeting 30% land protection by 2030
- Committed to addressing EU consumption impact on global biodiversity

2021:
- European Green Deal legislation developed
- EU Forest Strategy 2030 published
- Glasgow COP26: EU committed to halt deforestation by 2030

November 2021: EUROPEAN COMMISSION PROPOSAL
- Commission proposed regulation on deforestation-free products (COM(2021) 706 final)
- Identified seven commodity groups and their derived products

April 2023: REGULATION ADOPTED
- European Parliament and Council formally adopted Regulation (EU) 2023/1115 on 31 May 2023

29 June 2023: ENTRY INTO FORCE
- Regulation entered into force following publication in the Official Journal

December 2024: EUDR INFORMATION SYSTEM LAUNCH
- EUDR Information System (EUDR IS) operational from 4 December 2024

December 2024: APPLICATION DATE AMENDMENT
- Amendment extended application dates:
  - Large/medium operators: 30 December 2026
  - Small/micro operators: 30 June 2027

================================================================
EU FOREST STRATEGY 2030: CONTEXT FOR EUDR
================================================================

The EU Forest Strategy 2030, published in July 2021, set ambitious goals:
- Plant 3 billion additional trees in the EU by 2030
- Protect remaining EU primary and old-growth forests
- Sustainably manage all EU forests
- Mobilize forests as carbon sinks
- Support forest-based bioeconomy

The EUDR is the key trade policy complement to the EU Forest Strategy, addressing the external
dimension: forests outside the EU that supply EU markets.

================================================================
RELATIONSHIP BETWEEN EUDR AND EU CLIMATE/BIODIVERSITY GOALS
================================================================

EUROPEAN GREEN DEAL TARGETS:
- Climate neutrality by 2050
- 55% net GHG reduction by 2030 (compared to 1990)
- Zero net land degradation by 2050

EUDR'S CONTRIBUTION TO GREEN DEAL:
- Eliminates deforestation emissions embedded in EU imports
- Projects minimum 32 million tonnes CO2e annual emission reduction
- Protects forest carbon sinks that EU climate plans depend upon

BIODIVERSITY FRAMEWORK LINKAGE:
- Kunming-Montreal Global Biodiversity Framework (COP15, 2022): Halt nature loss by 2030
- EU committed to 30x30 target: 30% of land and seas protected by 2030
- EUDR protects biodiverse tropical forests that EU has no jurisdiction over directly
- Extraterritorial effect of EU market power used for conservation benefit

THE EVOLUTION FROM EUTR TO EUDR: KEY DIFFERENCES
================================================================

ASPECT              EU TIMBER REGULATION (EUTR)    EU DEFORESTATION REGULATION (EUDR)
                    Regulation (EU) No 995/2010     Regulation (EU) 2023/1115

Commodities         Wood and timber products only   7 commodities: cattle, cocoa, coffee,
                                                    oil palm, rubber, soya, wood

Key requirement     No illegally harvested timber   No deforestation/degradation after
                                                    31 December 2020

Geolocation         Not required                    Required: GPS coordinates of all
                                                    production plots

Country risk        Not formally classified          Formal 3-tier risk classification
classification      (Low/Standard/High)

Operator scope      Timber operators only            All operators placing 7 commodities
                                                    on EU market or exporting

Trader obligations  Limited                         Non-SME traders: operator-equivalent
                                                    SME traders: record-keeping

Penalties           Member State discretion         Minimum 4% of annual turnover
                    (inconsistent enforcement)

Monitoring system   Manual checks                   Electronic EUDR IS with customs
                                                    integration

Human rights        Not addressed                   Explicitly required in risk assessment
                                                    (FPIC, indigenous rights)

INTERNATIONAL RECEPTION AND DIPLOMATIC CONTEXT
================================================================

PRODUCER COUNTRY CONCERNS:
Several major producer countries have expressed concerns about EUDR implementation:
- Brazil: Largest affected exporter; concerns about smallholder compliance capacity;
  technical dialogues ongoing with EU
- Indonesia: Major palm oil and rubber exporter; engaged in bilateral technical discussions
- Cameroon, Cote d'Ivoire, Ghana: Concerns from African cocoa-producing nations about
  smallholder impacts
- Malaysia: Concerns about palm oil sector; raised at WTO and in bilateral negotiations

EU RESPONSE TO CONCERNS:
- EU clarified that the regulation applies equally to EU domestic production (no discrimination)
- Commission has provided country partnerships and capacity building funds
- Technical assistance programs launched for smallholder traceability systems
- Phased implementation timelines to allow adjustment

POTENTIAL TRADE DISPUTES:
- Several countries raised EUDR concerns in WTO technical barrier discussions
- EU position: The regulation is a non-discriminatory environmental measure consistent with WTO rules
- The WTO compatibility argument rests on EUDR applying to all operators, including EU domestic ones

================================================================
CIVIL SOCIETY AND NGO PERSPECTIVES
================================================================

NGO SUPPORT FOR EUDR:
- WWF, Greenpeace, Rainforest Alliance, and other major environmental organizations
  strongly supported the regulation's adoption
- Civil society pushed for broader scope (more commodities, other ecosystems)
- Ongoing advocacy for strong enforcement and meaningful penalties
- Concerns about certification scheme loopholes

NGO CONCERNS:
- Adequacy of enforcement resources in Member States
- Risk that smallholders bear disproportionate compliance burden
- Certification schemes accepted as insufficient by regulation but may still be over-relied upon
- Delayed application dates reduce urgency and allow continued deforestation

INDUSTRY RESPONSE:
- Large commodity traders (Bunge, Cargill, ADM, Louis Dreyfus) have invested in traceability systems
- Smaller and mid-tier traders face greater compliance costs
- Industry associations lobbied for extended implementation timelines
- Some sectors (cocoa, coffee) developed sector-wide compliance programs

================================================================
IMPLEMENTATION SUPPORT PROGRAMS
================================================================

EC-FUNDED PROGRAMS:
1. LIFE Programme: Funds environmental projects including deforestation monitoring
2. Global Europe (development cooperation): Supports producer country capacity building
3. EUDR Implementation Platform: Online business support tool

MEMBER STATE COMPETENT AUTHORITY READINESS:
- Member States had until 30 June 2024 to designate competent authorities
- Resources for enforcement vary significantly across Member States
- Large importing Member States (Germany, Netherlands, Belgium, France) are key enforcement
  nodes given port and commodity import volumes

KEY PORTS FOR EUDR ENFORCEMENT:
- Port of Rotterdam (Netherlands): Largest EU port; critical for soy, palm oil, timber imports
- Port of Antwerp (Belgium): Major commodity trading hub
- Port of Hamburg (Germany): Significant timber and coffee imports
- Port of Barcelona (Spain): Relevant for South American beef imports

================================================================
SCIENTIFIC BASIS: KEY STUDIES INFORMING THE REGULATION
================================================================

IMPACTS ASSESSMENT STUDIES:
The Commission's 2021 impact assessment for the EUDR cited:
- EU drives approximately 10% of global deforestation
- EU consumption of soy: 75% of EU demand linked to deforestation risk in South America
- EU consumption of palm oil: Significant contribution to Indonesian/Malaysian forest loss
- EU cocoa imports: ~40% of global cocoa imports; significant share from high-deforestation areas

PROJECTED BENEFITS:
- 71,920 km2 of forest saved annually if regulation achieves full compliance
- 32+ million tonnes CO2e annual emission reduction (minimum estimate)
- Biodiversity co-benefits: Protection of critical habitat for endangered species

ECONOMIC ANALYSIS:
- Compliance costs estimated at EUR 3.36-5.58 billion for first year across all operators
- Long-term benefits (ecosystem services, avoided climate damage) estimated to far outweigh costs
- Smallholder risks acknowledged; mitigation measures built into implementation approach
