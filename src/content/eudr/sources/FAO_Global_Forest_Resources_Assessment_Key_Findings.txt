================================================================
GLOBAL FOREST OVERVIEW: KEY STATISTICS
================================================================

TOTAL GLOBAL FOREST AREA:
- 4.06 billion hectares of forest globally (State of the World's Forests 2020)
- Forests cover approximately 31% of the world's total land area
- This equates to roughly 0.52 hectares of forest per person globally
- Roughly half of global forest area remains relatively intact
- Over one-third of global forest area is classified as primary forest

FOREST DISTRIBUTION BY COUNTRY:
Five countries hold more than half the world's forests:
1. Russian Federation
2. Brazil
3. Canada
4. United States of America
5. China
The top ten countries account for approximately two-thirds of all global forests.

FOREST TYPES:
- Tropical forests: Largest biome; predominantly in Latin America, Central Africa, Southeast Asia
- Boreal forests: Vast northern forests in Russia, Canada, Scandinavia
- Temperate forests: North America, Europe, East Asia
- Subtropical/Mediterranean forests: Smaller area; high biodiversity
- Mangrove forests: Coastal ecosystems critical for carbon storage and coastal protection
- Primary (old-growth) forests: 1.11 billion hectares; irreplaceable biodiversity reservoirs

================================================================
DEFORESTATION TRENDS AND RATES
================================================================

HISTORICAL DEFORESTATION (1990-2020):
- 420 million hectares of forest lost since 1990 through conversion to other land uses
- Equivalent to losing an area the size of the European Union since 1990

DEFORESTATION RATE BY DECADE:
- 1990s rate: Approximately 16 million hectares lost per year
- 2000s rate: Approximately 10 million hectares lost per year
- 2010s rate: Approximately 10 million hectares lost per year (2015-2020: officially 10 million ha/yr)
- 2020s rate: Varying; 2024 saw a record high of 30 million hectares of tree cover loss globally

RATE OF IMPROVEMENT (NET):
- Deforestation rate slowed over 30 years: from 16 million ha/yr to 10 million ha/yr
- However, 10 million ha/yr of loss is still extremely high by any absolute measure
- Net forest area change is partially offset by forest growth and planting in some regions
- Net loss: Approximately 4.7 million hectares per year in 2015-2020 (after accounting for gains)

PRIMARY FOREST LOSS:
- Over 80 million hectares of primary forest lost since 1990
- Primary forests are irreplaceable; once lost, they cannot be restored to original condition
  within human timescales

================================================================
MAIN DRIVERS OF DEFORESTATION
================================================================

AGRICULTURAL EXPANSION (DOMINANT DRIVER):
- Large-scale commercial agriculture: 40% of tropical deforestation (2000-2010)
- Local subsistence farming: 33% of tropical deforestation
- Combined agricultural expansion: approximately 73% of all tropical deforestation

SPECIFIC COMMODITY DRIVERS:
- Cattle ranching: Primary driver in Latin America, particularly Brazilian Amazon and Cerrado
- Soybean cultivation: Major driver in South American cerrado and Amazon frontier
- Palm oil: Primary driver of forest loss in Indonesia, Malaysia, and parts of Africa
- Cocoa: Primary driver of forest loss in West Africa (Cote d'Ivoire, Ghana)
- Coffee: Growing concern in Ethiopia, Uganda, and other East African highland forests
- Timber/logging: Enables agricultural access through road building; direct forest loss
- Rubber: Historically replaced natural forests in Southeast Asia

OTHER DRIVERS:
- Infrastructure development (roads, dams, mines)
- Urbanization
- Fire (natural and human-induced; increasingly linked to climate change)
- Overgrazing

================================================================
REGIONAL BREAKDOWN OF FOREST TRENDS
================================================================

AFRICA:
- Second-highest deforestation rate globally after South America
- Democratic Republic of Congo has the world's second-largest tropical rainforest
- Shifting cultivation and charcoal production are key drivers in Central Africa
- West Africa has lost the majority of its original forest cover; remnant forests under pressure

SOUTH AMERICA:
- Largest absolute area of tropical forest loss
- Amazon basin: World's largest remaining tropical rainforest
- Brazilian Amazon: Experienced high deforestation 2017-2020 (policy rollbacks)
- Cerrado (Brazilian savanna): Lost over 50% of native vegetation; high biodiversity

SOUTHEAST ASIA:
- High deforestation rates relative to forest area
- Indonesia and Malaysia: Palm oil and pulpwood plantations primary drivers
- Peatland destruction: Releases extremely high carbon per hectare
- Progress: Indonesia significantly reduced deforestation 2017-2024 through policy measures

EUROPE:
- Forest area is increasing in Europe (driven by afforestation and natural expansion)
- Forest quality concerns: Some primary forests in Eastern Europe under threat
- EU domestic forests protected; EUDR concerns international supply chain impacts

NORTH AMERICA:
- Relatively stable forest area but fire risk increasing dramatically
- Canada: Boreal forest faces increasing wildfire pressure
- Certification systems (FSC, SFI) widely adopted

================================================================
FORESTS AND BIODIVERSITY
================================================================

SPECIES RICHNESS IN FORESTS:
- Approximately 60,082 tree species documented globally
  - 45% belong to only ten plant families
  - 58% are single-country endemics (found nowhere else on Earth)
- Forests host an estimated 80% of the world's terrestrial biodiversity
- Tropical forests: 50%+ of all terrestrial species despite covering 10% of land area

VASCULAR PLANTS:
- Approximately 391,000 vascular plant species known
- 21% threatened with extinction; habitat loss is primary threat
- Forest clearing is the leading cause of plant species extinction

FUNGAL DIVERSITY:
- 144,000 named fungal species; estimated 2.2-3.8 million total species
- Mycorrhizal fungi essential for tree health; threatened by forest disturbance

VERTEBRATE DIVERSITY IN FORESTS:
- Approximately 70,000 vertebrate species globally
- Large proportion are forest-dependent
- Forest fragmentation isolates populations and reduces genetic diversity

PROTECTED FOREST AREAS:
- 18% of global forest area (700+ million hectares) in protected areas
- Regional variation: 31% in South America; 5% in Europe
- This exceeds the 17% Aichi Biodiversity Target for forests
- However, many protected forests are inadequately managed or enforced

================================================================
FORESTS AND HUMAN LIVELIHOODS
================================================================

EMPLOYMENT AND LIVELIHOODS:
- Over 86 million people work in formal forest-related jobs globally
- Approximately 880 million people collect fuelwood for cooking and heating
- Over 90% of people living in extreme poverty rely on forests for their livelihoods
- Indigenous peoples: An estimated 300-500 million people live in or depend directly on forests

FOOD SECURITY:
- Forests contribute to food security through timber, wild meat, fruits, nuts, and medicines
- Pollinators from forests support agricultural production in surrounding areas
- Water regulation by forests supports downstream agriculture

INDIGENOUS PEOPLES AND FORESTS:
- Indigenous peoples' territories cover approximately 22% of the world's land surface
- These lands hold approximately 80% of the world's remaining biodiversity
- Indigenous-managed territories often have lower deforestation rates than national parks
- Free, prior and informed consent (FPIC) is a critical principle recognized in the EUDR

================================================================
FOREST ECOSYSTEM SERVICES AND CLIMATE
================================================================

CARBON STORAGE:
- Forests store an estimated 662 billion tonnes of carbon globally (biomass and soil)
- Tropical forests: Approximately 471 Gt C stored
- Boreal forests: Approximately 272 Gt C stored (much in permafrost soil)

CARBON FLUX:
- Intact forests absorb approximately 2.6 Gt CO2 per year from the atmosphere
- Degraded and deforested areas release 4.8 Gt CO2 per year
- Net: Tropical forests may have become a small net carbon source due to degradation

WATER CYCLE REGULATION:
- Forests transpire enormous quantities of water, creating "flying rivers" that deliver
  rainfall to distant regions
- Amazon forests generate approximately 20 billion tonnes of water vapor per day
- Loss of Amazon forest could reduce rainfall by 20% in major agricultural regions of
  South America including soy and coffee production areas

EUDR RELEVANCE OF FOREST ECOSYSTEM SERVICES:
The EUDR's protection of forests thus protects not only carbon and biodiversity but also
the water and soil stability on which future agricultural production depends.

FAO FOREST RESOURCES ASSESSMENT 2020: REMOTE SENSING SURVEY
================================================================

Publication: FAO Forestry Paper No. 186, 2022
DOI: https://doi.org/10.4060/cb9970en
Coverage: Global; comparing land use dynamics for years 2000, 2010, and 2018

METHODOLOGY:
- The most comprehensive global land use monitoring exercise ever conducted
- 800+ local experts from 126 countries and territories participated
- Approximately 400,000 sample points collected between 2019 and 2020
- Physical and virtual workshops (34 total) trained national experts

KEY DISTINCTIONS MADE:
The survey differentiates between:
- Deforestation: Permanent loss of forest to other land use
- Forest degradation: Loss of forest quality without full conversion
- Forest disturbance: Temporary loss (from fire, storms) with recovery expected
- Afforestation/reforestation: Gain of forest on previously non-forested land

This distinction is critical for EUDR compliance, which covers both deforestation
AND forest degradation (when relevant to wood products).

GLOBAL LAND USE DYNAMICS (2000-2018):
- Deforestation rates were highest in tropical South America and Central Africa
- Some regions showed forest recovery; net loss continued globally
- Plantation forests expanded but did not compensate for natural forest loss
- Primary forests continued to decline in all tropical regions

================================================================
IMPLICATIONS FOR EUDR COMPLIANCE
================================================================

THE BASELINE PROBLEM:
The EUDR's cut-off date of 31 December 2020 requires operators to prove that land was NOT
deforested after that date. However:
- Reliable land use data for all production areas globally is not uniformly available
- Small-scale deforestation may not be captured in national statistics
- Some countries lack robust forest monitoring systems

HOW FAO DATA HELPS:
- FAO FRA provides country-level deforestation rate data usable in risk assessment
- Countries with high FAO-documented deforestation rates are more likely to be classified
  as high-risk under EUDR's benchmarking system
- FAO data supports EU Observatory's risk maps and country profiles

GAPS AND CHALLENGES:
- FAO data is reported at national level; sub-national variation can be significant
- National reporting quality varies; some countries have limited monitoring capacity
- The EUDR's plot-level geolocation requirement goes beyond what national-level data provides
- Remote sensing (satellite imagery) is essential to complement FAO statistics for plot-level
  compliance verification
