================================================================
2024 HEADLINE FINDINGS: RECORD TROPICAL FOREST LOSS
================================================================

PRIMARY FOREST LOSS - GLOBAL RECORD:
- The tropics lost 6.7 million hectares of primary rainforest in 2024
- This is an area nearly the size of Panama
- Loss increased 80% compared to 2023 levels
- Rate: 18 football (soccer) fields of primary forest lost per minute in 2024
- 2024 nearly doubled the rate of forest loss compared to 2023

GREENHOUSE GAS EMISSIONS FROM PRIMARY FOREST LOSS (2024):
- 3.1 gigatonnes (Gt) of CO2 equivalent emitted
- Equivalent to India's entire annual fossil fuel CO2 emissions

GLOBAL TREE COVER LOSS (all forests, not just primary):
- 30 million hectares of global tree cover lost in 2024
- Highest on record ever documented by Global Forest Watch
- 5% increase compared to 2023

================================================================
FIRE AS THE DOMINANT DRIVER IN 2024
================================================================

FIRE-DRIVEN DEFORESTATION (unprecedented scale in 2024):
- Wildfires burned 5 times more tropical primary forest in 2024 than in 2023
- Wildfires accounted for almost half (approximately 48%) of all tropical primary forest loss in 2024
- 2024 was the hottest year on record, with El Nino conditions amplifying drought and fire risk
- Global fire emissions: 4.1 Gt CO2e in 2024
  - Equivalent to more than 4 times the annual emissions from global air travel in 2023

NON-FIRE PRIMARY FOREST LOSS:
- Non-fire primary forest loss also increased 14% between 2023 and 2024
- Agricultural conversion remains the dominant non-fire driver
- Cattle ranching, soy cultivation, and other commodity crops are primary causes

================================================================
REGIONAL HOTSPOTS AND COUNTRY DATA
================================================================

BRAZIL (largest single contributor):
- Responsible for 42% of total tropical primary rainforest loss in 2024
- Amazon biome experienced a 110% increase in primary forest loss from 2023 to 2024
- 60% of Amazon loss was fire-driven in 2024
- Pantanal wetland lost 1.6% of its tree cover (more than double Brazil's national average rate)
- 2024 saw one of Brazil's worst fire seasons on record despite reduced deforestation in the
  Amazon from clearing (non-fire deforestation continued to decline due to policy enforcement)

BOLIVIA (second highest globally):
- 200% increase in primary forest loss in 2024
- Surpassed the Democratic Republic of Congo in absolute loss despite having only 40% of its
  forest area
- Almost 12% of Bolivia's total land area burned in 2024
- Chiquitano dry forest particularly affected

DEMOCRATIC REPUBLIC OF CONGO AND REPUBLIC OF CONGO:
- Both countries experienced their highest primary forest loss on record in 2024
- DRC remains the country with the largest area of primary tropical forest outside of Brazil
- Main drivers: shifting cultivation, charcoal production, timber removal, smallholder agriculture

LATIN AMERICA - ADDITIONAL COUNTRIES:
- Guatemala: Lost 2.7% of its primary forest in 2024
- Nicaragua: Highest percentage primary forest loss in the region at 4.7%
- Mexico: Tropical primary forest loss nearly doubled compared to 2023
- Peru: 135% increase in fire-related forest loss
- Guyana: Four-fold increase in primary forest loss (largely fire-driven)

POSITIVE DEVELOPMENTS:
- Indonesia: 11% decrease in primary forest loss in 2024 (attributed to strong government policy)
- Malaysia: 13% reduction in primary forest loss; dropped out of the global top 10 for the
  first time since monitoring began

================================================================
LONG-TERM TRENDS AND HISTORICAL CONTEXT (2000-2024)
================================================================

24-YEAR TREND (2000-2024):
- Forest clearing for permanent agriculture has been the largest driver of tropical primary
  forest loss over the 24-year monitoring period
- Commodity crops (cattle, soy, palm oil) account for the majority of agricultural-driven loss
- Fire has become an increasingly significant driver, particularly since 2015

EUDR-RELEVANT BASELINE (pre-2021 context):
- The EUDR cut-off date of 31 December 2020 was set against a backdrop of sustained high
  deforestation rates throughout 2017-2020
- Brazil: Deforestation peaked in 2019-2020 under weakened environmental enforcement
- Commodity-driven deforestation: Remained the dominant driver in South America and Southeast Asia
- Forest loss associated with cattle ranching, soy cultivation, and palm oil plantations was
  highest in the years immediately before the cut-off date

DEFORESTATION DRIVERS LINKED TO EUDR COMMODITIES (2001-2024 aggregate):
- Commodity-driven deforestation: Primary cause in Latin America and Southeast Asia
- Cattle ranching: Dominant driver in Brazilian Amazon
- Soybean cultivation: Significant in Cerrado and Amazon frontier areas
- Palm oil: Primary driver in Indonesian and Malaysian forests
- Cocoa: Significant driver in West African forests (Ghana, Cote d'Ivoire)
- Coffee: Growing concern in East African highland forests
- Rubber: Historically significant in Southeast Asia

================================================================
FORESTS AND CLIMATE: THE CARBON SINK CRISIS
================================================================

PRIMARY FORESTS AS CARBON SINKS:
- Tropical primary forests store an estimated 250 billion tonnes of carbon
- When primary forests are lost, they can shift from carbon sinks to carbon sources
- Forest degradation (not just outright clearing) releases significant carbon through
  reduced carbon sequestration capacity

ANNUAL CARBON LOSS RATES FROM DEFORESTATION:
- In 2024: 3.1 Gt CO2e released from tropical primary forest loss alone
- This does not include emissions from degraded (but not cleared) forests
- Forest loss contributes approximately 10% of global annual greenhouse gas emissions

THE DEGRADATION-FIRE FEEDBACK LOOP:
- Deforestation fragments forests, creating drier conditions at forest edges
- Dry conditions increase fire risk
- Fires kill trees and degrade forest structure, making remaining forests more fire-prone
- A "savannization" process can occur, transforming rainforest into drier ecosystem types

================================================================
MEETING THE GLASGOW DECLARATION GOALS
================================================================

GLASGOW FOREST DECLARATION (2021):
- 145 countries pledged to halt and reverse deforestation and land degradation by 2030
- The EU is a signatory through its Glasgow commitments

PROGRESS ASSESSMENT:
- To meet 2030 goals, annual forest loss must fall 20% each year from 2024 levels
- Of the 20 countries with the largest primary forests:
  17 have HIGHER primary forest loss today than when the Glasgow Declaration was signed in 2021
- 2024 data represents a significant setback to 2030 deforestation goals

WHAT THIS MEANS FOR EUDR:
- The urgency of the EUDR is underscored by accelerating deforestation
- Market-side measures (like the EUDR) are critical complements to producer-country policies
- The 2024 data shows that voluntary commitments alone are insufficient

================================================================
DEFORESTATION AND COMMODITY SUPPLY CHAINS
================================================================

GLOBAL SUPPLY CHAIN LINKAGES:
- Approximately 80% of tropical deforestation is driven by agricultural expansion
- The European Union imports significant volumes of all seven EUDR-covered commodities
- Key import origins for EU consumers:

  CATTLE/BEEF: Brazil, Argentina, Uruguay (South America primary)
  COCOA: Cote d'Ivoire, Ghana (together ~60% of world production)
  COFFEE: Brazil, Vietnam, Colombia, Ethiopia
  PALM OIL: Indonesia, Malaysia (together ~85% of world production)
  RUBBER: Thailand, Indonesia, Vietnam
  SOYA: Brazil, Argentina, Paraguay, USA
  WOOD/TIMBER: Multiple origins including tropical and temperate forests globally

CERTIFICATION SYSTEM COVERAGE (pre-EUDR baseline):
- Less than 20% of globally traded deforestation-risk commodities were covered by
  independent third-party sustainability certification as of 2021
- Certification uptake was uneven across commodities and geographies
- Voluntary certification proved insufficient to halt deforestation

================================================================
BIODIVERSITY DIMENSION
================================================================

FORESTS AND BIODIVERSITY:
- Tropical forests contain over half of the world's terrestrial species
- Primary forests harbor significantly more biodiversity than secondary or plantation forests
- Even partial forest degradation (not full clearing) can disrupt biodiversity

EXTINCTION RISK FROM DEFORESTATION:
- An estimated 15,000 plant species face extinction risk from habitat loss
- 70% of terrestrial vertebrates depend on forests for habitat
- Forest fragmentation isolates populations, reducing genetic diversity

EUDR AND BIODIVERSITY:
The regulation explicitly aims to reduce biodiversity loss associated with deforestation.
Protecting primary forests is the most effective biodiversity conservation measure because:
- Primary forests have developed complex ecosystem structures over centuries or millennia
- They contain irreplaceable endemic species
- They provide ecosystem services (water cycling, pollination, pest control) that support
  agricultural production in surrounding areas

================================================================
DATA METHODOLOGY NOTES
================================================================

Global Forest Watch uses:
- Satellite imagery from Landsat and Sentinel satellites
- Tree cover loss data defined as stand-replacement disturbance (canopy cover below 30% threshold)
- Primary forest data from Turubanova et al. 2018 and updated annually
- Fire data from FIRMS (Fire Information for Resource Management System)

IMPORTANT DISTINCTION:
"Tree cover loss" is not synonymous with "deforestation":
- Loss includes fire, logging, disease, and temporary land clearing
- Deforestation refers specifically to permanent conversion to non-forest use
- For EUDR compliance, the key metric is permanent conversion after 31 December 2020

LIMITATIONS:
- Satellite data has minimum detectable area thresholds
- Cloud cover in tropical regions can affect annual estimates
- Some forest degradation (not full clearing) is not captured in tree cover loss metrics
