<h2 class="text-2xl font-bold text-gray-800 mb-4">Seven Commodities: Cattle, Cocoa, Coffee, Oil Palm, Rubber, Soya, Wood</h2>

<HighlightBox>
  <p class="font-semibold text-green-800">Why these seven?</p>
  <p class="text-green-900 mt-1">The EUDR's seven covered commodities were selected because they collectively account for the vast majority of agricultural deforestation linked to EU consumption. These are not arbitrary choices: each commodity has documented evidence of driving significant forest clearance in major producing regions, and the EU is a significant market for all of them.</p>
</HighlightBox>

<h3 class="text-xl font-bold mt-6 mb-3">The Annex I Logic: Commodities and Their Products</h3>
<p class="mb-3">The EUDR operates on two levels. At the top level are the seven <strong>relevant commodities</strong>: the raw agricultural or forestry outputs that are associated with deforestation. Below them are the <strong>relevant products</strong> listed in Annex I: processed goods that contain, have been fed with, or have been made using those commodities. Both the raw commodity and the derived product trigger compliance obligations.</p>
<p class="mb-4">This two-level structure is essential to the regulation's effectiveness. If only raw commodities were covered, a company could import raw soy beans, process them into meal and oil within the EU, and sell the processed products without any traceability to the original farm. By covering processed products, the EUDR closes this gap and ensures that value chain processing does not break the compliance chain.</p>

<h3 class="text-xl font-bold mt-6 mb-3">Cattle and Cattle Products</h3>
<p class="mb-3">Cattle ranching is the single largest driver of deforestation in the Brazilian Amazon and Cerrado. When a forest is cleared for pasture, the cattle production that follows is linked to that deforestation event even if the animals are sold years later to different farms.</p>
<p class="mb-4">The EUDR covers not just live cattle and fresh or frozen bovine meat, but an extensive range of derived products:</p>
<ul class="list-disc ml-6 space-y-2 mb-4">
  <li>Leather and tanned leather products</li>
  <li>Leather footwear</li>
  <li>Collagen and gelatine derived from bovine hides</li>
  <li>Other products containing bovine leather as a significant component</li>
</ul>
<p class="mb-4">This means that luxury goods brands selling leather handbags, footwear manufacturers, and food companies using bovine gelatine are all within scope. The supply chain complexity for cattle is particularly high because animals frequently move between multiple farms before slaughter, and the hides travel a separate path from the meat, often to tanneries in different countries.</p>

<h3 class="text-xl font-bold mt-6 mb-3">Cocoa and Cocoa Products</h3>
<p class="mb-3">Cote d'Ivoire and Ghana together produce approximately 60% of the world's cocoa, much of it from areas that were forest within living memory. Cocoa farming expansion has been identified as the primary driver of deforestation in West Africa for several decades. The EU imports approximately 40% of global cocoa production.</p>
<p class="mb-4">Covered products include:</p>
<ul class="list-disc ml-6 space-y-2 mb-4">
  <li>Cocoa beans and husks</li>
  <li>Cocoa paste, butter, and powder</li>
  <li>Chocolate and chocolate products (including confectionery containing cocoa)</li>
</ul>

<h3 class="text-xl font-bold mt-6 mb-3">Coffee and Coffee Products</h3>
<p class="mb-3">Coffee is grown across tropical and subtropical regions, including Brazil, Vietnam, Colombia, Ethiopia, and Honduras. In Ethiopia, the birthplace of coffee, farming expansion threatens highland forests of exceptional biodiversity value. Vietnam's coffee boom drove significant forest loss in its central highlands. Uganda and other East African producers are experiencing growing pressure on forest lands from coffee cultivation.</p>
<p class="mb-4">Covered products include:</p>
<ul class="list-disc ml-6 space-y-2 mb-4">
  <li>Raw (green) coffee beans and roasted coffee</li>
  <li>Coffee extracts, essences, and concentrates</li>
  <li>Preparations and mixtures containing coffee</li>
</ul>

<h3 class="text-xl font-bold mt-6 mb-3">Oil Palm and Palm-Derived Products</h3>
<p class="mb-3">Palm oil is the world's most widely consumed vegetable oil and has been the primary driver of deforestation in Indonesia and Malaysia, which together account for approximately 85% of global palm oil production. Peatland forests destroyed for palm oil plantations release extraordinary quantities of stored carbon per hectare cleared.</p>
<p class="mb-4">Palm oil's pervasiveness in processed foods, cosmetics, and biofuels makes this one of the most complex supply chains under the EUDR. Covered products include:</p>
<ul class="list-disc ml-6 space-y-2 mb-4">
  <li>Palm oil and palm kernel oil</li>
  <li>Palm fatty acids and crude glycerol</li>
  <li>Margarine, shortening, and similar products</li>
  <li>Other food products containing palm oil as an ingredient</li>
  <li>Cosmetic and personal care products containing palm-derived ingredients</li>
</ul>

<h3 class="text-xl font-bold mt-6 mb-3">Rubber and Rubber Products</h3>
<p class="mb-3">Natural rubber is produced primarily in Thailand, Indonesia, and Vietnam from the Hevea brasiliensis tree. Rubber plantation expansion historically replaced natural forests across Southeast Asia. Thailand, Indonesia, and Vietnam together account for approximately 70% of global natural rubber production.</p>
<p class="mb-4">Covered products include:</p>
<ul class="list-disc ml-6 space-y-2 mb-4">
  <li>Natural rubber and latex</li>
  <li>Rubber tyres and inner tubes</li>
  <li>Vulcanised and hardened rubber products</li>
  <li>Rubber gloves, tubes, and other articles</li>
</ul>
<p class="mb-4">The inclusion of rubber tyres is particularly notable, drawing automotive manufacturers directly into the EUDR's compliance framework.</p>

<h3 class="text-xl font-bold mt-6 mb-3">Soya and Soy-Derived Products</h3>
<p class="mb-3">Soybean cultivation is the second-largest driver of deforestation in Latin America after cattle ranching, and the two are closely linked: soy is grown partly to feed the cattle that occupy cleared land further into the frontier. Brazil, Argentina, and Paraguay are the dominant producing countries for EU imports.</p>
<p class="mb-4">Covered products include:</p>
<ul class="list-disc ml-6 space-y-2 mb-4">
  <li>Soya beans and soya bean oil and meal</li>
  <li>Animal feed preparations containing soya</li>
  <li>Soya flour, groats, and textured soya protein</li>
  <li>Other food products where soya is a significant ingredient</li>
</ul>

<h3 class="text-xl font-bold mt-6 mb-3">Wood and Wood-Derived Products</h3>
<p class="mb-3">Wood was already covered by the predecessor EU Timber Regulation, which required operators to verify that timber had been legally harvested. The EUDR raises the bar: it requires not just legality but proof that wood was harvested without inducing forest degradation after 31 December 2020. The definition for wood differs slightly from the other commodities: it must be shown that harvesting did not cause forest degradation, not just deforestation.</p>
<p class="mb-4">The coverage of wood-derived products is the most extensive of any commodity group:</p>
<ul class="list-disc ml-6 space-y-2 mb-4">
  <li>Sawn timber, veneer, plywood, and particle board</li>
  <li>Pulp, paper, and paperboard</li>
  <li>Furniture containing wood</li>
  <li>Printed books, newspapers, and periodicals</li>
  <li>Wooden packaging materials</li>
  <li>Palm hearts (grouped with wood for regulatory purposes in Annex I)</li>
</ul>

<ExampleBox>
  <p class="font-semibold text-amber-800">A published novel and a car tyre: both within EUDR scope</p>
  <p class="mt-2">A European book publisher printing novels on paper sourced from wood pulp must ensure that the wood used to make that pulp did not come from forests degraded after 31 December 2020. A European car tyre manufacturer sourcing natural rubber from Indonesian or Thai plantations must ensure those plantations were not established on forest land cleared after that date. The EUDR's product scope reaches into sectors that many businesses did not initially consider forest-related at all.</p>
</ExampleBox>

<DeepDive title="Are certification schemes (FSC, RSPO, Rainforest Alliance) sufficient for EUDR compliance?">
  <p>No certification scheme alone provides automatic EUDR compliance. The regulation makes this explicit in its Recitals and FAQ. Certified products and supply chains can serve as one element of evidence within a due diligence system. Certification can reduce the risk assessment workload by providing independently verified information about production practices. However, operators must still:</p>
  <ul class="list-disc ml-4 mt-2 space-y-1">
    <li>Collect geolocation coordinates of all production plots.</li>
    <li>Conduct a risk assessment using the information collected.</li>
    <li>Submit a due diligence statement before placing products on the market.</li>
  </ul>
  <p class="mt-2">The Commission has indicated it may recognise certain certification schemes as providing supporting evidence in the risk assessment step, but this recognition does not substitute for the information collection and statement submission requirements. An operator cannot simply present an FSC or RSPO certificate and consider their obligations fulfilled.</p>
</DeepDive>
