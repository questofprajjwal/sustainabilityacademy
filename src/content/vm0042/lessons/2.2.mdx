<h2 class="text-2xl font-bold text-gray-800 mb-4">The Baseline Scenario: What Would Have Happened Without the Project?</h2>
<HighlightBox>
  <p class="font-semibold text-green-800">The counterfactual</p>
  <p class="text-green-900 mt-1">The baseline scenario represents the <strong>continuation of pre-project farming practices</strong>, what would have kept happening on this land if no carbon project existed. Carbon credits are calculated as the <em>difference</em> between project performance and baseline. A stronger baseline = more credits (all else equal).</p>
</HighlightBox>

<div class="bg-violet-50 border-2 border-violet-300 rounded-lg p-5 mb-6">
  <h4 class="font-bold text-violet-900 text-lg mb-2">Why Does the Baseline Matter So Much?</h4>
  <p class="text-sm text-violet-900 mb-3">The baseline is the single most important number in a carbon credit project. Without it, you have no way to prove that your project actually made a difference. Here is why:</p>

  <p class="text-sm font-semibold text-violet-800 mb-2">The core formula behind every carbon credit:</p>

  <EquationBreakdown
    title="Carbon Credit Equation"
    result="Credits | Carbon Credits Earned | The net emission reductions or removals that can be issued as VCUs, measured in tCO₂e | blue"
    inputs="Baseline | Baseline Emissions | The greenhouse gases the farm would have released (or carbon it would not have stored) under pre-project practices, in tCO₂e | green ;; Project | Project Emissions | The greenhouse gases the farm actually releases after adopting new practices, in tCO₂e | amber ;; Leakage | Leakage | Emissions that shifted outside the project boundary as a result of the project activity, in tCO₂e | violet ;; Uncertainty | Uncertainty Deduction | A conservative deduction reflecting measurement and model imprecision, in tCO₂e | rose"
    operator="-"
  />

  <p class="text-sm text-violet-900 mt-4 mb-2">In plain language: you only get credits for the <strong>improvement</strong> over what would have happened anyway. The baseline is the "would have happened anyway" part. Let's break this down:</p>

  <ul class="space-y-3 text-sm text-violet-900 mb-4">
    <li><strong>Baseline emissions</strong> = the greenhouse gases the farm <em>would have</em> released (or the carbon it <em>would not have</em> stored) if the farmer had kept doing what they were doing before. For example: "This farm was ploughing 3 times a year, applying 150 kg N/ha, and burning stubble, producing an estimated 3.2 tCO₂e/ha/yr."</li>
    <li><strong>Project emissions</strong> = the greenhouse gases the farm <em>actually</em> releases after adopting new practices. For example: "After switching to no-till and reducing fertilizer, the farm now emits 1.4 tCO₂e/ha/yr."</li>
    <li><strong>The difference</strong> = 3.2 - 1.4 = <strong>1.8 tCO₂e/ha/yr</strong>. That difference (minus leakage and uncertainty deductions) is what gets issued as carbon credits.</li>
  </ul>

  <AnalogyBox>
    <p class="font-semibold text-blue-800">Analogy: Your Electricity Bill</p>
    <p class="mt-1 text-sm text-blue-900">Imagine your electricity bill was 5,000/month before you installed solar panels. After installation, it dropped to 2,000/month. Your "savings" are 3,000/month, but only because you can prove what you <em>were</em> paying before. If you had no old bills to show, you could not prove you saved anything. The baseline is your old electricity bill. Without it, there is no way to calculate the improvement, and no credits can be issued.</p>
  </AnalogyBox>

  <p class="text-sm font-semibold text-violet-800 mt-4 mb-2">Why "a stronger baseline = more credits":</p>
  <p class="text-sm text-violet-900 mb-2">The <strong>bigger the gap</strong> between the old practices (baseline) and the new practices (project), the more credits you earn. Consider two farms that both switch to no-till:</p>
  <ResponsiveTable>
<table class="text-sm mb-3">
    <tr><th>Farm</th><th>Baseline Emissions</th><th>Project Emissions</th><th>Difference (Credits)</th></tr>
    <tr><td><strong>Farm A</strong>, was ploughing heavily, burning stubble, heavy fertilizer</td><td>4.5 tCO₂e/ha/yr</td><td>1.5 tCO₂e/ha/yr</td><td class="text-green-700 font-bold">3.0 tCO₂e/ha/yr</td></tr>
    <tr><td><strong>Farm B</strong>, was already doing reduced tillage and moderate fertilizer</td><td>2.0 tCO₂e/ha/yr</td><td>1.5 tCO₂e/ha/yr</td><td class="text-green-700 font-bold">0.5 tCO₂e/ha/yr</td></tr>
  </table>
</ResponsiveTable>
  <p class="text-sm text-violet-900">Both farms end up at the same project emissions, but Farm A earns <strong>6x more credits</strong> because its starting point (baseline) was much worse. This is why the baseline must be documented carefully and honestly, it directly determines how many credits you can claim.</p>

  <div class="bg-violet-100 rounded p-3 mt-4 text-sm text-violet-900">
    <p class="font-semibold mb-1">How the baseline feeds into credit registration:</p>
    <ol class="list-decimal pl-5 space-y-1">
      <li>You document the baseline (this lesson) and submit it in the <strong>Project Description Document (PDD)</strong>.</li>
      <li>An independent auditor (VVB) checks whether your baseline data is real, correctly sourced, and conservative.</li>
      <li>At each verification event (every 1-5 years), the auditor compares your <strong>actual project emissions</strong> against the <strong>baseline estimate</strong>.</li>
      <li>The difference (after leakage and uncertainty deductions) is submitted to <strong>Verra</strong>, which issues that many VCUs (Verified Carbon Units), each VCU = 1 tonne of CO₂e.</li>
      <li>Those VCUs are listed on the Verra registry, where corporate buyers purchase and retire them to offset their own emissions.</li>
    </ol>
    <p class="mt-2 font-medium">In short: <strong>no baseline - no difference - no credits - no revenue</strong>. Everything starts here.</p>
  </div>
</div>

<h3 class="text-xl font-bold mt-6 mb-3">How the Baseline Is Built</h3>
<p class="mb-3">Before you can measure improvement, you need to establish a starting point. The baseline asks: "What would this land have done if no one had intervened?"</p>
<p class="mb-4">For each quantification unit, the baseline is constructed by documenting farming practices over the <strong>historical look-back period</strong> (minimum 3 years, must include at least one complete crop rotation).</p>

<div class="bg-gray-50 border rounded p-4 mb-6">
  <p class="font-semibold text-gray-800 mb-3">Step-by-Step: Building a Schedule of Activities</p>
  <div class="space-y-3 text-sm">
    <div class="flex gap-3"><span class="bg-green-700 text-white rounded-full w-6 h-6 flex items-center justify-center flex-shrink-0 font-bold">1</span><div><strong>Define the look-back period:</strong> Set t = -x to t = -1 (the years before project start). Minimum x = 3 years.</div></div>
    <div class="flex gap-3"><span class="bg-green-700 text-white rounded-full w-6 h-6 flex items-center justify-center flex-shrink-0 font-bold">2</span><div><strong>Collect data on ALM practices</strong> for each year in the look-back period, for each QU.</div></div>
    <div class="flex gap-3"><span class="bg-green-700 text-white rounded-full w-6 h-6 flex items-center justify-center flex-shrink-0 font-bold">3</span><div><strong>Create the annual schedule:</strong> Document practices for t=-1, t=-2, t=-3, etc. This schedule repeats through the first baseline period.</div></div>
    <div class="flex gap-3"><span class="bg-green-700 text-white rounded-full w-6 h-6 flex items-center justify-center flex-shrink-0 font-bold">4</span><div><strong>Model or measure baseline emissions/SOC</strong> using this schedule (Approach 1, 2, or 3).</div></div>
    <div class="flex gap-3"><span class="bg-green-700 text-white rounded-full w-6 h-6 flex items-center justify-center flex-shrink-0 font-bold">5</span><div><strong>Reassess baseline</strong> every 10 years (or every 5 years if regional practices change rapidly).</div></div>
  </div>
</div>

<div class="bg-indigo-50 border-2 border-indigo-300 rounded-lg p-5 mb-6">
  <h4 class="font-bold text-indigo-900 text-lg mb-2">What Exactly Is the "Schedule of Activities"?</h4>
  <p class="text-sm text-indigo-900 mb-3">This term comes up repeatedly in VM0042. Think of it as a <strong>detailed farming calendar</strong>, a month-by-month (or season-by-season) record of everything a farmer does on their land in a typical year. It is the foundation of the entire baseline: without it, you cannot model or measure what emissions <em>would have been</em>.</p>

  <p class="text-sm font-semibold text-indigo-800 mb-2">What the schedule must cover:</p>
  <div class="grid grid-cols-1 md:grid-cols-2 gap-3 text-sm mb-4">
    <div class="bg-white rounded p-3 border border-indigo-200">
      <p class="font-semibold text-indigo-800 mb-1">Crops & Planting</p>
      <ul class="text-indigo-900 space-y-1 list-disc pl-4">
        <li>What crop is planted and when (e.g., "wheat sown in November")</li>
        <li>Crop varieties used</li>
        <li>Harvest date and what happens to the residue (burned, retained, removed)</li>
        <li>Any fallow periods, and how long the land sits empty</li>
      </ul>
    </div>
    <div class="bg-white rounded p-3 border border-indigo-200">
      <p class="font-semibold text-indigo-800 mb-1">Tillage & Soil Work</p>
      <ul class="text-indigo-900 space-y-1 list-disc pl-4">
        <li>Type of tillage (conventional plough, disc, chisel, or none)</li>
        <li>Number of passes per season</li>
        <li>Depth of tillage (e.g., 15 cm, 25 cm)</li>
        <li>Timing of each pass (e.g., "disc after harvest in April, plough before sowing in October")</li>
      </ul>
    </div>
    <div class="bg-white rounded p-3 border border-indigo-200">
      <p class="font-semibold text-indigo-800 mb-1">Fertilizers & Amendments</p>
      <ul class="text-indigo-900 space-y-1 list-disc pl-4">
        <li>Type of fertilizer (urea, DAP, compost, manure)</li>
        <li>Application rate (kg/ha) and timing</li>
        <li>Whether lime is applied, and how much</li>
        <li>Any organic amendments (compost, biochar, crop residue incorporation)</li>
      </ul>
    </div>
    <div class="bg-white rounded p-3 border border-indigo-200">
      <p class="font-semibold text-indigo-800 mb-1">Water & Livestock</p>
      <ul class="text-indigo-900 space-y-1 list-disc pl-4">
        <li>Irrigation method (flood, drip, rainfed) and schedule</li>
        <li>For rice: flooding duration and drainage events</li>
        <li>If livestock graze the land: stocking rate (heads/ha), grazing pattern, and season</li>
        <li>Manure management (left on field, collected, composted)</li>
      </ul>
    </div>
  </div>

  <AnalogyBox>
    <p class="font-semibold text-blue-800">Analogy: A Cooking Recipe for the Farm</p>
    <p class="mt-1 text-sm text-blue-900">Imagine you want to know how many calories someone ate last year. You would not just ask "what do you eat?", you would need a week-by-week meal plan: <em>what</em> was eaten, <em>how much</em>, and <em>when</em>. The schedule of activities is exactly that, but for the farm. It tells the model: "In January, the field was fallow. In March, the farmer ploughed at 20 cm depth. In April, 120 kg/ha urea was applied. In May, wheat was sown..." and so on through the year. This level of detail is what allows the biogeochemical model (Approach 1) or the IPCC calculations (Approach 3) to estimate emissions accurately.</p>
  </AnalogyBox>

  <p class="text-sm text-indigo-900 mt-3"><strong>Key point:</strong> The schedule is built from the look-back period (the 3-5 years before the project started). If the farmer grew rice-wheat in rotation, the schedule captures <em>both</em> seasons. This schedule then <strong>repeats on loop</strong> for the entire baseline period, the assumption being that without the project, the farmer would have kept doing the same thing year after year.</p>

  <ExampleBox>
    <p class="font-semibold text-amber-800">Sample Schedule: Rice-Wheat Farm in Uttar Pradesh</p>
    <ResponsiveTable>
<table class="mt-2 text-sm">
      <tr><th>Month</th><th>Activity</th><th>Details</th></tr>
      <tr><td>Jun</td><td>Rice transplanting</td><td>Paddy flooded, variety: Pusa Basmati 1121</td></tr>
      <tr><td>Jun-Oct</td><td>Continuous flooding</td><td>Field stays waterlogged for ~120 days</td></tr>
      <tr><td>Jul</td><td>Urea top-dress</td><td>60 kg N/ha applied</td></tr>
      <tr><td>Oct</td><td>Rice harvest</td><td>Stubble burned on field</td></tr>
      <tr><td>Nov</td><td>Tillage for wheat</td><td>2 passes, disc + cultivator at 15 cm</td></tr>
      <tr><td>Nov</td><td>Wheat sowing + DAP</td><td>Variety: HD-2967; 60 kg N/ha as DAP at sowing</td></tr>
      <tr><td>Jan</td><td>Urea top-dress</td><td>60 kg N/ha applied to wheat</td></tr>
      <tr><td>Apr</td><td>Wheat harvest</td><td>Straw removed for animal feed</td></tr>
      <tr><td>May</td><td>Fallow + tillage</td><td>1 plough pass at 20 cm before rice season</td></tr>
    </table>
</ResponsiveTable>
    <p class="mt-2 text-sm text-amber-700">This schedule tells the model everything it needs: <strong>total N applied</strong> (180 kg/ha/yr across both crops), <strong>flooding duration</strong> (120 days, needed for CH₄ calculation), <strong>tillage intensity</strong> (3 passes/yr, needed for SOC and fossil fuel calculations), and <strong>residue fate</strong> (rice stubble burned, wheat straw removed). Without any one of these details, the baseline estimate would be incomplete.</p>
  </ExampleBox>
</div>

<h3 class="text-xl font-bold mt-6 mb-3">The Data Hierarchy (Box 1 of VM0042)</h3>
<p class="mb-4">VM0042 specifies a ranked hierarchy for data used to establish baseline practices. Higher-tier data = less uncertainty = more credits. Auditors will scrutinise which tier was used and may require you to justify why higher-tier data was unavailable:</p>
<ResponsiveTable>
<table class="mb-4">
  <tr><th>Tier</th><th>Data Source</th><th>Example</th><th>Preference</th></tr>
  <tr><td><strong>1 (Best)</strong></td><td>Historical management records + documented evidence</td><td>Farm management logs, purchase receipts, GPS field records, satellite imagery</td><td class="text-green-700">Most preferred</td></tr>
  <tr><td><strong>2</strong></td><td>Historical management plans with written documentation</td><td>Agronomist recommendations, management plan documents</td><td class="text-blue-700">Preferred</td></tr>
  <tr><td><strong>3</strong></td><td>Signed attestation from farmer/landowner</td><td>Farmer statement supported by evidence from similar nearby fields</td><td class="text-amber-700">Acceptable</td></tr>
  <tr><td><strong>4 (Weakest)</strong></td><td>Regional/national average statistics</td><td>USDA National Agricultural Statistics, government census data (&le;20 years old)</td><td class="text-red-600">Least preferred</td></tr>
</table>
</ResponsiveTable>
<div class="bg-blue-50 border border-blue-200 rounded-lg p-4 mb-4 text-sm">
  <h4 class="font-semibold text-blue-800 mb-2">Practical guidance: Working down the tiers</h4>
  <p class="text-blue-900 mb-2">You start at Tier 1 and move down only when higher-tier data is unavailable. The methodology requires a justification for each step down:</p>
  <ul class="space-y-2 text-blue-800 list-disc pl-4">
    <li><strong>Tier 1 - Tier 2:</strong> Acceptable if the farm has no digital records, but there are written agronomist recommendations or management plan documents covering the look-back period. Common in well-documented commercial operations.</li>
    <li><strong>Tier 2 - Tier 3:</strong> The farmer provides a signed statutory declaration of their historical practices, supported by at least two corroborating lines of evidence (e.g., neighbouring farm records, dealer records, satellite imagery confirming crop patterns).</li>
    <li><strong>Tier 3 - Tier 4:</strong> Only where individual farm data is completely unattainable. Regional averages introduce the most uncertainty, typically requiring larger uncertainty deductions.</li>
  </ul>
  <p class="text-blue-900 mt-2 font-medium">A mixed-tier approach is common in practice: Tier 1 for tillage (satellite imagery confirms), Tier 3 for fertilizer rate (farmer attestation), Tier 4 for irrigation (no records available).</p>
</div>
<div class="bg-amber-50 border border-amber-200 rounded-lg p-3 mb-4 text-sm">
  <strong class="text-amber-800">Baseline reassessment:</strong> The baseline schedule of activities must be reassessed every <strong>10 years</strong> (or every 5 years where regional production practices change rapidly or new data becomes available). At reassessment, if the regional common practice has changed significantly, the baseline must be updated, which can reduce or eliminate the project's additionality for new practice changes that became common.
</div>

  <p class="mt-2 text-sm"><strong>Historical look-back:</strong> 5 years (t=-5 to t=-1)</p>

    <tr><th>Data Collected</th><th>Tier</th><th>Practice Documented</th></tr>
    <tr><td>5 years of farm management records</td><td class="text-green-700">Tier 1</td><td>Conventional tillage (3 passes/yr), 120 kg N/ha urea, no cover crops, stubble burned</td></tr>

  "Annual wheat crop; conventional tillage 3x per year at 15 cm depth; 120 kg N/ha urea in autumn; all stubble burned at harvest."</p>

<h3 class="text-xl font-bold mt-6 mb-3">Conservativeness Principle</h3>
<p class="mb-4">When multiple values are available for a baseline parameter, VM0042 requires using the value that results in the <strong>lowest expected emissions (or highest SOC) in the baseline scenario</strong>. This is conservative, it means you earn fewer credits when uncertain, rather than over-claiming.</p>

<div class="bg-amber-50 border border-amber-200 rounded p-4 mb-6">
  <p class="font-semibold text-amber-800">Example of conservativeness in practice:</p>
  <p class="mt-1 text-sm">An agronomist recommendation says "apply 100-150 kg N/ha." For the baseline, you use <strong>100 kg N/ha</strong> (the lower end), this produces lower N₂O baseline emissions, which means the project's N₂O reduction gets fewer credits. Conservativeness always favours the environment, not the developer.</p>
</div>

<div class="bg-green-50 border border-green-200 rounded p-4">
  <p class="font-semibold text-green-800">Key Takeaway</p>
  <p class="text-green-900 mt-1">The baseline scenario is <strong>not hypothetical</strong>, it must be grounded in documented historical practices. Use Tier 1 data wherever possible. The schedule of activities creates a repeating pattern of farming practices that the model or measurement framework uses to estimate what <em>would have happened</em> without the project.</p>
</div>
