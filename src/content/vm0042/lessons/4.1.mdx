<h2 class="text-2xl font-bold text-gray-800 mb-4">Why Uncertainty Matters</h2>
<HighlightBox>
  <p class="font-semibold text-green-800">Conservative by design</p>
  <p class="text-green-900 mt-1">Every carbon measurement has uncertainty. VM0042 requires projects to <em>quantify</em> that uncertainty and apply <em>deductions</em> to ensure every VCU issued represents at least one genuine tonne of CO₂e. The more uncertain your measurements, the fewer credits you receive.</p>
</HighlightBox>

<h3 class="text-xl font-bold mt-6 mb-3">Where Does Uncertainty Come From?</h3>
<p class="text-sm mb-3">Before we talk about deductions, it helps to understand <strong>why</strong> uncertainty exists in soil carbon projects in the first place. Unlike a factory where you can install a meter on a smokestack and measure exact emissions, soil carbon is invisible, unevenly distributed, and constantly changing. Every step in the measurement chain introduces some degree of "we're not 100% sure."</p>

<AnalogyBox>
  <p class="font-semibold text-blue-800">🗺️ Analogy: Estimating Rainfall Across a Country</p>
  <p class="mt-1 text-sm text-blue-900">Imagine you need to report the total rainfall across India last year. You have 500 rain gauges spread across the country, but India has deserts, mountains, coasts, and plains - each with wildly different rainfall. You can't put a gauge on every square metre. So you sample, you model, and you estimate. Your final number will be close to reality, but never exact. That gap between your estimate and reality is uncertainty. Soil carbon works the same way - except the "rainfall" is hidden underground.</p>
</AnalogyBox>

<p class="text-sm mb-2 font-semibold text-gray-800">Here's how uncertainty creeps in at each stage of a soil carbon project:</p>

<div class="space-y-3 mb-6">
  <div class="bg-white border border-gray-200 rounded-lg p-4 shadow-sm">
    <p class="font-semibold text-sm text-emerald-800">1. You can't sample every square metre of soil</p>
    <p class="text-sm text-gray-600 mt-1">A 5,000 ha farm might have 30 sampling points. That's 30 tiny cores representing 50 million square metres of soil. The carbon content varies naturally across the landscape - sandy patches have less, clay-rich hollows have more, old fence lines where manure accumulated have much more. Your 30 points might miss a high-carbon pocket or oversample a low-carbon zone.</p>
    <p class="text-xs text-gray-500 mt-1"><strong>This is sampling error</strong> - the biggest source of uncertainty in most projects.</p>
  </div>

  <div class="bg-white border border-gray-200 rounded-lg p-4 shadow-sm">
    <p class="font-semibold text-sm text-blue-800">2. Lab analysis isn't perfectly repeatable</p>
    <p class="text-sm text-gray-600 mt-1">Send the same soil sample to two accredited labs, and you'll get slightly different numbers. Even within the same lab, running the same sample twice gives slightly different results. The dry combustion machine has calibration drift, the technician's technique varies, and the sample prep (grinding, sieving) isn't identical each time.</p>
    <p class="text-xs text-gray-500 mt-1"><strong>This is measurement error</strong> - typically small (2-5%) but it adds up.</p>
  </div>

  <div class="bg-white border border-gray-200 rounded-lg p-4 shadow-sm">
    <p class="font-semibold text-sm text-violet-800">3. Models are simplifications of reality</p>
    <p class="text-sm text-gray-600 mt-1">If you're using Approach 1, a biogeochemical model like DNDC or DayCent simulates how carbon moves through the soil. But these models use equations that approximate complex biological processes - microbial decomposition, root exudation, fungal networks. No model captures every variable. Rainfall patterns, soil microbiome differences, and unusual weather events all create gaps between what the model predicts and what actually happens underground.</p>
    <p class="text-xs text-gray-500 mt-1"><strong>This is model prediction error</strong> - only applies to Approach 1 projects.</p>
  </div>

  <div class="bg-white border border-gray-200 rounded-lg p-4 shadow-sm">
    <p class="font-semibold text-sm text-amber-800">4. The baseline is a counterfactual - it never actually happened</p>
    <p class="text-sm text-gray-600 mt-1">Your baseline scenario asks: "What would have happened if the project never existed?" But you can't observe a world where you didn't intervene. You estimate it using control sites, historical data, or models. Each of these has its own uncertainty. Maybe the control site's soil is slightly different. Maybe weather patterns shifted. The baseline is always an educated guess, never a measurement.</p>
    <p class="text-xs text-gray-500 mt-1"><strong>This is baseline uncertainty</strong> - inherent to all carbon crediting, not just VM0042.</p>
  </div>
</div>

<ExampleBox>
  <p class="font-semibold text-amber-800">📐 Putting It in Numbers</p>
  <p class="text-sm mt-2">Consider a project that estimates a SOC gain of <strong>2.0 tC/ha</strong> over 5 years. Where could that number be wrong?</p>
  <ul class="text-sm mt-2 space-y-1">
    <li><strong>Sampling error (±15%):</strong> The true farm-wide average might be anywhere from 1.7 to 2.3 tC/ha - you just happened to sample spots that averaged 2.0</li>
    <li><strong>Lab error (±3%):</strong> The actual carbon concentration could be 1.94 to 2.06 tC/ha based on lab precision</li>
    <li><strong>Model error (±10%, Approach 1):</strong> The model predicted 2.0 but the soil biology responded slightly differently than simulated</li>
    <li><strong>Baseline uncertainty (±8%):</strong> Maybe the no-project scenario would have gained 0.1 tC/ha naturally, or lost 0.2 tC/ha - you estimated 0, but you're not certain</li>
  </ul>
  <p class="text-sm mt-2 text-amber-700">Combined, these errors don't simply add up - they're combined statistically (root sum of squares). But the point is clear: <strong>the 2.0 tC/ha number is your best estimate, not a fact</strong>. VM0042's deduction system exists to account for this gap.</p>
</ExampleBox>

<AnalogyBox>
  <p class="font-semibold text-blue-800">☕ Analogy: Coffee Bag Weight</p>
  <p class="mt-1 text-sm text-blue-900">You're selling 1 kg bags of coffee. If your scale is precise (±1g), you can confidently sell each bag. If your scale is imprecise (±100g), some bags might be only 900g, short-changing customers. To be fair, you'd add extra coffee to guarantee at least 1 kg. In carbon markets, the "extra coffee" is the <strong>uncertainty deduction</strong>, you claim fewer credits than measured, so every VCU represents at least one real tonne.</p>
</AnalogyBox>

<ExampleBox>
  <p class="font-semibold text-amber-800">📍 How Uncertainty Affected a Real Project</p>
  <p class="mt-2 text-sm">A conservation tillage project in Western Australia (2019) used Approach 2 with only 12 sampling plots across 3,000 ha, far fewer than the statistically required minimum. When the VVB ran the uncertainty calculation, the sampling error alone produced a 42% uncertainty. Per VM0042's deduction schedule, this triggered a 35% credit deduction. Instead of earning the expected 15,000 VCUs, the project received approximately 9,750 VCUs. A follow-up sampling campaign with 35 additional plots in the second monitoring period brought uncertainty down to 18%, recovering most of the lost credits.</p>
  <p class="mt-2 text-sm text-amber-700"><strong>Lesson:</strong> Investing an extra $15,000 in sampling in Year 1 would have secured $127,500 in additional revenue (5,250 VCUs × $24). Sampling design is not a cost, it is an investment.</p>
</ExampleBox>

<h3 class="text-xl font-bold mt-6 mb-3">Three Sources of Error in VM0042</h3>
<ResponsiveTable>
<table class="mb-6">
  <thead><tr><th>Error Source</th><th>What It Is</th><th>How to Reduce It</th></tr></thead>
  <tbody>
  <tr><td><strong>Model prediction error</strong></td><td>Biogeochemical model may not perfectly represent real soil processes (Approach 1 only)</td><td>Use well-validated models, update with true-up data</td></tr>
  <tr><td><strong>Sampling error</strong></td><td>Only a subset of the project area is sampled, the sample may not perfectly represent all soils</td><td>More sampling points, better stratification</td></tr>
  <tr><td><strong>Measurement error</strong></td><td>Lab analysis errors, bulk density measurement variability</td><td>Accredited labs, duplicate samples, standard methods</td></tr>
  </tbody>
</table>
</ResponsiveTable>

<h3 class="text-xl font-bold mt-6 mb-3">The Uncertainty Deduction Schedule</h3>
<p class="mb-3 text-sm">For each GHG source (especially SOC), VM0042 calculates a total uncertainty (as a % of the estimated carbon change) and applies a deduction:</p>
<ResponsiveTable>
<table class="mb-6">
  <thead><tr><th>Aggregate Uncertainty</th><th>Deduction Applied</th><th>Practical Meaning</th></tr></thead>
  <tbody>
  <tr><td>&lt;15%</td><td class="text-green-700">Minimal (15% tolerance)</td><td>Well-measured projects keep most credits</td></tr>
  <tr><td>15%-30%</td><td class="text-amber-700">Proportional deduction</td><td>Moderate sampling or model uncertainty</td></tr>
  <tr><td>&gt;30%</td><td class="text-red-600">Larger proportional deduction</td><td>Poor sampling design, consider improving</td></tr>
  </tbody>
</table>
</ResponsiveTable>
