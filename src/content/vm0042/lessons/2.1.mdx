<h2 class="text-2xl font-bold text-gray-800 mb-4">Drawing the Lines: Project Boundary</h2>
<HighlightBox>
  <p class="font-semibold text-green-800">What the project boundary defines</p>
  <p class="text-green-900 mt-1">The project boundary defines <strong>what you measure</strong> and <strong>what you count</strong>. It specifies which carbon pools and GHG emission sources must be included in your calculations — and which can be excluded. Getting this right prevents both under-counting and over-claiming.</p>
</HighlightBox>

<AnalogyBox>
  <p class="font-semibold text-blue-800">💳 Analogy: Auditing a Household Budget</p>
  <p class="mt-2 text-blue-900">The project boundary is like the scope of a household budget audit. The "boundary" determines which bank accounts and expense categories are in scope. If you only track the checking account but ignore the credit card, you get an incomplete picture. VM0042 requires you to account for all significant "accounts" — SOC, fossil fuel, lime, methane, and nitrous oxide — so the carbon balance is accurate.</p>
</AnalogyBox>

<ExampleBox>
  <p class="font-semibold text-amber-800">📍 Why Boundary Decisions Matter — A Real Case</p>
  <p class="mt-2 text-sm">In a 2021 VVB audit of a conservation agriculture project in East Africa, the auditor discovered that the project had excluded N₂O from nitrogen-fixing legume cover crops from its project boundary — without first demonstrating the exclusion was de minimis. Since the project had introduced legumes as a new practice (they weren't in the baseline), their N₂O emissions were a new project-scenario source that should have been included. The error required recalculation of ERRs, reducing the credit claim by approximately 8%. The lesson: every new GHG source introduced by the project must be deliberately included or justified as de minimis.</p>
</ExampleBox>

<h3 class="text-xl font-bold mt-6 mb-3">Carbon Pools</h3>
<ResponsiveTable>
<table class="mb-6">
  <tr><th>Carbon Pool</th><th>Status</th><th>When to Include</th></tr>
  <tr><td><strong>Soil Organic Carbon (SOC)</strong></td><td class="text-green-700 font-bold">MANDATORY — always</td><td>Every VM0042 project, to at least 30 cm depth</td></tr>
  <tr><td>Aboveground woody biomass</td><td class="text-amber-700">Conditional</td><td>Must include if project activities could significantly REDUCE this pool; optional otherwise (e.g., agroforestry adds trees)</td></tr>
  <tr><td>Belowground woody biomass</td><td class="text-amber-700">Optional</td><td>Where project significantly increases this pool (agroforestry)</td></tr>
  <tr><td>Aboveground non-woody biomass</td><td class="text-red-600">Excluded</td><td>Not subject to significant changes or changes are transient</td></tr>
  <tr><td>Dead wood & litter</td><td class="text-red-600">Excluded</td><td>Not significant for ALM projects</td></tr>
</table>
</ResponsiveTable>

<ExampleBox>
  <p class="font-semibold text-amber-800">🌱 Practical Examples: What Each Carbon Pool Looks Like on a Farm</p>
  <ul class="mt-2 space-y-3 text-sm">
    <li><strong>Soil Organic Carbon (SOC):</strong> The dark, crumbly organic matter in the top 30+ cm of soil. When a farmer switches from conventional tillage to no-till, decomposed root material, microbial biomass, and humus accumulate instead of being oxidised. A handful of healthy soil can contain more carbon than the plant growing above it. Example: A wheat field in Punjab that retains rice stubble instead of burning it adds ~1.2 tonnes of carbon per hectare per year to this pool.</li>
    <li><strong>Aboveground woody biomass:</strong> The trunks, branches, and leaves of trees and shrubs. On a Kenyan coffee farm that adds shade trees (agroforestry), each mature tree stores 50–200 kg of carbon in its wood. If the project removes trees instead (e.g., clearing hedgerows), this pool must be counted because carbon is being released.</li>
    <li><strong>Belowground woody biomass:</strong> The root systems of trees and shrubs. Those same shade trees on the coffee farm have root systems that store roughly 20–30% as much carbon as the aboveground wood. A mango tree's root system might hold 40–60 kg of carbon. This pool is optional — included only when the project significantly increases it.</li>
    <li><strong>Aboveground non-woody biomass (excluded):</strong> Annual crop stalks, grass blades, and leaves. A field of standing corn contains carbon in its stalks and leaves, but this pool is excluded because it is transient — the crop is harvested or dies each season, so there is no long-term accumulation.</li>
    <li><strong>Dead wood &amp; litter (excluded):</strong> Fallen branches, decomposing leaves on the forest floor, and crop debris. On ALM (agricultural land management) projects, this pool is too small and transient to measure meaningfully, so VM0042 excludes it.</li>
  </ul>
</ExampleBox>

<h3 class="text-xl font-bold mt-6 mb-3">GHG Sources & Sinks</h3>
<ResponsiveTable>
<table class="mb-6">
  <tr><th>Source</th><th>Gas</th><th>Included?</th><th>Why It Matters</th></tr>
  <tr><td>SOC stock change</td><td>CO₂</td><td class="text-green-700">Yes — mandatory</td><td>The main event — changes in soil carbon storage</td></tr>
  <tr><td>Fossil fuel combustion</td><td>CO₂</td><td class="text-amber-700">S* (conditional)</td><td>Farm machinery — tractors, pumps. More or less tillage = more or less fuel</td></tr>
  <tr><td>Liming</td><td>CO₂</td><td class="text-amber-700">S* (conditional)</td><td>Limestone/dolomite releases CO₂ as it dissolves</td></tr>
  <tr><td>Soil methanogenesis (rice)</td><td>CH₄</td><td class="text-amber-700">S* (conditional)</td><td>Flooded soils produce CH₄ — major for rice projects</td></tr>
  <tr><td>Enteric fermentation</td><td>CH₄</td><td class="text-green-700">Yes (if livestock present)</td><td>Cow digestion produces CH₄ — required for grazing projects</td></tr>
  <tr><td>Manure deposition</td><td>CH₄, N₂O</td><td class="text-green-700">Yes (if livestock present)</td><td>Manure on pastures emits both CH₄ and N₂O</td></tr>
  <tr><td>Nitrogen fertilizers</td><td>N₂O</td><td class="text-green-700">Yes (if N fertilized)</td><td>N inputs to soil produce N₂O via nitrification/denitrification</td></tr>
  <tr><td>Nitrogen-fixing species</td><td>N₂O</td><td class="text-green-700">Yes (if N-fixing crops)</td><td>Legumes release N from root nodules, causing N₂O</td></tr>
  <tr><td>Biomass burning — CO₂</td><td>CO₂</td><td class="text-red-700 font-medium">Excluded</td><td>CO₂ from burning is accounted as a carbon stock change (not an emissions source)</td></tr>
  <tr><td>Biomass burning — CH₄ &amp; N₂O</td><td>CH₄, N₂O</td><td class="text-amber-700">S* (conditional)</td><td>CH₄ and N₂O from biomass burning must be included where emissions change significantly vs. baseline</td></tr>
</table>
</ResponsiveTable>
<p class="text-sm text-gray-500 mb-4">S* = Must be included where the project activity significantly INCREASES emissions compared to baseline (&gt;5% of total GHG benefit). May be included where it REDUCES emissions.</p>

<ExampleBox>
  <p class="font-semibold text-amber-800">🔥 Practical Examples: GHG Sources &amp; Sinks in Everyday Farming</p>
  <ul class="mt-2 space-y-3 text-sm">
    <li><strong>SOC stock change (CO₂ — sink or source):</strong> When a farmer adopts no-till, crop roots and residues decompose slowly, building humus. The soil acts as a <em>carbon sink</em> — pulling CO₂ from the atmosphere. Conversely, ploughing exposes buried organic matter to oxygen, turning the soil into a <em>carbon source</em> that releases CO₂. This is the core of every VM0042 project.</li>
    <li><strong>Fossil fuel combustion (CO₂):</strong> Every tractor pass burns diesel. A conventional tillage farm may run 3–4 passes per season (plough, disc, harrow, plant), burning ~45 litres of diesel per hectare. Switching to no-till cuts this to 1 pass (direct seeding), saving ~30 litres/ha — that is 0.08 tCO₂/ha/yr less emitted.</li>
    <li><strong>Liming (CO₂):</strong> Farmers spread crusite limestone or dolomite to reduce soil acidity. When limestone dissolves in acidic soil, it releases CO₂ — about 0.44 tCO₂ per tonne of calcium carbonate applied. If the project eliminates or reduces liming, those emissions drop.</li>
    <li><strong>Soil methanogenesis — rice paddies (CH₄):</strong> In a flooded rice paddy, the waterlogged, oxygen-free soil is perfect for methane-producing microbes. A continuously flooded paddy in Vietnam can emit 4–5 tCO₂e/ha/season of CH₄. Alternate Wetting and Drying (AWD) — periodically draining the field — lets oxygen in and cuts CH₄ by 30–50%.</li>
    <li><strong>Enteric fermentation (CH₄):</strong> Cows and sheep belch methane as microbes in their rumen digest cellulose. A single beef cow produces ~58 kg CH₄/year (~1.6 tCO₂e). In a grazing project with 500 cattle, reducing the herd by 20% cuts enteric CH₄ by ~160 tCO₂e/year.</li>
    <li><strong>Manure deposition (CH₄ + N₂O):</strong> Cow dung on pastures decomposes anaerobically (producing CH₄) while urine nitrogen converts to N₂O through soil bacteria. Both must be tracked when livestock are part of the project.</li>
    <li><strong>Nitrogen fertilizers (N₂O):</strong> When a farmer spreads urea or ammonium nitrate, soil bacteria convert about 1% of the applied nitrogen into nitrous oxide — a gas 265× more potent than CO₂. Reducing fertiliser from 150 → 105 kg N/ha saves ~0.19 tCO₂e/ha/yr.</li>
    <li><strong>Nitrogen-fixing species (N₂O):</strong> Legumes like clover, lentils, or soybeans host bacteria in their root nodules that convert atmospheric N₂ into plant-available nitrogen. When these roots decompose, the released nitrogen can be converted to N₂O by soil microbes — so adding a legume cover crop <em>increases</em> this emission source even as it benefits SOC.</li>
    <li><strong>Biomass burning (CH₄ + N₂O):</strong> When farmers burn crop stubble (common in rice and sugarcane regions), the incomplete combustion produces CH₄ and N₂O. A project that eliminates stubble burning removes this emission source entirely.</li>
  </ul>
</ExampleBox>

<div class="bg-green-50 border border-green-200 rounded p-4">
  <p class="font-semibold text-green-800">Key Takeaway</p>
  <p class="text-green-900 mt-1"><strong>SOC is always mandatory.</strong> Other pools and GHG sources are included based on whether they are significant (>5% of total benefit) in your specific project. The de minimis rule lets you exclude sources that are trivially small — but you must demonstrate they are &lt;5% of total benefit first.</p>
</div>
