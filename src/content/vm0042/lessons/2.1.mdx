<h2 class="text-2xl font-bold text-gray-800 mb-4">Drawing the Lines: Project Boundary</h2>
<HighlightBox>
  <p class="font-semibold text-green-800">What the project boundary defines</p>
  <p class="text-green-900 mt-1">The project boundary defines <strong>what you measure</strong> and <strong>what you count</strong>. It specifies which carbon pools and GHG emission sources must be included in your calculations, and which can be excluded. Getting this right prevents both under-counting and over-claiming.</p>
</HighlightBox>

<AnalogyBox>
  <p class="font-semibold text-blue-800">💳 Analogy: Auditing a Household Budget</p>
  <p class="mt-2 text-blue-900">The project boundary is like the scope of a household budget audit. The "boundary" determines which bank accounts and expense categories are in scope. If you only track the checking account but ignore the credit card, you get an incomplete picture. VM0042 requires you to account for all significant "accounts", SOC, fossil fuel, lime, methane, and nitrous oxide, so the carbon balance is accurate.</p>
</AnalogyBox>

<ExampleBox>
  <p class="font-semibold text-amber-800">📍 Why Boundary Decisions Matter, A Real Case</p>
  <p class="mt-2 text-sm">In a 2021 VVB audit of a conservation agriculture project in East Africa, the auditor discovered that the project had excluded N₂O from nitrogen-fixing legume cover crops from its project boundary, without first demonstrating the exclusion was de minimis. Since the project had introduced legumes as a new practice (they weren't in the baseline), their N₂O emissions were a new project-scenario source that should have been included. The error required recalculation of ERRs, reducing the credit claim by approximately 8%. The lesson: every new GHG source introduced by the project must be deliberately included or justified as de minimis.</p>
</ExampleBox>

<h3 class="text-xl font-bold mt-6 mb-3">Carbon Pools</h3>

</table>

<ExampleBox>
  <p class="font-semibold text-amber-800">🌱 Think of Carbon Pools as "Storage Locations" on a Farm</p>
  <p class="mt-1 text-sm text-amber-700">Carbon on a farm is stored in different places, just like money can be in a bank account, a wallet, or loose change in your pocket. Each storage location is a "carbon pool."</p>
  <ul class="mt-3 space-y-3 text-sm">
    <li><strong>Soil Organic Carbon (SOC), the main bank account:</strong> This is the carbon locked inside the soil itself, the dark, rich organic matter in the top 30 cm. Picture a Punjab wheat farmer who stops burning rice stubble and instead leaves it on the field. That stubble slowly breaks down, feeding carbon into the soil. Over a year, the soil gains about 1.2 tonnes of carbon per hectare. This pool is <em>always</em> measured in VM0042 because it is where the biggest changes happen.</li>
    <li><strong>Aboveground woody biomass, the trees:</strong> The wood in trunks, branches, and large roots of trees. If a coffee farmer in Kenya plants shade trees between their coffee rows, each mature tree locks away 50–200 kg of carbon in its wood. You only need to track this pool if the project adds or removes trees.</li>
    <li><strong>Belowground woody biomass, the hidden roots:</strong> Tree roots underground. That same Kenyan shade tree has roots holding about a quarter of the carbon stored in its trunk and branches. Think of it as the "underground savings", counted only when the project plants new trees.</li>
    <li><strong>Aboveground non-woody biomass (excluded):</strong> Crop stalks, grass, and leaves, things that grow and die every season. A field of corn has carbon in its stalks, but it gets harvested or dies every year, so nothing accumulates long-term. VM0042 ignores this pool because it resets each season.</li>
    <li><strong>Dead wood &amp; litter (excluded):</strong> Fallen twigs, dead leaves, and crop debris lying on the ground. On farmland this is a tiny amount that comes and goes quickly, not worth measuring.</li>
  </ul>
</ExampleBox>

  <tr><td>SOC stock change</td><td>CO₂</td><td class="text-green-700">Yes, mandatory</td><td>The main event, changes in soil carbon storage</td></tr>

  <tr><td>Biomass burning, CO₂</td><td>CO₂</td><td class="text-red-700 font-medium">Excluded</td><td>CO₂ from burning is accounted as a carbon stock change (not an emissions source)</td></tr>
  <tr><td>Biomass burning, CH₄ &amp; N₂O</td><td>CH₄, N₂O</td><td class="text-amber-700">S* (conditional)</td><td>CH₄ and N₂O from biomass burning must be included where emissions change significantly vs. baseline</td></tr>
</table>
</ResponsiveTable>
<p class="text-sm text-gray-500 mb-4">S* = Must be included where the project activity significantly INCREASES emissions compared to baseline (&gt;5% of total GHG benefit). May be included where it REDUCES emissions.</p>

<ExampleBox>
  <p class="font-semibold text-amber-800">🔥 Where Do Greenhouse Gases Come From (and Go) on a Farm?</p>
  <p class="mt-1 text-sm text-amber-700">A <strong>source</strong> is anything that releases greenhouse gases into the air. A <strong>sink</strong> is anything that absorbs them. A farm can be both at the same time, the goal is to tip the balance toward absorption.</p>
  <ul class="mt-3 space-y-3 text-sm">
    <li><strong>Soil carbon change (CO₂), the big one:</strong> Healthy, undisturbed soil steadily absorbs CO₂ from the air (it is a <em>sink</em>). But when a farmer ploughs the field, buried organic matter gets exposed to air and releases CO₂ (it becomes a <em>source</em>). Switching to no-till is like sealing a jar, the carbon stays trapped in the soil instead of escaping.</li>
    <li><strong>Tractor fuel (CO₂):</strong> Every time a tractor drives across a field, it burns diesel and releases CO₂. Traditional farming needs 3–4 tractor passes per season (plough, disc, harrow, plant). No-till farming needs just 1 pass. Fewer passes = less diesel = less CO₂.</li>
    <li><strong>Liming (CO₂):</strong> Some soils are too acidic, so farmers spread crushed limestone to fix it. The problem: when limestone reacts with acidic soil, it releases CO₂, like opening a fizzy drink. Stop liming, and that CO₂ source disappears.</li>
    <li><strong>Flooded rice paddies (CH₄):</strong> When a rice paddy is flooded, the waterlogged soil has no oxygen. Microbes that thrive without oxygen produce methane (CH₄) as they eat organic matter, like a compost heap sealed underwater. Draining the paddy periodically (called AWD) lets air in and cuts methane by 30–50%.</li>
    <li><strong>Cow burps (CH₄):</strong> Cattle and sheep belch methane as their stomachs digest grass. A single cow belches enough methane each year to equal about 1.6 tonnes of CO₂. Fewer cattle on a ranch = fewer burps = less methane.</li>
    <li><strong>Manure on pastures (CH₄ + N₂O):</strong> Cow dung sitting in a field produces methane as it rots. The nitrogen in cow urine gets converted to nitrous oxide (N₂O) by soil bacteria. Both gases must be counted when livestock are part of the project.</li>
    <li><strong>Chemical fertilizers (N₂O):</strong> When a farmer spreads urea or other nitrogen fertilizers, soil bacteria convert a small portion (~1%) into nitrous oxide, a gas 265 times more powerful than CO₂. Using less fertilizer means less N₂O released.</li>
    <li><strong>Legume crops (N₂O):</strong> Plants like lentils, soybeans, and clover pull nitrogen from the air and store it in their roots. When those roots decompose, the nitrogen enters the soil and some of it turns into N₂O. So planting legumes is great for soil health, but it does create a small new source of greenhouse gas that must be counted.</li>
    <li><strong>Burning crop stubble (CH₄ + N₂O):</strong> After harvest, many farmers burn leftover stalks. The fire does not burn completely clean, it produces both CH₄ and N₂O. A project that stops stubble burning eliminates this source entirely.</li>
  </ul>
</ExampleBox>

<div class="bg-green-50 border border-green-200 rounded p-4">
  <p class="font-semibold text-green-800">Key Takeaway</p>
  <p class="text-green-900 mt-1"><strong>SOC is always mandatory.</strong> Other pools and GHG sources are included based on whether they are significant (>5% of total benefit) in your specific project. The de minimis rule lets you exclude sources that are trivially small, but you must demonstrate they are &lt;5% of total benefit first.</p>
</div>
