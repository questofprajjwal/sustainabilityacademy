<h2 class="text-2xl font-bold text-gray-800 mb-4">The Greenhouse Effect & Three Gases That Matter</h2>

<HighlightBox>
  <p class="font-semibold text-green-800">Why this matters for VM0042</p>
  <p class="text-green-900 mt-1">VM0042 requires you to measure and account for three greenhouse gases: <strong>CO₂, CH₄, and N₂O</strong>. Understanding why each matters, and how to compare them, is the foundation of everything else.</p>
</HighlightBox>

<h3 class="text-xl font-bold mt-6 mb-3">What Are Greenhouse Gases?</h3>
<p class="mb-3">In plain terms: greenhouse gases are invisible gases in the air that act like a heat trap. Sunlight passes through them on the way in, but when the ground tries to radiate heat back out, these gases absorb it and keep it near the surface.</p>
<p class="mb-4">Formal definition: greenhouse gases (GHGs) are atmospheric gases that absorb and re-emit infrared radiation, warming the lower atmosphere. The more GHGs accumulate, the stronger this warming effect becomes.</p>

<AnalogyBox>
  <p class="font-semibold text-blue-800">🎯 Analogy: Three Blankets</p>
  <ul class="mt-2 space-y-2 text-blue-900">
    <li><strong>CO₂</strong> is a thin cotton sheet, it's abundant and lasts 300–1,000 years in the atmosphere.</li>
    <li><strong>CH₄ (methane)</strong> is a wool blanket, 28× warmer than CO₂ per molecule, but only lasts ~12 years.</li>
    <li><strong>N₂O (nitrous oxide)</strong> is a heavy duvet, 265× warmer than CO₂ per molecule and lasts over 100 years.</li>
  </ul>
</AnalogyBox>

<h3 class="text-xl font-bold mt-6 mb-3">Global Warming Potential (GWP)</h3>
<p class="mb-3">Think of it this way: not all gases are equally dangerous. A small amount of methane heats the planet far more than the same amount of CO₂. To add them up fairly, scientists invented a single "currency", CO₂-equivalent (CO₂e).</p>
<p class="mb-4">Formal definition: <strong>Global Warming Potential (GWP)</strong> is a measure of how much heat a gas traps over 100 years relative to CO₂. It lets us convert any GHG into an equivalent CO₂ mass so all gases can be summed in a single number.</p>

<ResponsiveTable>
<table class="mb-6">
  <tr><th>Gas</th><th>GWP (AR5, 100-year)</th><th>Main Agricultural Source</th></tr>
  <tr><td>CO₂</td><td>1</td><td>Soil decomposition, fossil fuel, liming</td></tr>
  <tr><td>CH₄</td><td>28</td><td>Rice paddies, livestock (enteric fermentation)</td></tr>
  <tr><td>N₂O</td><td>265</td><td>Nitrogen fertilizers, manure</td></tr>
</table>
</ResponsiveTable>

<p class="mb-3">These conversions are used every day by real carbon projects around the world, not just in textbooks. Here are three examples:</p>
<ul class="list-disc ml-6 space-y-2 mb-4">
  <li><strong>Kenya Agricultural Carbon Project (KACP):</strong> Smallholder farms in western Kenya switching to conservation agriculture track CO₂, CH₄, and N₂O to generate Verified Carbon Units (VCUs). GWP conversions let the project report a single tCO₂e figure to Verra.</li>
  <li><strong>Indigo Ag Carbon Program (USA):</strong> Row-crop farmers in the US Midwest earn payments for cover crops and reduced tillage. The program uses GWP to convert N₂O reductions from lower nitrogen application into CO₂e credits.</li>
  <li><strong>Rice AWD projects (Vietnam, Philippines):</strong> Alternate Wetting and Drying drastically cuts CH₄ from flooded paddies. Multiplying the CH₄ reduction by GWP = 28 shows just how large the climate benefit is, often 2–5 tCO₂e/ha/season.</li>
</ul>

<ExampleBox>
  <p class="font-semibold text-amber-800">📐 Worked Example: Converting to CO₂e</p>
  <p class="mt-2">A rice paddy emits <strong>10 tonnes of CH₄</strong> per year.<br />
  CO₂e = 10 × 28 = <strong>280 tonnes CO₂e/year</strong></p>
  <p class="mt-2">A wheat field emits <strong>2 tonnes of N₂O</strong> per year from fertilizer.<br />
  CO₂e = 2 × 265 = <strong>530 tonnes CO₂e/year</strong></p>
  <p class="mt-2 text-amber-700 text-sm">Note: These are converted to CO₂e so they can be added together in the total GHG budget.</p>
</ExampleBox>

<h3 class="text-xl font-bold mt-6 mb-3">Agriculture's Share of Global Emissions</h3>
<p class="mb-3">Agriculture is one of the most significant contributors to climate change, and also one of the best opportunities to reverse it.</p>
<p class="mb-4">Agriculture contributes roughly <strong>10–12% of global GHG emissions</strong> directly. Including land-use change (clearing forests for new farmland), the share rises to nearly 24%. The three gases tracked in VM0042 account for the vast majority of farm-level emissions:</p>
<ul class="list-disc ml-6 space-y-2 mb-4">
  <li>CO₂ from soil disturbance (tillage, land conversion)</li>
  <li>CH₄ from rice paddies and livestock</li>
  <li>N₂O from nitrogen fertilizers and manure</li>
</ul>

<div class="bg-green-50 border border-green-200 rounded p-4">
  <p class="font-semibold text-green-800">Key Takeaway</p>
  <p class="text-green-900 mt-1">VM0042 tracks all three gases. Every emission reduction or carbon removal is expressed in <strong>tonnes CO₂-equivalent (tCO₂e)</strong> so they can be added together. One VCU = one tonne CO₂e.</p>
</div>
