<h2 class="text-2xl font-bold text-gray-800 mb-4">The Greenhouse Effect & Three Gases That Matter</h2>

<HighlightBox>
  <p class="font-semibold text-green-800">Why this matters for VM0042</p>
  <p class="text-green-900 mt-1">VM0042 requires you to measure and account for three greenhouse gases: <strong>CO₂, CH₄, and N₂O</strong>. Understanding why each matters — and how to compare them — is the foundation of everything else.</p>
</HighlightBox>

<h3 class="text-xl font-bold mt-6 mb-3">What Are Greenhouse Gases?</h3>
<p class="mb-4">Greenhouse gases (GHGs) trap heat in the Earth's atmosphere. Like a blanket around the planet, they prevent heat from escaping into space. The more GHGs, the warmer it gets.</p>

<AnalogyBox>
  <p class="font-semibold text-blue-800">🎯 Analogy: Three Blankets</p>
  <ul class="mt-2 space-y-2 text-blue-900">
    <li><strong>CO₂</strong> is a thin cotton sheet — it's abundant and lasts 300–1,000 years in the atmosphere.</li>
    <li><strong>CH₄ (methane)</strong> is a wool blanket — 28× warmer than CO₂ per molecule, but only lasts ~12 years.</li>
    <li><strong>N₂O (nitrous oxide)</strong> is a heavy duvet — 265× warmer than CO₂ per molecule and lasts over 100 years.</li>
  </ul>
</AnalogyBox>

<h3 class="text-xl font-bold mt-6 mb-3">Global Warming Potential (GWP)</h3>
<p class="mb-4">Because each gas has a different warming power and atmospheric lifetime, scientists use <strong>Global Warming Potential (GWP)</strong> to compare them. GWP expresses everything in <em>tonnes of CO₂-equivalent (CO₂e)</em>.</p>

<ResponsiveTable>
<table class="mb-6">
  <tr><th>Gas</th><th>GWP (AR5, 100-year)</th><th>Main Agricultural Source</th></tr>
  <tr><td>CO₂</td><td>1</td><td>Soil decomposition, fossil fuel, liming</td></tr>
  <tr><td>CH₄</td><td>28</td><td>Rice paddies, livestock (enteric fermentation)</td></tr>
  <tr><td>N₂O</td><td>265</td><td>Nitrogen fertilizers, manure</td></tr>
</table>
</ResponsiveTable>

<ExampleBox>
  <p class="font-semibold text-amber-800">📐 Worked Example: Converting to CO₂e</p>
  <p class="mt-2">A rice paddy emits <strong>10 tonnes of CH₄</strong> per year.<br />
  CO₂e = 10 × 28 = <strong>280 tonnes CO₂e/year</strong></p>
  <p class="mt-2">A wheat field emits <strong>2 tonnes of N₂O</strong> per year from fertilizer.<br />
  CO₂e = 2 × 265 = <strong>530 tonnes CO₂e/year</strong></p>
  <p class="mt-2 text-amber-700 text-sm">Note: These are converted to CO₂e so they can be added together in the total GHG budget.</p>
</ExampleBox>

<h3 class="text-xl font-bold mt-6 mb-3">Agriculture's Share of Global Emissions</h3>
<p class="mb-4">Agriculture contributes roughly <strong>10–12% of global GHG emissions</strong> directly (and more when land use change is included). The three gases in VM0042 together account for most of that:</p>
<ul class="list-disc ml-6 space-y-2 mb-4">
  <li>CO₂ from soil disturbance (tillage, land conversion)</li>
  <li>CH₄ from rice paddies and livestock</li>
  <li>N₂O from nitrogen fertilizers and manure</li>
</ul>

<div class="bg-green-50 border border-green-200 rounded p-4">
  <p class="font-semibold text-green-800">Key Takeaway</p>
  <p class="text-green-900 mt-1">VM0042 tracks all three gases. Every emission reduction or carbon removal is expressed in <strong>tonnes CO₂-equivalent (tCO₂e)</strong> so they can be added together. One VCU = one tonne CO₂e.</p>
</div>
