<h2 class="text-2xl font-bold text-gray-800 mb-4">Error Propagation & Monte Carlo Simulation</h2>
<HighlightBox>
  <p class="font-semibold text-green-800">Two methods to quantify uncertainty</p>
  <p class="text-green-900 mt-1">VM0042 provides two approaches for calculating the numerical uncertainty deduction: <strong>analytical error propagation</strong> (straightforward for independent errors) and <strong>Monte Carlo simulation</strong> (for complex, correlated inputs). Understanding these methods is essential for completing the ERR spreadsheet.</p>
</HighlightBox>

<h3 class="text-xl font-bold mt-6 mb-3">Method 1: Analytical Error Propagation</h3>
<p class="text-sm mb-3">When combining multiple independent measurements, error propagation rules determine how individual uncertainties combine into a total uncertainty.</p>

<div class="bg-gray-50 border rounded p-4 mb-4">
  <p class="font-semibold text-sm text-gray-800">Key Rule: Adding/Subtracting values → Add absolute variances</p>
  <p class="font-semibold text-sm text-gray-800 mt-2">Key Rule: Multiplying/Dividing values → Add relative (%) variances, then take square root</p>
</div>

<ExampleBox>
  <p class="font-semibold text-amber-800">📐 Worked Example: SOC Stock Uncertainty</p>
  <p class="mt-2 text-sm">Calculate SOC stock and its uncertainty:</p>
  <ResponsiveTable>
<table class="mt-2 text-sm">
    <tr><th>Input</th><th>Value</th><th>Relative Uncertainty</th></tr>
    <tr><td>OC concentration</td><td>1.8%</td><td>8.3% (±0.15%)</td></tr>
    <tr><td>Bulk density</td><td>1.35 g/cm³</td><td>5.9% (±0.08 g/cm³)</td></tr>
    <tr><td>Depth</td><td>30 cm</td><td>0% (assumed exact)</td></tr>
  </table>
</ResponsiveTable>
  <FormulaBox>
    SOC stock = 1.8% × 1.35 × 30 × 100 = 72.9 tC/ha<br />
    Combined uncertainty = √(8.3² + 5.9²) = √(68.9 + 34.8) = √103.7 = <strong>10.2%</strong><br />
    Absolute uncertainty = 72.9 × 10.2% = ±7.4 tC/ha
  </FormulaBox>
  <p class="mt-2 text-sm">95% confidence interval: 72.9 ± (2 × 7.4) = <strong>58.1 to 87.7 tC/ha</strong></p>
</ExampleBox>

<h3 class="text-xl font-bold mt-6 mb-3">Method 2: Monte Carlo Simulation</h3>
<AnalogyBox>
  <p class="font-semibold text-blue-800">🎴 Analogy: Playing Cards 10,000 Times</p>
  <p class="mt-1 text-sm text-blue-900">Error propagation is like using probability formulas to predict card game outcomes. Monte Carlo is like actually playing the game 10,000 times and recording all results. Both give you a distribution of outcomes, but Monte Carlo handles complex, correlated inputs better.</p>
</AnalogyBox>
<p class="text-sm mb-3">Process:</p>
<ol class="list-decimal ml-6 text-sm space-y-2 mb-4">
  <li>Define probability distributions for each uncertain input (OC%, bulk density, model parameters)</li>
  <li>Randomly sample from each distribution simultaneously</li>
  <li>Run the full calculation with those random values</li>
  <li>Repeat 10,000+ times</li>
  <li>Collect all results → forms a distribution of possible outcomes</li>
  <li>Read off the conservative estimate from the left tail of the distribution</li>
</ol>
<p class="text-sm mb-4 text-gray-600">MC simulation is <strong>required</strong> when using spectroscopy techniques for SOC measurement (because their errors are often non-linear and correlated with carbon content).</p>

<div class="bg-blue-50 border border-blue-200 rounded p-4 mb-4">
  <p class="font-semibold text-blue-800">📊 How VM0042 Uses the Monte Carlo Distribution</p>
  <p class="text-sm mt-2 text-blue-900">VM0042 does not simply use the "5th percentile." Instead, it uses a <strong>probability-of-exceedance</strong> construct: the uncertainty deduction percentage is set so that the probability of the true value being <em>lower</em> than (mean − deduction) is only 5% — i.e., a 95% confidence lower bound.</p>
  <div class="grid grid-cols-1 md:grid-cols-2 gap-3 mt-3 text-sm">
    <div class="bg-white p-3 rounded border">
      <p class="font-semibold text-gray-700">For a symmetric normal distribution:</p>
      <p class="mt-1 text-gray-600">The 5th percentile is at mean − 1.645σ. So a 10% relative uncertainty (σ/mean = 10%) gives a deduction of 1.645 × 10% = <strong>16.5%</strong> of the mean estimate. Because ≥15% triggers a deduction per VM0042's schedule, this project would face a deduction.</p>
    </div>
    <div class="bg-white p-3 rounded border">
      <p class="font-semibold text-gray-700">For non-symmetric or skewed distributions:</p>
      <p class="mt-1 text-gray-600">MC simulation directly gives you the 5th percentile value from the simulated distribution — no normal-distribution assumption needed. This is why MC is required for spectroscopy models with non-Gaussian error structures.</p>
    </div>
  </div>
  <p class="mt-3 text-sm text-blue-800"><strong>Deduction schedule:</strong> If 95% confidence interval (CI) half-width &lt;15% of mean → 0% deduction. 15–20% → 5% deduction. 20–30% → 15% deduction. >30% → 35% deduction. Uncertainty is measured as a % of the point estimate.</p>
</div>

<h3 class="text-xl font-bold mt-6 mb-3">Model True-Up (Approach 1 only)</h3>
<AnalogyBox>
  <p class="font-semibold text-blue-800">🏦 Analogy: Bank Reconciliation</p>
  <p class="mt-1 text-sm text-blue-900">If your personal records say you have $5,000 but the bank statement says $4,700, you adjust your records to match reality. In VM0042, if the model predicted 2.5 tC/ha gain but actual soil samples show 2.0 tC/ha, the model is recalibrated and past credits may be adjusted.</p>
</AnalogyBox>
<p class="text-sm mb-3">True-up process:</p>
<ul class="list-disc ml-6 text-sm space-y-2 mb-4">
  <li>At each SOC measurement event (≥every 5 years), compare model predictions with actual measurements</li>
  <li>If model systematically over-predicted: recalibrate model, adjust model prediction error estimate</li>
  <li>Credits already issued based on over-predicted values may need buffer pool adjustments</li>
  <li>True-up data is incorporated into the model validation dataset, improving future predictions</li>
</ul>

<div class="bg-green-50 border border-green-200 rounded p-4">
  <p class="font-semibold text-green-800">Key Takeaway</p>
  <p class="text-green-900 mt-1">Analytical error propagation: combine individual uncertainties by adding squared relative uncertainties, then taking the square root. Monte Carlo: run thousands of simulations to build a probability distribution. True-up: reality check the model against actual measurements every 5 years. These three mechanisms ensure the methodology's uncertainty framework is robust and honest.</p>
</div>
