{/* 3.3: Calculating Baseline & Project Emissions */}

<h2 class="text-2xl font-bold text-gray-800 mb-4">Calculating Baseline & Project Emissions</h2>
<HighlightBox>
  <p class="font-semibold text-green-800">Beyond SOC: accounting for all GHG sources</p>
  <p class="text-green-900 mt-1">VM0042 tracks multiple emission sources beyond soil carbon. For each source, you calculate <em>both</em> the baseline emission and the project emission. The difference is the reduction. This lesson walks through the main calculations with real numbers.</p>
</HighlightBox>

<AnalogyBox>
  <p class="font-semibold text-blue-800">🧾 Analogy: A Full Business Profit & Loss Statement</p>
  <p class="mt-2 text-blue-900">Calculating baseline and project emissions is like preparing a profit &amp; loss statement for a business — but for greenhouse gases. You list every revenue line (GHG reduction) and every expense line (new GHG source) in both the baseline and project scenarios. The net carbon benefit is what's left after subtracting all project-scenario GHGs from all baseline-scenario GHGs. Miss an expense line and your "profit" is overstated — which an auditor will catch.</p>
</AnalogyBox>

<h3 class="text-xl font-bold mt-6 mb-3">Fossil Fuel CO₂ — Equations 6 & 7</h3>
<FormulaBox>
  <p class="font-mono text-sm">EFF<sub>j</sub> = FFC<sub>j</sub> × EF<sub>CO₂,j</sub></p>
  <p class="text-gray-400 text-xs mt-1">Where: FFC = fuel consumption (litres); EF = emission factor (tCO₂/litre)</p>
</FormulaBox>
<ExampleBox>
  <p class="font-semibold text-amber-800">📐 Worked Example: Fossil Fuel Reduction from No-Till</p>
  <ResponsiveTable>
<table class="mt-2 text-sm">
    <tr><th>Scenario</th><th>Tractor Passes/yr</th><th>Fuel Use (L/ha/yr)</th><th>CO₂ (tCO₂/ha/yr)</th></tr>
    <tr><td>Baseline (conventional tillage)</td><td>3</td><td>45</td><td>45 × 0.00268 = <strong>0.121</strong></td></tr>
    <tr><td>Project (no-till)</td><td>1</td><td>15</td><td>15 × 0.00268 = <strong>0.040</strong></td></tr>
    <tr><td><strong>Reduction</strong></td><td>—</td><td>—</td><td class="text-green-700 font-bold">0.081 tCO₂/ha/yr</td></tr>
  </table>
</ResponsiveTable>
  <p class="mt-2 text-sm text-amber-700">Diesel EF = 0.00268 tCO₂/litre. For 5,000 ha: 0.081 × 5,000 = <strong>405 tCO₂/yr</strong> fossil fuel reduction.</p>
</ExampleBox>

<h3 class="text-xl font-bold mt-6 mb-3">Liming CO₂ — Equations 8 & 9</h3>
<FormulaBox>
  <p class="font-mono text-sm">EL = (M<sub>Limestone</sub> × 0.12 + M<sub>Dolomite</sub> × 0.13) × 44/12</p>
  <p class="text-gray-400 text-xs mt-1">Units: M in tonnes; result in tCO₂</p>
</FormulaBox>
<ExampleBox>
  <p class="font-semibold text-amber-800">📐 Example: Liming Emissions</p>
  <p class="text-sm mt-1">Baseline: 1 t/ha calcitic limestone (CaCO₃) applied annually.<br />
  CO₂ = 1 × 0.12 × (44/12) = <strong>0.44 tCO₂/ha/yr</strong><br />
  Project: No liming (practice eliminated).<br />
  Reduction = <strong>0.44 tCO₂/ha/yr</strong></p>
</ExampleBox>

<h3 class="text-xl font-bold mt-6 mb-3">N₂O from Nitrogen Fertilizers (Approach 3) — Equations 16–23</h3>
<p class="text-sm mb-3">N₂O emissions come from synthetic fertilizers, organic N (manure, compost), crop residue N, and N-fixing species. The IPCC approach:</p>
<FormulaBox>
  <p class="font-mono text-sm">Direct N₂O = N<sub>input</sub> (kg N/ha) × EF₁ × 44/28 × GWP<sub>N₂O</sub></p>
  <p class="text-gray-400 text-xs mt-1">EF₁ = 0.01 (IPCC Tier 1: 1% of applied N becomes N₂O-N); 44/28 converts N₂O-N to N₂O; GWP N₂O = 265</p>
</FormulaBox>
<ExampleBox>
  <p class="font-semibold text-amber-800">📐 N₂O Worked Example</p>
  <ResponsiveTable>
<table class="mt-2 text-sm">
    <tr><th>Scenario</th><th>N Applied (kg N/ha)</th><th>N₂O Emissions (tCO₂e/ha/yr)</th></tr>
    <tr><td>Baseline</td><td>150</td><td>150 × 0.01 × (44/28) × 265 ÷ 1000 = <strong>0.627</strong></td></tr>
    <tr><td>Project (30% N reduction)</td><td>105</td><td>105 × 0.01 × (44/28) × 265 ÷ 1000 = <strong>0.439</strong></td></tr>
    <tr><td><strong>Reduction</strong></td><td>—</td><td class="text-green-700 font-bold">0.188 tCO₂e/ha/yr</td></tr>
  </table>
</ResponsiveTable>
  <p class="mt-2 text-sm text-amber-700">Note: Always check if project adds new N sources (e.g., N-fixing cover crops add N to soil — creates new N₂O that must be counted as a project emission).</p>
</ExampleBox>

<h3 class="text-xl font-bold mt-6 mb-3">Soil Methanogenesis CH₄ (Rice Projects) — Equations 12–14</h3>
<p class="text-sm mb-3">Flooded rice paddies are anaerobic environments — perfect conditions for methanogenic archaea to produce CH₄ from decomposing organic matter. This is <strong>one of the largest single emission sources in agriculture</strong>, making rice water management projects among the most impactful.</p>
<FormulaBox>
  <p class="font-mono text-sm">ECH4_rice = EF<sub>baseline</sub> × SF<sub>w</sub> × SF<sub>s</sub> × SF<sub>p</sub> × t × 10⁻³ × GWP<sub>CH₄</sub></p>
  <p class="text-gray-400 text-xs mt-1">Where: EF<sub>baseline</sub> = 1.30 kg CH₄/ha/day (IPCC default for continuously flooded with no organic amendments); SF<sub>w</sub> = water regime scaling factor; SF<sub>s</sub> = soil type factor; SF<sub>p</sub> = organic amendment factor; t = cultivation days</p>
</FormulaBox>
<ExampleBox>
  <p class="font-semibold text-amber-800">📐 Rice AWD Example — Vietnam Delta</p>
  <ResponsiveTable>
<table class="mt-2 text-sm">
    <tr><th>Scenario</th><th>Water Regime</th><th>SF<sub>w</sub></th><th>Cultivation days</th><th>CH₄ (tCO₂e/ha/season)</th></tr>
    <tr><td>Baseline (continuous flood)</td><td>Flooded</td><td>1.0</td><td>120</td><td>1.30 × 1.0 × 1.0 × 1.0 × 120 × 0.001 × 28 = <strong>4.37</strong></td></tr>
    <tr><td>Project (AWD)</td><td>Single drainage</td><td>0.52</td><td>120</td><td>1.30 × 0.52 × 1.0 × 1.0 × 120 × 0.001 × 28 = <strong>2.27</strong></td></tr>
    <tr><td><strong>Reduction</strong></td><td>—</td><td>—</td><td>—</td><td class="text-green-700 font-bold">2.10 tCO₂e/ha/season</td></tr>
  </table>
</ResponsiveTable>
  <p class="mt-2 text-sm text-amber-700">At 2 seasons/year and 10,000 ha: <strong>42,000 tCO₂e/yr</strong> just from CH₄ reduction — before any SOC benefit. This shows why rice AWD can generate 2–5 tCO₂e/ha/yr.</p>
  <p class="mt-1 text-sm text-gray-600">⚠️ AWD also slightly increases N₂O (aerobic conditions favour nitrification/denitrification). This must be calculated using the IPCC approach and subtracted from the CH₄ reduction to get the net benefit. Typically N₂O increase = 10–20% of the CH₄ gain, so AWD is still strongly beneficial.</p>
</ExampleBox>

<h3 class="text-xl font-bold mt-6 mb-3">Manure Deposition CH₄ & N₂O (Grazing Projects) — Equations 13 & 21</h3>
<p class="text-sm mb-3">Livestock manure deposited on pastures generates both CH₄ (anaerobic decomposition in dung pats) and N₂O (nitrification of urine-N). Both must be included when livestock are present in the project.</p>
<FormulaBox>
  <p class="font-mono text-sm">ECH4_manure = Pop × MS<sub>pasture</sub> × EF<sub>CH4,manure</sub> × GWP<sub>CH₄</sub> / 1000</p>
  <p class="font-mono text-sm mt-2">EN2O_manure = Pop × Nex × MS<sub>pasture</sub> × EF₃ × 44/28 × GWP<sub>N₂O</sub> / 1000</p>
  <p class="text-gray-400 text-xs mt-1">Where: MS<sub>pasture</sub> = fraction of manure deposited on pasture; Nex = N excretion per head (kg N/head/yr); EF₃ = manure N₂O emission factor (default 0.02 for pastures)</p>
</FormulaBox>
<ExampleBox>
  <p class="font-semibold text-amber-800">📐 Manure Emissions — Argentina Grazing Project</p>
  <p class="text-sm mt-1"><strong>Setup:</strong> 500 beef cattle, Nex = 40 kg N/head/yr, 90% of manure deposited on pasture (MS = 0.9), 1,000 ha.</p>
  <ResponsiveTable>
<table class="mt-2 text-sm">
    <tr><th>Emission</th><th>Baseline (500 cattle)</th><th>Project (350 cattle, 30% fewer)</th></tr>
    <tr><td>CH₄ from manure</td><td>500 × 0.9 × 1.0 × 28/1000 = <strong>12.6 tCO₂e/yr</strong></td><td>350 × 0.9 × 1.0 × 28/1000 = <strong>8.8</strong></td></tr>
    <tr><td>N₂O from manure urine</td><td>500 × 40 × 0.9 × 0.02 × 44/28 × 265/1000 = <strong>11.1</strong></td><td>350 × ... = <strong>7.8</strong></td></tr>
    <tr><td class="font-bold">Combined manure reduction</td><td>—</td><td class="text-green-700 font-bold">7.1 tCO₂e/yr</td></tr>
  </table>
</ResponsiveTable>
  <p class="mt-2 text-sm text-amber-700">Together with enteric fermentation reduction (~20.3 tCO₂e/yr), the total livestock GHG reduction is ~27.4 tCO₂e/yr for 1,000 ha = 0.027 tCO₂e/ha/yr — a smaller component than SOC for grazing, but still required.</p>
</ExampleBox>

<h3 class="text-xl font-bold mt-6 mb-3">Enteric Fermentation CH₄ (Grazing Projects) — Equation 11</h3>
<FormulaBox>
  <p class="font-mono text-sm">CH4_ent = (GWP<sub>CH₄</sub> × Pop<sub>livestock</sub> × EF<sub>ent</sub>) / 1000 / Area</p>
  <p class="text-gray-400 text-xs mt-1">EF<sub>ent</sub> = emission factor in kg CH₄/head/yr (from IPCC Table for livestock type)</p>
</FormulaBox>
<ExampleBox>
  <p class="font-semibold text-amber-800">📐 Grazing Example</p>
  <p class="text-sm mt-1">Baseline: 500 cattle on 1,000 ha. EF = 58 kg CH₄/head/yr (Beef cattle, Tier 1).<br />
  CH₄ = (28 × 500 × 58) / 1000 / 1,000 ha = <strong>0.812 tCO₂e/ha/yr</strong></p>
  <p class="text-sm mt-1">Project: 400 cattle (20% stocking rate reduction).<br />
  CH₄ = (28 × 400 × 58) / 1000 / 1,000 = <strong>0.650 tCO₂e/ha/yr</strong><br />
  Reduction = <strong>0.162 tCO₂e/ha/yr</strong></p>
</ExampleBox>

<div class="bg-amber-50 border border-amber-200 rounded p-4 mb-4">
  <p class="font-semibold text-amber-800">⚠️ Important: Emissions go BOTH ways</p>
  <p class="text-sm mt-1">The project scenario may have HIGHER emissions for some sources (e.g., more fuel for irrigation pumping, more N₂O from N-fixing cover crops). Always calculate emissions for both scenarios — don't assume the project reduces everything.</p>
</div>

<div class="bg-green-50 border border-green-200 rounded p-4">
  <p class="font-semibold text-green-800">Key Takeaway</p>
  <p class="text-green-900 mt-1">Every emission source in the project boundary must be calculated for BOTH baseline and project scenarios. Emission reductions = Baseline − Project. When project emissions exceed baseline for a source (e.g., more fuel), this counts as a negative reduction (i.e., it reduces your net credit claim).</p>
</div>
