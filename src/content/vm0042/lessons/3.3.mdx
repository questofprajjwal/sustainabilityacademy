<h2 class="text-2xl font-bold text-gray-800 mb-4">Calculating Baseline & Project Emissions</h2>
<HighlightBox>
  <p class="font-semibold text-green-800">Beyond SOC: accounting for all GHG sources</p>
  <p class="text-green-900 mt-1">VM0042 tracks multiple emission sources beyond soil carbon. For each source, you calculate <em>both</em> the baseline emission and the project emission. The difference is the reduction. This lesson walks through the main calculations with real numbers.</p>
</HighlightBox>

<AnalogyBox>
  <p class="font-semibold text-blue-800">🧾 Analogy: A Full Business Profit & Loss Statement</p>
  <p class="mt-2 text-blue-900">Calculating baseline and project emissions is like preparing a profit &amp; loss statement for a business, but for greenhouse gases. You list every revenue line (GHG reduction) and every expense line (new GHG source) in both the baseline and project scenarios. The net carbon benefit is what's left after subtracting all project-scenario GHGs from all baseline-scenario GHGs. Miss an expense line and your "profit" is overstated, which an auditor will catch.</p>
</AnalogyBox>

<h3 class="text-xl font-bold mt-6 mb-3">Soil Methanogenesis CH₄ (Rice Projects), Equations 12–14</h3>
<p class="text-sm mb-3">Flooded rice paddies are anaerobic environments, perfect conditions for methanogenic archaea to produce CH₄ from decomposing organic matter. This is <strong>one of the largest single emission sources in agriculture</strong>, making rice water management projects among the most impactful.</p>
<EquationBreakdown
  title="Equations 12-14 - Rice Paddy Methane Emissions"
  result="ECH4<sub>rice</sub> | Rice CH₄ Emissions | Total methane from flooded rice paddies, converted to tCO₂e/ha/season | blue"
  inputs="EF<sub>base</sub> | Baseline Emission Factor | Default CH₄ emission rate for continuously flooded rice with no organic amendments: 1.30 kg CH₄/ha/day (IPCC) | green ;; SF<sub>w</sub> | Water Regime Factor | Scaling factor based on how the field is flooded. Continuous flood = 1.0, single drainage (AWD) = 0.52 | amber ;; SF<sub>p</sub> | Organic Amendment Factor | Adjusts for straw or manure incorporation. No amendments = 1.0, straw added increases it | violet ;; t | Cultivation Days | Number of days the rice crop is growing in the field per season (typically 90-120 days) | rose ;; GWP<sub>CH₄</sub> | Methane GWP | Global warming potential of methane over 100 years. Value is 28 | cyan"
  operator="×"
/>
<ExampleBox>
  <p class="font-semibold text-amber-800">📐 Rice AWD Example, Vietnam Delta</p>
  <ResponsiveTable>
<table class="mt-2 text-sm">
    <thead><tr><th>Scenario</th><th>Water Regime</th><th>SF<sub>w</sub></th><th>Cultivation days</th><th>CH₄ (tCO₂e/ha/season)</th></tr></thead>
    <tbody>
    <tr><td>Baseline (continuous flood)</td><td>Flooded</td><td>1.0</td><td>120</td><td>1.30 × 1.0 × 1.0 × 1.0 × 120 × 0.001 × 28 = <strong>4.37</strong></td></tr>
    <tr><td>Project (AWD)</td><td>Single drainage</td><td>0.52</td><td>120</td><td>1.30 × 0.52 × 1.0 × 1.0 × 120 × 0.001 × 28 = <strong>2.27</strong></td></tr>
    <tr><td><strong>Reduction</strong></td><td>-</td><td>-</td><td>-</td><td class="text-green-700 font-bold">2.10 tCO₂e/ha/season</td></tr>
    </tbody>
  </table>
</ResponsiveTable>
  <p class="mt-2 text-sm text-amber-700">At 2 seasons/year and 10,000 ha: <strong>42,000 tCO₂e/yr</strong> just from CH₄ reduction, before any SOC benefit. This shows why rice AWD can generate 2–5 tCO₂e/ha/yr.</p>
  <p class="mt-1 text-sm text-gray-600">⚠️ AWD also slightly increases N₂O (aerobic conditions favour nitrification/denitrification). This must be calculated using the IPCC approach and subtracted from the CH₄ reduction to get the net benefit. Typically N₂O increase = 10–20% of the CH₄ gain, so AWD is still strongly beneficial.</p>
</ExampleBox>

<h3 class="text-xl font-bold mt-6 mb-3">Enteric Fermentation CH₄ (Grazing Projects), Equation 11</h3>
<EquationBreakdown
  title="Equation 11 - Enteric Fermentation CH₄"
  result="CH4<sub>ent</sub> | Enteric CH₄ Emissions | Methane from livestock digestion (belching), in tCO₂e/ha/yr | blue"
  inputs="Pop<sub>livestock</sub> | Livestock Population | Number of animals in the project area | green ;; EF<sub>ent</sub> | Enteric Emission Factor | kg CH₄ per head per year from digestion.   operator="×"
/>
<ExampleBox>
  <p class="font-semibold text-amber-800">📐 Grazing Example</p>
  <p class="text-sm mt-1">Baseline: 500 cattle on 1,000 ha. EF = 58 kg CH₄/head/yr (Beef cattle, Tier 1).<br />
  CH₄ = (28 × 500 × 58) / 1000 / 1,000 ha = <strong>0.812 tCO₂e/ha/yr</strong></p>
  <p class="text-sm mt-1">Project: 400 cattle (20% stocking rate reduction).<br />
  CH₄ = (28 × 400 × 58) / 1000 / 1,000 = <strong>0.650 tCO₂e/ha/yr</strong><br />
  Reduction = <strong>0.162 tCO₂e/ha/yr</strong></p>
</ExampleBox>

<div class="bg-amber-50 border border-amber-200 rounded p-4 mb-4">
  <p class="font-semibold text-amber-800">⚠️ Important: Emissions go BOTH ways</p>
  <p class="text-sm mt-1">The project scenario may have HIGHER emissions for some sources (e.g., more fuel for irrigation pumping, more N₂O from N-fixing cover crops). Always calculate emissions for both scenarios, don't assume the project reduces everything.</p>
</div>

<div class="bg-green-50 border border-green-200 rounded p-4">
  <p class="font-semibold text-green-800">Key Takeaway</p>
  <p class="text-green-900 mt-1">Every emission source in the project boundary must be calculated for BOTH baseline and project scenarios. Emission reductions = Baseline − Project. When project emissions exceed baseline for a source (e.g., more fuel), this counts as a negative reduction (i.e., it reduces your net credit claim).</p>
</div>
