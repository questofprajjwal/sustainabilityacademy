<h2 class="text-2xl font-bold text-gray-800 mb-4">Economics & Decision-Making</h2>
<HighlightBox>
  <p class="font-semibold text-green-800">Is the project financially viable?</p>
  <p class="text-green-900 mt-1">Understanding the economics of a VM0042 project is essential before investing in project development. This lesson covers costs, revenue potential, and the decision factors that determine whether a project makes financial sense.</p>
</HighlightBox>

<AnalogyBox>
  <p class="font-semibold text-blue-800">🏗️ Analogy: Opening a Restaurant</p>
  <p class="mt-2 text-blue-900">Project economics for a VM0042 project work like opening a restaurant. You have large upfront costs (fit-out = PDD preparation; kitchen equipment = model setup; health inspection = validation audit). Then recurring operating costs (staff = monitoring; health checks = periodic verification). Revenue only starts once you open. If your restaurant is too small, the fixed costs eat all your profit — exactly why minimum scale matters in carbon projects.</p>
</AnalogyBox>

<h3 class="text-xl font-bold mt-6 mb-3">Timeline to First Credits — Reality Check</h3>
<p class="text-sm mb-3">A common mistake is underestimating how long it takes from project idea to first VCU issuance. Here is a realistic timeline:</p>

<table class="text-sm">

    <tr><td>PDD preparation</td><td>3–6 months</td><td>Technical writing, baseline scenario documentation, GHG calculations</td><td>$30–$100K</td></tr>

    <tr><td>Validation by VVB</td><td>3–6 months</td><td>VVB review, site visit, corrective actions resolved</td><td>$30–$60K</td></tr>
    <tr><td>Verra registration</td><td>2–4 months</td><td>Registry review, public comment period (30 days)</td><td>Registration fee</td></tr>
    <tr><td>First monitoring period</td><td>12–60 months</td><td>Farmers implement practices; soil sampling at end</td><td>Ongoing monitoring</td></tr>

    <tr><td class="font-bold text-green-700">First VCUs issued</td><td class="font-bold text-green-700">24–36 months from start</td><td class="font-bold">Best case; often 3–4 years total</td><td class="font-bold">All upfront costs already spent</td></tr>
  </table>
</ResponsiveTable>
</div>
<div class="bg-amber-50 border border-amber-200 rounded p-3 mb-6 text-sm">
  <p class="font-semibold text-amber-800">⚠️ Cash flow implication</p>
  <p class="mt-1 text-amber-900">The project developer spends $200,000–$400,000 upfront before receiving any revenue. This requires working capital or upfront offtake agreements (advance purchase contracts) from credit buyers. Many projects de-risk this by securing <strong>forward purchase agreements</strong> at a lower price ($10–$15/VCU) in exchange for guaranteed purchase, providing capital to fund development.</p>
</div>

<h3 class="text-xl font-bold mt-6 mb-3">Voluntary Carbon Market Dynamics</h3>
<p class="text-sm mb-3">Carbon credit prices are not fixed — they move with supply, demand, policy signals, and credit quality perception. Understanding market dynamics helps project developers make informed go/no-go decisions.</p>
<ResponsiveTable>
<table class="mb-4 text-sm">
  <tr><th>Period</th><th>Typical ALM Credit Price</th><th>Key Driver</th></tr>
  <tr><td>2018–2020</td><td>$3–$8/VCU</td><td>Low demand; limited corporate commitments</td></tr>
  <tr><td>2021</td><td>$10–$20/VCU</td><td>Corporate net-zero pledges surge; COP26 momentum</td></tr>
  <tr><td>2022–2023</td><td>$5–$15/VCU</td><td>Media scrutiny of credit quality; buyer caution</td></tr>
  <tr><td>2024–2025</td><td>$8–$25/VCU</td><td>Quality differentiation; high-integrity projects command premiums</td></tr>
</table>
</ResponsiveTable>
<div class="grid grid-cols-1 md:grid-cols-2 gap-3 mb-6 text-sm">
  <div class="bg-green-50 border border-green-200 rounded p-3">
    <p class="font-semibold text-green-800">Factors commanding price premium:</p>
    <ul class="mt-1 space-y-1 text-green-900">
      <li>✅ CCB or SD VISta co-benefit certification</li>
      <li>✅ Article 6 eligibility (ITMO-eligible credits)</li>
      <li>✅ High additionality / strong barrier evidence</li>
      <li>✅ Smallholder / developing country story</li>
      <li>✅ Soil carbon label / verified permanence claim</li>
    </ul>
  </div>
  <div class="bg-red-50 border border-red-200 rounded p-3">
    <p class="font-semibold text-red-700">Factors causing price discount:</p>
    <ul class="mt-1 space-y-1 text-red-800">
      <li>❌ Weak additionality documentation</li>
      <li>❌ High uncertainty (large deduction applied)</li>
      <li>❌ Old vintage (>5 years from issuance)</li>
      <li>❌ Developed country with no poverty co-benefit</li>
      <li>❌ No community benefit sharing plan</li>
    </ul>
  </div>
</div>

<h3 class="text-xl font-bold mt-6 mb-3">Key Decision Factors</h3>
<div class="grid grid-cols-2 gap-3 mb-6 text-sm">
  <div class="bg-green-50 border border-green-200 rounded p-3">
    <p class="font-semibold text-green-800">Favourable conditions:</p>
    <ul class="mt-1 text-green-900 space-y-1">
      <li>✅ Large project area (&gt;5,000 ha)</li>
      <li>✅ High-impact practices (rice AWD, degraded grassland)</li>
      <li>✅ Existing farm records (lower baseline cost)</li>
      <li>✅ Grouped project structure (aggregate small farms)</li>
      <li>✅ Carbon price above $20/VCU</li>
    </ul>
  </div>
  <div class="bg-red-50 border border-red-200 rounded p-3">
    <p class="font-semibold text-red-700">Challenging conditions:</p>
    <ul class="mt-1 text-red-800 space-y-1">
      <li>❌ Small scale (&lt;1,000 ha)</li>
      <li>❌ Low SOC change potential (already high SOC baseline)</li>
      <li>❌ No farm records (high baseline cost)</li>
      <li>❌ Fragmented landholding (many small plots)</li>
      <li>❌ Carbon price below $15/VCU</li>
    </ul>
  </div>
</div>

<div class="bg-green-50 border border-green-200 rounded p-4">
  <p class="font-semibold text-green-800">Key Takeaway</p>
  <p class="text-green-900 mt-1">Project economics depend on area scale, practice type, approach choice, and carbon price. Fixed costs (development, validation, verification) make minimum viable project size approximately 5,000–10,000 ha for Approach 1, or smaller with Approach 3. Grouped projects that aggregate thousands of smallholder farms can overcome this scale barrier.</p>
</div>
