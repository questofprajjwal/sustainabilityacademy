<h2 class="text-2xl font-bold text-gray-800 mb-4">Soil as a Carbon Bank</h2>

<HighlightBox>
  <p class="font-semibold text-green-800">The core concept of VM0042</p>
  <p class="text-green-900 mt-1">VM0042 revolves around <strong>Soil Organic Carbon (SOC)</strong>, the carbon stored in soil from decomposed plant and animal matter. Understanding how the "soil carbon bank" works is the foundation of the entire methodology.</p>
</HighlightBox>

<AnalogyBox>
  <p class="font-semibold text-blue-800">🏦 Analogy: The Soil Carbon Bank Account</p>
  <p class="mt-2 text-blue-900">Think of soil as a bank account for carbon:</p>
  <ul class="mt-2 space-y-1 text-blue-900">
    <li><strong>Deposits (inputs):</strong> Plant roots dying, crop residues decomposing, compost, manure</li>
    <li><strong>Withdrawals (losses):</strong> Tillage accelerating decomposition, residue burning, bare fallow (no plant cover)</li>
    <li><strong>Balance:</strong> SOC stock, measured in tonnes of carbon per hectare (tC/ha)</li>
  </ul>
  <p class="mt-2 text-blue-900">Conventional farming has been making more <em>withdrawals</em> than deposits for decades. VM0042 issues "receipts" (carbon credits) when a farm starts making net deposits again.</p>
</AnalogyBox>

<h3 class="text-xl font-bold mt-6 mb-3">What Is Soil Organic Carbon (SOC)?</h3>
<p class="mb-3">In simple terms: when plants grow, they pull CO₂ out of the air and turn it into organic matter, roots, stems, leaves. When they die and decompose, much of that carbon moves into the soil. That carbon is SOC.</p>
<p class="mb-4">Formal definition: SOC is carbon stored in soil organic matter, decomposed plant residues, root material, microbial biomass, and humus. It differs from inorganic carbon (like calcium carbonate in limestone) and from CO₂ in soil air pores.</p>

<ExampleBox>
  <p class="font-semibold text-amber-800">📍 Real Projects Measuring SOC</p>
  <ul class="mt-2 space-y-2 text-sm">
    <li><strong>Soil Carbon Initiative (Australia):</strong> Australian farms under the Carbon Farming Initiative have their SOC measured at 30 cm depth before and after switching to no-till and pasture management, the difference determines Emission Reduction Fund credits.</li>
    <li><strong>Nori Carbon Marketplace (USA):</strong> US corn and soy farmers are paid per tonne of CO₂ sequestered in topsoil, verified by soil sampling and the COMET-Farm model.</li>
    <li><strong>ICAR-CSSRI India:</strong> Research stations in Punjab and Haryana track how residue retention from rice-wheat systems raises SOC over 5-year periods, providing validation data for models used in VM0042 projects.</li>
  </ul>
</ExampleBox>

<ResponsiveTable>
<table class="mb-6">
  <tr><th>SOC Stock (typical)</th><th>Farming System</th></tr>
  <tr><td>40–80 tC/ha</td><td>Native grassland or forest (high SOC)</td></tr>

<h3 class="text-xl font-bold mt-6 mb-3">How Does SOC Get into Soil?</h3>
<ul class="list-disc ml-6 space-y-2 mb-4">
  <li><strong>Crop roots:</strong> When roots die, carbon stays in soil. Deep-rooted crops (like cover crops or grasses) deposit more carbon.</li>
  <li><strong>Crop residues:</strong> Leaving stubble on the field lets microbes decompose it, adding carbon to the soil.</li>
  <li><strong>Compost & manure:</strong> Organic amendments directly add carbon.</li>
  <li><strong>Reduced tillage:</strong> Tillage breaks up soil aggregates, exposing previously protected organic matter to rapid decomposition, releasing CO₂. Less tillage = less loss.</li>
</ul>

<ExampleBox>
  <p class="font-semibold text-amber-800">📐 Real-World Numbers</p>
  <p class="mt-2">A 1,000 ha farm switches from conventional tillage to no-till + cover crops. Over 5 years:</p>
  <ul class="mt-2 space-y-1">
    <li>SOC increases from <strong>25 tC/ha → 27 tC/ha</strong></li>
    <li>Change: +2 tC/ha × 1,000 ha = <strong>2,000 tC total</strong></li>
    <li>Converting C to CO₂: × (44/12) = <strong>7,333 tCO₂ removed from atmosphere</strong></li>
    <li>= approximately <strong>7,333 potential carbon credits</strong></li>
  </ul>
</ExampleBox>

<h3 class="text-xl font-bold mt-6 mb-3">Why SOC Matters Beyond Carbon</h3>
<p class="mb-3">Here's the exciting part: building SOC is not just good for the climate. It is good for the farmer too. Healthier soil grows better crops.</p>
<p class="mb-4">Higher SOC improves soil health in multiple ways, which is why "regenerative agriculture" and carbon credits often go hand in hand:</p>
<ul class="list-disc ml-6 space-y-2 mb-4">
  <li>Better water-holding capacity (drought resilience)</li>
  <li>Improved soil structure (less compaction)</li>
  <li>Higher nutrient availability (reduced fertilizer need)</li>
  <li>Greater biodiversity of soil organisms</li>
</ul>

<div class="bg-amber-50 border border-amber-300 rounded-lg p-4 mb-4">
  <h4 class="font-semibold text-amber-800 mb-2">Important: Soil Carbon Saturation (Equilibrium)</h4>
  <p class="text-amber-900 text-sm">Soils do not accumulate carbon indefinitely. Each soil has a <strong>saturation capacity</strong>, a maximum SOC level determined by clay content, mineralogy, climate, and organic matter inputs. As improved practices are applied, SOC accumulates quickly at first, then slows, and eventually reaches a new steady-state equilibrium (typically within 20–40 years).</p>
  <p class="text-amber-900 text-sm mt-2"><strong>Why this matters for VM0042 projects:</strong></p>
  <ul class="text-amber-800 text-sm mt-1 list-disc pl-4 space-y-1">
    <li>Carbon <em>removal</em> VCUs (from SOC accumulation) will naturally taper off over the project lifetime as soils approach saturation</li>
    <li>After equilibrium, the project still generates <em>emission reduction</em> VCUs (from reduced N₂O, CH₄, fossil fuel) but no new removal VCUs from additional SOC gain</li>
    <li>Financial models must account for this tapering, projects cannot project a constant high credit yield for 30+ years from SOC alone</li>
    <li>VM0042 projects with high-clay soils in wet climates have higher saturation capacity (more potential); degraded sandy soils in dry climates approach saturation faster</li>
  </ul>
</div>
