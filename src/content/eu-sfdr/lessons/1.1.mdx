Article 3 of SFDR requires every financial market participant and financial adviser in scope to publish and maintain on their website information about their **policies on the integration of sustainability risks** in their investment decision-making process (for FMPs) or investment advice process (for financial advisers).

This is the foundational entity-level disclosure obligation, it applies to every firm in scope, regardless of whether any of their products are classified as Article 8 or Article 9.

In plain English: every firm covered by SFDR must publicly explain what it does (or does not do) when it comes to managing ESG-related risks to investment returns.

## What Is a Sustainability Risk Integration Policy?

A sustainability risk integration policy describes how the firm considers ESG-related events and conditions that could cause a material negative impact on the value of investments. The emphasis here is on **financial materiality**, sustainability risks are framed as inputs to investment risk management, not as ethical commitments.

<AnalogyBox>
Think of this like a bank publishing its credit risk management policy. The bank doesn't lend because it dislikes default risk in a moral sense, it manages default risk because defaults cost money. Similarly, SFDR's Article 3 policy is about managing ESG risks that can hurt investment returns. The question is: how does the firm identify, assess, and respond to those risks?
</AnalogyBox>

<HighlightBox>
The sustainability risk policy under Article 3 is about risk management, not values-based investing. A firm must explain how it considers the risk that, for example, climate change regulation might reduce the value of holdings in carbon-intensive companies, or that a governance scandal might destroy shareholder value in an investee. The question is: how does the firm manage these risks?
</HighlightBox>

The policy must be published on the firm's website and kept up to date. It does not need to be filed with a regulator, but it is subject to supervisory review. It must be written clearly and be easily accessible, buried in the depths of a website or written in impenetrable legal language would not meet the spirit of the requirement.

## What Must the Policy Cover?

SFDR Article 3 does not specify exhaustive content requirements for the policy, those are developed through the Level 2 framework and supervisory guidance. However, the policy should address, at a minimum:

**1. How sustainability risks are identified**

The firm must describe the ESG factors it monitors and how it sources information about them. Does it rely on internal ESG research? Third-party data providers? Engagement with investee companies? Each approach has different implications for data quality and coverage.

**2. How sustainability risks are assessed**

The firm must explain its methodology for evaluating the materiality of sustainability risks. This might include:
- ESG scoring or rating systems
- Scenario analysis (e.g., testing portfolios against different climate scenarios)
- Sector-specific risk frameworks
- Portfolio-level carbon footprint metrics

**3. How sustainability risks are integrated into investment decisions**

The firm must describe how identified and assessed sustainability risks influence actual investment decisions. This is the critical link: identifying a risk but taking no action does not constitute genuine integration. The policy should describe whether sustainability risks lead to exclusions, weighting adjustments, engagement, or some combination.

**4. The likely impact of sustainability risks on returns**

Article 3 requires the firm to describe the expected impact of sustainability risks on the returns of financial products it makes available. This does not require precise quantification, but the firm should indicate whether it expects sustainability risks to have a high, medium, or low impact, and explain the basis for that view.

<ExampleBox>
**Example, Substantive Article 3 policy extract:**

"[Firm X] integrates sustainability risks, ESG events or conditions that could cause material negative impacts on investment value, through the following process:

*Identification*: Our ESG research team reviews company disclosures, third-party ESG ratings from [Provider Y], and controversy alerts. We monitor 12 environmental factors (including carbon emissions, water stress, and biodiversity impact), 8 social factors (including labour standards, supply chain practices, and human rights), and 6 governance factors (including board composition, executive remuneration, and audit quality).

*Assessment*: For each holding and prospective investment, we assign a proprietary ESG risk score from 1 (low risk) to 5 (high risk). Companies scoring 4 or 5 are reviewed by our ESG oversight committee. We conduct annual climate scenario analysis at the portfolio level using the NGFS scenarios.

*Integration*: Sustainability risk scores feed into our investment case assessments. Portfolio managers must document how they have considered sustainability risks in their investment memos. Companies scoring 5 on governance risk are excluded from all equity strategies. For other high-risk scores, we may reduce position sizes or initiate engagement.

*Expected impact on returns*: We consider sustainability risks to have a medium-to-high potential impact on long-term returns across most of our strategies, particularly in relation to climate transition risks in energy-intensive sectors."
</ExampleBox>

## The "Not Relevant" Option

Article 3(2) allows a firm to state in its policy that it deems sustainability risks "not relevant" to its investment decisions, provided the firm explains why. This option is available, but increasingly difficult to substantiate under supervisory scrutiny.

The ESMA Q&As (Q&A I.5, confirmed by ESA guidance published 25 July 2024) make clear that a firm cannot use the Article 6(1) second subparagraph "not relevant" option (the equivalent provision for product-level disclosures) to override sustainability risk obligations under other EU law. For example, an AIFM that deems sustainability risks "not relevant" for SFDR purposes must still comply with its obligation to consider sustainability risks as part of its risk management framework under AIFMD's Commission Delegated Regulation (EU) 231/2013 (Article 18(5)).

<HighlightBox>
Regulators and NCAs have become increasingly sceptical of blanket "not relevant" statements. For most mainstream investment strategies, at least some investee companies will be exposed to climate transition risks, governance risks, or social controversies. A credible "not relevant" position requires a documented, evidence-based case that sustainability risks have no material impact on the firm's specific investment universe and strategy, a difficult position to maintain for diversified managers.
</HighlightBox>

## Interaction with Article 4 (PAI)

The Article 3 sustainability risk policy is closely related to but distinct from the Article 4 PAI consideration statement.

The risk policy focuses on how sustainability risks (ESG factors that affect investment value) are managed. The PAI statement focuses on how the firm's investment decisions affect sustainability factors (impacts on the environment and society). These are two directions of the same ESG coin:

- **Risk**: How does ESG affect my portfolio?
- **Impact**: How does my portfolio affect ESG?

Firms should ensure their Article 3 policy and Article 4 statement are consistent and mutually reinforcing, not contradictory.

## Remuneration Linkage

Article 5 requires remuneration policies to be consistent with the integration of sustainability risks. The Article 3 policy should therefore be cross-referenced to, or aligned with, the firm's remuneration framework. If the firm claims in its Article 3 policy that sustainability risk scores influence investment decisions, the remuneration structure should not provide incentives that undermine this (e.g., purely short-term performance bonuses that incentivise ignoring long-term sustainability risks).

## Practical Compliance Checklist

To ensure Article 3 compliance, firms should verify:

- The policy is published on the firm's website in a prominent and accessible location
- The policy is written in plain language understandable to the target audience
- The policy covers identification, assessment, integration, and expected impact on returns
- The policy is reviewed and updated at least annually, or sooner if the firm's approach changes
- If the "not relevant" position is taken, it is clearly reasoned and documented
- The policy is consistent with the firm's actual investment processes (described in internal procedures)
- The policy is consistent with Article 4 and Article 5 obligations

Consistency between what is disclosed externally and what happens internally is critical. A policy that claims robust sustainability risk integration but is not reflected in actual investment processes is a misrepresentation, and one that supervisors can investigate by reviewing investment memos, committee minutes, and portfolio construction records.
