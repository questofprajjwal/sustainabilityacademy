SFDR is not enforced by a single regulator sitting in Brussels. Instead, it operates through a network of institutions, EU-level standard setters and national supervisors, who together create, interpret, and enforce the rules. Understanding this network helps you know who to watch for guidance and who can take action against your firm.

## The European Commission

The **European Commission** is the primary legislative actor in the SFDR framework. It adopted SFDR as a Level 1 regulation in 2019 and subsequently adopted the Regulatory Technical Standards (Commission Delegated Regulation (EU) 2022/1288) as a Level 2 measure in April 2022.

The Commission also issues interpretive guidance. The **EC SFDR FAQ (April 2023)** represents the Commission's own answers to questions about how certain SFDR provisions should be interpreted as a matter of EU law. These answers, highlighted in blue in the ESMA Consolidated Q&As, carry significant interpretive authority, the Commission is clarifying provisions it drafted. However, they are not legally binding in the same way as the regulation itself, and only the Court of Justice of the EU has authority to authoritatively interpret EU law.

<HighlightBox>
The Commission's April 2023 FAQ is particularly important because it clarified the definition of sustainable investment (Article 2(17)), confirming that SFDR does not prescribe a specific methodology for assessing whether an investment qualifies as sustainable. Financial market participants must disclose their own methodology, but there is no single regulatory test. This flexibility is both a feature and a challenge.
</HighlightBox>

The Commission is also responsible for the **SFDR review** process that began in 2023, which may result in significant amendments to the framework.

## The European Supervisory Authorities (ESAs)

Three ESAs share responsibility for SFDR implementation:

**ESMA (European Securities and Markets Authority)** leads on issues relating to investment funds (UCITS, AIFs), investment firms, and market integrity. ESMA is the primary author of Q&A guidance on SFDR application and coordinates the joint ESA positions.

**EBA (European Banking Authority)** leads on issues relating to credit institutions and their portfolio management and investment advisory activities.

**EIOPA (European Insurance and Occupational Pensions Authority)** leads on issues relating to insurance undertakings (IBIPs), insurance intermediaries, and institutions for occupational retirement provision (IORPs).

Together, the three ESAs form the **Joint Committee of the European Supervisory Authorities**, which produces the Consolidated SFDR Q&As and coordinates positions on cross-sectoral issues. The Q&As document carries the joint committee reference JC 2023 18 and was most recently updated on 4 November 2025.

<AnalogyBox>
Think of the three ESAs as three specialist regulators watching different segments of the same motorway. ESMA monitors the investment fund lane. EBA monitors the banking lane. EIOPA monitors the insurance lane. The Joint Committee coordinates when vehicles cross lanes, for example, a bank-owned UCITS management company sits at the intersection of the EBA and ESMA lanes. The SFDR Q&As are the shared highway code that all three regulators apply.
</AnalogyBox>

## National Competent Authorities (NCAs)

**National Competent Authorities** are the Member State regulators responsible for day-to-day supervision of SFDR compliance within their jurisdiction. Key NCAs include:

- **AMF** (Autorité des marchés financiers), France
- **BaFin** (Bundesanstalt für Finanzdienstleistungsaufsicht), Germany
- **CBI** (Central Bank of Ireland), Ireland (a major fund domicile)
- **CSSF** (Commission de Surveillance du Secteur Financier), Luxembourg (the largest EU fund domicile)
- **AFM** (Autoriteit Financiële Markten), Netherlands
- **FCA** (Financial Conduct Authority), UK (no longer directly bound by SFDR post-Brexit, but monitors equivalent obligations)

NCAs have enforcement powers over the entities they supervise within their jurisdiction. Where a firm breaches SFDR obligations, the NCA can take supervisory action including requiring the firm to update or correct disclosures, imposing supervisory measures, and in serious cases, initiating administrative or criminal proceedings under national law.

Article 14 of SFDR requires Member States to ensure that NCAs have the powers and resources necessary to supervise compliance with SFDR obligations.

<ExampleBox>
**Real-world NCA action, BaFin and DWS:**

In 2022, German regulator BaFin and US authorities conducted searches of **DWS** offices in Frankfurt related to greenwashing allegations. The allegation was that DWS had overstated the extent to which ESG criteria were integrated into its investment products. DWS's former sustainability chief had raised concerns internally before going public.

This case illustrates how NCAs can investigate mismatches between SFDR disclosures and actual investment practice. The DWS case became a benchmark reference for the market: sustainability claims in SFDR disclosures must reflect reality, not aspiration. DWS subsequently paid a $19 million settlement with the SEC in 2023 related to the greenwashing allegations.
</ExampleBox>

<HighlightBox>
SFDR does not harmonise sanctions. Each Member State sets its own penalties for SFDR breaches under national law. This creates some variation in the severity of sanctions across the EU. NCAs are required to cooperate under SFDR and through the ESA framework, but actual enforcement intensity varies across jurisdictions. Ireland and Luxembourg, as the dominant EU fund domiciles, are particularly significant supervisory hubs.
</HighlightBox>

## Financial Market Participants and Financial Advisers

The regulated community itself, investment managers, insurance companies, pension funds, and advisory firms, are not merely subjects of the regulation; they are active participants in its implementation. Large asset managers have invested significantly in data infrastructure, legal analysis, and product reclassification to comply with SFDR.

Industry associations play an important intermediary role:
- **EFAMA** (European Fund and Asset Management Association), represents UCITS and AIF managers
- **Insurance Europe**, represents the insurance industry
- **PensionsEurope**, represents pension fund managers

These associations engage with ESAs and the Commission during consultations and contribute to developing market practice standards for SFDR implementation.

## Data Providers

A critical but often overlooked part of the SFDR ecosystem is the **ESG data provider** sector. SFDR's PAI indicator framework requires firms to report quantitative data on 18 mandatory indicators, including greenhouse gas emissions, carbon footprint, and controversies involving human rights violations. This data must be sourced at the investee company level.

Most investment managers do not collect this data themselves. They rely on third-party ESG data providers such as MSCI ESG Research, Sustainalytics, Bloomberg ESG, Refinitiv, and S&P Global Trucost. The quality and coverage of these data providers significantly affects the reliability of SFDR disclosures.

<ExampleBox>
**Example, Data gaps in the SFDR ecosystem:**

An Irish-domiciled UCITS fund classified as Article 8 invests in 300 companies globally. Its PAI statement must include data on greenhouse gas emissions (Scope 1, 2, and 3) for each investee. Many smaller companies in emerging markets do not publish Scope 3 emissions data.

The fund manager purchases data from an ESG data provider such as **MSCI ESG Research** or **Sustainalytics**. For companies without reported data, the provider uses estimation models. The fund manager must disclose in its PAI statement what proportion of data is based on reported versus estimated figures, and what data sources and methodologies were used.

This means the quality of the SFDR disclosure chain runs from: company sustainability reporting (CSRD/ESRS) → ESG data aggregation → fund manager disclosure → investor understanding.
</ExampleBox>

## The Court of Justice of the European Union (CJEU)

While not an operational SFDR supervisor, the **CJEU** is the ultimate arbiter of EU law interpretation. Only the CJEU can authoritatively interpret whether a provision of SFDR means what the Commission, ESAs, or NCAs say it means. No SFDR case has yet reached the CJEU, but as supervisory enforcement intensifies and disputes arise, judicial interpretation will become increasingly important in defining the boundaries of compliance.

## Putting the Ecosystem Together

A financial market participant operating in this ecosystem must navigate:
1. The Level 1 SFDR text (the regulation itself)
2. The Level 2 RTS (Commission Delegated Regulation 2022/1288 and its amendment 2023/363)
3. ESMA/ESA Q&As and other guidance
4. NCA-specific expectations in their home jurisdiction and in any Member State where they market products
5. Industry association guidance and market practice norms

Effective SFDR compliance requires monitoring all of these sources continuously, as the framework is still maturing and guidance evolves regularly.
