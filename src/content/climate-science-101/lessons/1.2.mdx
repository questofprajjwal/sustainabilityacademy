<h2 class="text-2xl font-bold text-gray-800 mb-4">Paleoclimate: 800,000 Years of Evidence</h2>

<HighlightBox>
  <p class="font-semibold text-green-800">The planet's own memory reveals the full story</p>
  <p class="text-green-900 mt-1">Instrumental records only extend back to around 1850. But Earth has been recording its own climate history for millions of years in ice, sediment, coral, and tree rings. Paleoclimate evidence provides the long-term context that shows just how unprecedented current warming and CO₂ levels are, and how tightly the two are linked.</p>
</HighlightBox>

<h3 class="text-xl font-bold mt-6 mb-3">What Is Paleoclimatology?</h3>
<p class="mb-3">Paleoclimatology is the study of past climates before the era of systematic measurements. Scientists reconstruct past temperatures, CO₂ concentrations, sea levels, and precipitation patterns from <strong>proxy records</strong>: natural archives that preserve information about past climate conditions. By analysing these records, researchers can extend our understanding of Earth's climate back hundreds of thousands of years.</p>
<p class="mb-4">The value of paleoclimate data is not just historical curiosity. It shows us how the climate system behaves across a wide range of conditions, how sensitive it is to changes in greenhouse gases, and whether current changes are within natural variability or genuinely unprecedented.</p>

<h3 class="text-xl font-bold mt-6 mb-3">Ice Cores: A 800,000-Year Climate Archive</h3>
<p class="mb-3">The most powerful paleoclimate tool is the <strong>ice core</strong>. In Antarctica and Greenland, snow accumulates year after year and compresses into ice, trapping air bubbles that contain samples of the ancient atmosphere. Scientists drill into these ice sheets and extract cores up to 3.2 kilometres long, providing a continuous climate record stretching back 800,000 years.</p>
<p class="mb-4">From a single ice core, scientists can reconstruct:</p>
<ul class="list-disc ml-6 space-y-2 mb-4">
  <li><strong>Past atmospheric CO₂ and CH₄ concentrations</strong>, measured directly from trapped air bubbles</li>
  <li><strong>Past temperatures</strong>, derived from the ratio of heavy to light oxygen isotopes (δ¹⁸O) in the ice itself</li>
  <li><strong>Volcanic eruptions</strong>, preserved as sulfate layers</li>
  <li><strong>Dust storms and biomass burning</strong>, preserved as particulate layers</li>
</ul>

<AnalogyBox>
  <p class="font-semibold text-blue-800">Analogy: Earth's Own Time Capsules</p>
  <p class="mt-2 text-blue-900">Each layer of Antarctic ice is like a time capsule sealed in a particular year. The older and deeper the ice, the further back in time it takes you. The air bubbles trapped within are literally samples of the ancient atmosphere: small bottles of air from tens of thousands of years ago that scientists can analyze directly. No other archive provides such direct access to past atmospheric composition.</p>
</AnalogyBox>

<h3 class="text-xl font-bold mt-6 mb-3">The Ice Age Cycles: A Repeating Pattern</h3>
<p class="mb-3">Ice core data reveals one of the most striking patterns in Earth's climate history: <strong>glacial-interglacial cycles</strong>. Over the past 800,000 years, Earth has cycled through approximately eight full ice ages (glacials) and warm periods (interglacials), driven primarily by periodic variations in Earth's orbit around the sun, known as the <strong>Milankovitch cycles</strong>.</p>
<p class="mb-4">During glacial periods, global temperatures were roughly 4–7°C cooler than today, ice sheets covered large parts of North America and Europe, and sea levels were up to 120 metres lower. During interglacials (like the current Holocene epoch), ice retreated and climates were similar to or slightly cooler than pre-industrial conditions.</p>

<ExampleBox>
  <p class="font-semibold text-amber-800">CO₂ and Temperature Moved Together for 800,000 Years</p>
  <p class="mt-2">The ice core record shows a remarkably consistent pattern: during glacial periods, atmospheric CO₂ dropped to around 180 ppm; during warm interglacials, it rose to around 280 ppm. This 100 ppm natural range sustained temperature swings of 4–7°C, demonstrating the powerful relationship between CO₂ and global temperature. Today's CO₂ of 422 ppm is not just above the interglacial maximum. It is <strong>50% above</strong> the highest natural level in 800,000 years. The IPCC AR6 states with high confidence that CO₂ concentrations in 2019 were higher than at any time in at least 2 million years.</p>
</ExampleBox>

<h3 class="text-xl font-bold mt-6 mb-3">Other Paleoclimate Archives</h3>
<p class="mb-3">Beyond ice cores, scientists use a variety of other proxy records to reconstruct past climates:</p>

<ResponsiveTable>
<table class="mb-6">
  <tr><th>Proxy</th><th>What It Records</th><th>Time Range</th></tr>
  <tr><td>Tree rings (dendrochronology)</td><td>Annual temperature and precipitation</td><td>Up to up to 10,000 years</td></tr>
  <tr><td>Ocean sediment cores</td><td>Ocean temperatures, salinity, ice volume</td><td>Millions of years</td></tr>
  <tr><td>Coral skeletons</td><td>Ocean temperature, salinity, storm intensity</td><td>Up to up to 500 years per coral</td></tr>
  <tr><td>Cave formations (speleothems)</td><td>Rainfall, temperature, cave environment</td><td>Up to up to 500,000 years</td></tr>
  <tr><td>Pollen records</td><td>Vegetation types, inferred climate zones</td><td>Tens of thousands of years</td></tr>
  <tr><td>Lake and ocean varves</td><td>Annual layers recording seasonal deposition</td><td>Thousands to millions of years</td></tr>
</table>
</ResponsiveTable>

<p class="mb-4">Each proxy has its own strengths and limitations. Scientists combine multiple proxies using a technique called <strong>multi-proxy reconstruction</strong> to cross-check results and identify the most robust climate signals. The "hockey stick" temperature reconstructions published since the late 1990s use exactly this approach, combining dozens of independent proxies to reconstruct global temperature over the past 2,000 years.</p>

<h3 class="text-xl font-bold mt-6 mb-3">The Last 2,000 Years: Context for Modern Warming</h3>
<p class="mb-3">High-resolution proxy records covering the last 2,000 years show that while natural climate variability did produce warmer and cooler periods (such as the Medieval Warm Period and the Little Ice Age), the rate and magnitude of 20th and 21st century warming is unprecedented. The IPCC AR6 finds with high confidence that <strong>global surface temperature has increased faster since 1970 than in any other 50-year period over at least the last 2,000 years</strong>.</p>
<p class="mb-4">Furthermore, temperatures during the most recent decade exceed those of the most recent multi-century warm period, which occurred around 6,500 years ago. Current temperatures are the highest in at least the last 125,000 years, approximately the era of the Last Interglacial period.</p>

<h3 class="text-xl font-bold mt-6 mb-3">Sea Level and Ice Sheet Evidence</h3>
<p class="mb-3">Past sea level records (derived from coral reef positions, sediment deposits, and geological markers) confirm that ice sheets and sea levels respond substantially to temperature changes. During the Last Interglacial (around 125,000 years ago), when temperatures were 0.5–1.5°C above pre-industrial levels (comparable to today), global sea levels were likely 5–10 metres higher than today, suggesting significant Greenland and Antarctic ice loss.</p>
<p class="mb-4">This is a sobering benchmark. The temperatures we are currently experiencing already match those associated with dramatically different sea levels in Earth's past, levels that would eventually be reached if current ice sheet trajectories continued for centuries.</p>

<DeepDive title="How accurate are paleoclimate reconstructions?">
  <p class="mb-3">All proxy records have uncertainties. Tree rings can be affected by factors other than temperature (moisture, CO₂ fertilization). Ocean sediment records have age uncertainties and the signal is smoothed over time. Individual proxies can mislead. However, the strength of paleoclimate science lies in the convergence of multiple independent lines of evidence.</p>
  <p>When ice cores, ocean sediments, tree rings, and cave formations all independently point to the same temperature history, the probability that they are all wrong in the same direction becomes vanishingly small. Calibration against the instrumental record (where overlap exists) further validates the proxies.</p>
</DeepDive>
