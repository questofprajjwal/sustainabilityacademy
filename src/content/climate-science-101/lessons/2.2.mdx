<h2 class="text-2xl font-bold text-gray-800 mb-4">Methane, Nitrous Oxide, and F-Gases</h2>

<HighlightBox>
  <p class="font-semibold text-green-800">Key Idea</p>
  <p class="text-green-900 mt-1">While CO<sub>2</sub> is the dominant warming gas by volume, methane (CH<sub>4</sub>), nitrous oxide (N<sub>2</sub>O), and fluorinated gases (F-gases) are critically important because of their high warming potency per molecule and, in some cases, their rapid growth trajectories. Together, non-CO<sub>2</sub> greenhouse gases account for roughly 35-40% of total annual warming forcing from anthropogenic sources.</p>
</HighlightBox>

<h3 class="text-xl font-bold mt-6 mb-3">Methane (CH<sub>4</sub>): The Short-Lived but Potent Gas</h3>

<p class="mb-3">Methane is the second most important human-emitted greenhouse gas after CO<sub>2</sub>. Its Global Warming Potential over 100 years (GWP100) is approximately 27-30 times that of CO<sub>2</sub> in IPCC AR6 (updated from 28 in AR5). Over 20 years, the GWP20 is even higher, around 80 times, reflecting methane's powerful near-term warming punch.</p>

<p class="mb-4">The IPCC AR6 WGI reports that methane concentrations reached 1,866 parts per billion (ppb) in 2019, higher than at any time in at least 800,000 years. Since 1750, CH<sub>4</sub> has increased by 156%, far exceeding natural multi-millennial variability. Total anthropogenic CH<sub>4</sub> emissions were approximately 11 GtCO<sub>2</sub>-eq per year in 2019, roughly 18% of total GHG emissions.</p>

<AnalogyBox>
  <p class="font-semibold text-blue-800">Analogy: A Powerful but Temporary Heater</p>
  <p class="mt-2 text-blue-900">If CO<sub>2</sub> is like permanently insulating a house, methane is like turning on a powerful space heater. The heater warms the room very quickly and intensely, but if you turn it off, the effect diminishes within years rather than centuries. This is why cutting methane emissions can reduce warming more rapidly than cutting CO<sub>2</sub>, making methane reduction a high-priority near-term lever in climate policy.</p>
</AnalogyBox>

<h3 class="text-xl font-bold mt-6 mb-3">Sources of Methane</h3>

<p class="mb-3">Methane has both natural and anthropogenic sources. Natural sources include wetlands (the single largest natural source), termites, and geological seeps. Human-caused sources are now substantially larger than natural flows and include:</p>

<ul class="list-disc ml-6 space-y-2 mb-4">
  <li><strong>Fossil fuel production and use:</strong> Extraction, processing, and distribution of coal, oil, and natural gas leak methane at every stage. This is sometimes called fugitive emissions.</li>
  <li><strong>Agriculture - enteric fermentation:</strong> Cattle, sheep, and other ruminant livestock produce methane in their digestive systems. This is one of the largest agricultural emission sources globally.</li>
  <li><strong>Agriculture - rice cultivation:</strong> Flooded rice paddies create anaerobic (oxygen-free) conditions in which microbes decompose organic matter and produce methane, which bubbles to the surface.</li>
  <li><strong>Waste - landfills and wastewater:</strong> Decomposition of organic waste in landfills and sewage systems generates substantial methane.</li>
</ul>

<h3 class="text-xl font-bold mt-6 mb-3">Nitrous Oxide (N<sub>2</sub>O): The Forgotten Gas</h3>

<p class="mb-3">Nitrous oxide is the third most important greenhouse gas in terms of total radiative forcing. Its GWP100 is 273 in AR6 (commonly cited as approximately 265 from AR5 values used in many inventories), meaning each molecule traps hundreds of times more heat than CO<sub>2</sub> over a century. It also persists in the atmosphere for over a century on average.</p>

<p class="mb-4">N<sub>2</sub>O concentrations reached 332 ppb in 2019, a 23% increase since 1750. Total anthropogenic N<sub>2</sub>O emissions were approximately 2.7 GtCO<sub>2</sub>-eq per year in 2019. Agriculture is by far the dominant source, primarily through:</p>

<ul class="list-disc ml-6 space-y-2 mb-4">
  <li><strong>Synthetic nitrogen fertilizers:</strong> When nitrogen fertilizers are applied to soils, soil microbes convert a fraction of the nitrogen into N<sub>2</sub>O through processes called nitrification and denitrification. This is the single largest human source of N<sub>2</sub>O.</li>
  <li><strong>Animal manure:</strong> Decomposition of manure on fields and in storage also generates N<sub>2</sub>O.</li>
  <li><strong>Industrial processes:</strong> Adipic acid and nitric acid production, used in the manufacture of nylon and fertilizers, generate N<sub>2</sub>O as a by-product.</li>
</ul>

<ExampleBox>
  <p class="font-semibold text-amber-800">The Fertilizer Dilemma</p>
  <p class="mt-2 text-amber-900">Global food security depends on synthetic nitrogen fertilizers. The Haber-Bosch process, which fixes atmospheric nitrogen into ammonia, enabled the Green Revolution and today supports the food supply for roughly half of humanity. But nitrogen fertilizers are the primary driver of rising N<sub>2</sub>O emissions. Reducing N<sub>2</sub>O without harming food production requires precision agriculture techniques: applying the right amount of nitrogen at the right time and in the right place to minimise excess nitrogen in soil.</p>
</ExampleBox>

<h3 class="text-xl font-bold mt-6 mb-3">Fluorinated Gases (F-Gases): Small Volume, Large Impact</h3>

<p class="mb-3">F-gases are a family of synthetic compounds that include hydrofluorocarbons (HFCs), perfluorocarbons (PFCs), sulphur hexafluoride (SF<sub>6</sub>), and nitrogen trifluoride (NF<sub>3</sub>). These gases do not occur naturally; they were created by the chemical industry for specific applications.</p>

<p class="mb-4">F-gases are characterised by extremely high GWPs (often thousands to tens of thousands times CO<sub>2</sub>) and very long atmospheric lifetimes (some persist for thousands of years). Their contribution to total emissions in CO<sub>2</sub>-equivalent terms was approximately 1.4 GtCO<sub>2</sub>-eq in 2019, but the IPCC AR6 WGIII notes that F-gases showed the highest relative growth of any GHG group since 1990 (354% increase), albeit from a very low baseline.</p>

<ul class="list-disc ml-6 space-y-2 mb-4">
  <li><strong>HFCs:</strong> Used in refrigerators, air conditioners, and aerosol sprays. Introduced as replacements for ozone-depleting CFCs under the Montreal Protocol, they are potent greenhouse gases.</li>
  <li><strong>PFCs and SF<sub>6</sub>:</strong> Used in semiconductor manufacturing, electrical switchgear, and the aluminium industry. Some have GWPs exceeding 20,000.</li>
</ul>

<DeepDive title="The Montreal Protocol: An Unexpected Climate Connection">
  <p class="mb-3">The Montreal Protocol (1987) was designed to protect the ozone layer by phasing out chlorofluorocarbons (CFCs). It succeeded spectacularly and is considered the most effective international environmental agreement in history. As a side effect, it also prevented enormous quantities of warming, since CFCs are also potent greenhouse gases.</p>
  <p>However, the replacement chemicals introduced, primarily HFCs, are not ozone-depleting but are strong greenhouse gases. The Kigali Amendment (2016) to the Montreal Protocol specifically targets HFC phase-down. Implementing the Kigali Amendment is projected to avoid up to 0.5°C of warming by 2100.</p>
</DeepDive>

<h3 class="text-xl font-bold mt-6 mb-3">Why Non-CO<sub>2</sub> Gases Matter for Policy</h3>

<p class="mb-3">Understanding non-CO<sub>2</sub> greenhouse gases is crucial for two reasons. First, they represent a significant and growing share of total warming. Second, many can be reduced more quickly and cost-effectively than CO<sub>2</sub>. Methane reductions in particular provide near-term climate benefits because the gas is removed from the atmosphere within years rather than centuries. Aggressive methane action between now and 2030 can meaningfully slow the rate of warming in the critical near-term window.</p>

<p class="mb-4">Each gas has distinct sources, policy levers, and co-benefits. A comprehensive climate strategy cannot focus solely on CO<sub>2</sub> and must address the full portfolio of greenhouse gases.</p>
