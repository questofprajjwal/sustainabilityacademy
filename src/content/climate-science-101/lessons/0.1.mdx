<h2 class="text-2xl font-bold text-gray-800 mb-4">Earth's Energy Balance</h2>

<HighlightBox>
  <p class="font-semibold text-green-800">Why this matters</p>
  <p class="text-green-900 mt-1">Every discussion of climate change ultimately comes back to one question: how much energy is Earth gaining or losing? Earth's energy balance is the master control dial of global temperature. Understand it and you understand why the climate is warming.</p>
</HighlightBox>

<h3 class="text-xl font-bold mt-6 mb-3">The Sun as Earth's Power Source</h3>
<p class="mb-3">The sun is Earth's only significant source of incoming energy. It delivers radiation continuously at an average of roughly <strong>1,361 watts per square meter</strong> at the top of the atmosphere (a value known as the solar constant). Because Earth is a sphere and rotates, this energy is spread across the whole planet, averaging to about <strong>340 watts per square meter</strong> over the entire surface.</p>
<p class="mb-4">Not all of this energy reaches the surface. About 30% is immediately reflected back to space by clouds, ice, snow, and other bright surfaces before it can warm anything. This reflectivity is called <strong>albedo</strong>. The remaining 70% is absorbed by the atmosphere, oceans, and land.</p>

<AnalogyBox>
  <p class="font-semibold text-blue-800">Analogy: Earth as a Thermostat</p>
  <p class="mt-2 text-blue-900">Imagine a room with a heater on one wall and an open window on the opposite wall. The heater pumps in energy constantly. If the window lets out exactly as much heat as the heater produces, the room stays at a steady temperature. If you close the window (adding greenhouse gases), heat builds up and the room warms until it reaches a new, higher equilibrium where enough heat escapes again. Earth works exactly this way.</p>
</AnalogyBox>

<h3 class="text-xl font-bold mt-6 mb-3">Outgoing Radiation and Equilibrium</h3>
<p class="mb-3">To balance the incoming solar energy, Earth radiates energy back to space as <strong>infrared (heat) radiation</strong>. The amount of radiation an object emits increases with its temperature, as described by the Stefan-Boltzmann law. So when Earth warms, it radiates more energy outward until a new balance is struck.</p>
<p class="mb-4">In a stable climate, incoming energy equals outgoing energy. This is called <strong>radiative equilibrium</strong>. The current average surface temperature of approximately 15°C (59°F) is the temperature at which Earth historically achieved this balance. Without the natural greenhouse effect, Earth's equilibrium temperature would be around -18°C (0°F), far too cold for most life.</p>

<h3 class="text-xl font-bold mt-6 mb-3">Radiative Forcing: What Disturbs the Balance</h3>
<p class="mb-3">When something changes how much energy enters or leaves Earth's system, scientists measure the imbalance in terms of <strong>radiative forcing</strong>, expressed in watts per square meter (W/m²). A positive forcing (such as adding greenhouse gases) traps more heat and pushes temperatures higher. A negative forcing (such as a large volcanic eruption injecting reflective particles) cools the planet.</p>
<p class="mb-4">The IPCC AR6 report finds that the total human-caused radiative forcing from 1750 to 2019 is approximately <strong>+2.72 W/m²</strong>. This means human activities are currently trapping an additional 2.72 watts of energy for every square meter of Earth's surface: a persistent, ongoing energy imbalance that drives warming.</p>

<ExampleBox>
  <p class="font-semibold text-amber-800">Worked Example: The Energy Imbalance in Context</p>
  <p class="mt-2">Earth's surface area is approximately 510 trillion square meters. Multiply that by the current energy imbalance of roughly 0.9 W/m² (the net imbalance after accounting for the ocean's heat absorption) and you get about <strong>460 terawatts</strong> of excess energy accumulating continuously. That is equivalent to detonating about 25 Hiroshima-scale atomic bombs every second, and the overwhelming majority of that heat is going directly into the oceans.</p>
</ExampleBox>

<h3 class="text-xl font-bold mt-6 mb-3">Where Does the Extra Heat Go?</h3>
<p class="mb-3">When the energy budget is out of balance, energy must go somewhere. According to NOAA data, the ocean absorbs approximately <strong>91% of the excess heat</strong> trapped by greenhouse gases. The breakdown of where warming energy accumulates:</p>

<p class="mb-4">This distribution explains a critical fact: the surface air temperature rise we experience day-to-day represents only a small fraction of the total planetary warming. The ocean acts as a vast heat buffer, slowing surface warming but also locking in future warming that will be released for centuries even if emissions stop today.</p>

<h3 class="text-xl font-bold mt-6 mb-3">Albedo: The Reflectivity Feedback</h3>
<p class="mb-3">Earth's albedo (average about 0.30, meaning 30% of sunlight is reflected) is not fixed. Snow and ice reflect 80–90% of incoming sunlight. Dark ocean water reflects only about 6%. As warming melts ice, dark ocean is exposed, absorbing more sunlight and driving further warming. This <strong>ice-albedo feedback</strong> is one of the reasons the Arctic is warming more than twice as fast as the global average.</p>

<DeepDive title="How do scientists measure Earth's energy imbalance?">
  <p class="mb-3">Measuring a planetary energy imbalance of less than 1 W/m² requires extraordinary precision. Scientists use two complementary approaches:</p>
  <ul class="list-disc ml-6 space-y-2 mb-3">
    <li><strong>Satellite radiometry:</strong> NASA's CERES (Clouds and the Earth's Radiant Energy System) instruments measure both incoming solar radiation and outgoing infrared radiation from space, allowing direct calculation of the net energy flux.</li>
    <li><strong>Ocean heat content tracking:</strong> A global network of over 3,900 Argo floats drifts through the ocean measuring temperature and salinity at depths up to 2,000 meters. Rising ocean heat content is the most direct measure of the planetary energy imbalance.</li>
  </ul>
  <p>Both approaches independently confirm the positive energy imbalance and give scientists confidence that Earth is indeed accumulating heat.</p>
</DeepDive>
