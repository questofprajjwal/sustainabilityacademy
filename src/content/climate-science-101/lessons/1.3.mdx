<h2 class="text-2xl font-bold text-gray-800 mb-4">Observed Changes: Ice, Oceans, and Extremes</h2>

<HighlightBox>
  <p class="font-semibold text-green-800">The warming is not just in thermometers. It is visible across the entire planet.</p>
  <p class="text-green-900 mt-1">Temperature records are the most direct measure of warming, but dozens of other indicators confirm the same story: shrinking ice sheets, retreating glaciers, rising seas, warming oceans, and more frequent extreme events. Each line of evidence is consistent with what physics predicts from a warming climate, and collectively they form an overwhelming case.</p>
</HighlightBox>

<h3 class="text-xl font-bold mt-6 mb-3">Arctic Sea Ice: The Canary in the Coal Mine</h3>
<p class="mb-3">Arctic sea ice is one of the most visually dramatic indicators of climate change. Since satellite measurements began in 1979, the extent of Arctic sea ice in summer has declined by approximately <strong>12.1% per decade</strong>, a loss of roughly 78,000 square kilometres per year, comparable to the area of South Carolina. In 2011–2020, annual average Arctic sea ice area reached its lowest level since at least 1850.</p>
<p class="mb-4">Perhaps more striking than extent is the change in ice age and thickness. In the mid-1980s, roughly one-third of winter Arctic sea ice was "multiyear ice" (ice that had survived multiple summers) and accumulated to several metres thick. By 2020, this proportion had collapsed to just 5%. The Arctic is now dominated by thin, first-year ice that melts easily each summer.</p>

<AnalogyBox>
  <p class="font-semibold text-blue-800">Analogy: Arctic Amplification as a Double Heater</p>
  <p class="mt-2 text-blue-900">Sea ice reflects about 80% of incoming sunlight back to space. When it melts, it exposes dark ocean water that absorbs about 94% of incoming sunlight: a dramatic flip from highly reflective to highly absorbing. This is why the Arctic is warming more than twice as fast as the global average: each degree of warming melts more ice, which exposes more dark ocean, which absorbs more heat, which causes more warming. This ice-albedo feedback loop acts like a second heater switched on by the first warming pulse.</p>
</AnalogyBox>

<h3 class="text-xl font-bold mt-6 mb-3">Ice Sheets and Glaciers: Mass Loss Accelerating</h3>
<p class="mb-3">The Greenland and Antarctic ice sheets together hold enough ice to raise global sea levels by approximately 65 metres if fully melted. Both are losing mass at accelerating rates:</p>
<ul class="list-disc ml-6 space-y-2 mb-4">
  <li><strong>Greenland:</strong> Lost an average of 279 billion tonnes of ice per year between 1993 and 2019 (NASA). Ice loss rates increased sevenfold between 1992–2001 and 2012–2016 (NOAA).</li>
  <li><strong>Antarctica:</strong> Lost an average of 148 billion tonnes of ice per year between 1993 and 2019 (NASA). Ice loss nearly quadrupled between 1992–2001 and 2012–2016.</li>
  <li><strong>Mountain glaciers worldwide:</strong> The global retreat of glaciers since the 1950s, with almost all of the world's glaciers retreating synchronously, is unprecedented in at least the last 2,000 years (IPCC AR6, medium confidence).</li>
</ul>
<p class="mb-4">The IPCC AR6 concludes with very high confidence that human influence is the main driver of the global retreat of glaciers since the 1990s.</p>

<h3 class="text-xl font-bold mt-6 mb-3">Sea Level Rise: Accelerating Trend</h3>
<p class="mb-3">Global mean sea level has risen by approximately <strong>20 cm (8 inches) between 1901 and 2018</strong>, with an accelerating rate:</p>

<ResponsiveTable>
<table class="mb-6">
  <tr><th>Period</th><th>Rate of Sea Level Rise</th><th>Key Driver</th></tr>
  <tr><td>1901–1971</td><td>1.3 mm/yr</td><td>Thermal expansion dominant</td></tr>
  <tr><td>1971–2006</td><td>1.9 mm/yr</td><td>Thermal expansion + glacier melt</td></tr>
  <tr><td>2006–2018</td><td>3.7 mm/yr</td><td>Ice sheet losses increasing</td></tr>
  <tr><td>2023 (record high)</td><td>101.4 mm above 1993 level</td><td>Accelerating ice + thermal expansion</td></tr>
</table>
</ResponsiveTable>

<p class="mb-4">Sea level rise has two main causes: <strong>thermal expansion</strong> (warm water expands) and <strong>meltwater addition</strong> from glaciers and ice sheets. From 1970 through recent decades, these two contributions have been roughly equal. However, as ice sheet losses accelerate, the meltwater contribution is growing rapidly. Future sea level rise will depend critically on the fate of the Greenland and West Antarctic ice sheets.</p>

<h3 class="text-xl font-bold mt-6 mb-3">Ocean Warming and Acidification</h3>
<p class="mb-3">The upper 100 metres of the ocean has warmed by approximately 0.33°C since 1969 (NASA). More broadly, the ocean has absorbed over 90% of the excess heat from the enhanced greenhouse effect since the 1970s (NOAA). Ocean heat content has increased consistently at all depths that can be measured.</p>
<p class="mb-4">A separate but equally serious consequence of rising CO₂ is <strong>ocean acidification</strong>. When CO₂ dissolves in seawater, it forms carbonic acid, lowering the pH. Since industrialization began, surface ocean pH has declined from approximately 8.21 to 8.10, a change that represents a <strong>30% increase in acidity</strong> on the logarithmic pH scale. The IPCC AR6 states with virtually certain confidence that human-caused CO₂ emissions are the main driver of current ocean acidification. This threatens shellfish, corals, and any organism that builds calcium carbonate structures.</p>

<ExampleBox>
  <p class="font-semibold text-amber-800">Ocean Acidification in Numbers</p>
  <p class="mt-2">pH is a logarithmic scale, so a change of 0.1 pH units represents about a 26% increase in hydrogen ion concentration (acidity). The ocean's pH has dropped from 8.21 to 8.10 since pre-industrial times, a 0.11 unit decline. That corresponds to approximately <strong>30% more acidic</strong> than before industrialization. Projections show pH could drop to 7.7–7.8 under high-emission scenarios by 2100, conditions not seen in the surface ocean for at least 2 million years.</p>
</ExampleBox>

<h3 class="text-xl font-bold mt-6 mb-3">Extreme Weather Events</h3>
<p class="mb-3">The IPCC AR6 finds substantial evidence that climate change has already altered the frequency and intensity of extreme weather events:</p>
<ul class="list-disc ml-6 space-y-2 mb-4">
  <li><strong>Heat extremes:</strong> It is virtually certain that hot extremes have become more frequent and more intense across most land regions since the 1950s, while cold extremes have become less frequent. Human-induced climate change is identified as the main driver with high confidence.</li>
  <li><strong>Heavy precipitation:</strong> The frequency and intensity of heavy precipitation events have increased since the 1950s over most land areas where data is available (high confidence).</li>
  <li><strong>Marine heatwaves:</strong> Marine heatwaves have approximately doubled in frequency since the 1980s, contributing to coral bleaching events of unprecedented scale.</li>
  <li><strong>Tropical cyclones:</strong> The global proportion of major Category 3–5 tropical cyclones has likely increased over the last four decades. Rainfall associated with tropical cyclones has increased (high confidence).</li>
  <li><strong>Compound events:</strong> The frequency of concurrent heatwaves and droughts has increased on the global scale, creating greater stress on ecosystems and food systems than either hazard alone.</li>
</ul>

<h3 class="text-xl font-bold mt-6 mb-3">The Fingerprint Pattern: More Than Just "Warming"</h3>
<p class="mb-3">A critical point often missed in popular discussions is that the pattern of observed changes is not random warming. It matches the specific <strong>fingerprint</strong> predicted by greenhouse gas theory. The stratosphere (upper atmosphere) is cooling while the troposphere (lower atmosphere) warms. Arctic warming exceeds tropical warming. Nights warm faster than days. These patterns are precisely what greenhouse gas forcing predicts and are inconsistent with natural drivers such as changes in solar output.</p>
<p class="mb-4">This fingerprint approach, which compares observed patterns to predicted patterns from different potential causes, is central to the attribution science covered in Lesson 1.4.</p>

<DeepDive title="What about natural variability explaining some observed changes?">
  <p class="mb-3">Natural variability is real and does cause year-to-year and decade-to-decade fluctuations in all these indicators. El Niño years produce higher-than-average sea level and temperature. Volcanic eruptions cause temporary ice mass gains. Internal ocean variability affects precipitation patterns.</p>
  <p>However, the long-term trends across all of these indicators (ice extent, glacier mass, sea level, ocean heat content, and extreme event frequency) are in directions and at magnitudes that cannot be explained by natural variability alone. Statistical analysis consistently finds the observed trends far exceed what would be expected from internal variability over these timescales.</p>
</DeepDive>
