## What Is Step C?

Step C is the assessment step. The undertaking takes the long list of IROs identified in Step B and applies materiality criteria to determine which of them are actually material and must be reported.

EFRAG IG1 paragraph 82 describes it: "In this step, the undertaking applies criteria for assessing impact and financial materiality in order to determine the material actual and potential impacts and the material risks and opportunities. This then forms the basis for determining material information based on the ESRS topical disclosure requirements."

Step C has three sub-steps:

1. Impact materiality assessment (section 3.3.1 of EFRAG IG1)
2. Financial materiality assessment (section 3.3.2 of EFRAG IG1)
3. Consolidating the outcomes of the two assessments, including their interaction (section 3.3.3 of EFRAG IG1)

## Impact Materiality Assessment

### The Core Criteria

ESRS 1 paragraph 42 requires the undertaking to apply the criteria set under section 3.4 using appropriate quantitative and/or qualitative thresholds. The criteria differ depending on whether the impact is negative or positive, and whether it is actual or potential.

<HighlightBox>
Key criteria for impact materiality:

**Actual negative impacts:** Materiality is based on the **severity** of the impact alone. Severity is determined by three factors: scale (how grave the impact is), scope (how widespread it is, meaning the number of people affected or the extent of environmental damage), and irremediable character (the extent to which the impact can be remedied).

**Potential negative impacts:** Materiality is based on both **severity** and **likelihood**. In the case of a potential negative human rights impact, the severity of the impact takes precedence over its likelihood (ESRS 1 paragraph 45).

**Actual positive impacts:** Materiality is based on scale and scope.

**Potential positive impacts:** Materiality is based on scale, scope, and likelihood (ESRS 1 paragraph 46).
</HighlightBox>

### Applying the Criteria

EFRAG IG1 paragraph 85 explains that the undertaking applies the severity criteria to the list of impacts defined in Step B. For actual negative impacts, the criteria are scale, scope, and irremediable character. For potential negative impacts, the undertaking estimates the likelihood of the impact occurring and maps it onto the relevant time horizon.

EFRAG IG1 paragraph 115 provides detail on what severity means in practice:

- **Scale**: how grave the impact is, for example, the extent of infringement of access to basic life necessities or freedoms.
- **Scope**: how widespread the impact is, meaning the number of individuals affected or the extent of the environmental damage.
- **Irremediable character**: the extent to which the impact can be remediated, for example through compensation or restitution, and whether the people affected can be restored to a situation at least equivalent to their situation before the negative impact.

EFRAG IG1 paragraph 118 notes that any of the three characteristics of severity can make an impact severe, but the characteristics are often interdependent. Irremediable character could increase the perceived scale. In turn, the greater the scale or scope, the harder it often is to remediate.

### Thresholds

ESRS 1 paragraph 42 states that appropriate thresholds are necessary to determine which impacts are identified and addressed by the undertaking as material. EFRAG IG1 paragraph 84 confirms that the undertaking applies objective criteria using appropriate quantitative and/or qualitative thresholds. ESRS 1 does not prescribe how to set thresholds, so the undertaking exercises judgement based on its specific facts and circumstances (EFRAG IG1 paragraph 27).

EFRAG IG1 paragraph 117 notes that when setting thresholds, priority should be given to any supportable evidence that provides as much objectivity as possible to the materiality conclusion. However, reasonable quantification of potential impacts may not always be possible.

## Financial Materiality Assessment

EFRAG IG1 section 3.3.2 explains that material risks and opportunities for the undertaking generally derive from impacts, dependencies, or other factors such as exposure to climate hazards or changes in regulation that address systemic risks (paragraph 89).

To assess their materiality, appropriate quantitative and/or qualitative thresholds are used, based on financial effects in terms of performance, financial position, cash flows, and access to and cost of capital.

EFRAG IG1 paragraph 90 states that sustainability risks and opportunities are assessed based on:

1. Their **likelihood of occurrence**
2. The **potential magnitude of their financial effects** in the short-, medium-, and long-term

The undertaking applies a set of objective thresholds for likelihood and magnitude, and considers the nature of the effects of the identified risks and opportunities.

EFRAG IG1 paragraph 134 adds that the undertaking should also consider that a sustainability matter may be financially material even if its financial effects are not reliably measurable at the reporting date. In this case, the thresholds may rely on qualitative factors and ranges of possible effects such as high, medium, or low.

## Consolidating the Two Outcomes

Once the impact materiality assessment and financial materiality assessment are complete, the undertaking consolidates the results to arrive at the full list of material IROs (EFRAG IG1 paragraph 97).

A matter is material if it meets the criteria for impact materiality, or financial materiality, or both. The two assessments are inter-related. As ESRS 1 paragraph 38 explains, impact materiality and financial materiality assessments are inter-related, and the interdependencies between these two dimensions shall be considered.

EFRAG IG1 paragraph 98 notes that once the undertaking has assessed individual IROs, it may aggregate the resulting IROs for reporting purposes, following ESRS 1 paragraph 56. Those in charge of this activity may also validate the aggregated double materiality results with management to assess and validate the completeness of the list of material IROs.

## Material IROs Must Be Reported Even Without Actions in Place

An important principle is that the outcome of the materiality assessment should not exclude any material IROs, even if the undertaking has not yet taken any actions to address them.

EFRAG IG1 paragraph 26 states: "If the undertaking identifies a large number of IROs, it may prioritise them for management purposes. However, for reporting purposes this assessment should not exclude any material IROs, particularly when the undertaking has not addressed or fully addressed these material IROs through its policies, targets and action plans. This is useful information and shall therefore be included irrespective of whether actions have been taken, or are planned to be taken, to address them."

The ESRS 1 paragraph 33 also confirms that if the undertaking has not adopted policies, implemented actions, or set targets to manage a given material matter, it must state this, but no additional information is required.

## Documenting and Evidencing the Assessment

ESRS 1 paragraph 36 requires the undertaking to establish how it applies criteria, including appropriate thresholds, to determine the information it discloses on metrics for material sustainability matters and as entity-specific disclosures.

EFRAG IG1 paragraph 83 notes that emphasis is placed on setting appropriate qualitative or quantitative thresholds to assess the materiality of IROs and related disclosures. ESRS 2 paragraphs 53 and 59 require disclosing how these thresholds have been set or applied.

The assessment process should be based on supportable evidence and rely, to the maximum extent possible, on objective information, while implementing the impact materiality and financial materiality criteria specified in the ESRS (EFRAG IG1 paragraph 28).

<DeepDive title="How does this connect to what gets disclosed?">
Once a matter has been assessed as material, the undertaking identifies the information to be reported based on the Disclosure Requirements in the relevant topical ESRS (EFRAG IG1 paragraph 47).

The concept of materiality of information then applies at the Disclosure Requirement level or datapoint level: the undertaking shall include information assessed to be material (ESRS 1 paragraph 34(a)) and may omit information assessed to be not material if it is not needed to meet the objective of the Disclosure Requirement (ESRS 1 paragraph 34(b)).

ESRS 2 IRO-2 requires an explanation of the determination of the information to be disclosed in relation to material IROs, including the use of thresholds and/or how the undertaking has implemented the criteria in ESRS 1 chapter 3.2 (EFRAG IG1 paragraph 53).
