## What Is Financial Materiality in Sustainability Reporting?

Financial materiality for sustainability reporting has a broader scope than the materiality concept used when deciding what goes into a company's financial statements. ESRS 1 paragraph 47 states clearly that "the scope of financial materiality for sustainability reporting is an expansion of the scope of materiality used in the process of determining which information should be included in the undertaking's financial statements."

This matters because a sustainability topic can be financially material even if it does not yet show up as a line item in the accounts.

<HighlightBox>
A sustainability matter is material from a financial perspective if it triggers, or could reasonably be expected to trigger, material financial effects on the undertaking. This is the case when a sustainability matter generates risks or opportunities that have a material influence, or could reasonably be expected to have a material influence, on the undertaking's development, financial position, financial performance, cash flows, its access to finance or cost of capital over the short-, medium- or long-term. (ESRS 1 paragraph 49)
</HighlightBox>

## How a Sustainability Matter Triggers Financial Effects

The connection between a sustainability topic and a company's finances runs through risks and opportunities. A sustainability matter becomes financially material when it generates risks or opportunities that affect the company's financial situation. Risks and opportunities may derive from past events or future events.

Importantly, financial materiality is not limited to matters that are within the control of the company itself. It also includes information on material risks and opportunities attributable to the company's business relationships, even beyond the scope of consolidation used in the preparation of financial statements (ESRS 1 paragraph 49).

## Examples of Financial Effects

The financial effects that can make a sustainability matter material include effects on:

- **Development**: how the company's business can grow or contract over time
- **Financial position**: the value of assets and liabilities on the balance sheet
- **Financial performance**: revenues, costs, and profitability
- **Cash flows**: the timing and size of actual cash receipts and payments
- **Access to finance**: the ability to raise debt or equity from capital markets
- **Cost of capital**: the rate of return that lenders and investors require

These effects are assessed across three time horizons: short-term, medium-term, and long-term (ESRS 1 paragraph 49).

## Who Are the Primary Users This Is Designed For?

The financial materiality assessment corresponds to identifying information that is considered material for primary users of general-purpose financial reports. These are investors, lenders, and others who make decisions about providing resources to the company.

Information is considered material for these users if omitting, misstating, or obscuring it could reasonably be expected to influence the decisions they make on the basis of the company's sustainability statement (ESRS 1 paragraph 48).

EFRAG IG1 paragraph 34 summarises this directly: "The financial materiality assessment corresponds to the identification of information that is considered material for primary users of general-purpose financial reports in making decisions relating to providing resources to the entity."

<AnalogyBox>
Think of a food company growing crops in a region where rainfall is unreliable. The company does not control the weather, but its financial results are highly sensitive to it. A bad season raises input costs, reduces yields, and squeezes margins. An investor needs to know about that sensitivity even if no drought has actually occurred yet. Financial materiality in sustainability reporting works the same way: it asks how sensitive the company is to external sustainability conditions, not just what has already happened on the balance sheet.
</AnalogyBox>

## The Role of Dependencies on Natural, Human, and Social Resources

Dependencies are a specific and important source of financial risk. ESRS 1 paragraph 50 explains that dependencies on natural, human, and social resources can be sources of financial risks or opportunities in two ways:

- They may influence the company's ability to continue to use or obtain the resources needed in its business processes, as well as the quality and pricing of those resources.
- They may affect the company's ability to rely on the relationships needed in its business processes on acceptable terms.

The company must consider how it is affected by its dependencies on the availability of natural, human, and social resources at appropriate prices and quality, regardless of whether it has potential impacts on those resources (ESRS 1 paragraph 40).

This means even a company that causes no environmental damage can still face financially material sustainability risks if the natural resources it depends on are under pressure from other sources.

## Assessing the Materiality of Risks and Opportunities

The materiality of risks and opportunities is assessed based on a combination of two factors (ESRS 1 paragraph 51):

1. The **likelihood of occurrence** of the risk or opportunity
2. The **potential magnitude** of the financial effects

Both dimensions must be considered together. A risk with very large potential financial effects but very low probability may or may not be material. The undertaking applies appropriate quantitative and qualitative thresholds to make this judgement.

<DeepDive title="How does financial materiality relate to impact materiality?">
Financial materiality and impact materiality are the two dimensions of double materiality. They are inter-related. In general, the starting point is the assessment of impacts, because material impacts trigger, in most cases, material risks and opportunities.

However, there may also be material risks and opportunities that are not related to the undertaking's impacts. These can arise, for example, from dependencies on resources that the undertaking does not itself affect.

Impacts are captured by impact materiality regardless of whether they are financially material. A sustainability impact may be financially material from the start or may become financially material over time (ESRS 1 paragraph 38).

EFRAG IG1 paragraph 35 states: "Material risks and opportunities generally derive from impacts and dependencies."
</DeepDive>
