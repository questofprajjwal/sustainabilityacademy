## Where Do Material Risks and Opportunities Come From?

Material risks and opportunities for an undertaking generally derive from two sources: impacts and dependencies.

EFRAG IG1 paragraph 35 explains that "the undertaking's impacts on people or the environment, combined with changes to strategy, including in investments, as well as in management decisions made to address such impacts, may give rise to risks and opportunities. Material risks and opportunities generally derive from impacts and dependencies."

This means the financially material risks and opportunities an undertaking faces are rarely unconnected to its sustainability activities. They flow through either what the company does to the world (impacts) or what the world's condition does to the company (dependencies).

## Understanding Dependencies

Dependencies are the undertaking's reliance on the availability of natural, human, and social resources at appropriate prices and quality. ESRS 1 paragraph 40 states that the undertaking shall consider how it is affected by these dependencies, regardless of its potential impacts on those resources.

Dependencies can create financial risk or opportunity in two ways (ESRS 1 paragraph 50):

- They may influence the undertaking's ability to continue to use or obtain the resources needed in its business processes, and the quality and pricing of those resources.
- They may affect the undertaking's ability to rely on the relationships needed in its business processes on acceptable terms.

<ExampleBox>
EFRAG IG1 paragraph 38 provides three concrete examples of dependencies creating financial risk even where the undertaking itself has no material impact on the resource:

**Pollinators:** An undertaking active in organic agriculture depends on pollinators for its crop yields. The number of pollinators in the local area is decreasing because of pesticide use by other agricultural businesses. The undertaking's own practices may be sound, but the decline in pollinators still creates a financial risk to its harvests and revenues.

**Coastal flooding:** An undertaking has a factory that runs on renewable energy and produces low greenhouse gas emissions. The factory is located in a coastal erosion area and is exposed to climate-related physical risks such as flooding or extreme weather. The undertaking has not caused the climate hazard, but the dependency on that location creates financial exposure.

**Human capital turnover:** An undertaking that provides legal services may experience an elevated level of staff turnover because a local competitor is offering higher salaries, even if both undertakings have adequate working conditions. The undertaking is dependent on human capital and may experience lower income until new staff are recruited.
</ExampleBox>

## Risks and Opportunities That Do Not Derive from Impacts

EFRAG IG1 paragraph 38 makes clear that material risks and opportunities also arise in the absence of material impacts connected to the undertaking. These arise, for example, from dependencies on natural and human resources. Risks can also arise from sustainability-related regulatory developments that address systemic risks. For example, new pollution abatement legislation may require significant capital expenditure and expose the undertaking to sanctions (EFRAG IG1 paragraph 39).

## How Magnitude and Likelihood Are Used to Assess Materiality

The materiality of risks and opportunities is assessed based on a combination of two factors (ESRS 1 paragraph 51):

1. **Likelihood of occurrence**: how probable it is that the risk or opportunity will materialise
2. **Potential magnitude of financial effects**: how large the impact on the undertaking's financial position, performance, cash flows, access to finance, or cost of capital could be

EFRAG IG1 paragraph 90 elaborates: "Sustainability risks and opportunities are assessed based on their likelihood of occurrence and the potential magnitude of their financial effects in the short-, medium- and long-term. Therefore, the undertaking is required to go through the list of potential material risks and opportunities in step B above and apply a set of objective thresholds for likelihood and magnitude as well as consider the nature of the effects of the identified risks and opportunities."

## A Matter Can Be Material from One Perspective Without the Other

A sustainability matter can be material from an impact perspective without necessarily having material financial risks or opportunities. The reverse is also true.

EFRAG IG1 explains this clearly in the sidebar to paragraph 37: "When a matter is material due to impact materiality, the same material matter may or may not result in material risks and/or material opportunities. Most of the material matters also give rise to financial risks and/or opportunities. It is only when an undertaking identifies that material risks and/or opportunities exist that the undertaking discloses the financial effects relating to the matter. However, where a matter is material due to its impacts only, the undertaking does not have to disclose risks and opportunities (or its related financial effects) that are not material."

This separation is important in practice. A company may have a material negative impact on a local ecosystem that does not create a material financial risk at this time. It still has to report that impact. Equally, a company may face a material financial risk from a regulatory change on a topic where its own operations do not create a material impact. It still has to report that risk.

## Material Impacts or Risks Arising from Actions to Address Sustainability Matters

A further complexity is that the actions an undertaking takes to address its sustainability impacts or risks can themselves create new material impacts or risks in relation to other sustainability matters. ESRS 1 section 3.6 addresses this directly.

For example (ESRS 1 paragraph 52):

- An action plan to decarbonise production that involves abandoning certain products might have material negative impacts on the undertaking's own workforce and result in material risks due to redundancy payments.
- An action plan of an automotive supplier to focus on the supply of electric vehicles might lead to stranded assets for the production of supply parts for conventional vehicles.

In these situations, the undertaking must disclose the existence of these material negative impacts or material risks together with the actions that generate them, and provide a cross-reference to the topic to which the impacts or risks relate (ESRS 1 paragraph 53).

<DeepDive title="How are impact materiality and financial materiality connected in practice?">
EFRAG IG1 paragraph 36 describes a practical perspective for organising the materiality assessment process:

First, identify impacts. Then, assess whether those impacts lead to risks and opportunities. Finally, identify separately any risks and opportunities that do not come from impacts, such as those that derive from dependencies where the undertaking does not affect the resource in question.

For most material impacts, a material risk and/or opportunity may emerge over time, even if it does not exist at the reporting date (EFRAG IG1 paragraph 37).

This means the two assessments are not truly independent: the output of the impact materiality assessment directly feeds into the financial materiality assessment.
</DeepDive>
