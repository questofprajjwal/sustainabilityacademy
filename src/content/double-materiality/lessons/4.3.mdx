Many companies preparing their first ESRS sustainability statement will have already conducted materiality assessments under other frameworks, particularly the GRI Standards or frameworks like TCFD. EFRAG IG1 Chapter 4 explains how those prior assessments can be leveraged, and where they need to be supplemented.

## The GRI Standards and Impact Materiality

The GRI Standards are a widely used global framework for sustainability reporting. An assessment performed under the GRI Universal Standards is focused on impact materiality: the most significant impacts of the organisation on the economy, environment, and people, including impacts on their human rights (GRI 1 Foundation 2021, Section 2.1 and 2.2).

GRI 1: Foundation 2021 states that the GRI Standards enable an organisation to publicly disclose its most significant impacts on the economy, environment, and people, including impacts on their human rights, and how the organisation manages these impacts. The process of determining material topics under GRI is informed by the ongoing identification and assessment of impacts, which involves engaging with relevant stakeholders and experts (GRI 1, Section 2.2).

Because the impact dimension is the same under GRI Universal Standards and ESRS, even though the exact scope of environmental, social, and governance matters may not be identical, a GRI materiality assessment constitutes a good basis for the assessment of impacts under the ESRS (EFRAG IG1 paragraph 138). The financial materiality dimension is the element to be added when moving from the GRI Universal Standards reporting framework to that of the ESRS.

EFRAG IG1 key point 11 states: "When undertakings perform an assessment under the GRI Universal Standards, the GRI assessment constitutes a good basis for the assessment of impacts under the ESRS."

### Synergies Between GRI and ESRS Impact Assessment

Several synergies exist for undertakings currently reporting under the GRI Universal Standards 2021 framework (EFRAG IG1 paragraph 139):

- The impact materiality assessment process in ESRS 1 Chapter 3.3 and ESRS 2 IRO-1 and IRO-2 are aligned in substance with GRI Universal Standards. The GRI materiality assessment can be the starting point for the ESRS double materiality assessment, with the addition of financial materiality.
- The universe of potential impacts identified using the GRI Universal Standards is compatible in principle with the list of sustainability matters in ESRS 1 paragraph AR 16. A GRI materiality assessment can inform the process of identifying entity-specific impacts.
- The role of due diligence and stakeholder engagement, which is central to both frameworks in informing the impact materiality assessment, is consistent between GRI and ESRS.

## How ESRS Financial Materiality Aligns with ISSB

IFRS S1, published by the International Sustainability Standards Board (ISSB), focuses on financial materiality. It requires a reporting entity to disclose sustainability-related financial information that is material to primary users of general-purpose financial reports when making decisions about providing resources to the entity.

EFRAG IG1 key point 12 states: "An undertaking that applies the ESRS is expected to be able to comply with the identification of sustainability-related information on risks and opportunities under IFRS Sustainability Disclosure Standards (also known as ISSB Standards). This reflects an alignment in scope between financial materiality in ISSB standards and the ESRS."

EFRAG IG1 paragraph 140 states that the criteria for financial materiality and materiality of information in the ESRS and the corresponding materiality approach in IFRS S1 are aligned. EFRAG IG1 paragraph 141 notes that the financial materiality assessment in ESRS 1 corresponds to the identification of information needed by primary users of general-purpose financial reports when making decisions relating to the provision of resources to the undertaking, which is aligned with IFRS S1 paragraph 1. Between the two standards, the definition of financial materiality is aligned.

A practical consequence of this alignment is that an undertaking applying ESRS is expected to comply with the identification of risks and opportunities under IFRS using the outcome of its ESRS assessment of financial materiality. In other words, the same financial materiality assessment process can support the identification of risks and opportunities for both IFRS and ESRS purposes (EFRAG IG1 paragraph 142).

One difference in scope: while ESRS considers both investors and other stakeholders in assessing what is decision-useful, IFRS S1 is focused on the needs of investors. However, the financial materiality dimension of ESRS is also focused on investors and is therefore aligned with IFRS S1 (EFRAG IG1 paragraph 143).

<HighlightBox>
**Comparing GRI, ESRS, and ISSB materiality concepts**

**GRI (impact materiality only):** Focuses on the organisation's most significant impacts on the economy, environment, and people, including impacts on human rights. Determined through stakeholder engagement and due diligence. Does not include a financial materiality dimension.

**ESRS (double materiality):** Covers both impact materiality (same dimension as GRI, though the scope of sustainability matters may differ slightly) and financial materiality (risks and opportunities that affect the company's development, financial position, performance, cash flows, access to finance, or cost of capital). A matter is material under ESRS if it meets either or both criteria.

**ISSB / IFRS S1 (financial materiality only):** Focuses on sustainability-related risks and opportunities that could reasonably be expected to affect the entity's cash flows, access to finance, or cost of capital over the short, medium, or long term. Oriented toward investors and other primary users of general-purpose financial reports. Does not include an impact materiality dimension.

The key difference between GRI and ESRS is that ESRS adds the financial materiality dimension to impact materiality. The key difference between ISSB and ESRS is that ESRS adds the impact materiality dimension to financial materiality.
</HighlightBox>

## The OECD Due Diligence Guidance and UN Guiding Principles

ESRS 1 paragraph 59 describes due diligence as the process by which undertakings identify, prevent, mitigate, and account for how they address actual and potential negative impacts on the environment and people. This process is described in the international instruments of the UN Guiding Principles on Business and Human Rights and the OECD Guidelines for Multinational Enterprises.

EFRAG IG1 paragraph 145 confirms that the materiality assessment of an undertaking's IROs is informed by the outcome of its due diligence process, as defined in the international instruments of the UN Guiding Principles on Business and Human Rights and the OECD Guidelines for Multinational Enterprises.

The due diligence process:

- Includes steps to identify and assess negative impacts caused and contributed to by the undertaking, as well as those connected to its own operations, products, or services through its business relationships. This is particularly useful when analysing the upstream and downstream value chain and identifying the impacts originating there.
- Provides criteria for management to prioritise actions based on the severity and likelihood of the impacts previously identified (EFRAG IG1 paragraph 146).
- Can help an undertaking both to identify its negative actual and potential impacts and to assess their materiality for reporting purposes based on the criteria of severity and likelihood (EFRAG IG1 paragraph 147).
- Also helps identify affected stakeholders whose engagement informs the materiality assessment of IROs (EFRAG IG1 paragraph 148).

## The Practical Benefit of Starting from an Existing Assessment

EFRAG IG1 paragraph 78 notes that an undertaking could start from the matters as informed by existing processes, such as GRI reporting or internal processes such as due diligence and risk management, and then compare the matters identified with the list in ESRS 1 AR 16 for completeness.

Starting from an existing GRI or TCFD assessment, rather than from scratch, offers several practical advantages:

- The universe of material topics has already been identified and tested through stakeholder engagement, reducing duplication of effort.
- The due diligence process already provides evidence about the severity and likelihood of impacts, which maps directly onto the ESRS impact materiality criteria.
- Existing GRI disclosures may already cover many of the topical ESRS disclosure requirements, since the impact materiality dimension is aligned in substance.
- For financial materiality, a TCFD or equivalent climate risk assessment can provide the starting point for identifying material climate-related risks and opportunities under the financial materiality dimension of ESRS.

The additional step required when moving from GRI to ESRS is to add the financial materiality assessment, covering risks and opportunities and their potential financial effects. The additional step when moving from ISSB or TCFD to ESRS is to add the impact materiality assessment, covering impacts on people and the environment.

<ExampleBox>
**Example: Using a GRI report as the starting point**

A European manufacturing company has been reporting under the GRI Universal Standards for five years. Its GRI materiality assessment identified eight material topics, including climate change, worker health and safety, and responsible sourcing.

When preparing for ESRS reporting, the company uses its GRI assessment as the starting point for the impact materiality dimension. The topics and their prioritisation carry over largely intact, since the impact dimension is aligned. The company then reviews the list against ESRS 1 AR 16 to check for completeness and identifies two additional subtopics not previously assessed.

The new step is adding financial materiality: the company assesses which of its sustainability-related risks and opportunities have material financial effects on its business. This includes climate transition risks (carbon cost exposure) and physical climate risks (flooding risk to its main production site). The combined outcome of impact and financial materiality forms the double materiality assessment under ESRS.
</ExampleBox>
