Double materiality is the core principle that determines what a company must report in its sustainability statement under the European Sustainability Reporting Standards (ESRS). It requires companies to look at sustainability topics from two distinct directions at the same time.

<HighlightBox>
A sustainability matter is "material" when it meets the criteria for impact materiality, financial materiality, or both. Both dimensions must be considered. The term "materiality" throughout the ESRS refers to double materiality unless otherwise specified. (ESRS 1, paragraph 37)
</HighlightBox>

## The two dimensions of double materiality

**Double materiality has two dimensions:**

**1. Impact materiality**

A sustainability matter is material from an impact perspective when it relates to the company's actual or potential, positive or negative impacts on people or the environment over the short, medium, or long term. These impacts include those connected with the company's own operations and with its upstream and downstream value chain, including through its products and services and through its business relationships. (ESRS 1, paragraph 43)

**2. Financial materiality**

A sustainability matter is material from a financial perspective if it triggers, or could reasonably be expected to trigger, material financial effects on the company. This is the case when a sustainability matter generates risks or opportunities that have a material influence, or could reasonably be expected to have a material influence, on the company's development, financial position, financial performance, cash flows, access to finance, or cost of capital over the short, medium, or long term. (ESRS 1, paragraph 49)

<AnalogyBox>
Think of double materiality like two overlapping circles, similar to a Venn diagram. The left circle represents impact materiality: sustainability matters where the company has effects on people or the environment. The right circle represents financial materiality: sustainability matters that generate risks or opportunities affecting the company's finances. The overlapping area in the middle represents matters that are material from both perspectives at once. A matter only needs to fall into one of the three areas to be considered material and therefore reported. This is how EFRAG IG1 Figure 1(a) illustrates the scope of double materiality.
</AnalogyBox>

## What are IROs?

Throughout the ESRS, the term **IROs** stands for **impacts, risks and opportunities**. This collective term reflects the double materiality perspective:

- "Impacts" refers to the positive and negative sustainability-related impacts connected with the company's business, identified through an impact materiality assessment. It covers both actual impacts and potential future impacts. (ESRS 1, paragraph 14(a))
- "Risks and opportunities" refers to the company's sustainability-related financial risks and opportunities, including those deriving from dependencies on natural, human, and social resources, identified through a financial materiality assessment. (ESRS 1, paragraph 14(b))

The materiality assessment is the process by which the company determines which sustainability IROs are material. Once determined, these material IROs become the basis for the sustainability statement. (EFRAG IG1, paragraph 1)

## Why CSRD requires double materiality

The Corporate Sustainability Reporting Directive (CSRD) requires large companies to include sustainability information in their management reports. Specifically, this means two categories of information:

1. Information necessary to understand the company's impacts on sustainability matters (the impact dimension).
2. Information necessary to understand how sustainability matters affect the company's development, performance, and position (the financial dimension).

ESRS are the standards companies use to fulfil this requirement. The ESRS sustainability statement provides users with information about the company's material impacts on people and the environment, and the material effects of sustainability matters on the company's development, performance, and position. (ESRS 1, paragraph 2)

## How this differs from "single materiality"

Traditional financial statements use a single materiality concept that focuses only on information relevant to investors and lenders for making resource-allocation decisions. This is the outside-in direction only: how external conditions affect the company's finances.

The scope of financial materiality under ESRS is an expansion of this traditional concept. It applies the same logic but within the broader context of sustainability reporting. (ESRS 1, paragraph 47)

Impact materiality adds a second, distinct direction: the inside-out view. This captures how the company's activities affect people and the environment, regardless of whether those impacts also create financial consequences for the company. Impacts are captured by impact materiality irrespective of whether or not they are financially material. (ESRS 1, paragraph 38)

<HighlightBox>
A sustainability impact may be financially material from the start, or it may become financially material over time, when it could reasonably be expected to affect the company's financial position, financial performance, cash flows, its access to finance, or cost of capital. (ESRS 1, paragraph 38)
</HighlightBox>

## A matter can be material from one or both dimensions

Impact materiality and financial materiality are interrelated and their interdependencies must be considered. In general, the starting point is the assessment of impacts. However, there may also be material risks and opportunities that are not related to the company's impacts.

When a matter is material due to impact materiality, the same material matter may or may not also result in material risks or opportunities. Most material impact matters do also give rise to financial risks or opportunities. However, where a matter is material due to its impacts only, the company does not have to disclose risks and opportunities (or their related financial effects) that are not material. (EFRAG IG1, paragraph 37 and interaction box)

<ExampleBox>
**Example: A matter material from one dimension only**

An undertaking in the extractive industry assesses health and safety as a material negative impact, due to the frequency and severity of work-related accidents in its location. At the reporting date, the financial effects are not considered material. In this case, the impacts, risks, and opportunities on this matter are not aggregated, and the matter is regarded as material from an impact materiality dimension only. (EFRAG IG1, paragraph 185)

**Example: A matter material from both dimensions**

An oil and gas company identifies a material negative impact from not consulting or reaching an agreement with indigenous people about land use for extraction and community relocation. At the reporting date, the company does not expect protests. However, the community may later protest, halting the production site and causing material costs due to production days lost or the abandonment of the project. This matter is material from an impact perspective and may become material from a financial perspective as well. (EFRAG IG1, paragraph 37(a))
</ExampleBox>
