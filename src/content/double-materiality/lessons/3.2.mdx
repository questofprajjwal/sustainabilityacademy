## What Is Step B?

Step B is the identification step. Its purpose is to compile a comprehensive list of all actual and potential impacts, risks, and opportunities (IROs) related to sustainability matters across the undertaking's own operations and its upstream and downstream value chain. This list is then carried forward to Step C for assessment.

EFRAG IG1 paragraph 73 describes it as follows: "In this step, the undertaking identifies the actual and potential impacts, risks and opportunities (IROs) relating to environmental, social and governance matters across its own operations and in its upstream and downstream value chain. The outcome will be a 'long' list of impacts, risks and opportunities for further assessment and analysis in subsequent steps."

The list produced in Step B is intentionally comprehensive. It is not yet filtered for materiality. That filtering happens in Step C.

## The Two Sources of the IRO Universe

The universe of IROs that an undertaking identifies in Step B comes from two sources working together.

**Source 1: The ESRS 1 AR16 list of sustainability matters.** The undertaking uses the list of sustainability matters in ESRS 1 paragraph AR16 to support this process and to ensure completeness (EFRAG IG1 paragraph 74). This list covers the full range of environmental, social, and governance topics, subtopics, and sub-subtopics that could be relevant to undertakings in general.

**Source 2: Entity-specific identification.** The undertaking also identifies matters specific to its own facts and circumstances that may not be captured in the AR16 list. EFRAG IG1 paragraph 74 notes that the undertaking should "consider entity-specific sustainability matters not covered in that list, if any." Until sector-specific standards are issued, sector sustainability matters must be identified and assessed as entity-specific matters. Possible resources for this include the IFRS industry-based guidance and GRI Sector Standards.

EFRAG IG1 paragraph 77 explains that the process of identification may start with the screening of the AR16 list and then be complemented with additional entity-specific matters. These may follow from either internal processes such as due diligence, risk management, or grievance mechanisms, or from external sources such as stakeholder engagement. Alternatively, the undertaking may start from the matters already identified through existing processes and then compare those against the AR16 list for completeness. The two approaches can be combined.

<ExampleBox>
Consider a large retail company identifying its IRO universe across environmental, social, and governance topics.

**Environmental:** Potential impacts and risks related to packaging waste in own operations; actual impacts from energy use in stores and logistics; financial risks from carbon pricing on transport; dependencies on water availability in supplier regions for agricultural products.

**Social:** Actual impacts on working conditions in the own workforce including health and safety in warehouses; potential impacts on workers in upstream supplier factories across garment and food supply chains; financial risks from labour disputes or reputational damage from supply chain practices; opportunities from fair wage programmes attracting better staff.

**Governance:** Potential negative impacts from corruption risks in procurement relationships in higher-risk markets; financial risks from regulatory non-compliance; impacts related to business conduct practices with suppliers.

This list does not yet indicate which of these IROs are material. It simply captures everything that could plausibly be relevant given the company's business model, value chain, and operating contexts. Step C will then determine which of these warrant disclosure.
</ExampleBox>

## The Role of Due Diligence in Identifying Negative Impacts

The due diligence process is a key input to Step B, particularly for identifying negative impacts. ESRS 1 paragraph 59 defines due diligence as "the process by which undertakings identify, prevent, mitigate and account for how they address the actual and potential negative impacts on the environment and people connected with their business."

ESRS 1 paragraph 60 explains that the identification and assessment of negative impacts connected with the undertaking's own operations and its upstream and downstream value chain, including through its products or services and through its business relationships, is a core element of the due diligence process. It is this aspect of the due diligence process that informs the assessment of material impacts.

EFRAG IG1 paragraph 103 confirms that "the outcome of the undertaking's ongoing due diligence processes that are in place is generally useful to inform the materiality assessment." This means an undertaking with established due diligence processes can draw on the findings from those processes rather than conducting a separate and independent identification exercise. The due diligence process references are the OECD Guidelines for Multinational Enterprises and the UN Guiding Principles on Business and Human Rights.

## Identifying Risks and Opportunities That Do Not Derive from Impacts

Step B also requires identifying risks and opportunities that arise in the absence of material impacts. EFRAG IG1 paragraph 38 states clearly that "material risks and opportunities also arise in the absence of material impacts connected to the undertaking, such as when they arise from dependencies on natural and human resources."

This part of the identification covers:

- Dependencies on natural resources such as water, land, biodiversity, and ecosystem services that could be disrupted.
- Dependencies on human resources that could be affected by external labour market conditions.
- Dependencies on social resources such as community relationships and regulatory conditions.
- Risks arising from sustainability-related regulatory developments, such as new pollution abatement legislation that requires significant capital expenditure.

ESRS 1 paragraph 40 requires the undertaking to consider how it is affected by its dependencies on the availability of natural, human, and social resources at appropriate prices and quality, irrespective of its potential impacts on those resources.

## What Level of Granularity to Use

The undertaking needs to decide at what level of granularity to identify IROs. EFRAG IG1 paragraph 42 explains that the level of granularity ranges from topic to subtopic level and, in some cases, to sub-subtopic level. The table in that paragraph shows examples:

For ESRS E3 (Water and marine resources), the topic is "Water and marine resources," the subtopic is "Water," and sub-subtopics include "Water consumption," "Water withdrawals," and "Water discharges."

For ESRS S1 (Own workforce), the topic is "Own workforce," a subtopic is "Other work-related rights," and sub-subtopics include "Child labour," "Forced labour," "Adequate housing," and "Privacy."

EFRAG IG1 paragraph 80 notes that the undertaking may aggregate or disaggregate the IROs at the most appropriate level according to its facts and circumstances. It should relate the names it uses for sustainability matters to the corresponding items in the ESRS 1 AR16 list when these differ.

<HighlightBox>
The undertaking is not required to assess each matter down to the sub-subtopic level in all cases. The appropriate level of granularity depends on the nature of the matter and the undertaking's specific circumstances. The purpose is to ensure that no genuinely material IRO is obscured by aggregating it into a broader category (ESRS 1 paragraph 56).
</HighlightBox>

## What Step B Produces

The output of Step B is a comprehensive, unfiltered list, sometimes called a "long list," of actual and potential IROs related to sustainability matters. For each identified IRO, the undertaking also captures whether it relates to own operations and/or the upstream or downstream value chain, as well as the relevant time horizon for potential impacts and risks and opportunities (EFRAG IG1 paragraph 76). This long list becomes the input to Step C, where each IRO is assessed against materiality criteria to determine which ones are material and must be reported.
