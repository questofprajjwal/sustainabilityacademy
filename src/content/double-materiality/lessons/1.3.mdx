Stakeholders are central to the double materiality assessment. Understanding who they are, what role each group plays, and how to engage with them shapes the quality and completeness of a company's materiality assessment.

## Two groups of stakeholders

ESRS 1 defines stakeholders as those who can affect or be affected by the company. There are two main groups: (ESRS 1, paragraph 22)

**(a) Affected stakeholders:** Individuals or groups whose interests are affected or could be affected, positively or negatively, by the company's activities and its direct and indirect business relationships across its value chain.

**(b) Users of sustainability statements:** Primary users of general-purpose financial reporting (existing and potential investors, lenders, and other creditors, including asset managers, credit institutions, and insurance undertakings) and other users of sustainability statements, including the company's business partners, trade unions and social partners, civil society and non-governmental organisations, governments, analysts, and academics.

Some, but not all, stakeholders may belong to both groups. (ESRS 1, paragraph 23)

<AnalogyBox>
Think of it this way: "Who is affected by us?" identifies one group, the affected stakeholders, whose lives, rights, and wellbeing can be impacted by what the company does. "Who reads our reports?" identifies the second group, the users of sustainability statements, who rely on the disclosed information to make decisions, form views, or hold the company to account. These are different questions, and they often point to different people. A local community living near a factory is affected by air emissions, but they may never read the annual report. A pension fund manager reads the sustainability statement closely but may not be directly affected by the company's operations.
</AnalogyBox>

## Why engagement with affected stakeholders is central

Engagement with affected stakeholders is central to the company's on-going due diligence process and to the sustainability materiality assessment. This includes its processes to identify and assess actual and potential negative impacts, which then inform the assessment process to identify the material impacts for the purposes of sustainability reporting. (ESRS 1, paragraph 24)

EFRAG IG1 explains that engagement with affected stakeholders, for example consulting with them and incorporating their views into the materiality assessment, helps to substantiate their perspectives when determining the relevance of sustainability matters to them. For example, this includes engaging with the company's employees and their representatives on health and safety matters. Such engagement also includes feedback received from affected stakeholders from ongoing processes of engagement as part of the company's business practices. (EFRAG IG1, paragraph 105)

Engagement with affected stakeholders informs the identification and assessment of material impacts. It can help with the assessment of severity, likelihood, and time horizons, and also helps ensure the completeness of the material impacts identified. (EFRAG IG1, paragraph 199)

Dialogue with affected stakeholders may assist during various steps of the materiality assessment. However, separate engagement with affected stakeholders in each step of the materiality assessment is not necessary, as companies may already have ongoing engagement with them to use. (EFRAG IG1, paragraph 107)

## When financial materiality stakeholders are relevant

For the financial materiality dimension, a different subset of stakeholders becomes relevant. It may be appropriate to engage with the company's business functions as well as with investors of the company and other financial counterparties (such as banks) to assess, validate, and ensure the completeness of the list of material risks and opportunities. (EFRAG IG1, paragraph 93)

The conclusions of the ESRS financial materiality assessment should be based on supportable evidence. This may include the views and interests of users of sustainability statements, including investors. The company may leverage existing mechanisms of dialogue with shareholders, other investors, and, in some cases, lenders to support its financial materiality assessment process. (EFRAG IG1, paragraph 111)

## FAQs on stakeholder engagement from EFRAG IG1

### FAQ 15: Do the ESRS mandate active engagement with affected stakeholders?

The ESRS require disclosure on the materiality assessment and its outcomes but do not mandate specific behaviour on stakeholder engagement or the due diligence process. (EFRAG IG1, paragraph 197)

However, ESRS 1 paragraph 45 states that the impact materiality assessment is informed by the company's due diligence process. In addition, ESRS 1 paragraph 24 points to affected stakeholder engagement as central to the materiality assessment. Engagement with affected stakeholders is a tool that supports the company's business processes (for example, due diligence) as well as the management of sustainability matters. The company, when preparing its sustainability statement, can leverage its engagement with affected stakeholders per its due diligence process, if applicable. (EFRAG IG1, paragraph 198)

### FAQ 16: Can the company prioritise some categories of affected stakeholders?

Engagement with affected stakeholders helps the company understand which sustainability matters are sources of concern for the respective stakeholders and how they are affected. This information may be useful input for the assessment. (EFRAG IG1, paragraph 200)

ESRS 1 paragraph 22(a) states that affected stakeholders are the individuals or groups whose interests are affected or could be affected, positively or negatively, by the company's activities and its direct and indirect business relationships across its value chain. The concept of key stakeholders (or "relevant stakeholders" in international instruments) rests on the idea that not all stakeholders will be equally affected by the company's activities. Furthermore, the company is to identify which stakeholders' views are to be taken into account in connection with a specific activity. It also builds upon the idea that the degree of impact on stakeholders may inform the degree of engagement specifically for prioritisation. (EFRAG IG1, paragraph 201)

The company may not engage with all stakeholders for all sustainability matters. Engagement with stakeholders who are not affected by the specific sustainability matter is not meaningful. Therefore, the company may engage with different groups of affected stakeholders for different matters. (EFRAG IG1, paragraph 203)

### FAQ 17: What is the role of silent stakeholders and how should they be considered?

There may be stakeholders who cannot voice their concerns. In the ESRS, nature has been identified as a silent stakeholder. Nature is an essential part of the sustainability context of the company and the value chain it operates in. Nature, unlike other stakeholders, cannot voice its concerns on its own, neither verbally nor in writing. Data from scientific sources (such as scientific studies on the planetary boundaries or scientifically validated data) may give nature a voice, as it may explain the state of nature, such as the health of bird populations, the state of water bodies, or the condition of a forest. (EFRAG IG1, paragraph 204)

Channels monitoring the concerns of silent stakeholders can provide valuable input to the materiality assessment for impacts, dependencies, and, where applicable, the subsequent risks and opportunities for the company. (EFRAG IG1, paragraph 205)

Examples of considering silent stakeholders include: identifying the silent stakeholders likely to be impacted by the company's activities and the actual and associated potential impacts; conducting research such as reviews of scientific studies, articles, and environmental impact assessments; and using proxies such as organisations that legitimately represent the silent stakeholder, or, for considerations about nature, organisations that assess the current and future state of the ecosystem, water resources, or climate. (EFRAG IG1, paragraph 206)

In situations where engagement with affected stakeholders is not possible, for instance because such engagement would put them at risk, the company may consider appropriate alternatives. This may include consulting credible independent experts, a non-governmental organisation representing the affected community, or, for environmental matters, scientific articles and reports. (EFRAG IG1, paragraph 108)
