Impact materiality is one of the two dimensions of double materiality. It asks a company to look outward: what effects does the company have on people and the environment?

## Definition of impact materiality

A sustainability matter is material from an impact perspective when it pertains to the company's material actual or potential, positive or negative **impacts on people or the environment** over the short, medium, or long term. (ESRS 1, paragraph 43)

<HighlightBox>
Impacts on people or the environment include impacts in relation to environmental, social, and governance matters. (ESRS 1, paragraph 44)
</HighlightBox>

## Scope: own operations plus the value chain

The scope of impact materiality is broad. Impacts include those connected with the company's own operations and upstream and downstream value chain, including through its products and services, as well as through its business relationships. Business relationships include those in the company's upstream and downstream value chain and are not limited to direct contractual relationships. (ESRS 1, paragraph 43)

This means the materiality assessment must consider impacts arising at all levels: the company's own facilities and activities, suppliers and other upstream partners, as well as distributors, customers, and other downstream actors.

The assessment also includes material IROs connected to the company through its direct and indirect business relationships in the upstream or downstream value chain. (EFRAG IG1, paragraph 25)

## Actual vs potential impacts

The definition distinguishes between actual and potential impacts. This distinction matters because different assessment criteria apply to each.

**Actual impacts** are impacts that are already occurring. They exist in the present reporting period.

**Potential impacts** are impacts that have not yet materialised but could occur in the future. They are impacts the company is connected to but that are not yet happening.

The materiality of a negative impact is informed by the due diligence process defined in the international instruments of the UN Guiding Principles on Business and Human Rights and the OECD Guidelines for Multinational Enterprises. (ESRS 1, paragraph 45)

- For **actual negative impacts**, materiality is based on the **severity** of the impact.
- For **potential negative impacts**, materiality is based on the **severity and likelihood** of the impact.
- For **actual positive impacts**, materiality is based on the **scale and scope** of the impact.
- For **potential positive impacts**, materiality is based on the **scale, scope, and likelihood** of the impact. (ESRS 1, paragraphs 45-46; EFRAG IG1, paragraph 85)

## Positive vs negative impacts treated differently

Positive and negative impacts are not assessed the same way. For negative impacts, an additional factor comes in: **irremediable character**, which measures whether harm can be undone. This applies to negative impacts because it captures the gravity of harm in a way that does not apply symmetrically to positive outcomes. Positive impacts do not carry the same additional factor. (ESRS 1, paragraph 45-46)

<HighlightBox>
In the case of a potential negative human rights impact, the severity of the impact takes precedence over its likelihood. This reflects the seriousness with which potential human rights harms must be treated, even when they are not yet certain to occur. (ESRS 1, paragraph 45)
</HighlightBox>

## The role of due diligence

The materiality assessment for negative impacts is informed by the due diligence process. Due diligence is the process by which companies identify, prevent, mitigate, and account for how they address actual and potential negative impacts on the environment and people connected with their business. These include negative impacts connected with the company's own operations and its upstream and downstream value chain, including through its products or services, as well as through its business relationships. (ESRS 1, paragraph 59)

Due diligence is an on-going practice that responds to and may trigger changes in the company's strategy, business model, activities, business relationships, operating, sourcing, and selling contexts. It is described in the international instruments of the UN Guiding Principles on Business and Human Rights and the OECD Guidelines for Multinational Enterprises. (ESRS 1, paragraph 59)

The identification of material impacts also supports the identification of material sustainability risks and opportunities, which are often a product of such impacts. (ESRS 1, paragraph 60)

<ExampleBox>
**Example: Actual vs potential impacts for a manufacturing company**

Consider a manufacturing company that uses a river for cooling water at its production site.

**Actual negative impact:** The company is currently discharging warm water into the river, which is measurably reducing fish populations in the local ecosystem. This impact is happening now. Its materiality is assessed based on severity alone (scale, scope, and whether the environmental damage can be remediated).

**Potential negative impact:** The company stores chemical solvents near the riverbank. A spill has not yet occurred, but one could occur in the future. The materiality of this potential negative impact is assessed based on both its severity (how bad the contamination would be) and its likelihood (how probable is a spill given current storage practices).

**Actual positive impact:** The company runs a job training programme that employs workers from local communities with limited formal qualifications. People are currently benefiting from employment and skills development. Materiality is assessed based on scale (how significant the benefit is per person) and scope (how many people are affected).

**Potential positive impact:** The company plans to convert part of its operations to produce recycled packaging materials, which would reduce plastic waste in the supply chain if implemented. This benefit has not yet occurred. Materiality is assessed based on scale, scope, and likelihood of the project going ahead.
</ExampleBox>
