Once a company has identified its actual and potential impacts, it must assess whether each impact is material. This assessment is based on specific criteria: severity, likelihood, and the distinction between actual and potential impacts.

## Severity and its three factors

Severity is the primary criterion for assessing the materiality of negative impacts. It is determined by three factors: (ESRS 1, paragraph 45; ESRS 1 Appendix A, AR 10)

**(a) Scale:** How grave the negative impact is, or how beneficial the positive impact is for people or the environment. For negative impacts, this measures the extent of infringement of access to basic life necessities or freedoms such as education or livelihood. (EFRAG IG1, paragraph 115(a))

**(b) Scope:** How widespread the negative or positive impacts are. In the case of environmental impacts, scope may be understood as the extent of environmental damage or a geographical perimeter. In the case of impacts on people, scope may be understood as the number of people adversely affected. (EFRAG IG1, paragraph 115(b))

**(c) Irremediable character:** Whether and to what extent the negative impact could be remediated, that is, restoring the environment or affected people to their prior state. The underlying question is whether there are any limits to the ability of restoring the environment or those affected to a situation at least the same as, or equivalent to, their situation before the negative impact. (EFRAG IG1, paragraph 115(c))

<HighlightBox>
Any one of the three characteristics, scale, scope, or irremediable character, can make a negative impact severe. In the case of a potential negative human rights impact, the severity of the impact takes precedence over its likelihood. (ESRS 1 Appendix A, AR 11)
</HighlightBox>

The three characteristics are also often interdependent. Irremediable character could impact severity by increasing its scale. In turn, it is often the case that the greater the scale or the wider the scope of an impact, the harder it is to remediate. A case-by-case assessment is to be performed to conclude whether any of the three characteristics makes the impact severe. (EFRAG IG1, paragraph 118)

## Severity for positive impacts

For positive impacts, the same concept of severity applies but without the irremediable character factor: (ESRS 1, paragraph 46)

- For **actual positive impacts**, materiality is based on scale and scope.
- For **potential positive impacts**, materiality is based on scale, scope, and likelihood.

## When likelihood applies

Likelihood is only relevant to **potential impacts**, not actual ones. This is a direct consequence of the definitions:

- An actual impact is already occurring. Since it is already happening, there is no probability to estimate for whether it will occur.
- A potential impact has not yet materialised. Because it might or might not occur in the future, the probability of occurrence must be considered.

For **potential negative impacts**, both severity and likelihood must be considered. For **potential negative human rights impacts**, severity takes precedence over likelihood. This means a potential human rights harm of high severity must be treated as material even if its likelihood is judged to be low. (ESRS 1, paragraph 45)

Likelihood can be measured or determined qualitatively or quantitatively depending on the available information. It could be described using general terms (such as "unlikely" or "highly likely"), mathematically using statistical terms (such as "10 per cent"), or by using frequency over a given time period (such as "once every 10 years"). (EFRAG IG1, paragraph 122)

## Setting thresholds

ESRS 1 sets criteria for the materiality assessment but does not prescribe specific thresholds to determine when a matter is material or not. The assessment therefore requires the exercise of judgement. Each company needs to set thresholds based on ESRS 1 criteria as well as its own specific facts and circumstances. The need for judgement will be higher whenever the information and evidence about the materiality of a given IRO is inconclusive. (EFRAG IG1, paragraph 27)

Companies shall establish how they apply criteria, including appropriate thresholds, to determine the information they disclose. (ESRS 1, paragraph 36)

Appropriate thresholds are necessary to determine which impacts, risks, and opportunities are identified and addressed by the company as material, and to determine which sustainability matters are material for reporting purposes. (ESRS 1, paragraph 42)

<ExampleBox>
**Example: Applying the three severity factors to water pollution**

A factory upstream of a local community discharges untreated wastewater into a river.

**Scale:** The contamination contains heavy metals at levels that exceed safe limits for drinking water. The gravity of the impact on people (loss of access to clean water, health risks) and on the environment (death of aquatic life, soil contamination from irrigation) is high.

**Scope:** The river is the primary water source for three villages with a combined population of approximately 8,000 people. The environmental damage extends for 15 kilometres downstream. The scope is therefore wide.

**Irremediable character:** Heavy metal contamination in river sediment can persist for decades and is extremely difficult to reverse. Restoring the river to its prior condition would require extensive and uncertain remediation efforts. The irremediable character of the impact is high.

In this example, all three factors point to a severe impact. Even if only one factor had been high, for example only scale or only irremediable character, that alone would be sufficient to make the impact severe and therefore potentially material. The company would then assess whether this actual impact meets its materiality threshold.
</ExampleBox>

<DeepDive title="How does EFRAG IG1 illustrate the threshold-setting process?">
EFRAG IG1 Chapter 3.6 illustrates two approaches for setting thresholds.

For actual negative impacts, the undertaking can map each identified impact across the three severity characteristics (scale, scope, irremediable character) using a simple scoring framework, for example low, medium, or high for each. The combination of scores across the three characteristics informs whether the impact crosses the materiality threshold. (EFRAG IG1, paragraph 119 and Figure 4)

For potential negative impacts, the likelihood dimension is added. The undertaking combines the three severity factors into an overall severity score and then maps each impact across a two-axis framework: severity on one axis and likelihood on the other. The threshold for what counts as material is defined by the undertaking itself, reflecting its specific facts and circumstances. Impacts in the high severity and high likelihood area of the framework will clearly be material. Impacts with very low probability may or may not be material depending on their severity; for instance, a potential impact with catastrophic environmental consequences related to environmental hazards could be assessed as material even at very low likelihood. (EFRAG IG1, paragraph 123 and Figure 5)
</DeepDive>
