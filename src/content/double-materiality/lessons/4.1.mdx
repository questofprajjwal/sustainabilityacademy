A threshold is the point at which an impact, risk, or opportunity crosses from being non-material to material. Thresholds help a company decide which items must be reported and which can be left out. Without thresholds, every minor issue would appear equally important, making the sustainability statement hard to use.

## Why ESRS Does Not Specify Fixed Thresholds

ESRS 1 does not prescribe how to set thresholds. This is deliberate. The standards take a principles-based approach because no single threshold could work fairly for all companies across all sectors, sizes, and geographies (ESRS 1 paragraph 42; EFRAG IG1 paragraph 27).

Instead, ESRS 1 sets the criteria that thresholds must reflect: severity and likelihood for impact materiality, and magnitude and likelihood of financial effects for financial materiality. Companies must then apply those criteria using their own judgement, taking into account their specific facts and circumstances.

<HighlightBox>
ESRS 1 sets criteria, not fixed numbers. Companies set their own thresholds using those criteria. EFRAG IG1 and ESRS 2 IRO-1 both require companies to disclose and explain the thresholds they have used, so that readers can assess the judgements made.
</HighlightBox>

ESRS 2 paragraphs 53 and 59 require the company to disclose how its thresholds have been set or applied. This transparency requirement is key: even though thresholds vary, users of the sustainability statement must be able to understand and challenge the choices made.

## Thresholds for Impact Materiality

For impact materiality, thresholds are applied based on the **severity** of the impact and, for potential impacts, also their **likelihood** (ESRS 1 paragraphs 45 and 46; EFRAG IG1 paragraphs 113 to 114).

### Severity for Negative Impacts

The severity of an actual or potential negative impact is assessed from the perspective of affected people or the environment. Severity is determined by three characteristics (EFRAG IG1 paragraph 115):

- **Scale:** how grave the impact is, meaning the extent of infringement of access to basic life necessities or freedoms such as education or livelihood.
- **Scope:** how widespread the impact is, meaning the number of individuals affected or the extent of environmental damage.
- **Irremediable character:** the extent to which the impact can be remediated, for example through compensation or restitution. The underlying question is whether there are limits to the ability of restoring the environment or those affected to a situation at least equivalent to their situation before the negative impact.

Any one of the three characteristics can make an impact severe, but they are often interdependent. For example, irremediable character can increase the effective scale of an impact. In turn, the greater the scale or the wider the scope, the harder it often is to remediate (EFRAG IG1 paragraph 118).

### What "Irremediable Character" Means

Irremediable character refers specifically to the limits on putting things right after a negative impact has occurred. When an impact cannot be fully undone, through compensation, restitution, or restoration, its irremediable character is higher. This raises its severity and, all else being equal, makes it more likely to be material. An irreversible environmental harm, such as the permanent loss of a species habitat, scores high on irremediable character even if its scope appears limited (EFRAG IG1 paragraph 115(c)).

### Severity for Positive Impacts

For positive impacts, materiality is based on scale and scope only. There is no irremediable character dimension for positive impacts, because the concept of remedy does not apply in the same way (ESRS 1 paragraph 46; EFRAG IG1 paragraph 114).

### Adding Likelihood for Potential Impacts

For actual impacts, materiality rests on severity alone. For potential impacts, likelihood must also be considered alongside severity. Likelihood refers to the probability of the impact happening. It can be described qualitatively (for example, "unlikely" or "highly likely"), by statistical terms (for example, "10 per cent"), or by frequency over time (for example, "once every 10 years") (EFRAG IG1 paragraph 122).

For potential negative human rights impacts, ESRS 1 paragraph 45 states that severity takes precedence over likelihood. This reflects the approach in the international due diligence instruments referenced in the standards.

<HighlightBox>
Priority should be given to any supportable evidence that provides as much objectivity as possible to the materiality conclusion. Reasonable quantification of potential impacts may not always be possible, but qualitative assessment remains valid (EFRAG IG1 paragraph 117).
</HighlightBox>

## Thresholds for Financial Materiality

For financial materiality, ESRS do not prescribe a specific threshold definition. ESRS 1 paragraph AR 15 states that once the company has identified its risks and opportunities, it shall determine which of them are material for reporting. This is based on a combination of the likelihood of occurrence and the potential magnitude of financial effects, determined on the basis of appropriate thresholds (EFRAG IG1 paragraph 131).

### Quantitative and Qualitative Approaches

The company may refer to absolute monetary thresholds or to relative monetary thresholds, such as a percentage of a line item in its primary financial statements, its revenues, costs, total assets, or net equity (EFRAG IG1 paragraph 132).

However, a company should also consider that a sustainability matter may be financially material even if its financial effects are not reliably measurable at the reporting date. In that case, the thresholds will rely on qualitative factors and ranges of possible effects, such as high, medium, or low impact on financial performance (EFRAG IG1 paragraph 134).

One important difference from financial statement materiality is the time horizon. The time horizon for financial materiality assessment in sustainability reporting is longer than the typical time horizon factored into financial statements. This means the company must consider the cumulative effect of a sustainability matter on revenues, costs, or other financial items over a lengthy period. Similarly, a threshold for likelihood must consider the cumulative probability over a period of time to cover the long-term horizon (EFRAG IG1 paragraph 132).

Financial materiality is not limited to financial effects on items already recognised in the financial statements. The company must also consider financial effects associated with dependencies on natural and social resources that do not yet meet the criteria for accounting recognition (EFRAG IG1 paragraph 133).

<DeepDive title="What about reputational risks?">
Where a company is exposed to reputational risks that are of interest to investors, those risks may be financially material even if a direct effect on cash flows cannot be quantified. Reputational risk may influence the availability of finance or the cost of funding, and therefore may be financially material through that channel (EFRAG IG1 paragraph 135).
</DeepDive>

## Documenting Threshold Decisions

The ESRS do not prescribe specific documentation formats, but it is reasonable to expect a certain level of documentation to be needed for internal purposes (EFRAG IG1 paragraph 186). Such documentation can inform those in charge of governance, help assurance providers perform their work, and support the disclosures required under ESRS 2 IRO-1.

ESRS 2 IRO-1 requires the company to explain how it determined the materiality of each impact, including the qualitative and quantitative thresholds used. This disclosure helps users of the sustainability statement understand the judgement exercised by the company.

Good practice in documenting thresholds includes recording: what criteria were used and why, how severity and likelihood were assessed for each impact, what quantitative or qualitative scale was applied, and how financial magnitude was defined and estimated for risks and opportunities.

<ExampleBox>
**Example 1: Threshold-setting for a GHG emissions impact**

A manufacturing company assesses its Scope 1 and 2 greenhouse gas emissions as an actual negative impact on the climate. For impact materiality, it assesses severity using scale (contribution to global temperature rise), scope (global environmental damage), and irremediable character (CO2 remains in the atmosphere for centuries, giving this dimension a high rating). Because this is an actual impact, likelihood is not separately assessed. The company sets its threshold so that any emission source contributing more than 1 per cent of its total annual Scope 1 and 2 emissions is reviewed individually for material disclosure.

For financial materiality, the company identifies a related risk: the risk that carbon pricing legislation increases its operating costs. It sets a threshold based on potential financial magnitude relative to its cost base, using a qualitative scale (low, medium, high) for scenarios where precise monetary estimates are not yet available. It documents the threshold decisions in its materiality assessment workbook and discloses a summary in the ESRS 2 IRO-1 disclosures.

**Example 2: Threshold-setting for a supply chain labour risk**

A fashion retailer identifies potential negative impacts on workers in its upstream supply chain as a result of poor working conditions among its tier-two suppliers. For impact materiality, it assesses severity using scale (the degree of labour rights infringement), scope (the number of workers potentially affected), and irremediable character (whether affected workers can be restored to their prior situation). Because this is a potential impact, likelihood is also considered: the company uses findings from its due diligence process and third-party audits to estimate that the risk occurs with moderate probability. It sets a threshold that captures any supplier category where the combined severity and likelihood exceeds a "medium" rating on its internal assessment matrix.

For financial materiality, the company considers the financial risk of supply chain disruption, legal costs, and reputational damage if these labour issues become public. It sets a threshold based on the potential impact on revenues and brand value, documented in a narrative qualitative assessment.
</ExampleBox>
