To understand how double materiality works in practice, it helps to understand the structure of the standards themselves. The European Sustainability Reporting Standards (ESRS) form a set of 12 standards organised into three categories, covering specific reporting areas and using consistent drafting conventions.

## Three categories of ESRS standards

There are three categories of ESRS: (ESRS 1, paragraph 4)

**(a) Cross-cutting standards:** These apply to all companies regardless of which sector they operate in. The two cross-cutting standards are ESRS 1 (General Requirements) and ESRS 2 (General Disclosures). Cross-cutting standards and topical standards are sector-agnostic, meaning they apply to all undertakings across all sectors.

**(b) Topical standards:** These cover specific Environmental (E), Social (S), and Governance (G) topics. They are also sector-agnostic. Each topical standard covers a sustainability topic structured into topics, sub-topics, and, where necessary, sub-sub-topics.

**(c) Sector-specific standards:** These apply to all companies within a given sector. They address impacts, risks, and opportunities that are likely to be material for all companies in that sector and that are not covered, or not sufficiently covered, by topical standards. Sector-specific standards achieve a high degree of comparability. (ESRS 1, paragraph 10)

ESRS 1 describes the architecture of the standards, explains drafting conventions and fundamental concepts, and sets out general requirements for preparing and presenting sustainability information. (ESRS 1, paragraph 6)

ESRS 2 establishes Disclosure Requirements on the information that the company shall provide at a general level across all material sustainability matters in the reporting areas of governance, strategy, impact, risk and opportunity management, and metrics and targets. (ESRS 1, paragraph 7)

## The four reporting areas

The Disclosure Requirements in ESRS 2, in topical ESRS, and in sector-specific ESRS are structured into four reporting areas: (ESRS 1, paragraph 12)

**(a) Governance (GOV):** The governance processes, controls, and procedures used to monitor, manage, and oversee impacts, risks, and opportunities.

**(b) Strategy (SBM):** How the company's strategy and business model interact with its material impacts, risks, and opportunities, including how the company addresses those impacts, risks, and opportunities.

**(c) Impact, risk and opportunity management (IRO):** The processes by which the company identifies impacts, risks, and opportunities and assesses their materiality, and manages material sustainability matters through policies and actions.

**(d) Metrics and targets (MT):** The company's performance, including targets it has set and progress towards meeting them.

## "Shall disclose" vs "may disclose"

The ESRS use specific language to distinguish between different degrees of obligation: (ESRS 1, paragraph 18)

- **"shall disclose"** indicates that the provision is prescribed by a Disclosure Requirement or datapoint. Disclosure is mandatory.
- **"may disclose"** indicates voluntary disclosure to encourage good practice.

In addition, ESRS use "shall consider" when referring to issues, resources, or methodologies that the company is expected to take into account when preparing a given disclosure, if applicable.

## Application Requirements vs Disclosure Requirements

Each Disclosure Requirement consists of one or more distinct datapoints. The term "datapoint" can also refer to a narrative sub-element of a Disclosure Requirement. (ESRS 1, paragraph 16)

In addition to Disclosure Requirements, most ESRS also contain **Application Requirements**. Application Requirements support the application of Disclosure Requirements and have the same authority as other parts of an ESRS. (ESRS 1, paragraph 17)

<HighlightBox>
Application Requirements are not optional guidance. They have the same authority as Disclosure Requirements and must be applied when relevant to a given disclosure.
</HighlightBox>

## ESRS 2 disclosures are mandatory regardless of materiality

A central rule in the ESRS architecture is this: irrespective of the outcome of its materiality assessment, the company shall always disclose the information required by ESRS 2 General Disclosures. This means all the Disclosure Requirements and datapoints specified in ESRS 2 must be reported. (ESRS 1, paragraph 29)

This is confirmed by EFRAG IG1: "Disclosure Requirements in ESRS 2 addressing cross-cutting matters are to be reported irrespective of the outcome of the materiality assessment." (EFRAG IG1, paragraph 4)

The reason for this rule is that ESRS 2 covers general information about the company's governance, strategy, and its overall process for identifying and managing material impacts, risks, and opportunities. This information is relevant to all users of the sustainability statement regardless of which specific topics a company has assessed as material.

<DeepDive title="What happens when a specific topical matter is assessed as not material?">
When the company concludes that a sustainability matter is material as a result of its materiality assessment, it shall disclose information according to the Disclosure Requirements in the corresponding topical and sector-specific ESRS. (ESRS 1, paragraph 30(a))

If the company concludes that climate change is not material and therefore omits all disclosure requirements in ESRS E1, it shall disclose a detailed explanation of the conclusions of its materiality assessment with regard to climate change, including a forward-looking analysis of the conditions that could lead the company to conclude that climate change is material in the future. (ESRS 1, paragraph 32)

For topics other than climate change, if the company concludes a topic is not material, it may briefly explain the conclusions of its materiality assessment for that topic. (ESRS 1, paragraph 32)
</DeepDive>

## Entity-specific disclosures

In addition to the disclosures laid down in the three categories of ESRS, when a company concludes that an impact, risk, or opportunity is not covered, or not covered with sufficient granularity by an ESRS but is material due to its specific facts and circumstances, it shall provide additional entity-specific disclosures to enable users to understand the company's sustainability-related impacts, risks, or opportunities. (ESRS 1, paragraph 11)
