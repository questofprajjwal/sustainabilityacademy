## What Is Step A?

Step A is the first step in the materiality assessment process. Its purpose is to build a clear picture of the undertaking's context before attempting to identify or assess any impacts, risks, or opportunities.

EFRAG IG1 paragraph 68 describes it this way: "In this step, the undertaking develops an overview of its activities and business relationships, the context in which these take place and an understanding of its key affected stakeholders. This overview provides key inputs to identify the undertaking's IROs."

Step A has three components:

1. Activities and business relationships
2. Other contextual information
3. Understanding of affected stakeholders

<AnalogyBox>
Step A is like a doctor taking a patient history before making a diagnosis. Before a doctor can identify what is wrong or recommend treatment, they need to understand who the patient is, what their daily life looks like, where they live and work, and what conditions run in their background. Without that context, any diagnosis risks missing the real causes. In the same way, before an undertaking can identify which sustainability matters are relevant to it, it must first understand its own activities, geography, relationships, and the people its operations affect.
</AnalogyBox>

## Activities and Business Relationships

EFRAG IG1 paragraph 69 explains that activities and business relationships are approached through:

- The analysis of the undertaking's business plan, strategy, financial statements and, when applicable, other information provided to investors.
- The undertaking's activities, products and services, and the geographic locations of these activities.
- The mapping of the undertaking's business relationships and upstream and/or downstream value chain, including the type and nature of those relationships.

Understanding the value chain is important because impacts, risks, and opportunities do not only arise from the undertaking's own operations. They also arise through the upstream and downstream activities connected to the undertaking through its direct and indirect business relationships. ESRS 1 paragraph 43 states that impacts include those connected with the undertaking's own operations and its upstream and downstream value chain, including through its products and services and through its business relationships.

## Other Contextual Information

Beyond the business model itself, EFRAG IG1 paragraph 70 identifies other factors that can help identify particular sources of IROs:

- The analysis of the undertaking's relevant legal and regulatory landscape.
- The analysis of published documentation, such as media reports, analysis of peers, existing sector-specific benchmarks, and other publications on general sustainability trends and scientific articles.

The sectors the undertaking operates in and the geographies it works across are particularly important. Different sectors are associated with different sustainability risks and impacts. Different geographies carry different regulatory environments, ecosystem conditions, and social contexts that shape which sustainability matters are relevant.

## Understanding of Affected Stakeholders

EFRAG IG1 paragraph 71 explains that this component is aimed at understanding which stakeholders are, or are likely to be, affected by the undertaking's own operations and its upstream and downstream value chain, including their views and interests. This understanding helps the undertaking identify its key affected stakeholders.

To develop this understanding, two activities are helpful:

- An analysis of the existing stakeholder engagement initiatives, such as via communication, investor relations, business management, sales and procurement teams.
- A mapping of affected stakeholders across the undertaking's activities and business relationships.

## Using ESRS 1 AR16 as a Starting Point

A key tool available to the undertaking in Step A is the list of sustainability matters set out in ESRS 1 Application Requirement 16 (AR16). This list covers environmental, social and human rights, and governance sustainability matters, categorised by topics, subtopics, and sub-subtopics.

ESRS 1 paragraph 27 states that "the Application Requirements in Appendix A of this Standard include a list of sustainability matters covered in topical ESRS, categorised by topics, sub-topics and sub-sub-topics, to support the materiality assessment."

EFRAG IG1 paragraph 74 confirms that the undertaking should use this list to support the process and to ensure completeness. The list acts as a comprehensive prompt: it covers the full range of environmental, social, and governance topics that could potentially be relevant, so the undertaking does not inadvertently overlook an entire topic area.

The three levels in the list are (EFRAG IG1 paragraph 42):

- **Topic**: for example, "Own workforce" or "Water and marine resources"
- **Subtopic**: for example, "Working conditions" or "Water"
- **Sub-subtopic**: for example, "Health and safety" or "Water consumption"

EFRAG IG1 paragraph 43 notes that "the three levels of granularity (i.e., topics, subtopics and sub-subtopics) are collectively called 'sustainability matters'."

<HighlightBox>
The AR16 list is a starting point, not a final answer. The undertaking also needs to consider entity-specific sustainability matters that may not be captured in that list, particularly sector-specific sustainability matters that are relevant to its particular business model, geographies, and value chain relationships (EFRAG IG1 paragraph 74).
</HighlightBox>

## Using Existing Internal Processes

The undertaking does not need to start from scratch. EFRAG IG1 paragraph 77 explains that the process of identifying potential matters may start with the screening of the AR16 list and then be complemented with additional entity-specific matters. These may follow from either internal processes, for example due diligence processes, risk management, or grievance mechanisms, or from external sources such as stakeholder engagement.

EFRAG IG1 paragraph 78 notes an alternative approach: the undertaking could start from the matters already identified through existing processes, such as GRI reporting or internal processes such as due diligence and risk management, and then compare those matters against the AR16 list for completeness. The two approaches can also be combined.

Existing enterprise risk management processes, sustainability strategies, and due diligence outcomes are therefore all relevant inputs to Step A. They may already contain information about which sustainability matters the undertaking has previously identified as potentially significant.

## What Step A Produces

The output of Step A is not a list of material matters. That determination comes later in Step C. The output of Step A is an informed picture of the undertaking's context: its business model, strategy, value chain, sectors, and geographies, and an initial understanding of the stakeholders its activities affect. This context then shapes which sustainability matters the undertaking considers in Step B when it identifies the universe of actual and potential IROs.
