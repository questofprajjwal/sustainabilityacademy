A company's sustainability impacts do not stop at its own factory gates or office doors. Most significant impacts, risks, and opportunities connected to a company are found throughout the chain of activities that produce and distribute its products and services. ESRS 1 Chapter 5 establishes how the sustainability statement must address this broader scope.

## What Upstream and Downstream Value Chain Means

The value chain includes all the activities and relationships that are involved in creating a company's products or services and in delivering them to customers and end-users.

The **upstream value chain** covers the activities and business relationships of suppliers, raw material producers, and other parties that provide inputs to the company. For example, a food company's upstream value chain includes its agricultural suppliers, packaging manufacturers, and logistics providers.

The **downstream value chain** covers the activities connected to the distribution, sale, use, and end-of-life treatment of the company's products or services. For a car manufacturer, the downstream value chain includes dealerships, car users, and end-of-life vehicle processors.

The term **business relationships** in ESRS is broader than direct contractual partners. It includes actors across the entire upstream and downstream value chain, including beyond the first tier (ESRS 1 paragraph 43).

## The Reporting Undertaking and Scope Alignment

The **reporting undertaking** is the legal entity or group that is preparing the sustainability statement. ESRS 1 paragraph 62 states that the sustainability statement shall be for the same reporting undertaking as the financial statements. For example, if the reporting undertaking is a parent company required to prepare consolidated financial statements, the sustainability statement covers the entire consolidated group.

This alignment between financial statements and the sustainability statement means:

- The sustainability statement covers the same entities as the consolidated group used for financial reporting.
- The reporting boundary for sustainability is set by the same consolidation scope used to prepare financial statements.
- Sustainability reporting that deviates from this scope requires specific justification.

## When Value Chain Information Must Be Included

The sustainability statement is extended to include value chain information when material impacts, risks, and opportunities are connected to the company through its direct and indirect business relationships in the upstream or downstream value chain (ESRS 1 paragraph 63).

This does not mean the company must report on every actor in the value chain. ESRS 1 paragraph 64 states clearly that paragraph 63 does not require information on each and every actor in the value chain, but only the inclusion of material upstream and downstream value chain information. The information shall be extended to include value chain information only in relation to the parts of the value chain for which the matter is material.

The company shall include material value chain information when this is necessary to:

- allow users of sustainability statements to understand the company's material impacts, risks, and opportunities; and
- produce a set of information that meets the qualitative characteristics of information set out in Appendix B of ESRS 1 (ESRS 1 paragraph 65).

When determining at which level within its own operations and its upstream and downstream value chain a material sustainability matter arises, the company shall use its assessment of impacts, risks, and opportunities following the double materiality principle (ESRS 1 paragraph 66).

<HighlightBox>
Value chain information is required only where a sustainability matter is material in that part of the value chain. It is not required for every supplier or customer, nor for every sustainability topic. The scope is driven by the outcome of the materiality assessment.
</HighlightBox>

## What Is Required "When Material" vs. What Is Always Required

Some disclosures under ESRS 2 are required regardless of the outcome of the materiality assessment. These include all the Disclosure Requirements in ESRS 2 General Disclosures, such as governance disclosures and the description of the materiality assessment process itself (ESRS 1 paragraph 29).

By contrast, topical disclosures, including value chain information related to specific topics, are only required when the relevant sustainability matter has been assessed as material. If, for example, water use in the supply chain is not a material topic for a company, it is not required to collect and report value chain data on supplier water consumption.

## Handling Cases Where Value Chain Data Is Not Available

The ability of a company to obtain value chain information may vary, depending on factors such as its contractual arrangements, the level of control it exercises over operations outside its consolidation scope, and its buying power (ESRS 1 paragraph 68).

Where the company cannot collect value chain information after making reasonable efforts to do so, ESRS 1 paragraph 69 requires it to estimate the information to be reported about its upstream and downstream value chain. It should do this by using all reasonable and supportable information, such as sector-average data and other proxies.

<HighlightBox>
When value chain data is unavailable despite reasonable effort, estimation using sector-average data and proxies is permitted under ESRS 1 paragraph 69. The estimate must still meet the qualitative characteristics of information. Crucially, the use of such estimates must not result in information that lacks the specificity and context necessary to interpret the disclosures (ESRS 1 paragraph 72).
</HighlightBox>

### Policies, Actions, and Targets vs. Metrics

The treatment of estimation differs depending on the type of disclosure involved. For policies, actions, and targets, the company's sustainability statement shall include upstream and downstream value chain information to the extent that those policies, actions, and targets involve actors in the value chain.

For metrics, however, sector-average proxies are often available, particularly for environmental matters. ESRS 1 paragraph 71 notes that in many cases, especially for environmental metrics, proxies allow the company to comply with reporting requirements without collecting data from every actor in its upstream and downstream value chain. A common example is using sector-average emission factors when calculating Scope 3 GHG emissions, rather than collecting primary activity data from each individual supplier.

## Challenges for SMEs in the Value Chain

Obtaining value chain information is also challenging in the case of small and medium-sized enterprises (SMEs) and other upstream or downstream value chain entities that are not themselves subject to ESRS reporting requirements under Articles 19a and 29a of Directive 2013/34/EU (ESRS 1 paragraph 70; ESRS 2 BP-2 Disclosures in relation to specific circumstances).

SMEs in the supply chain are unlikely to have the same sustainability data collection systems as large listed companies. This creates a practical tension: the reporting company needs value chain data, but its smaller suppliers may not be able to provide it in the required format or at the required level of detail.

<ExampleBox>
**Example: Dealing with missing supplier data**

A large apparel company must report on labour conditions in its upstream value chain as a material topic. Its tier-one suppliers are large factories with established reporting systems. However, its tier-two suppliers, which produce raw materials such as cotton, are predominantly small family farms and cooperatives in lower-income countries with limited data infrastructure.

The company has made reasonable efforts to collect primary data from these tier-two suppliers but has been unable to obtain sufficient coverage. Following ESRS 1 paragraph 69, it uses sector-average data from recognised industry sources and international databases to estimate the number of workers potentially exposed to labour rights risks in that part of the value chain. It discloses the estimation approach, the sources used, and the limitations of the estimate in its sustainability statement.
</ExampleBox>

## The Transitional Provision on Value Chain

ESRS 1 Chapter 10.2 contains a transitional provision specifically related to the value chain chapter. This provision recognises that collecting value chain information is one of the most demanding aspects of ESRS compliance, particularly in the first years of application.

The transitional provision allows companies, during the first years of applying the ESRS, to limit the value chain information reported in cases where it is not yet possible to collect the required information despite reasonable efforts. Companies making use of this provision must disclose that they have done so and explain the steps being taken to collect the information in future reporting periods.

This transitional relief is intended to give companies time to build the data collection systems and supplier engagement processes needed to meet the full value chain information requirements, while still providing users with transparency about the current limitations of the reported data.
