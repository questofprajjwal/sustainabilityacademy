<h3 class="text-xl font-bold mt-6 mb-3">The Climate Context: Why 1.5C Matters</h3>

<p class="mb-3">The Paris Agreement, adopted in 2015, committed participating nations to "holding the increase in global average temperature to well below 2 degrees Celsius above pre-industrial levels and pursuing efforts to limit the temperature increase to 1.5 degrees Celsius above pre-industrial levels." That 1.5C figure is not an arbitrary round number. It represents the threshold below which scientists believe the most catastrophic and irreversible climate impacts can be avoided.</p>

<p class="mb-3">The Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change's <strong>Special Report on Global Warming of 1.5C</strong> (SR15), published in 2018, made the stakes concrete. The report found that even the difference between 1.5C and 2C of warming involves substantially worse outcomes for human health, food security, water availability, biodiversity, and the frequency of extreme weather events. The risks associated with exceeding 1.5C are far higher than previously acknowledged. SR15 also found that to limit warming to 1.5C with no or limited overshoot, global net CO₂ emissions must reach zero around 2050, accompanied by rapid reductions in non-CO₂ greenhouse gases.</p>

<p class="mb-4">More recently, the IPCC's <strong>Sixth Assessment Report</strong> (AR6, 2021) confirmed that climate change is already affecting every region on Earth, with its impacts increasingly visible in extreme weather events, worsened droughts, and heightened risk of forest fires. The window for meaningful action is narrow and closing.</p>

<AnalogyBox>
  <p class="font-semibold text-blue-800">Think of the carbon budget like a fuel tank</p>
  <p class="mt-2 text-blue-900">The atmosphere can absorb only a finite quantity of CO₂ before temperatures exceed 1.5C. Scientists have quantified this as a "carbon budget" -- roughly 500 gigatonnes of CO₂ remaining from 2020 onward for a 50% chance of staying within 1.5C. Every tonne emitted draws down that budget. Companies that set science-based targets are effectively committing to use only their fair share of what remains.</p>
</AnalogyBox>

<h3 class="text-xl font-bold mt-6 mb-3">The Corporate Climate Action Problem</h3>

<p class="mb-3">Against this backdrop, the number of businesses committing to reach net-zero emissions has grown rapidly. Corporate climate pledges have proliferated, especially since the Paris Agreement and again following COP26 in Glasgow in 2021. On the surface, this looks like progress. But there is a serious problem: not all net-zero targets are equal.</p>

<p class="mb-3">Without adhering to a common definition, net-zero targets can be inconsistent, vague, or disconnected from any underlying scientific basis. A company could claim to be "net zero by 2050" while planning emissions reductions of only 20%, compensating the rest with cheap and poorly verified carbon offsets. Another company might use a different base year, a different scope boundary, or a different definition of what counts as an emission reduction. The collective climate impact of hundreds of inconsistent pledges is strongly limited.</p>

<p class="mb-4">Business leaders need a robust, science-based framework for setting net-zero targets. Without it, they risk continuing to invest in business models that are inconsistent with the goals of the Paris Agreement -- and risk reputational damage when those pledges are scrutinized. The Science Based Targets initiative (SBTi) was developed precisely to fill this gap.</p>

<h3 class="text-xl font-bold mt-6 mb-3">What Is a Science-Based Target?</h3>

<p class="mb-3">A <strong>science-based target</strong> is a greenhouse gas (GHG) emissions reduction target that is in line with the level of decarbonization required by climate science to limit global warming to 1.5C above pre-industrial levels. The critical word is "in line with." A science-based target is not simply a commitment to reduce emissions -- it is a commitment calibrated to what the global carbon budget actually permits.</p>

<p class="mb-3">The SBTi defines science-based targets more precisely as targets covering the reduction of greenhouse gas emissions in line with what is needed to keep global heating below catastrophic levels and reach net-zero by 2050 at the latest. Under the SBTi Corporate Net-Zero Standard, reaching net-zero at the corporate level means two things:</p>

<ul class="list-disc ml-6 space-y-2 mb-4">
  <li>Reducing scope 1, 2, and 3 emissions to zero or a residual level consistent with reaching net-zero emissions at the global or sector level in eligible 1.5C-aligned pathways; and</li>
  <li>Permanently neutralizing any residual emissions at the net-zero target year and any GHG emissions released into the atmosphere thereafter.</li>
</ul>

<p class="mb-4">In plain terms: first, cut as deeply as science requires; then, permanently remove whatever remains. Offsetting cannot substitute for reduction, and carbon credits cannot be used to count as emission reductions toward meeting near-term or long-term science-based targets.</p>

<HighlightBox>
  <p class="font-semibold text-green-800">What makes a target "science-based"</p>
  <p class="mt-2 text-green-900">A target is science-based when it is derived from a mitigation pathway consistent with the carbon budgets that science says are necessary to limit warming to 1.5C. The reduction rate is not chosen by the company -- it is determined by the pathway. Companies commit to meet that rate, not to define it themselves.</p>
</HighlightBox>

<h3 class="text-xl font-bold mt-6 mb-3">What Science-Based Targets Are NOT</h3>

<p class="mb-3">Understanding what science-based targets are requires understanding what they are not. Several common corporate practices do not constitute science-based targets, and it is important not to conflate them.</p>

<p class="mb-3"><strong>They are not voluntary pledges without a scientific basis.</strong> A company announcing "we will be carbon neutral by 2040" has made a pledge. It is not a science-based target unless the ambition -- the specific percentage reduction, the scope coverage, the base year -- has been derived from a 1.5C-aligned mitigation pathway and independently validated.</p>

<p class="mb-3"><strong>They are not achieved through carbon offsets.</strong> Carbon credits -- the purchase of carbon offset certificates -- can only be considered as an option for neutralizing residual emissions after the long-term science-based target has been achieved. They do not count toward the emission reductions required to meet near-term or long-term SBTs. A company that claims to have "offset" its way to net zero has not met the SBTi definition.</p>

<p class="mb-3"><strong>They are not renewable energy claims alone.</strong> Procuring 100% renewable electricity is valuable and encouraged, but it does not by itself constitute a complete science-based target. It addresses only scope 2 electricity emissions and must be part of a broader target covering scope 1, 2, and 3 emissions.</p>

<p class="mb-4"><strong>They are not "avoided emissions" claims.</strong> A company that produces electric vehicles might argue it "avoids" millions of tonnes of CO₂ by displacing petrol vehicles. Avoided emissions occur outside a company's own value chain and are not a reduction of that company's inventory. They do not count toward SBTi targets.</p>

<ExampleBox>
  <p class="font-semibold text-amber-800">Example: The difference between a pledge and an SBT</p>
  <p class="mt-2 text-amber-900">Company A announces: "We will be net zero by 2050." It plans to reduce direct emissions by 30% and offset the remainder with forestry credits. This is a pledge -- not a science-based target.<br /><br />
  Company B submits targets to the SBTi. It commits to reduce absolute scope 1 and 2 GHG emissions 50% by 2030 from a 2019 base year (1.5C-aligned, derived from the cross-sector pathway at 4.2% linear annual reduction), and to reduce scope 3 emissions 40% over the same period (well-below 2C aligned). The SBTi validates these targets against the criteria. Company B now holds science-based targets.</p>
</ExampleBox>

<h3 class="text-xl font-bold mt-6 mb-3">Who Sets Science-Based Targets?</h3>

<p class="mb-3">The SBTi Corporate Net-Zero Standard is designed for companies and financial institutions worldwide. The primary audience is companies -- specifically, those that wish to commit to setting and submitting science-based net-zero targets for validation. The standard covers most corporate sectors, with specific sector guidance available for power, buildings, transport, forest-land-and-agriculture (FLAG), cement, steel, and others.</p>

<p class="mb-3"><strong>Large companies</strong> follow the full validation route, submitting targets through the SBTi's Validation Portal and paying a validation fee. Their targets are assessed against the full criteria set out in the Corporate Net-Zero Standard and the Corporate Near-Term Criteria.</p>

<p class="mb-3"><strong>Small and medium-sized enterprises (SMEs)</strong> have access to a streamlined route -- the SME Streamlined Target Validation Route -- which allows them to bypass the initial commitment letter step and immediately select from predefined target options. This route is designed to reduce the burden for smaller organizations while maintaining scientific alignment.</p>

<p class="mb-3"><strong>Financial institutions</strong> use a separate standard -- the SBTi Financial Institutions Net-Zero Standard -- rather than the Corporate Net-Zero Standard covered in this course. Their targets address financed emissions (the GHG impact of their lending and investment portfolios).</p>

<p class="mb-4">It is worth noting that the SBTi does not currently assess targets for cities, local governments, or non-profit organizations. These actors can use climate-science-based target-setting methods through the Science Based Targets Network (SBTN) guidance.</p>

<h3 class="text-xl font-bold mt-6 mb-3">The Scale of Adoption</h3>

<p class="mb-3">The SBTi was founded in 2015 through a partnership between CDP, the United Nations Global Compact, the We Mean Business Coalition, the World Resources Institute (WRI), and the World Wide Fund for Nature (WWF). The SBTi is incorporated as a UK charity, with a subsidiary -- SBTi Services Limited -- that hosts the target validation services.</p>

<p class="mb-3">Since its founding, the pace of corporate adoption has been remarkable. By the mid-2020s, more than 10,000 companies across the world had either committed to or already had approved science-based targets through the SBTi. These companies collectively represent a significant proportion of global market capitalization and span every major sector and geography.</p>

<p class="mb-4">The Corporate Net-Zero Standard itself was first published on 28 October 2021, following an extensive development process that included two public consultations, a road-testing process with 84 companies, and guidance from a Net-Zero Expert Advisory Group (EAG) of 42 experts from academia, civil society, and business. Version 1.3 was published in September 2025, incorporating corrections and clarifications based on accumulated experience with target validation.</p>

<DeepDive title="Why near-term emissions reductions cannot wait">
  <p class="mb-3">One of the most important scientific insights underlying science-based targets is that near-term emissions reductions are not interchangeable with long-term ones. Every tonne of CO₂ emitted before 2030 draws down the remaining carbon budget that would otherwise be available to the global economy in the 2030s and 2040s. Near-term targets galvanize the action required for significant emissions reductions to be achieved by around 2030 -- a critical milestone for not exceeding the global emissions budget.</p>
  <p>A company that plans to delay action until 2040 and then rapidly decarbonize would still have emitted far more cumulative CO₂ than one that began reducing emissions today, even if both arrived at zero by 2050. This is why the SBTi requires both a near-term target (5-10 years) and a long-term target (by 2050 at latest) -- the near-term target ensures the emission reduction trajectory is consistent with the carbon budget from the start.</p>
</DeepDive>
