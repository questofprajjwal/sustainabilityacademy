Having an SBTi-validated target is not the end of the journey -- it is a commitment that comes with structured obligations to report, review, and where necessary recalculate. The SBTi criteria that govern ongoing target maintenance are among the least understood but most important: they determine whether a company's target remains credible and current as its business evolves.

## Annual Reporting: Criterion C25

**Criterion C25** establishes the annual reporting obligation. Every company with approved SBTi targets must publicly disclose, on an annual basis:

1. An updated GHG inventory covering at minimum the scopes included in the approved targets
2. Progress against each approved near-term target and, where applicable, the net-zero target

The disclosure must be made publicly available. It cannot sit only in internal reports or be shared exclusively with the SBTi.

<HighlightBox>
"Publicly available" under C25 means accessible to external stakeholders without requiring a login or a formal data access request. A PDF on the company's website, a CDP response, or an entry in an annual report all qualify. A target tracker spreadsheet accessible only to sustainability team members does not.
</HighlightBox>

Annual reporting serves several functions simultaneously:

- It creates **accountability** by making trajectory visible to investors, customers, employees, and civil society
- It enables **early identification of deviations** so that corrective action can be taken before a target becomes unachievable
- It generates **historical data** that allows the five-year review process to assess genuine progress rather than relying on projections

### Recommended Disclosure Channels (R12)

Recommendation R12 identifies preferred channels for annual disclosure:
- **CDP:** Provides structured, auditable disclosures in a format used by thousands of institutional investors and procurement teams
- **Annual Report or Integrated Report:** Reaches financial stakeholders and is typically subject to external assurance
- **Sustainability or ESG Report:** Allows narrative context alongside quantitative data

Most companies with SBTi targets use CDP as their primary disclosure vehicle, since CDP questionnaires include dedicated SBTi fields and CDP responses are publicly searchable.

## Understanding Recalculation: Criterion C27

A base year is fixed at the time of target validation -- but the emissions landscape of a company changes over time. Acquisitions, disposals, changes in methodology, and shifts in Scope 3 coverage can all affect the base year emissions in ways that would make progress comparisons unfair.

**Criterion C27** establishes that when a defined set of triggering events occurs and the cumulative impact on base year emissions exceeds **5%** of total base year emissions, the company must recalculate the base year.

### The Six Recalculation Triggers

1. **Scope 3 exceeds the 40% threshold:** If, after target validation, a company discovers (through an updated Scope 3 screening) that Scope 3 represents more than 40% of its total footprint -- and it has not yet set a Scope 3 target -- this triggers both the requirement to set a Scope 3 target and potentially a recalculation of the Scope 3 base year.

2. **Consolidation approach change:** Switching from one GHG Protocol consolidation approach to another (e.g., from financial control to operational control) changes which entities are included and at what ownership fraction. This must trigger a full recalculation.

3. **Exclusion changes:** If a company adds or removes emission sources from its inventory boundary (e.g., previously excluded a refrigeration category that is now included), the base year must be recalculated to include the category consistently.

4. **Structural changes (mergers, acquisitions, disposals):** A company that acquires a new subsidiary brings new emissions into its footprint. A company that divests a business removes emissions. Either way, base year emissions must be adjusted to ensure progress is measured against a like-for-like starting point.

5. **Data or methodology changes:** If a company switches to a more accurate emission factor database, adopts a more precise measurement approach, or corrects a material error discovered in historical data, the revised methodology should be applied retroactively to the base year.

6. **Other significant changes:** A catch-all category for changes that substantively affect the base year inventory beyond the specific triggers above.

<ExampleBox>
**Scenario: Acquisition triggers recalculation**

A chemical company was validated in 2022 with Scope 1+2 base year emissions of 400,000 tCO2e. In 2024, it acquires a specialty coatings manufacturer with annual Scope 1+2 emissions of 25,000 tCO2e.

Step 1 -- Calculate the acquisition's share of base year emissions:
25,000 / 400,000 = 6.25%

Step 2 -- Compare to threshold:
6.25% > 5%, so recalculation is mandatory.

Step 3 -- Update base year:
The company must add the acquired entity's 2022 emissions to the base year (not just current year), restating the base year emissions as 425,000 tCO2e (or as closely as the historical data allows).

The 2030 target -- previously expressed as a reduction from 400,000 tCO2e -- must now be restated as a reduction from 425,000 tCO2e. The required percentage reduction does not change, but the absolute tonnage target does.
</ExampleBox>

### The 5% Significance Threshold

The 5% threshold is cumulative -- not per-event. If a company makes three small acquisitions in a year, each representing 2% of base year emissions, the cumulative effect is 6%, which exceeds the threshold. Companies should track cumulative structural changes since the last base year assessment, not just evaluate each change in isolation.

<AnalogyBox>
Think of the 5% threshold like a ship's ballast. Small adjustments to cargo distribution (small structural changes) do not require the ship to return to port for recalibration. But if enough small changes accumulate -- or one large change occurs -- the ship's centre of gravity has shifted enough that recalibration is necessary to ensure stable navigation.
</AnalogyBox>

## The Mandatory Five-Year Review: Criterion C26

Beyond annual reporting and event-driven recalculation, the SBTi requires all companies to undergo a formal **five-year review** of their targets. The review is mandatory -- not optional -- and must be documented and disclosed.

**Criterion C26** specifies:
- All companies must review their approved targets at least every five years
- Companies whose targets were first approved in 2020 or earlier were required to complete their first review by the end of 2025
- The review must assess whether the targets remain compliant, whether the base year requires recalculation, and whether scope coverage is still complete

### What Does a Five-Year Review Actually Involve?

The five-year review is not a simple administrative sign-off. It requires companies to:

1. **Update the GHG inventory** to cover all material scopes and categories using the latest methodologies and emission factors
2. **Assess whether the approved target ambition remains consistent** with the SBTi's current version of its criteria (criteria are periodically updated; a target approved under an older version may not meet current requirements)
3. **Review Scope 3 materiality** -- have new categories become material that were not included in the original target?
4. **Check for recalculation triggers** -- do structural changes since validation require a base year recalculation?
5. **Consider whether to strengthen the target** -- if the company is significantly ahead of trajectory, does the target still represent genuine ambition?

<HighlightBox>
The five-year review creates a scheduled mechanism for targets to evolve alongside business change. Without it, a company that grew its operations significantly since validation could continue to report progress against an outdated target that understates its real footprint.
</HighlightBox>

## Target Validity and the Announcement Window: C28

Criterion C28 addresses the gap between when a target is approved by the SBTi and when it is publicly visible to stakeholders. It states that companies must publicly announce their approved targets within **6 months** of receiving approval from the SBTi.

This prevents a scenario where a company obtains a validated target but chooses not to announce it publicly -- perhaps because the targets are less ambitious than the company had initially implied to investors, or because the announcement is being held for a particular investor relations moment.

The 6-month window is a hard deadline. A company that misses it is technically in breach of criterion C28 and could have its target delisted from the SBTi registry.

### Annual Validity Checks (R13)

Recommendation R13 encourages companies to conduct an annual internal check of whether their approved target projections remain on track. This means:
- Comparing the current year's actual emissions to the implied trajectory
- Identifying whether the company is ahead, on track, or behind its annual reduction milestone
- Considering whether any planned changes (new facilities, energy contracts, product line changes) will materially affect future-year emissions

While R13 is a recommendation rather than a mandatory criterion, the information it generates is directly relevant to the C25 annual disclosure and the C26 five-year review. Companies that conduct rigorous annual validity checks are typically better prepared for both.

## Practical Compliance Checklist

The following checklist summarises the key ongoing obligations for companies with approved SBTi targets:

<ExampleBox>
**Annual obligations:**
- Update GHG inventory for the most recent reporting year (Scope 1, 2, and any Scope 3 categories in targets)
- Report actual emissions progress against each approved target
- Publish updated inventory and progress via CDP, annual report, or sustainability report (C25 + R12)
- Conduct internal validity check against target trajectory (R13)
- Assess whether any recalculation triggers have occurred during the year (C27)

**Event-driven obligations:**
- If a structural change (M&A, disposal, consolidation change) results in cumulative impact >5% of base year emissions, perform and disclose base year recalculation (C27)
- If newly published sector guidance applies to the company, align targets within 6 months (C24)
- If the Scope 3 footprint screening reveals material categories not previously included, assess whether Scope 3 target is still adequate

**Five-yearly obligations:**
- Conduct full five-year review -- update inventory, check ambition adequacy, review scope coverage, document and disclose findings (C26)
- Resubmit for validation if substantive target changes are made

**On approval:**
- Announce approved targets publicly within 6 months of SBTi approval (C28)
</ExampleBox>

<HighlightBox>
Companies that treat SBTi compliance as a one-time validation exercise rather than an ongoing programme of data quality, reporting, and governance will increasingly struggle as the five-year review cycle arrives and stakeholder scrutiny intensifies. Embedding target management into regular sustainability governance -- board oversight, quarterly data reviews, dedicated staff accountability -- is the most effective way to ensure ongoing compliance.
</HighlightBox>
