Not all "net-zero" claims are equal. A company can declare itself net-zero in a press release, purchase a bundle of forestry offsets, and announce the milestone with minimal change to its actual operations. The SBTi Corporate Net-Zero Standard was created precisely to prevent this. It provides a rigorous, science-aligned definition of corporate net-zero -- one that requires deep emissions reduction before any form of neutralisation is considered.

This lesson unpacks that definition, explains the four structural pillars that make up a compliant net-zero target, and clarifies the important distinction between the SBTi's conception of net-zero and the looser notion of "carbon neutrality" used elsewhere in the market.

## The SBTi Two-Part Definition of Corporate Net-Zero

The Corporate Net-Zero Standard defines reaching net-zero at the corporate level as requiring two things to happen -- and both are mandatory:

**Part 1 -- Reduce to a residual level.** The company must reduce its scope 1, scope 2, and scope 3 emissions to zero, or to a residual level consistent with reaching net-zero emissions at the global or sector level in eligible 1.5 degrees C-aligned pathways.

**Part 2 -- Permanently neutralise residual emissions.** Whatever emissions remain at the net-zero target year must be counterbalanced through the permanent removal and storage of carbon from the atmosphere. This neutralisation must be maintained for all GHG emissions released into the atmosphere thereafter.

<HighlightBox>
A company cannot claim to have reached net-zero until both conditions are met: (1) the long-term science-based target for all scopes has been achieved, and (2) residual emissions have been permanently neutralised. Meeting one condition without the other does not constitute corporate net-zero under the Standard.
</HighlightBox>

The sequence matters. Neutralisation is not a shortcut that can substitute for abatement. It is a final step that is only activated after a company has done the hard work of eliminating the overwhelming majority of its emissions through genuine operational change.

## What Is a "Residual Level"?

The phrase "residual level" is defined by reference to 1.5 degrees C-aligned global pathways, not by a company's own assessment of what it can feasibly eliminate. In practice, the cross-sector pathway used by the SBTi requires companies to reduce absolute scope 1 and 2 emissions by at least 90% from their base year by the long-term target date (no later than 2050). Most sector-specific pathways also converge on 90% or more absolute reduction.

This 90% threshold is not arbitrary. It reflects the aggregate level of decarbonisation needed across the economy to reach net-zero CO2 globally by 2050 while staying within the remaining carbon budget for a 50% likelihood of limiting warming to 1.5 degrees C.

<AnalogyBox>
Think of it like emptying a swimming pool. You cannot claim the pool is empty just because you have removed most of the water. The SBTi's framework says: drain at least 90% of the water through genuine effort, and only then may you neutralise the remaining puddle at the bottom with absorption materials. Claiming "net-zero" by throwing in absorbent material while the pool is still mostly full is not acceptable.
</AnalogyBox>

The residual emissions that cannot be eliminated -- typically from hard-to-abate industrial processes, agricultural sources, or legacy assets -- are the only emissions that neutralisation is designed to address.

## The Four Pillars of a Corporate Net-Zero Target

The Standard organises a corporate net-zero target into four key elements. Together, these constitute a complete commitment.

### Pillar 1 -- Near-Term Science-Based Target

A near-term SBT covers a 5-to-10-year horizon and specifies the rate at which the company must reduce its emissions in line with 1.5 degrees C pathways. These targets are essential because the next decade is the critical window for avoiding overshoot of the global carbon budget. Near-term emissions reductions are not interchangeable with long-term reductions -- cutting emissions in 2030 has a fundamentally larger impact on limiting warming than cutting the same quantity in 2045, because the cumulative budget is consumed continuously.

### Pillar 2 -- Long-Term Science-Based Target

A long-term SBT sets the overall level of decarbonisation required, showing the company how far it must reduce value chain emissions to align with net-zero at the global or sector level by 2050 or sooner. Unlike near-term targets, long-term targets are target-year independent: the required depth of reduction does not change based on when the company sets its target date. A company aiming for net-zero in 2040 must reach the same residual level as one aiming for 2050 -- it simply gets there earlier.

### Pillar 3 -- Neutralisation of Residual Emissions

At the point the long-term SBT is achieved, any remaining emissions must be permanently neutralised. The Standard specifies that only permanent carbon removal qualifies -- the physical removal and durable storage of CO2 from the atmosphere. Carbon avoidance credits (such as those from avoided deforestation or clean cooking projects) do not count toward neutralisation, because they do not remove existing CO2 from the atmosphere; they only prevent new emissions from being added. This is a hard boundary in the Standard, not a matter of interpretation.

### Pillar 4 -- Beyond Value Chain Mitigation (BVCM)

Companies are strongly encouraged -- though not required -- to go further than their science-based abatement targets by taking action or making investments outside their own value chain. This is known as beyond value chain mitigation. BVCM includes activities that avoid or reduce GHG emissions elsewhere in the economy, and activities that remove and store GHGs from the atmosphere. BVCM is treated as a contribution to broader societal climate goals, not as a substitute for internal abatement.

<HighlightBox>
The four pillars work in a specific sequence. Near-term SBTs drive action this decade. Long-term SBTs define the destination. Neutralisation addresses what cannot be eliminated. BVCM expands the company's positive impact beyond its own footprint. None of the pillars is optional for a complete net-zero claim -- except that BVCM is strongly recommended rather than formally required.
</HighlightBox>

## Net-Zero vs Carbon Neutral: A Critical Distinction

"Carbon neutral" and "net-zero" are often used interchangeably in corporate communications, but they represent very different levels of ambition under the SBTi framework.

**Carbon neutrality** is typically achieved by purchasing carbon credits -- most often avoidance-based credits from projects that prevent deforestation, methane capture, or similar -- to offset a company's reported emissions. No abatement is required. A company can remain at its current emissions level, buy enough credits, and claim to be carbon neutral. This approach may have value at the margin, but it does not contribute to the systemic transformation required to limit warming.

**Net-zero under the SBTi Standard** requires:
- Reducing emissions to a residual level through genuine operational change (at least 90% reduction)
- Permanently neutralising only the residual remainder using carbon removal -- not avoidance credits
- Demonstrating this through science-aligned near-term and long-term targets with third-party validation

<ExampleBox>
**Example: Why the distinction matters in practice**

Company A buys certified forest-protection credits equivalent to 100% of its current emissions and declares itself carbon neutral. No operational change occurs. Its Scope 1 and 2 emissions continue at the same level.

Company B sets an SBTi-validated near-term target to reduce Scope 1 and 2 emissions by 42% by 2030 and a long-term net-zero target requiring 90%+ reduction by 2045. It invests in electrification, renewable energy procurement, and supply chain transformation. At 2045, it neutralises its residual 8% through direct air capture contracts.

Both companies use the word "net-zero" in public materials. Only Company B meets the SBTi definition. The difference is not semantic -- it is the difference between a company that is actively decarbonising and one that is paying to maintain the status quo.
</ExampleBox>

## Why Residual Emissions Must Be Permanently Removed

The Standard's insistence on permanent removal -- rather than avoidance -- for neutralisation is grounded in climate physics. To reach net-zero CO2 at the global level, the flow of CO2 into the atmosphere must equal the flow being durably removed. Avoidance credits do not remove CO2; they prevent future emissions from occurring. While avoidance has value for climate mitigation broadly, it cannot close the mass balance required for genuine net-zero.

Permanence also matters because the climate system responds to cumulative concentrations of CO2 over centuries. A tonne of CO2 removed and stored for 1,000 years provides a fundamentally different climate service than a tonne of CO2 "saved" by protecting a forest that could be cleared in 30 years. The Standard therefore requires that neutralisation uses carbon removal approaches with high durability -- ideally geological or mineralisation-based storage with century-scale permanence.

<DeepDive title="What counts as permanent carbon removal?">
The SBTi does not endorse specific removal technologies, but the principle of permanence points toward a small set of candidates. Geological storage of CO2 (as in direct air capture paired with geological sequestration) offers very high durability. Enhanced weathering and mineralisation are also promising. Biological approaches such as afforestation or soil carbon are considered lower durability because the stored carbon can be re-released through wildfire, disease, or land-use change.

As carbon removal markets mature, the SBTi expects companies to build portfolios of removal approaches that demonstrate permanence. Guidance on acceptable removal methods is expected to be updated as the market evolves and methodologies are validated. Companies making net-zero commitments should begin building relationships with high-integrity removal providers well in advance of their long-term target date.
</DeepDive>

## The Role of the Standard in a Credible Net-Zero Claim

The Corporate Net-Zero Standard provides the infrastructure for a credible claim. It sets minimum ambition thresholds, specifies which accounting methods are valid, defines what qualifies as neutralisation, and requires third-party validation by the SBTi. A company that follows this process and achieves validation can make a claim that is independently verifiable and directly comparable to other validated companies.

This matters not only for credibility with external stakeholders -- investors, regulators, customers -- but also for internal planning. A validated net-zero target forces the company to develop a credible decarbonisation roadmap with near-term milestones, identifies where the hard abatement challenges lie, and quantifies the residual emissions that will eventually require neutralisation. The discipline of target-setting under the Standard is itself a governance and strategy tool.

<HighlightBox>
Key takeaways from this lesson:

- SBTi net-zero requires reducing emissions to a residual level (typically 90%+) AND permanently neutralising what remains.
- The four pillars are: near-term SBT, long-term SBT, neutralisation, and BVCM.
- Carbon neutrality (offset-based) is not the same as SBTi net-zero.
- Only permanent carbon removal counts toward neutralisation -- avoidance credits do not.
- Near-term reductions are not interchangeable with long-term reductions. The next decade's action is irreplaceable for limiting cumulative warming.
</HighlightBox>
