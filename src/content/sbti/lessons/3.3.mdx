Setting a scientifically rigorous target and expressing it in language that is clear, complete, and compliant with SBTi requirements are two separate skills. A company that has done rigorous technical work can still undermine the credibility of its commitment through vague, incomplete, or misleading target language. This lesson covers how to formulate target statements that meet SBTi criteria, what the validation process involves, and the ongoing obligations that arise once a target is approved.

## The Four Components of a Compliant Target Statement

Every SBTi-compliant target statement must contain four elements:

1. **Base year** -- the reference year from which reductions are measured
2. **Target year** -- the year by which the reduction must be achieved
3. **Scope** -- which emission scopes (and where relevant, categories) the target covers
4. **Percentage reduction** -- the absolute or intensity-based reduction committed to

A statement missing any one of these components is incomplete. A company that states "we will cut our emissions significantly by 2030" has communicated something about ambition, but nothing about measurability or accountability.

<HighlightBox>
For near-term targets, the SBTi requires target statements to cover at minimum Scope 1 and Scope 2 as a combined or separate target, with Scope 3 required where it represents 40% or more of total footprint. For the overarching net-zero target, the statement must encompass all material scopes and explicitly link to a long-term (2050 or earlier) timeframe.
</HighlightBox>

## Formulating Near-Term Target Language

### Well-Formed Near-Term Target Examples

The following examples illustrate compliant near-term target language for different method choices.

<ExampleBox>
**Compliant near-term target (Scope 1+2, Method B.1, base year 2019):**

"Acme Manufacturing commits to reduce absolute Scope 1 and Scope 2 GHG emissions 46.2% by 2030, from a 2019 base year, in line with a 1.5 degree Celsius pathway."

Analysis: Contains base year (2019), target year (2030), scope (Scope 1 and Scope 2), and percentage reduction (46.2%). The phrase "in line with a 1.5 degree Celsius pathway" links the target to the applicable science, though the SBTi does not require this phrase to appear verbatim.
</ExampleBox>

<ExampleBox>
**Compliant near-term target (Scope 3, Method B.7 supplier engagement):**

"Acme Manufacturing commits that 67% of its suppliers by spend, covering purchased goods and services, will have science-based targets by 2027."

Analysis: Contains base activity (supplier spend), target year (2027), scope coverage (Scope 3, Category 1, via supplier engagement), and quantitative threshold (67%). The base year is implicit -- the commitment is measured against the state of the supplier base at the time of target validation.
</ExampleBox>

<ExampleBox>
**Non-compliant near-term target (common errors):**

"GlobalCo will reduce its carbon footprint by 2030."
-- Missing: scope definition, base year, percentage reduction, and target type.

"We aim to halve our emissions by 2035."
-- Missing: scope, base year, and confirmation the 50% reduction meets minimum SBTi ambition levels.

"Our emissions intensity will improve by 20% this decade."
-- Missing: specific base year, target year, scope, and the 20% is not sufficient for a well-below-2C intensity target (which requires minimum ~7% compound annual reduction).
</ExampleBox>

## Formulating Net-Zero Target Language

For the overarching net-zero target under the Corporate Net-Zero Standard, the statement must link near-term action to a long-term commitment to neutralise residual emissions.

<ExampleBox>
**Compliant net-zero target statement:**

"Acme Manufacturing commits to reach net-zero GHG emissions across the value chain by no later than 2050. This encompasses:
- A near-term commitment to reduce absolute Scope 1 and 2 emissions by 46.2% by 2030 from a 2019 base year
- A near-term commitment to reduce absolute Scope 3 emissions by 30% by 2030 from a 2019 base year
- A long-term commitment to reduce all Scope 1, 2, and 3 emissions by at least 90% by 2050 from a 2019 base year
- A commitment to neutralise any residual emissions (up to 10% of base year) with permanent carbon removals"

Analysis: This statement contains a long-term reduction target (90% by 2050), explicit scope coverage (Scope 1, 2, and 3), a base year (2019), explicit provision for neutralisation of residuals, and near-term milestones that align with the SBTi's near-term criteria.
</ExampleBox>

<ExampleBox>
**Non-compliant net-zero target statement:**

"We will be net-zero by 2050 by reducing our emissions and offsetting the rest."
-- Problems: "the rest" is undefined, no base year specified, no scope coverage stated, no clarity on whether offsets are permanent removals or avoided-emission credits, and no indication of how much reduction vs. how much offsetting.

"Our operations will be carbon neutral by 2040 through renewable energy and nature-based offsets."
-- Problems: "carbon neutral" and "net-zero" are not interchangeable under SBTi definitions; "operations" typically implies Scope 1 and 2 only (missing Scope 3); the target relies heavily on offsets rather than demonstrating first a 90%+ reduction before neutralisation.
</ExampleBox>

<HighlightBox>
A critical distinction in the net-zero standard: companies must achieve at least a **90% absolute reduction** across all scopes before neutralisation of residuals. The maximum residuals that may be neutralised are 10% of base year emissions. A company cannot set a net-zero target that relies on offsets to substitute for deep emission reductions.
</HighlightBox>

## The SBTi Validation Process

Once a company has formulated its target language and completed its technical target-setting work, it submits its targets for formal validation by the SBTi. The process proceeds in several stages.

### Step 1: Commit

Before submission, companies typically go through a public commitment step, signing the SBTi commitment letter. This signals publicly that the company intends to submit science-based targets within 24 months. It is a reputational commitment rather than a validated target.

### Step 2: Submit Target for Validation

The company submits its targets using the SBTi's target submission form (or sector-specific equivalent), along with:
- Base year emissions data
- Current year inventory data
- Target-setting workbooks or calculations
- Sector classification
- Evidence of GHG Protocol alignment

### Step 3: Assessment Against Criteria Indicators

SBTi assessors review the submission against the full set of criteria -- both the near-term criteria (currently version 5.3) and, where applicable, the Net-Zero Standard criteria. Each criterion has one or more **assessment indicators** that define what constitutes compliance.

The assessment is typically pass/fail at the criteria level, with assessors noting where additional evidence or revisions are required. Common reasons for returned submissions include:
- Base year emissions data that does not cover all required scopes
- Percentage reduction that does not meet minimum ambition thresholds
- Scope 3 screening that appears to underestimate the Scope 3 footprint
- Method selection that is inconsistent with the company's sector

### Step 4: Approval and Publication

If the submission passes assessment, the SBTi approves the targets and lists the company on its public target dashboard. The SBTi's website publicly records the company name, date of approval, target type, target year, and summary target description.

<HighlightBox>
Criterion C28 states that a company must publicly announce its approved targets within **6 months** of validation approval. Failing to make a public announcement is itself a criterion breach.
</HighlightBox>

The typical review timeline from submission to approval is approximately **10 to 16 weeks**, though this varies with submission volume and the complexity of the company's operations.

## Ongoing Reporting Requirements: C25

Validation is not the end of the journey -- it is the beginning of a reporting obligation.

**Criterion C25** requires companies to report annually on:
- Their GHG inventory (updated for the most recent reporting year)
- Progress against each approved target

This annual reporting must be made publicly available. The SBTi does not mandate a specific disclosure platform, but recommends that companies disclose through CDP (formerly the Carbon Disclosure Project), their annual report, or their corporate sustainability or responsibility report.

<HighlightBox>
The SBTi recommends (R12) that companies disclose through CDP as the primary mechanism for annual progress reporting. CDP's questionnaire includes dedicated fields for SBTi target status and progress, making it the most visible and verifiable disclosure platform.
</HighlightBox>

Annual reporting creates accountability for progress. If a company is falling behind its trajectory -- for example, its emissions are not declining at the rate implied by the target -- stakeholders will see this in the public disclosure, and the company will need to explain what actions it is taking to get back on track.

## The Mandatory Five-Year Review: C26

All companies with approved targets must complete a **mandatory five-year review**. This is not merely a check-in -- it is a requirement to formally reassess, and where necessary update, the approved targets.

**Criterion C26** requires that:
- All targets be reviewed at least every five years
- Companies approved in 2020 or earlier were required to complete their first review by 2025
- The review must consider whether base year recalculation is required and whether scope coverage remains complete

The five-year review is designed to ensure targets remain current as emissions profiles, business models, and available data evolve. A company that acquired two large businesses since its initial target was set, or one that has substantially expanded its Scope 3 footprint through new product lines, may find that its original targets no longer reflect its full impact.

<ExampleBox>
**Scenario: What triggers a five-year review revision?**

A consumer goods company had its near-term targets validated in 2021. In 2024, a Scope 3 screening update revealed that its value chain emissions had grown significantly due to a new product category launched in 2022. The Scope 3 now constitutes 48% of the total footprint, but the original 2021 target only included Scope 3 Categories 1 and 11 (together, 35% of the total at that time).

At the 2026 five-year review, the company must update its Scope 3 boundary to reflect the current footprint and set new or revised targets that adequately cover the categories now material to its operations.
</ExampleBox>

## The Six-Month Public Announcement Window: C28

Once the SBTi has communicated approval to a company, the company has six months to make its targets public. This requirement (C28) ensures that targets do not remain private between approval and the company's next annual reporting cycle.

The public announcement should include:
- The full target language as approved
- The base year and target year for each target
- The method used
- A statement that the targets have been validated by the SBTi

Most companies fulfill this obligation through a press release, an update to their corporate website, and/or through a CDP update. The SBTi also lists approved companies publicly on its own platform, providing an independent verification source.

## Recommended Disclosure Platforms (R12)

The SBTi does not mandate a single disclosure channel, but **Recommendation R12** identifies the following as preferred:

- **CDP:** The most structured and auditable platform for annual GHG and target progress disclosure
- **Annual report or integrated report:** Reaches financial stakeholders and is subject to external audit oversight
- **Sustainability or corporate responsibility report:** Provides narrative context for non-financial stakeholders

Companies are encouraged to disclose in multiple places. At minimum, making disclosures accessible on the company's own website is expected.

<HighlightBox>
Consistent, accessible, and timely disclosure builds the trust that makes science-based targets meaningful. A target that is validated but invisible to stakeholders does not serve the purpose of driving systemic change. The reporting and disclosure requirements in C25, C26, C28, and R12 are designed to close the gap between a target on paper and verifiable progress in practice.
</HighlightBox>
