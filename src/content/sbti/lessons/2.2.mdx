Once the target boundary is established, a second set of criteria governs how emissions within that boundary must be measured, reported, and accounted for. A target is only as credible as the inventory behind it. Criteria C7 through C12 address the methodological integrity of that inventory -- covering which calculation methods are acceptable, how scope 2 must be handled, what a complete scope 3 inventory looks like, how biogenic emissions are treated, and crucially, what carbon credits and avoided emissions cannot be used for.

## Method Validity: Using Approved Tools (C7)

**C7** states that targets must be modeled using the latest version of methods and tools approved by the SBTi. Targets modeled using previous versions of those tools or methods can only be submitted to the SBTi for validation within 6 months of the publication of the revised method or sector-specific tools.

This rule exists to prevent a company from locking in a favorable target using an outdated methodology that has since been superseded by more demanding science. When SBTi publishes a revised calculation tool -- for example, a new version of the Corporate Near-term Tool or updated sector-specific guidance -- companies that have not yet submitted their targets have a 6-month window to still use the old version. After that window closes, all new submissions must use the latest version.

<HighlightBox>
C7 key rule: Targets must use the latest SBTi-approved methods and tools. A target modeled on a superseded tool version can only be submitted within 6 months of the new tool's publication. After that 6-month window, only the current version is accepted.
</HighlightBox>

This also means that companies partway through their target-setting process need to monitor SBTi tool releases. If a new tool version is published while a company is preparing its submission, it must assess whether the 6-month grace period applies or whether it must remodel using the new version.

<AnalogyBox>
Think of C7 like building codes. A contractor cannot use a code that was superseded two years ago just because they started their plans under that version. There is a reasonable transition window after a new code takes effect, but after that window, the new code applies. SBTi's method validity rule works the same way.
</AnalogyBox>

## Scope 2 Accounting Approach: Consistency Is Required (C8)

Scope 2 emissions from purchased electricity can be calculated using two different approaches defined in the GHG Protocol Scope 2 Guidance:

- **Location-based:** Uses average emission factors for the regional or national grid where the company operates.
- **Market-based:** Uses contractual instruments (energy attribute certificates, power purchase agreements, supplier-specific factors) to reflect the actual electricity product purchased.

**C8** requires that companies disclose which approach they are using -- location-based or market-based -- to calculate base year emissions and to track performance against the science-based target. The GHG Protocol requires reporting scope 2 using both approaches. However, a single and consistent approach must be used for setting and tracking progress toward a science-based target.

<HighlightBox>
C8 key rule: A company must choose one approach -- location-based or market-based -- for its target baseline and stick with it throughout the target period. You cannot set the target using location-based figures and then track progress using market-based figures (or vice versa), as this introduces inconsistency that undermines target integrity.
</HighlightBox>

<ExampleBox>
**Example -- C8 in practice:**

A technology company sets its scope 2 baseline using the market-based approach at 80,000 tCO2e (reflecting renewable energy contracts already in place). It targets a 42% reduction to 46,400 tCO2e by 2030.

Each year it must track its scope 2 emissions using the same market-based approach. If it later purchases additional renewable electricity certificates, those will reduce its market-based scope 2 figure and count as progress. However, the company cannot switch to reporting location-based figures in years where market-based figures are less favorable. The approach must be consistent from base year to target year.

Note: companies using location-based methods for their scope 2 target can still set a renewable electricity target under C21 provided they have the capacity to demonstrate active sourcing through market instruments.
</ExampleBox>

## Scope 3 Inventory: Full Coverage Required (C9)

Before setting scope 3 targets, a company must know what it is targeting. **C9** requires that companies complete a scope 3 inventory covering gross scope 3 emissions for all relevant emissions sources according to the GHG Protocol Corporate Value Chain (Scope 3) Accounting and Reporting Standard.

This is a prerequisite, not an aspiration. A company cannot submit scope 3 targets based on partial estimates covering only its largest categories. The inventory must cover all relevant scope 3 categories, assessed against the GHG Protocol's minimum boundary thresholds (Table 5.4) and relevance criteria (Table 6.1).

Relevance for scope 3 category inclusion is determined against the minimum boundary in Table 5.4 of the GHG Protocol Scope 3 Standard. Beyond those minimum requirements, all transport-related emissions across all sectors must be reported on a well-to-wheel (WTW) basis. All use-phase emissions from third-party distributed fossil fuels must be reported in scope 3 category 11 for all companies engaged in distribution activity.

For companies with financed emissions (scope 3 category 15), the PCAF Global GHG Accounting and Reporting Standard for the Financial Industry may be used. However, emissions beyond the minimum requirements of scope 3 category 15 investments (per Table 5.9 of the GHG Protocol Scope 3 Standard) do not count toward mandatory scope 3 targets and should be treated as optional under R2.

## Biogenic Accounting: A Special Regime (C10)

Companies that use bioenergy or have biogenic emissions within their operations or value chain face additional accounting requirements under **C10**, which was updated in V5.3.

Companies with biogenic emissions shall:

1. Report CO2 emissions and removals in line with GHG Protocol guidance.
2. Include net CO2 emissions from biogenic sources in the target boundary for scopes 1, 2, and 3 (note: biogenic scope 3 emissions must only be included if the net scope 3 coverage would otherwise fall below the minimum threshold), and track these net emissions when reporting progress against science-based targets.
3. Include the required footnote in their target language: *"The target boundary includes land-related emissions and removals from biogenic feedstocks."* Companies intending to use bioenergy or other biogenic emissions sources during the target timeframe must also include this footnote in their target language.
4. Report all upstream CO2e emissions from bioenergy and biogenic feedstocks in the main GHG inventory. These upstream emissions must include:
   - CO2e from direct land use change (LUC) -- note that reporting emissions from indirect LUC is optional.
   - Non-LUC emissions, including N2O and CH4 from land management.
   - CO2e from processing and distribution.
5. Follow any future GHG Protocol guidance on bioenergy accounting once released, to maintain alignment with C11.

<HighlightBox>
C10 key rule: Bioenergy is not automatically zero-carbon in the SBTi framework. Net biogenic CO2 emissions must be reported and included in target boundaries. Negative emissions due to biogenic removals shall not be counted as progress toward SBTs. Removals not directly associated with biogenic feedstock production are also not accepted as progress.
</HighlightBox>

<DeepDive title="Why does biogenic accounting matter so much?">
The standard assumption in many national GHG inventories is that biogenic CO2 from burning biomass is carbon-neutral because the carbon was recently absorbed from the atmosphere by plant growth. However, this assumption breaks down when land use change is involved (e.g., clearing forest to grow energy crops), when the carbon cycle payback time is long, or when upstream N2O and CH4 emissions from land management are significant.

The SBTi's C10 requirement for net biogenic accounting prevents companies from claiming large emission reductions simply by switching to bioenergy without accounting for the full upstream footprint of that bioenergy. A company burning forest biomass for heat must account for the LUC CO2 emissions and the methane from decomposing logging residues, not just the combustion CO2.

R3 (Biofuel certification) recommends that companies using or producing biofuels for transport support their bioenergy GHG accounting with recognized biofuel certifications to disclose that land-related emissions and removals data represents the relevant biofuel feedstock production.

R4 (Bioenergy data reporting) recommends that companies report direct biogenic CO2 emissions and removals from bioenergy separately. Emissions and removals of CO2 associated with bioenergy shall be reported as net emissions per C10, at minimum. Companies are encouraged to also report gross emissions and gross removals.
</DeepDive>

## Carbon Credits Cannot Count as Emission Reductions (C11)

This criterion is one of the most frequently misunderstood aspects of the SBTi framework. **C11** is unambiguous: the use of carbon credits must not be counted as emission reductions toward the progress of companies' near-term science-based targets.

Carbon credits may only be considered for two specific purposes:
1. Neutralizing residual emissions at the net-zero stage (see the SBTi Corporate Net-Zero Standard Criteria C28).
2. Financing additional climate mitigation beyond a company's science-based emission reduction targets -- what the SBTi calls Beyond Value Chain Mitigation, or BVCM (see the SBTi Corporate Net-Zero Standard Criteria R9).

<HighlightBox>
C11 key rule: Carbon credits -- voluntary offsets, carbon removal credits, nature-based credits -- cannot be used to demonstrate progress toward a near-term science-based target. Every tonne of reduction claimed against the target must come from actual emission reductions within the company's target boundary. Credits are for neutralization and BVCM only, not for near-term target compliance.
</HighlightBox>

<ExampleBox>
**Example -- What C11 prohibits:**

A logistics company has a target to reduce scope 1 and 2 emissions 46% by 2030 from a 2020 base year. In 2026, its actual emissions have fallen only 20%. It purchases high-quality forestry carbon credits equivalent to 26% of its base year emissions.

Under C11, this does not count. The company cannot apply those credits to demonstrate it has met its 46% target. The credits are entirely separate from the target. The company is still 26 percentage points short on actual emission reductions.

What the company can legitimately do with those credits: report them as BVCM contributions -- voluntary climate finance that goes beyond its science-based target obligations -- and communicate them transparently as a separate initiative.
</ExampleBox>

## Avoided Emissions: A Separate Accounting System (C12)

**C12** clarifies that avoided emissions fall under a separate accounting system from corporate inventories and do not count toward near-term science-based emission reduction targets.

Avoided emissions are emission reductions that occur outside a company's value chain as a result of the company's products or services -- for example, the greenhouse gas savings customers achieve by using a company's energy-efficient equipment instead of a conventional alternative. These are important for understanding a company's climate contribution to the broader economy, but they are distinct from the company's own scope 1, 2, and 3 inventory.

<AnalogyBox>
Avoided emissions are like revenue generated by a company's customers -- it matters for understanding the company's economic contribution, but it does not belong on the company's own income statement. Similarly, avoided emissions matter for understanding a company's climate contribution, but they do not belong in the company's own GHG inventory or progress tracking against an SBT.
</AnalogyBox>

Companies offering climate-positive products -- solar panels, heat pumps, electric vehicles -- may legitimately publicize the avoided emissions associated with their products, but this must be clearly separated from their own inventory-based target reporting. Combining the two would fundamentally misrepresent whether the company is on track with its own science-based target.

## The Accounting Criteria in Summary

<ResponsiveTable>
| Criterion | Core Rule |
|-----------|-----------|
| C7 | Use latest SBTi-approved method; 6-month grace period after new version published |
| C8 | Disclose location-based or market-based for S2; use one approach consistently for setting and tracking |
| C9 | Complete full S3 inventory per GHG Protocol before setting S3 targets |
| C10 | Bioenergy users must report net biogenic CO2 in target boundary; include LUC, N2O, CH4, processing emissions |
| C11 | Carbon credits cannot count as S1/2/3 emission reductions toward near-term SBT progress |
| C12 | Avoided emissions are a separate accounting system; cannot count toward targets |
</ResponsiveTable>

These six criteria collectively ensure that the emissions figure underpinning a science-based target is calculated on a consistent, complete, and methodologically current basis. They prevent companies from gaming the accounting -- by switching methods mid-target, ignoring biogenic emissions, or offsetting their way to apparent compliance without genuine decarbonization.
