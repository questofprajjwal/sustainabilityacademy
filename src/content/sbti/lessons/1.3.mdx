After a company has reduced its emissions as far as science-aligned pathways require, two distinct but related questions arise. First: what happens to the emissions that genuinely cannot be eliminated? Second: what can and should the company do beyond its own value chain to accelerate the broader transition? These are the domains of neutralisation and beyond value chain mitigation -- the third and fourth pillars of the Corporate Net-Zero Standard.

Understanding both concepts precisely is important, because they are often conflated, misapplied, or used interchangeably in corporate communications. The Standard draws firm lines between them.

## Neutralisation: Permanent Removal of Residual Emissions

### What Neutralisation Means

Neutralisation under the SBTi Standard refers to the measures a company takes to remove carbon from the atmosphere and permanently store it, in order to counterbalance the impact of any GHG emissions that remain unabated after the long-term science-based target has been achieved.

Two categories of residual emissions must be neutralised:

1. **Emissions remaining within the target boundary** after the long-term SBT is achieved -- those that could not be eliminated despite deep abatement efforts.
2. **Emissions excluded from the long-term target boundary** and from the GHG inventory -- for example, certain legacy or minor emission sources. These must also be neutralised when the company claims net-zero.

The Standard is explicit: a company cannot claim to have reached net-zero until both the long-term SBT for all scopes has been achieved and residual emissions have been neutralised.

### Why Only Permanent Removal Counts

The requirement for permanent removal -- rather than avoidance credits -- is one of the most technically important distinctions in the Standard. Avoidance-based carbon credits (such as those from renewable energy projects, methane capture, or forest protection) represent GHG emissions that were prevented from occurring. They do not remove CO2 already in the atmosphere. For net-zero accounting to be physically meaningful, the balance at the net-zero target year must reflect a genuine mass balance: the CO2 being counterbalanced must actually be extracted and durably stored.

Carbon avoidance credits can have real value for climate mitigation as part of BVCM (discussed below), but they serve a different function from removal. Mixing them with neutralisation would create a false accounting -- claiming a zero balance while actual atmospheric CO2 concentrations continued to accumulate from the company's residual emissions.

Permanence also addresses the temporal dimension. The climate system responds to cumulative CO2 concentrations over centuries. Carbon removed and stored geologically for 1,000 years provides a fundamentally different climate service than carbon "stored" in a forest that could burn within decades. High-integrity neutralisation requires removal approaches with durable, ideally geological, storage.

<HighlightBox>
Carbon credits from avoidance projects (renewable energy, forest protection, methane capture) do not count toward neutralisation of residual emissions. Only permanent carbon removal qualifies. This boundary is clearly stated in the Standard and reflects the physical requirements of net-zero accounting.
</HighlightBox>

### When Neutralisation Applies

Neutralisation becomes relevant only at the point the long-term SBT is achieved -- not before. The Standard is deliberately structured to prevent companies from using neutralisation as a substitute for near-term abatement.

This temporal restriction reflects two important principles. First, the carbon budget cannot accommodate widespread neutralisation before deep abatement has occurred. If companies were permitted to neutralise freely during the near-term period, the incentive to invest in genuine operational decarbonisation would be weakened. Second, current carbon removal supply (particularly high-integrity, permanent removal) is extremely limited relative to global demand. Reserving it for residual emissions at net-zero, rather than allowing it to substitute for abatement, is the most climate-effective allocation.

Criterion C11 of the Near-Term Criteria formalises this boundary: carbon credits -- whether avoidance or removal -- may not be used to count toward progress against near-term SBTs. A company's reported progress against its near-term target must reflect actual reductions in scope 1, scope 2, and in-scope scope 3 emissions.

<AnalogyBox>
Think of neutralisation like the final seal on a container. First you have to remove almost everything from the container (deep abatement). Only then do you apply the final hermetic seal (neutralisation) to ensure the last trace molecules cannot escape. Trying to seal the container while it is still mostly full is not meaningful -- the seal does not address what is inside. The Standard prevents companies from treating the seal as a substitute for emptying the container.
</AnalogyBox>

### Preparing for Neutralisation Before Net-Zero

While the formal neutralisation commitment only applies at the net-zero target year, the Standard (Recommendation 10) encourages companies to begin planning and disclosing their neutralisation approach well in advance. This includes:

- Disclosing planned milestones for neutralisation investments
- Making near-term investments to help scale carbon removal solutions
- Demonstrating the integrity of commitments to neutralise at net-zero

This forward-looking planning matters because high-quality permanent carbon removal (direct air capture, enhanced weathering, biochar with geological storage) takes time to develop, contract, and verify. A company that waits until its net-zero target year to arrange neutralisation may find that supply is constrained, prices are high, and the quality of available removal is unverified.

<ExampleBox>
**Example: Microsoft's removal-focused approach**

Microsoft has committed to being carbon negative by 2030 and to removing all the carbon the company has ever emitted by 2050. Crucially, Microsoft's approach centres on carbon removal rather than avoidance offsets. The company has invested in a portfolio of removal technologies including direct air capture, bioenergy with carbon capture and storage (BECCS), and enhanced weathering. Microsoft explicitly distinguishes between removal (which it treats as valid for its neutralisation goal) and avoidance (which it treats as a co-benefit of BVCM-style support for nature-based projects, but not as a substitute for removal). This separation mirrors the logic of the SBTi Standard.
</ExampleBox>

## Beyond Value Chain Mitigation (BVCM)

### Definition and Scope

Beyond value chain mitigation is defined in the Standard as mitigation action or investments that fall outside a company's value chain. This is a broad category. It includes:

- Activities that **avoid or reduce** GHG emissions outside the value chain -- for example, funding a renewable energy project in a developing country, or supporting methane abatement in agricultural supply chains that are not directly the company's own.
- Activities that **remove and store GHGs** from the atmosphere -- including high-quality carbon removal credits purchased to go beyond a company's net-zero commitment.

BVCM is explicitly positioned as an action taken in addition to, and not instead of, the company's science-based targets for its own value chain. The Standard is emphatic on this point: BVCM investments do not substitute for the rapid and deep reduction of a company's own value chain emissions.

<HighlightBox>
BVCM is "above and beyond." It is what companies do after -- or alongside -- meeting their SBTs to contribute to broader societal climate goals. It does not reduce a company's near-term or long-term SBT requirements by any amount.
</HighlightBox>

### The Two BVCM Goals

The SBTi frames BVCM around two distinct but complementary goals:

**Goal 1 -- Peak emissions mid-decade.** BVCM should help deliver additional near-term mitigation outcomes consistent with achieving the peaking of global emissions in the mid-2020s and the halving of global emissions by 2030. This goal addresses the urgency of the current decade. Even as companies work on their own value chains, the scale of the global mitigation challenge means that additional investment in reductions elsewhere -- particularly in sectors or geographies where capital is scarce -- can have significant near-term impact.

**Goal 2 -- Finance for the net-zero transition.** BVCM should drive additional finance into the scale-up of nascent climate solutions and enabling activities that unlock the systemic transformation needed to achieve net-zero globally by mid-century. This goal recognises that many critical technologies -- direct air capture, green hydrogen, long-duration energy storage -- are not yet cost-competitive and will not scale without significant investment flowing in from companies and institutions willing to accept higher near-term costs.

### The Four BVCM Principles

The SBTi identifies four principles that should guide how companies approach BVCM. These principles help ensure that BVCM investments are genuinely additive rather than performative.

**Scale.** BVCM should maximise climate action. This means prioritising activities with large and demonstrable mitigation impact rather than those that are merely visible or reputationally convenient.

**Financing Need.** BVCM should focus on activities that are under-financed -- particularly nascent or early-stage mitigation solutions that cannot attract capital at scale without support from committed buyers or investors. Funding a wind farm in a country with an active and competitive renewable energy market may have limited additionality; funding early-stage direct air capture facilities or community-scale clean cooking solutions in underserved markets may have much higher marginal impact.

**Co-benefits.** BVCM should support activities that generate quantifiable benefits for people and nature alongside their climate impact. The alignment with the Sustainable Development Goals (SDGs) is explicitly mentioned in the Standard. Clean cooking, for example, reduces indoor air pollution and improves health outcomes in addition to avoiding methane and CO2 emissions. Nature-based restoration projects can generate biodiversity, water quality, and community livelihood benefits alongside carbon outcomes.

**Climate Justice.** BVCM should address inequality by prioritising investments and actions that benefit communities most vulnerable to climate impacts and least responsible for historical emissions. This reflects the principle that the costs and harms of climate change are not borne equally, and that corporate investment can and should direct resources toward communities where both the need and the co-benefit potential are highest.

<ExampleBox>
**Example: How a company might structure BVCM**

A large consumer goods company sets its SBTi-validated near-term target (42% Scope 1 and 2 reduction by 2030) and long-term net-zero target (90%+ reduction by 2045). Separately, it announces a BVCM commitment of US$50 million per year directed at:
- Purchasing forward contracts for direct air capture removal (aligned with Financing Need and Scale)
- Funding clean cookstove distribution in sub-Saharan Africa (aligned with Co-benefits and Climate Justice)
- Investing in soil carbon enhancement programs in smallholder agricultural supply chains that are not yet within its SBT boundary (aligned with Scale and Co-benefits)

None of these BVCM investments counts toward the company's SBT progress. They are tracked and reported separately as the company's contribution to societal climate goals. The company reports annually on the nature and scale of BVCM activity, as recommended in Recommendation 9 of the Standard.
</ExampleBox>

### How BVCM Relates to But Does Not Substitute for SBTs

The relationship between BVCM and SBTs is additive, not substitutive. This bears repeating because the historical practice in voluntary carbon markets has been to use external offsets as a substitute for internal reduction. That model -- buy credits equal to your emissions, declare neutrality -- is explicitly rejected by the SBTi framework.

The Standard notes that decarbonising a company's value chain in line with science and reaching net-zero by 2050 is increasingly becoming the minimum societal expectation. BVCM is what companies do in addition to meeting that minimum -- not instead of it. A company that invests heavily in BVCM while failing to meet its near-term SBT is not in compliance with the Standard. Conversely, a company that meets all its SBT requirements and also engages in substantial BVCM is demonstrating genuine climate leadership.

<DeepDive title="The SBTi's 'Above and Beyond' report on BVCM">
The SBTi has published detailed guidance on BVCM in its "Above and Beyond" report, which provides a framework for how companies can design and implement credible BVCM strategies. Key themes from the report include:

The need for additionality: BVCM should support activities that would not happen without the company's investment. Buying credits from projects that are already fully funded and operational does not meet the additionality test.

The preference for near-term impact: given the urgency of the current decade for limiting warming, BVCM that delivers real mitigation outcomes before 2030 is more valuable than long-dated commitments.

Transparency and reporting: companies should report annually on the nature, scale, and verified impact of BVCM activities. Vague commitments without verified outcomes undermine the credibility of the approach.

The "Raising the Bar" report provides further guidance for companies that want to accelerate their BVCM adoption. Both documents are available on the SBTi website and are complementary to the Corporate Net-Zero Standard.
</DeepDive>

<HighlightBox>
Key takeaways from this lesson:

- Neutralisation means permanent carbon removal to counterbalance residual emissions at and after the net-zero target year.
- Carbon avoidance credits do not count toward neutralisation -- only permanent removal qualifies.
- Carbon credits (avoidance or removal) cannot be used to count toward progress against near-term SBTs (Criterion C11).
- BVCM is mitigation action or investment outside a company's own value chain.
- The two BVCM goals are: (1) peak global emissions in the mid-2020s and halve by 2030; (2) finance the scale-up of nascent climate solutions for the net-zero transition.
- The four BVCM principles are: Scale, Financing Need, Co-benefits, and Climate Justice.
- BVCM adds to SBT commitments but never substitutes for them. Near-term and long-term SBT requirements are unaffected by BVCM activity.
</HighlightBox>
