Once a company has a complete, GHG Protocol-aligned emissions inventory and a representative base year, the next challenge is choosing how to translate that inventory into a credible, SBTi-compliant target. The SBTi provides a suite of target-setting methods, each suited to different scopes, sectors, and data environments. Choosing the right method is not a matter of picking the easiest option -- it requires understanding the scientific pathway each method is based on, which scopes it applies to, and what level of ambition it demands.

## The Core Logic: Modelled Pathways to 1.5 Degrees

All SBTi target-setting methods are anchored to a common underlying logic: global greenhouse gas emissions must fall in line with one of the IPCC's modelled pathways to limit warming to 1.5 degrees Celsius above pre-industrial levels. The methods differ in how they translate that global budget constraint into a company-specific reduction requirement.

Two broad approaches underpin the methods:

- **Absolute contraction:** the company's total gross emissions fall by a specified percentage, regardless of growth in output or revenue
- **Intensity-based reduction:** the company's emissions per unit of output (physical or economic) converge toward a sector benchmark, allowing for growth in absolute terms provided intensity improves sufficiently

<HighlightBox>
For Scope 1 and Scope 2, the SBTi strongly prefers absolute contraction targets. Intensity-based methods are permitted for Scope 3 where absolute data is difficult to obtain, but companies should not use intensity methods to avoid genuine emissions reductions where absolute approaches are feasible.
</HighlightBox>

## Method B.1: Cross-Sector Absolute Reduction

**Method B.1** is the most straightforward and widely applicable method. It requires the company to reduce its absolute Scope 1 and Scope 2 emissions at a rate consistent with the required global decarbonisation pathway.

For a base year on or before 2020, the minimum required reduction rate is **4.2% per year** (Linear Annual Reduction, or LAR) for a 1.5-degree-aligned near-term target. This 4.2% LAR is the compound annual reduction implied by the Science Based Targets initiative's Selected Scenarios for the near-term horizon.

### Adjusting for Later Base Years

Many companies set their base year after 2020. For a company using a base year of 2022, for example, the global decarbonisation pathway has already "moved on" by two years since 2020. The company must account for those two missed years of required reduction.

The adjustment formula is:

<FormulaBox>
Required cumulative reduction = LAR x (Target Year - Base Year)

For base years after 2020, adjusted required reduction = LAR x (Target Year - 2020)

Example: Base year 2022, target year 2030, LAR 4.2%
Adjusted required reduction = 4.2% x (2030 - 2020) = 4.2% x 10 = 42%
But the target period is only 8 years (2022 to 2030).
The 42% must be achieved over those 8 years.
</FormulaBox>

In practice, a company with a 2022 base year aiming to achieve a 1.5C-aligned near-term target by 2030 needs to reduce its Scope 1 and 2 emissions by at least 42% in absolute terms over that eight-year period. This is meaningfully more demanding than it would appear if you simply applied 4.2% per year for eight years (which would yield roughly 28%).

<ExampleBox>
**Example: Calculating the required reduction with a 2022 base year**

A company has Scope 1 + Scope 2 (market-based) base year emissions of 120,000 tCO2e, with a base year of 2022 and a target year of 2030.

Step 1 -- Calculate the adjusted required percentage reduction:
LAR x (Target Year -- 2020) = 4.2% x (2030 -- 2020) = 42%

Step 2 -- Calculate the required reduction in absolute terms:
120,000 tCO2e x 42% = 50,400 tCO2e

Step 3 -- Calculate the target emissions level:
120,000 -- 50,400 = 69,600 tCO2e by 2030

The company must reduce its combined Scope 1 and 2 emissions from 120,000 to no more than 69,600 tCO2e by 2030 to meet a 1.5C-aligned near-term target under Method B.1.
</ExampleBox>

## Method B.3: Sectoral Decarbonization Approach (SDA)

The **Sectoral Decarbonization Approach (SDA)**, described in Method B.3, takes a different approach. Rather than asking a company to cut its absolute emissions by a universal rate, SDA aligns a company's physical emission intensity with a sector-specific decarbonisation pathway.

The underlying idea is that some sectors -- notably heavy industry and transport -- may need to grow in output to meet human needs, even while decarbonising. A cement company serving a rapidly developing economy might produce more cement over time, but the carbon intensity of that cement (measured in tCO2e per tonne of cement produced) should decline in line with the global pathway for the cement sector.

<AnalogyBox>
The SDA method is like telling every runner in a race that they must cross the same finishing line by the same time -- but acknowledging that some start closer and some farther away. The intensity convergence pathway is the finishing line; every company in that sector must reach it, regardless of where they started.
</AnalogyBox>

### Which Sectors Use SDA?

The SDA was originally developed for a small number of energy-intensive, homogeneous sectors where a common physical intensity metric is meaningful:

- **Power/electricity generation** (tCO2e per MWh generated)
- **Iron and steel** (tCO2e per tonne of steel)
- **Cement** (tCO2e per tonne of cementitious materials)
- **Buildings** (tCO2e per m2 of floor area)
- **Passenger transport** (gCO2e per passenger-km)
- **Freight transport** (gCO2e per tonne-km)
- **Aluminium** (tCO2e per tonne of aluminium)

For companies operating in these sectors, sector-specific guidance documents typically specify whether SDA is available, required, or preferred.

<HighlightBox>
SDA is only available for Scope 1 and Scope 2 targets in the sectors listed above. For sectors without a defined SDA pathway, companies must use Method B.1 (absolute contraction) for Scope 1 and Scope 2.
</HighlightBox>

## Method B.4: Renewable Electricity Target (Scope 2 Only)

For **Scope 2 emissions only**, the SBTi accepts a standalone renewable electricity procurement target as one component of a broader Scope 2 strategy, provided it meets minimum thresholds:

- At least **80% renewable electricity** by 2025
- **100% renewable electricity** by 2030

Method B.4 is not a complete Scope 2 method on its own -- it must be used alongside either B.1 or B.3 for Scope 1 emissions, and it only covers the electricity component of Scope 2. Companies that also purchase steam, heat, or cooling will need to address those separately.

Renewable electricity targets under B.4 must be met through credible procurement mechanisms -- power purchase agreements (PPAs), energy attribute certificates (EACs) meeting quality criteria, or on-site generation. Simply claiming the grid is becoming greener is not sufficient.

## Method B.5: Physical Intensity Reduction (Scope 3)

For Scope 3 emissions, absolute data can be extremely difficult to obtain -- particularly for value chain categories that depend on hundreds of suppliers or millions of consumers. The SBTi therefore provides intensity-based methods for Scope 3.

**Method B.5** allows companies to set a Scope 3 target based on physical intensity (emissions per unit of output). To be well-below-2C aligned, the required annual reduction rate in intensity is at least **7% compound annual reduction** in intensity.

The choice of physical intensity metric should be appropriate to the sector and value chain activity in question -- for example:
- A food manufacturer might use tCO2e per tonne of product
- A logistics company might use gCO2e per tonne-km of freight moved
- A retailer might use tCO2e per item sold

Companies using B.5 must specify clearly what physical output metric they are using and ensure it is tracked consistently throughout the target period.

## Method B.6: Economic Intensity Reduction (Scope 3)

**Method B.6** is similar to B.5, but uses economic output -- revenue -- as the denominator rather than a physical metric. This makes it accessible to companies with diverse product lines where a single physical intensity metric is impractical.

The minimum required economic intensity reduction is the same as B.5: at least **7% compound annual reduction** for well-below-2C alignment.

<DeepDive title="Why is the Scope 3 intensity threshold 7% rather than 4.2%?">
The 4.2% linear annual reduction rate (LAR) applies to absolute Scope 1 and 2 targets aligned to 1.5 degrees. For Scope 3 intensity methods, the SBTi uses a **7% compound annual reduction** as the minimum for well-below-2C alignment (not 1.5C alignment, which would require a higher rate).

This reflects several realities: Scope 3 data is less accurate, supply chain transformation takes longer than direct operational changes, and many Scope 3 emissions are driven by customer behaviour that is harder to control. The 7% rate is therefore calibrated to the well-below-2C pathway as a practical floor, with companies encouraged to set more ambitious targets where data allows.

For 1.5C-aligned absolute Scope 3 targets, the implied annual reduction is higher -- approximately 8--15% depending on the sector -- which is why intensity methods are often used as a pragmatic alternative.
</DeepDive>

## Method B.7: Supplier and Customer Engagement Targets (Scope 3)

**Method B.7** is a unique approach available for Scope 3 Categories 1 (purchased goods and services) and 11 (use of sold products). Rather than requiring a direct emissions reduction target, it allows companies to set a target based on the proportion of suppliers or customers who themselves have SBTi-approved targets or are taking verified action.

For a supplier engagement target under B.7, the minimum requirement is that a specified percentage of the company's Scope 3 Category 1 spend (typically 67% or more) comes from suppliers who have their own SBTi-validated targets. This cascades the ambition of science-based targeting through supply chains.

<ExampleBox>
**Example: A supplier engagement target statement under B.7**

"By 2027, 70% of our suppliers by spend covering purchased goods and services (Scope 3, Category 1) will have science-based targets."

This target does not require the company to directly measure or reduce its suppliers' emissions. Instead, it commits the company to the process of engaging its supply chain and requiring suppliers to take on their own verified commitments. Over time, as supplier targets are set and achieved, the company's Scope 3 Category 1 footprint should fall as a consequence.
</ExampleBox>

## Choosing the Right Method: A Decision Framework

The SBTi's Getting Started Guide provides a decision logic that most companies follow:

1. **Start with sector classification.** Is the company operating in a sector with dedicated sector guidance (e.g., power, steel, cement, buildings, finance)? If yes, that guidance may mandate specific methods or intensity metrics.

2. **For Scope 1 and 2:** Default to Method B.1 (absolute reduction). Use B.3 (SDA) if the company is in an eligible sector and sector guidance explicitly permits or requires it. Method B.4 can supplement Scope 2 through renewable electricity procurement.

3. **For Scope 3:** If absolute data is available and reliable, use an absolute reduction target (4.2% LAR for 1.5C). If not, use B.5 (physical intensity), B.6 (economic intensity), or B.7 (supplier engagement), depending on the category and data availability.

4. **Mixed targets are allowed.** A company can use Method B.1 for Scope 1+2 and Method B.7 for Scope 3 Category 1, provided each target individually meets the criteria.

<HighlightBox>
The SBTi does not permit companies to "cherry-pick" the least demanding method simply to minimise effort. The validation process scrutinises the method selection against the company's sector, data availability, and organisational circumstances. A company that could reasonably use an absolute method but opts for intensity without clear justification may have its submission returned for revision.
</HighlightBox>

## A Note on Method Selection and the Validation Process

Selecting a method is not a one-time decision made in isolation. During the SBTi validation process, assessors will check that:

- The chosen method is appropriate given the company's sector and scope coverage
- The base year, target year, and percentage reduction are internally consistent
- The implied reduction trajectory is consistent with a recognised climate pathway
- The company has not used an intensity method to avoid substantive absolute reductions where absolute data is feasible

Keeping clear records of the rationale for method selection -- including what alternatives were considered and why they were ruled out -- is good practice and can streamline the validation process.
