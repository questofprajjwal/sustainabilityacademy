Before a company can set a science-based target, it must first establish two foundational elements: a clearly defined base year and a complete, GHG Protocol-aligned emissions inventory. These two steps are not simply administrative formalities. They define the starting line against which all future progress will be measured, and the integrity of that starting line determines the credibility of the entire target.

## What Is a Base Year?

A **base year** is the historical reference point against which a company measures its emissions reductions. If a company commits to reducing its Scope 1 and 2 emissions by 50% by 2030, that 50% reduction is measured relative to the base year's emission level. Choose a high base year and the target looks more ambitious than it is; choose an artificially low one and future reductions appear smaller than reality.

<HighlightBox>
Criterion C13 of the SBTi Near-Term Criteria states that the base year must be no earlier than 2015. The Scope 1 and Scope 2 targets must share the same base year. This does not mean the Scope 3 target must use that same base year, though consistency is best practice.
</HighlightBox>

The SBTi does not require companies to use the most recent year as their base year. Many companies use 2019 (the last full pre-pandemic year) because it is considered more representative of their normal operations than the disrupted years that followed. What matters most is that the base year reflects a **representative year** -- one that is neither anomalously high nor anomalously low relative to the company's typical activity level.

## Selecting a Representative Base Year

Choosing an appropriate base year requires judgment. The SBTi's Getting Started Guide advises companies to avoid years that are clearly outliers -- for example, a year in which a major facility was unexpectedly offline, a year distorted by a one-time acquisition, or a year where activity levels were suppressed by an external shock. The pandemic years of 2020 and 2021 are widely regarded as poor choices for this reason.

<AnalogyBox>
Think of a base year like the starting weight recorded at the beginning of a fitness programme. If you weigh yourself on a day you happen to be retaining unusual water weight, your starting number looks high and the subsequent "progress" looks more impressive than it really is. The same logic applies to emissions: the base year should reflect genuine, representative performance -- not a convenient anomaly.
</AnalogyBox>

When a company has limited historical data, it may need to use the earliest year for which complete, verifiable data exists. The SBTi allows this, provided the year is no earlier than 2015. Companies should document why their chosen base year is representative and be prepared to justify that choice during the validation process.

## The Requirement for a Recent Inventory

Selecting a historical base year is only part of the picture. The SBTi also requires that companies demonstrate a **current, complete inventory** at the time of submission. For submissions in 2025, the most recent inventory included in the target submission must cover a year no earlier than 2023. This requirement ensures that the company has genuinely engaged with its current emissions profile, not merely repackaged old data.

This is particularly important because corporate emissions profiles shift over time -- through acquisitions, disposals, changes to energy contracts, and evolving supply chains. A company whose base year is 2018 but whose latest inventory is also 2018 cannot credibly demonstrate what has changed in the intervening years.

## Building a Complete GHG Inventory

A GHG inventory is a systematic quantification of all greenhouse gas emissions (and, where applicable, removals) attributable to the reporting company. The SBTi requires that inventories comply with the **GHG Protocol Corporate Accounting and Reporting Standard** (often called the Corporate Standard), the most widely adopted framework for corporate greenhouse gas accounting globally.

### Scope 1: Direct Emissions

**Scope 1** covers direct emissions from sources owned or controlled by the company. This includes:

- Combustion of fuels in company-owned boilers, furnaces, or process equipment
- Combustion in company-owned or leased vehicles
- Emissions from industrial processes (e.g., cement calcination, chemical reactions)
- Fugitive emissions -- unintentional releases of greenhouse gases, such as refrigerant leaks

Scope 1 data is generally the most within the company's direct control and, with proper metering and monitoring, can be highly accurate.

### Scope 2: Indirect Energy Emissions

**Scope 2** covers emissions associated with the generation of purchased electricity, steam, heat, or cooling consumed by the company. The GHG Protocol requires companies to report Scope 2 under two methods:

- **Location-based method:** Uses the average emission factor for the regional or national grid where consumption occurs. This reflects the actual physical mix of electricity generation in that grid.
- **Market-based method:** Reflects the emission factors of specific electricity contracts, such as renewable energy certificates (RECs), Guarantees of Origin (GOs), or power purchase agreements (PPAs). A company that has contracted 100% renewable electricity will report near-zero Scope 2 under the market-based method, even if the local grid is fossil-fuel intensive.

<HighlightBox>
The SBTi requires companies to report both location-based and market-based Scope 2. For target-setting, market-based Scope 2 is used when available (subject to quality criteria for energy attribute certificates). This means that renewable energy procurement can directly reduce a company's Scope 2 target baseline.
</HighlightBox>

### Scope 3: Value Chain Emissions

**Scope 3** encompasses all other indirect emissions that occur in a company's value chain -- both upstream (suppliers, raw materials, business travel, waste) and downstream (use of sold products, end-of-life treatment). The GHG Protocol Corporate Value Chain (Scope 3) Standard identifies **15 categories**:

**Upstream categories (1--8):**
1. Purchased goods and services
2. Capital goods
3. Fuel- and energy-related activities (not in Scope 1 or 2)
4. Upstream transportation and distribution
5. Waste generated in operations
6. Business travel
7. Employee commuting
8. Upstream leased assets

**Downstream categories (9--15):**
9. Downstream transportation and distribution
10. Processing of sold products
11. Use of sold products
12. End-of-life treatment of sold products
13. Downstream leased assets
14. Franchises
15. Investments

For most companies, Scope 3 represents the largest share of their total footprint. Category 1 (purchased goods and services) and Category 11 (use of sold products) tend to dominate for manufacturers and consumer goods companies.

<ExampleBox>
**Example: A consumer electronics manufacturer**

A company manufacturing smartphones will find that its Scope 1 and 2 emissions -- from its assembly plants and offices -- are relatively small. The vast majority of its footprint lies in Scope 3:
- Category 1: Raw materials extraction (rare earth metals, aluminium, glass) and component manufacturing
- Category 11: Consumer electricity use during the operational life of the device
- Category 12: End-of-life treatment, including e-waste processing

Without measuring and targeting Scope 3, any near-term or net-zero commitment would be largely cosmetic.
</ExampleBox>

## Consolidation Approaches

Before building the inventory, a company must decide on its **organisational boundary** -- which entities and operations it will include. The GHG Protocol offers three approaches:

- **Operational control:** The company accounts for 100% of emissions from operations over which it has operational control, regardless of ownership share. This is the most common approach.
- **Financial control:** The company accounts for 100% of emissions from operations over which it has financial control (i.e., the ability to direct financial and operating policies).
- **Equity share:** The company accounts for emissions proportional to its ownership stake in each operation.

<HighlightBox>
The SBTi requires companies to be consistent in their consolidation approach across years. Switching from financial control to operational control mid-target period is a significant change that would trigger a mandatory base year recalculation under Criterion C27.
</HighlightBox>

The choice of consolidation approach affects which emissions are counted. A company that operates joint ventures will include or exclude those venture's emissions differently depending on which approach it uses. Companies operating in joint ventures and complex ownership structures should carefully consider which approach best reflects their actual influence over emissions before committing to a base year.

## Base Year Recalculation Triggers

Even after a base year has been set and a target validated, circumstances may require revising the base year emissions figure. This is called a **base year recalculation**. The SBTi's Criterion C27 states that recalculation is required when a structural change affects more than **5%** of the company's base year emissions.

The six main triggers for recalculation are:
1. Scope 3 emissions are found to exceed 40% of total footprint (requiring them to be brought into the target)
2. Change of consolidation approach
3. Changes to which emission sources are included or excluded
4. Significant mergers, acquisitions, or disposals (structural changes)
5. Material changes in data collection methodology or emission factors
6. Other changes that significantly affect the base year inventory

A recalculation is not a failure -- it is a mechanism to ensure the target remains a fair and meaningful representation of actual performance. However, it does require transparency: companies must disclose what changed, why, and what the revised base year emissions are.

## Putting It Together

A well-constructed base year inventory gives a company the foundation it needs to set a credible target. It should be:

- **Complete:** covering all material Scope 1, Scope 2 (both methods), and Scope 3 categories
- **Consistent:** using a single consolidation approach applied uniformly
- **Transparent:** with methodology and data sources documented
- **Accurate:** using primary data where possible, especially for material categories
- **Representative:** reflecting normal operating conditions, not an exceptional year

With a solid inventory in place, the next step is selecting the right target-setting method -- which is where Lesson 3.2 picks up.
