<h3 class="text-xl font-bold mt-6 mb-3">The SBTi as an Organisation</h3>

<p class="mb-3">The Science Based Targets initiative (SBTi) is a corporate climate action organization that enables companies and financial institutions worldwide to play their part in combating the climate crisis. It develops standards, tools, and guidance that allow companies to set greenhouse gas (GHG) emissions reduction targets in line with what is needed to keep global heating below catastrophic levels and reach net-zero by 2050 at the latest.</p>

<p class="mb-3">Legally, the SBTi is incorporated as a <strong>UK charity</strong> (charity number 1205768, company number 14960097), registered at 66 Lincoln's Inn Fields, London, England. A wholly owned subsidiary, <strong>SBTi Services Limited</strong> (company number 15181058), hosts the target validation services -- the commercial and operational arm that receives validation submissions and issues approvals. Together, these two entities are referred to as "the SBTi Group."</p>

<p class="mb-4">The initiative grew out of a partnership among five founding and facilitating organizations: <strong>CDP</strong>, the <strong>United Nations Global Compact (UNGC)</strong>, the <strong>We Mean Business Coalition</strong>, the <strong>World Resources Institute (WRI)</strong>, and the <strong>World Wide Fund for Nature (WWF)</strong>. These partners facilitated the SBTi's growth and development, providing scientific, technical, and convening capacity.</p>

<AnalogyBox>
  <p class="font-semibold text-blue-800">Think of the SBTi as a standards body plus a certification body</p>
  <p class="mt-2 text-blue-900">The SBTi charity develops and owns the standards (like ISO developing management system standards). SBTi Services Limited validates companies against those standards (like an accredited certification body issuing an ISO certificate). A company submits its targets to SBTi Services, which checks them against the criteria published by the SBTi charity and either approves or rejects them.</p>
</AnalogyBox>

<h3 class="text-xl font-bold mt-6 mb-3">The Four Pillars of the Net-Zero Standard Framework</h3>

<p class="mb-3">The SBTi Corporate Net-Zero Standard sets out a framework with four key elements that together make up a complete corporate net-zero commitment. Understanding these four pillars is essential to understanding how the entire system fits together.</p>

<h4 class="text-lg font-semibold mt-4 mb-2">Pillar 1: Near-Term Science-Based Targets</h4>
<p class="mb-3">Near-term targets are <strong>5-10 year GHG mitigation targets in line with 1.5C pathways</strong>. They are set from the date the target is submitted to the SBTi for validation, and they must have a target year of 2030 or 5-10 years from submission. Near-term targets are where most of the immediate action happens -- they galvanize the reductions needed by around 2030, which is the critical milestone for not exceeding the global carbon budget.</p>

<p class="mb-3">For scope 1 and 2 emissions, near-term targets must be aligned to 1.5C pathways. Since July 2022, the SBTi has required near-term scope 1 and 2 targets to be 1.5C aligned, with a minimum linear annual reduction rate of 4.2% for companies with a base year on or before 2020. For scope 3 emissions, near-term targets must be aligned to well-below 2C pathways -- a lower bar reflecting the greater complexity of value chain emissions data.</p>

<h4 class="text-lg font-semibold mt-4 mb-2">Pillar 2: Long-Term Science-Based Targets</h4>
<p class="mb-3">Long-term targets show companies how much they must reduce value chain emissions to align with reaching net-zero at the global or sector level in eligible 1.5C pathways, by <strong>2050 or sooner</strong> (2040 for power and maritime transport sector SDA targets). Under the cross-sector pathway, the minimum reduction required for long-term targets is 90% across all scopes from a 2020 base year.</p>

<p class="mb-3">An important technical distinction separates near-term and long-term targets: near-term targets are <strong>target year dependent</strong>, meaning the required reduction percentage changes depending on when the target year is set. Long-term targets are <strong>target year independent</strong> -- the required overall reduction (e.g., 90%) does not change based on the target year chosen. Companies choose their long-term target year (any year up to 2050) and the required reduction remains the same.</p>

<h4 class="text-lg font-semibold mt-4 mb-2">Pillar 3: Neutralization of Residual Emissions</h4>
<p class="mb-3">After achieving the long-term science-based target, some residual emissions may remain -- emissions that have proven technically or economically impossible to eliminate completely. These residual emissions must be <strong>permanently neutralized</strong> through the removal and permanent storage of carbon from the atmosphere.</p>

<p class="mb-3">A company cannot claim to have reached net-zero until the long-term science-based target for all scopes is achieved and the company has neutralized its residual emissions. Crucially, neutralization means permanent removal -- not the purchase of avoidance credits. The SBTi recommends companies begin planning and investing in carbon removal pathways now, well in advance of their net-zero target year.</p>

<h4 class="text-lg font-semibold mt-4 mb-2">Pillar 4: Beyond Value Chain Mitigation (BVCM)</h4>
<p class="mb-3">Beyond value chain mitigation refers to mitigation action or investments that fall <strong>outside</strong> of a company's value chain. This includes activities that avoid or reduce GHG emissions, and those that remove and store GHGs from the atmosphere, in addition to the company's near-term and long-term science-based targets. BVCM is a recommendation (R9), not a mandatory criterion, but the SBTi strongly encourages it.</p>

<p class="mb-4">BVCM has two goals: deliver additional near-term mitigation to help achieve the peaking of global emissions in the mid-2020s and the halving of global emissions by 2030; and drive additional finance into nascent climate solutions needed to achieve net-zero by mid-century globally. BVCM is not a substitute for reducing a company's own value chain emissions -- it is an additional contribution.</p>

<ExampleBox>
  <p class="font-semibold text-amber-800">Example: How the four pillars work together in practice</p>
  <p class="mt-2 text-amber-900">A global food and beverage company commits to the SBTi Corporate Net-Zero Standard.<br /><br />
  <strong>Near-term (Pillar 1):</strong> It commits to reduce absolute scope 1 and 2 GHG emissions 50% by 2030 from a 2019 base year, and reduce scope 3 purchased goods emissions 30% per tonne of product by 2030.<br /><br />
  <strong>Long-term (Pillar 2):</strong> It commits to reach net-zero GHG emissions across the value chain by 2050 from a 2019 base year (90% reduction).<br /><br />
  <strong>Neutralization (Pillar 3):</strong> It discloses plans to permanently neutralize the remaining ~10% of emissions through direct air capture and storage by 2050.<br /><br />
  <strong>BVCM (Pillar 4):</strong> It annually invests in high-quality carbon removal and avoidance projects beyond its own value chain, reporting the nature and scale of those actions publicly.</p>
</ExampleBox>

<h3 class="text-xl font-bold mt-6 mb-3">How to Read SBTi Criteria: The Notation System</h3>

<p class="mb-3">When working with SBTi documents, you will encounter precise language that has specific technical meaning. Understanding this language is essential -- it is the difference between something a company must do and something it merely should consider.</p>

<p class="mb-3">The Net-Zero Standard Section 5.1.1 defines the terminology explicitly:</p>

<ul class="list-disc ml-6 space-y-2 mb-4">
  <li><strong>"Shall" or "must"</strong> -- indicates a <strong>criterion</strong>: a requirement that companies must meet for their targets to be in conformance with the Corporate Net-Zero Standard. Failure to meet a criterion means the targets cannot be validated.</li>
  <li><strong>"Should"</strong> -- indicates a <strong>recommendation</strong>: best practice that companies are strongly encouraged to follow, but which does not preclude validation if not followed.</li>
  <li><strong>"May"</strong> -- indicates an <strong>option</strong>: something that is permissible or allowable. Companies may choose to do it or not.</li>
</ul>

<p class="mb-3">The Standard also uses the notation system for labelling individual criteria and recommendations:</p>

<ul class="list-disc ml-6 space-y-2 mb-4">
  <li><strong>The letter "C" preceding a number indicates a criterion</strong> (e.g., C1, C25, C28). A criterion is a requirement. All C-numbered items must be met for validation.</li>
  <li><strong>The letter "R" preceding a number indicates a recommendation</strong> (e.g., R1, R9). A recommendation is best practice but not mandatory.</li>
</ul>

<ExampleBox>
  <p class="font-semibold text-amber-800">Reading criteria in practice</p>
  <p class="mt-2 text-amber-900">In the Net-Zero Standard you will find statements like:<br /><br />
  <strong>C3 -- Scope 1 and scope 2:</strong> "The targets <em>shall</em> cover company-wide scope 1 and scope 2 emissions." -- This is a hard requirement. A company that excludes 30% of its scope 2 emissions fails this criterion.<br /><br />
  <strong>R1 -- Setting organizational boundaries:</strong> "The SBTi strongly recommends that a company's organizational boundary...is consistent with the organizational boundary used in the company's financial accounting." -- This is a recommendation. Not following it will not prevent validation, but it is considered best practice.<br /><br />
  <strong>C4 -- Scope 3:</strong> "If a company's relevant scope 3 emissions are 40% or more of total scope 1, 2, and 3 emissions, they <em>shall</em> be included in near-term science-based targets." -- Hard requirement for companies meeting the threshold.</p>
</ExampleBox>

<h3 class="text-xl font-bold mt-6 mb-3">Near-Term vs Long-Term: A Comparison</h3>

<p class="mb-3">The distinction between near-term and long-term targets is central to the SBTi framework. The table below summarises the key differences.</p>

<ResponsiveTable>
<table class="mb-6">
  <tr><th></th><th>Near-Term Targets</th><th>Long-Term / Net-Zero Targets</th></tr>
  <tr><td><strong>Timeframe</strong></td><td>5-10 years from submission; target year 2030 or within that window</td><td>By 2050 or sooner (2040 for power and maritime SDA)</td></tr>
  <tr><td><strong>Scope 1 and 2 ambition</strong></td><td>1.5C aligned (minimum 4.2% LAR for base year on/before 2020)</td><td>1.5C aligned; minimum 90% reduction (cross-sector)</td></tr>
  <tr><td><strong>Scope 3 ambition</strong></td><td>Well-below 2C aligned (minimum 2.5% LAR for cross-sector ACA)</td><td>1.5C aligned; minimum 90% reduction (cross-sector)</td></tr>
  <tr><td><strong>Scope 1 and 2 boundary</strong></td><td>95% minimum coverage</td><td>95% minimum coverage</td></tr>
  <tr><td><strong>Scope 3 boundary</strong></td><td>67% minimum if scope 3 is &gt;40% of total</td><td>90% minimum (all companies)</td></tr>
  <tr><td><strong>Target year dependency</strong></td><td>Target year dependent -- ambition changes with target year</td><td>Target year independent -- overall reduction fixed regardless of target year</td></tr>

</ResponsiveTable>

<DeepDive title="Why scope 3 ambition is lower for near-term targets">
  <p class="mb-3">You may notice that near-term scope 3 targets are required to align with "well-below 2C" rather than 1.5C. This is not a concession on ambition -- it reflects a pragmatic acknowledgement of the current state of scope 3 data quality. Scope 3 emissions cover an enormous range of upstream and downstream activities across a company's value chain, and the data to set and track 1.5C-aligned scope 3 targets at scale is not yet universally available.</p>
  <p>The SBTi's "expansive boundary" approach addresses this gradually: near-term scope 3 targets require 67% coverage; long-term scope 3 targets require 90% coverage and must align with 1.5C. As data quality and availability improve over the next decade, companies are expected to tighten their scope 3 ambition progressively.</p>
</DeepDive>
