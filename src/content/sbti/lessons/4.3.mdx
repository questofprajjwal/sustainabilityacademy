The SBTi framework was built initially around industrial and commercial companies whose emissions arise primarily from energy use and industrial processes. Over time, it became clear that a significant portion of global emissions -- and a significant number of corporate footprints -- could not be adequately captured by the standard approach. This lesson examines the FLAG framework for land-sector emissions, the SME route for smaller companies, the Financial Institutions standard for the finance sector, and how combined vs. separate targets work in practice.

## What Is FLAG?

**FLAG** stands for **Forest, Land and Agriculture**. It refers to a category of emissions and removals that arise from land use and land-use change -- activities such as deforestation for agricultural expansion, tillage of soils, livestock management, and the management (or loss) of forests, wetlands, and peatlands.

FLAG emissions are fundamentally different from fossil fuel combustion emissions in several ways:

- They include both **emissions** (deforestation releases stored carbon, rice paddies emit methane, ruminants emit methane) and **removals** (forests sequester carbon, cover crops build soil organic matter)
- They are **biological** in nature -- governed by living systems with natural variability, not just engineering decisions
- They require a **different accounting methodology** because land-use change emissions are often one-time, while sequestration is ongoing and reversible

<HighlightBox>
The SBTi published dedicated FLAG Guidance (currently version 1.1) to address land-sector emissions. Companies with material FLAG activities cannot treat these emissions as interchangeable with fossil fuel emissions -- they must follow the FLAG pathway, which defines separate abatement and sequestration requirements.
</HighlightBox>

## Who Must Set FLAG Targets?

Not every company needs a FLAG target. The SBTi's FLAG Guidance establishes a threshold based on the share of total company emissions attributable to land-sector activities:

- Companies with **FLAG activities constituting a material portion** of their total footprint must set a dedicated FLAG target in addition to (or as part of) their standard targets
- The FLAG Guidance defines specific categories of companies for whom FLAG is typically material: agricultural producers, food and beverage manufacturers, retailers with significant food and agricultural supply chains, forestry companies, and consumer goods companies with land-intensive ingredient sourcing

The threshold assessment is done as part of the Scope 3 screening process. If a company determines that its Scope 3 Category 1 emissions (purchased goods and services) include significant land-use change and agricultural process emissions, it is likely to fall within the FLAG target requirement.

<ExampleBox>
**Example: Identifying FLAG materiality**

A global food and beverage company buys significant volumes of palm oil, soy, beef, and dairy. Its Scope 3 screening reveals:

- Land-use change from deforestation associated with palm oil and soy sourcing: 1.2 million tCO2e
- Agricultural process emissions from beef and dairy production (methane, nitrous oxide): 800,000 tCO2e
- Total FLAG-related Scope 3 emissions: 2.0 million tCO2e
- Total company footprint: 2.4 million tCO2e

FLAG emissions constitute 83% of the total footprint. This company must follow the FLAG Guidance and set FLAG targets -- not just a standard Scope 3 engagement target.
</ExampleBox>

## The FLAG Pathway

The FLAG pathway is a sector-specific decarbonisation trajectory for land-sector emissions. It defines how aggregate global FLAG emissions must decline to be consistent with the 1.5C or well-below-2C scenarios.

The key features of the FLAG pathway are:

1. **Separate abatement and sequestration trajectories:** Unlike fossil fuel pathways where the goal is simply to reduce net emissions, the FLAG pathway distinguishes between reducing emissions (cutting deforestation, changing livestock management) and increasing removals (restoring forests, improving soil carbon). Both are required.

2. **No substitution between FLAG and fossil fuel reductions:** A company cannot use FLAG sequestration to offset its fossil fuel emissions in the near-term target. FLAG targets must be set and tracked separately from non-FLAG targets.

3. **FLAG-specific intensity metrics:** The FLAG Guidance specifies appropriate intensity metrics for different agricultural commodities and land types, reflecting the biological variability of land systems.

<AnalogyBox>
Think of FLAG targets and non-FLAG targets as two separate bank accounts. You cannot transfer funds between them -- a surplus in one does not compensate for a deficit in the other. Each account must be managed to hit its own balance target by the specified date.
</AnalogyBox>

## Combined vs. Separate FLAG and Non-FLAG Targets

Companies with both FLAG and non-FLAG emissions face a choice in how they structure their target commitments:

- **Combined target approach:** A single target statement covers all emission sources, but the target is set at an ambition level that meets both FLAG and non-FLAG pathway requirements simultaneously
- **Separate target approach:** Distinct targets are set for FLAG activities and for non-FLAG activities, each with its own base year emissions, reduction percentage, and target year

The SBTi generally requires the **separate approach** for companies where FLAG is material, because the science behind the FLAG pathway and the non-FLAG pathway (typically the cross-sector absolute reduction approach) reflects different decarbonisation timelines and different abatement options. Combining them risks allowing one pathway's overperformance to mask underperformance in the other.

<HighlightBox>
Under the FLAG Guidance, a company's net-zero commitment must include net-zero FLAG emissions in addition to net-zero fossil-fuel-based emissions. A company that achieves fossil fuel net-zero by 2050 but still has net positive land-sector emissions has not achieved SBTi-aligned net-zero.
</HighlightBox>

## The SME Streamlined Route

The SBTi recognises that the standard target-setting process -- with its requirement for complete Scope 3 inventories, formal validation submissions, and detailed technical workbooks -- places a significant burden on smaller organisations. The **SME (small and medium enterprise) streamlined route** was designed to make science-based targets accessible to companies that lack the resources for a full SBTi submission.

### Who Qualifies as an SME?

The SBTi defines SMEs as companies with:
- Fewer than 500 employees, **OR**
- Annual revenue below $50 million (or equivalent in local currency), **AND**
- Not listed on a public stock exchange (unless the exchange applies an SME listing category)

### What the SME Route Involves

The SME route is substantially simplified:

1. **No formal commitment step:** SMEs can bypass the 24-month commitment letter process and proceed directly to target setting
2. **Simplified inventory scope:** SMEs must cover Scope 1 and Scope 2 but can use simplified methods for Scope 3 screening
3. **Predefined target options:** SMEs select from a menu of pre-approved near-term target types (absolute reduction options at specified percentages and timeframes) rather than having to build targets from first principles using technical workbooks
4. **Optional net-zero target:** SMEs are not required to set a net-zero target, though they are encouraged to do so
5. **Lighter documentation requirements:** The submission process is streamlined, with simplified data templates

<ExampleBox>
**Scenario: An SME setting targets via the streamlined route**

A software services company with 120 employees and $8 million annual revenue wants to set science-based targets. Under the standard route, it would need to complete a detailed Scope 3 screening, use the SBTi's target-setting workbooks, and submit formal documentation.

Under the SME route, the company:
- Confirms it qualifies (under 500 employees, under $50 million revenue, not listed)
- Measures its Scope 1 and Scope 2 emissions for the base year
- Selects from the predefined near-term options (e.g., "reduce absolute Scope 1 and 2 emissions by 50% by 2030 from a 2019 base year")
- Submits a simplified form directly to the SBTi

The target is reviewed and, if the company's emissions data is complete, approved without requiring the full technical validation process applied to larger companies.
</ExampleBox>

## Financial Institutions: The FINZ Standard

Financial institutions -- banks, asset managers, insurance companies, and private equity -- present unique challenges for science-based target setting. Unlike an industrial company, a bank's direct emissions (heating its offices, powering its data centres) are typically small relative to its influence on the broader economy through its lending and investment portfolios.

The SBTi developed a separate standard for financial institutions, known as **FINZ** (Financial Institutions Net-Zero Standard), to address this.

### What Makes Financial Institutions Different?

The dominant emissions category for financial institutions is **Scope 3 Category 15: Investments** -- the emissions associated with the companies, projects, and infrastructure they finance. A large commercial bank may have financed billions of dollars of coal plants, oil fields, and high-emitting manufacturing. The emissions from those financed assets dwarf the bank's direct operational footprint.

<DeepDive title="How are financed emissions calculated?">
Financed emissions are calculated using the Partnership for Carbon Accounting Financials (PCAF) methodology. The basic approach allocates a share of each borrower's or investee's emissions to the financial institution, proportional to the institution's financial exposure relative to the total enterprise value of the borrower/investee.

For a listed equity investment:
Financed emissions = (Loan or equity value / Total Enterprise Value including cash) x Borrower's emissions

This attribution approach is imperfect -- it can double-count emissions across the financial system when multiple institutions hold different pieces of the same company's capital structure -- but it provides the most workable foundation for measuring a financial institution's influence over emissions.

The PCAF Standard (currently the most widely referenced methodology for Scope 3 Category 15) covers seven asset classes: listed equity and corporate bonds, business loans and unlisted equity, project finance, commercial real estate, mortgages, motor vehicle loans, and sovereign bonds.
</DeepDive>

### Key Features of the FINZ Standard

- Financial institutions set **portfolio-level targets** rather than company-level emission reduction targets
- Targets typically require a certain percentage of financed assets to be aligned with 1.5C pathways by a defined year
- Engagement with portfolio companies is a central mechanism -- financial institutions are expected to use their influence as lenders and shareholders to encourage portfolio companies to set their own science-based targets
- The FINZ standard requires both near-term engagement targets and long-term portfolio alignment commitments

<HighlightBox>
Financial institutions cannot currently use the standard SBTi criteria to validate their targets. They must use the FINZ standard. Companies within a financial institution's investment portfolio are assessed under the standard corporate criteria -- the distinction is about which entity submits to which standard.
</HighlightBox>

Understanding which framework applies to your organisation is the essential first step before any target-setting work begins. Proceeding under the wrong framework -- for example, using the standard criteria when the FLAG Guidance applies -- will result in a submission that fails validation, regardless of the quality of the technical work behind it.
