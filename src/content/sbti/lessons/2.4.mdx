For most companies across most sectors, scope 3 emissions are not just a significant share of their total footprint -- they are the dominant share. A consumer goods company might find that 85% or more of its emissions come from raw material extraction, ingredient processing, product use by consumers, and end-of-life disposal -- all scope 3. A financial institution's financed emissions can dwarf its operational footprint by orders of magnitude. Even a technology company making physical hardware will typically find that manufacturing and end-of-life processing generate far more emissions than running its offices or data centers.

This dominance of scope 3 makes it both the most important area for corporate climate action and the hardest to act on. Companies do not own their suppliers' factories, do not control how customers use their products, and do not have direct authority over their distribution partners' fleets. Addressing scope 3 requires influence, not just management.

## Why Scope 3 Is the Hard Problem

The fundamental difficulty of scope 3 stems from three interrelated factors:

**Data gaps:** A company has detailed records of its own energy bills and fuel purchases (scope 1 and 2). For scope 3, it typically relies on industry-average emission factors, supplier-provided data, or spend-based estimates that may be several years old and subject to significant uncertainty.

**Lack of direct control:** A company cannot unilaterally decarbonize its suppliers. It can specify requirements, provide support, and use its purchasing power as leverage -- but it cannot simply switch off a supplier's coal-fired furnace.

**Category breadth:** The GHG Protocol identifies 15 scope 3 categories. A large multinational may need to quantify and track emissions across many of them simultaneously, each requiring different data sources and calculation methodologies.

Despite these difficulties, the SBTi has built a framework -- across C6, C18, C19, and R9 -- that requires meaningful action on scope 3 while acknowledging its distinctive character.

## The 67% Coverage Rule Revisited (C6)

Lesson 2.1 introduced C6 as a boundary criterion. It is worth examining the mechanics in detail here, because the 67% rule is what directly shapes the scope 3 target portfolio a company must construct.

**C6** requires that companies set one or more emission reduction near-term targets and/or supplier or customer engagement targets that collectively cover at least 67% of total reported and excluded scope 3 emissions, considering the minimum boundary of each scope 3 category.

The 67% figure is not a soft target -- it is the minimum threshold for SBTi validation. A company that constructs targets covering 60% of its scope 3 inventory will be returned for revision.

<HighlightBox>
C6 mechanics: The denominator is total reported and excluded scope 3 emissions at minimum boundary. The numerator is all scope 3 emissions covered by any qualifying near-term target -- whether an absolute reduction target on a specific category, an intensity target on a category, or an engagement target (under C19) on a supplier or customer segment. The sum must reach 67% or more.
</HighlightBox>

How to reach 67% in practice depends on a company's emission profile. A company whose scope 3 is dominated by a single category (e.g., use of sold products for an appliance manufacturer, or purchased goods for a food company) may be able to achieve 67% coverage with a single well-designed target on that category. A company with a more fragmented scope 3 profile will need targets across multiple categories.

<ExampleBox>
**Example -- Building a scope 3 target portfolio to reach C6:**

A consumer electronics company reports the following minimum-boundary scope 3 emissions:

- Category 1 (purchased goods and services): 480,000 tCO2e (48%)
- Category 11 (use of sold products): 250,000 tCO2e (25%)
- Category 12 (end-of-life treatment): 80,000 tCO2e (8%)
- Category 4 (upstream transportation): 90,000 tCO2e (9%)
- All other categories combined: 100,000 tCO2e (10%)

Total: 1,000,000 tCO2e. Required coverage: 670,000 tCO2e (67%).

Option A -- Target two categories:
- Absolute reduction target on Cat 1 (480,000) + absolute reduction target on Cat 11 (250,000) = 730,000 covered = 73%. C6 met.

Option B -- Combine reduction target with engagement target:
- Supplier engagement target on Cat 1 covering 70% of spend (covering approx. 336,000 tCO2e) + absolute reduction target on Cat 11 (250,000) + absolute target on Cat 4 (90,000) = 676,000 = 67.6%. C6 met.

Option C -- Attempt engagement-only:
- Supplier engagement target on Cat 1 covering 60% of spend (approx. 288,000) + engagement target on Cat 4 (90,000) = 378,000 = 37.8%. C6 not met. More targets needed.
</ExampleBox>

## Two Types of Scope 3 Targets

The SBTi framework recognizes two fundamentally different approaches to addressing scope 3, each with its own criteria.

### Type 1: Emission Reduction Targets

An emission reduction target commits the company to achieving a specific percentage reduction in the absolute or intensity-based emissions of a specific scope 3 category (or categories) by a defined target year, measured against a base year.

These targets follow the same ambition standard as C18 -- at minimum well-below 2C aligned. They are the most direct form of scope 3 commitment: the company commits to measurable tonnes of CO2e coming out of a specific part of its value chain.

Examples of well-formulated emission reduction targets on scope 3:
- "Reduce absolute scope 3 category 1 emissions 30% by 2030 from a 2022 base year."
- "Reduce scope 3 category 11 emissions 40% per device sold by 2030 from a 2022 base year, using an approved sector pathway."

### Type 2: Supplier and Customer Engagement Targets (C19)

An engagement target takes a different approach. Rather than committing the company to reducing tonnes directly, it commits the company to influencing the companies in its supply chain or customer base to set their own science-based targets. The theory of change is that if a large buyer with many suppliers can drive each of those suppliers to commit to 1.5C-aligned targets, the collective impact across the supply chain exceeds what the buying company could achieve through procurement specifications alone.

**C19** governs all engagement targets. Four requirements must all be met:

### Boundary (C19)

Companies may set engagement targets across upstream or downstream scope 3 categories. There is no restriction on which categories can be the subject of an engagement target, as long as those categories fall within the company's scope 3 boundary. A manufacturer might target category 1 (purchased goods) suppliers; a retailer might target category 11 customers; a bank might target category 15 portfolio companies.

### Formulation (C19)

Companies must provide information in the target language on one of two metrics:

1. **Emissions coverage:** What percentage of emissions from relevant upstream and/or downstream categories is covered by the engagement target.
2. **Spend coverage (fallback):** If emissions data is not available, what percentage of annual procurement spend is covered by the target.

The SBTi requires that if spend coverage is used, the company must also provide an estimate of the emissions coverage associated with that spend for validation purposes -- to demonstrate that the C6 67% threshold is met, by the supplier or customer target alone or together with other scope 3 targets.

<HighlightBox>
C19 formulation rule: The target language must include a specific percentage -- either emissions coverage or spend coverage. Vague language like "engaging key suppliers" or "working with our supply chain on climate" does not satisfy C19. A number must be stated.
</HighlightBox>

### Timeframe (C19)

Engagement targets shall be fulfilled within a maximum of 5 years from the date the company's target is submitted to the SBTi for validation.

This is a stricter timeframe than the 10-year maximum permitted for regular near-term targets under C13. The SBTi's rationale is that supplier engagement is a process of influence and relationship-building that should be completed within a reasonable window -- companies cannot set an engagement target for 2035 and consider their scope 3 addressed for the next decade.

<HighlightBox>
C19 five-year rule: If a company submits to the SBTi in 2025, any engagement target it sets must be fulfilled by 2030 at the latest. The company cannot set an engagement target with a 2032 or 2035 deadline and count it toward C6 coverage.
</HighlightBox>

### Ambition Level (C19)

The company's suppliers and/or customers shall have science-based emission reduction targets in line with the latest version of the SBTi Corporate Near-Term Criteria.

This means the engagement target cannot simply ask suppliers to "have some kind of GHG target" or to "disclose their emissions." The bar is SBTi-aligned science-based targets -- validated under the same framework this course covers. The buying company is effectively asking its suppliers to go through the SBTi validation process themselves.

**R9 -- Supplier engagement:** The SBTi recommends that companies guide their suppliers toward using SBTi guidance and tools available to set science-based targets. SBTi validation of supplier science-based targets is recommended but not required. Additionally, it is recommended to use 2030 or earlier as the supplier engagement target year.

The distinction between required and recommended here is important: the ambition level requirement in C19 (SBTi Near-Term Criteria-aligned targets) is mandatory, but actual SBTi validation of the supplier's target is recommended, not required. A supplier that has internally set a 1.5C-aligned target but has not submitted it to the SBTi for formal validation can still count toward the engagement target.

## What Qualifies -- and What Does Not

<ResponsiveTable>
| Engagement Initiative | Qualifies Under C19? | Why |
|----------------------|---------------------|-----|
| "70% of suppliers by spend will have SBTi-validated near-term targets by 2030" | Yes | Specific %, right ambition standard, within 5 years |
| "65% of emissions from Cat 1 will be covered by suppliers with SBTi-aligned targets by 2029" | Yes | Emissions-based coverage, right ambition, within 5 years |
| "We will work with key suppliers on emissions reduction" | No | No specific percentage stated; no ambition standard specified |
| "50% of suppliers will disclose their GHG emissions by 2030" | No | Disclosure is not the same as having science-based targets |

| "80% of suppliers by spend will have SBTi-aligned targets by 2033" | No | 2033 is more than 5 years from a 2025 submission -- exceeds C19 timeframe |
| "100% of major suppliers will have SBTi-validated targets by 2028" | Yes | Specific %, right ambition, within 5 years -- but check what "major suppliers" covers for C6 |

## How Engagement Targets Combine With Reduction Targets to Meet 67%

In practice, most companies use a mix of both target types to satisfy C6. Emission reduction targets are used for scope 3 categories where the company has enough data quality and operational influence to commit to measurable tonne reductions. Engagement targets are used where the company's influence runs primarily through its purchasing relationships rather than through direct operational levers.

<ExampleBox>
**Example -- Combining both target types:**

A food and beverage company has total minimum-boundary scope 3 emissions of 2,000,000 tCO2e:

- Category 1 (agricultural raw materials): 1,300,000 tCO2e (65%)
- Category 11 (use of sold products -- retail refrigeration): 200,000 tCO2e (10%)
- Category 4 (upstream transport): 150,000 tCO2e (7.5%)
- Category 12 (packaging end-of-life): 100,000 tCO2e (5%)
- All other categories: 250,000 tCO2e (12.5%)

The company sets:
1. Supplier engagement target: "75% of agricultural raw material suppliers by spend (covering approximately 975,000 tCO2e) will have SBTi-aligned science-based targets by 2030." -- Engagement target under C19, covering 48.75%.
2. Absolute reduction target: "Reduce scope 3 category 11 emissions 35% by 2030 from a 2022 base year" -- covers 200,000 tCO2e = 10%.
3. Absolute reduction target: "Reduce scope 3 category 4 upstream transport emissions 30% by 2030 from a 2022 base year" -- covers 150,000 tCO2e = 7.5%.

Combined coverage: 48.75% + 10% + 7.5% = 66.25%. This falls just short of 67%. The company would need to add one more target -- perhaps covering category 12 (packaging, 5%) -- to reach the C6 threshold.

Revised set: Add "Reduce scope 3 category 12 end-of-life treatment emissions 25% by 2030 from a 2022 base year" -- covers 100,000 tCO2e = 5%.

Total coverage: 71.25%. C6 is now met.
</ExampleBox>

## Practical Guidance for Supplier Engagement Programs

R9 recommends guiding suppliers to use SBTi's own guidance and tools. This is practically important: a company with hundreds or thousands of suppliers cannot individually support each one through a bespoke target-setting process. Pointing suppliers to the SBTi Corporate Near-term Tool, providing access to SBTi training resources, and funding participation in SBTi's SME validation route for smaller suppliers are all ways of scaling the engagement program.

The engagement target itself -- the formal commitment counted under C6 -- must be fulfilled by the deadline, but the process of building supplier capacity to set and validate targets typically needs to begin years before the deadline. A company setting a 2025-submission engagement target fulfilled by 2030 needs to begin supplier outreach in 2025 or 2026 at the latest to allow time for suppliers to complete inventory work, select methodologies, and go through the SBTi validation process.

<DeepDive title="The difference between engagement targets and FLAG targets for food sector companies">
For companies with significant forest, land, and agriculture (FLAG) emissions -- food and beverage processors, retailers, agricultural commodity traders -- there is an additional layer of scope 3 requirements. These companies may be required to set FLAG targets (see lesson 4.3) that are separate from and complementary to their energy/industry near-term targets.

For the FLAG scope 3 category specifically, engagement targets must still meet C19's requirements (boundary, formulation, 5-year window, SBTi-aligned ambition). However, a FLAG near-term target covering at least 67% of FLAG-related scope 3 emissions is required separately from the 67% coverage for non-FLAG scope 3 emissions.

The practical implication: food sector companies may need a distinct engagement target portfolio for their agricultural supply chains (FLAG) and a separate portfolio for their non-FLAG scope 3 categories (energy, transport, manufacturing).
</DeepDive>

Scope 3 is where the real work of corporate decarbonization happens. A company that decarbonizes its offices and fleet but leaves its supply chain and product use-phase untouched has addressed a small fraction of its total climate impact. The C6, C18, and C19 framework forces companies to confront the full magnitude of their value chain emissions and to commit -- in measurable, time-bound, auditable terms -- to driving change where the emissions are largest.
