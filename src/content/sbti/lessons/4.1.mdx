The SBTi framework began as a set of general criteria applicable to all companies. Over time, as the initiative grew and sector-specific complexities became apparent, the SBTi developed dedicated guidance for industries where general methods are insufficient to capture the full decarbonisation challenge. Today, sector-specific guidance is a central feature of the SBTi landscape -- and for companies operating in covered sectors, it is not optional.

## Why Sector-Specific Guidance Exists

Different industries have fundamentally different emission profiles, technology pathways, and decarbonisation timelines. A retailer and a cement manufacturer both consume energy, but the routes to reducing their emissions look entirely different. The retailer's largest challenge may be Scope 3 Category 1 (supply chain) and Category 11 (consumer use of products). The cement manufacturer's dominant challenge is process emissions from calcination -- a chemical reaction that releases CO2 even if all energy is renewable.

Applying a single set of general methods across all sectors would either:
- Set insufficiently ambitious targets for sectors where deeper cuts are technically feasible
- Or set impossibly demanding targets for sectors constrained by process chemistry, long asset lives, or limited abatement technology

Sector-specific guidance resolves this tension by defining appropriate pathways, intensity metrics, and minimum ambition levels tailored to each industry's decarbonisation reality.

<HighlightBox>
Criterion C24 of the Near-Term Criteria states that once sector-specific guidance is published and applicable to a company, that company must align with the sector guidance within 6 months of publication. For companies submitting targets for the first time after the guidance is published, alignment is required from the outset.
</HighlightBox>

## What Sector Guidance Adds Beyond the General Criteria

General near-term criteria establish minimum requirements applicable to all companies. Sector guidance goes further in several ways:

### Sector-Specific Decarbonisation Pathways

Each sector guidance document defines one or more scientifically derived pathways showing how the sector's aggregate emissions (or emission intensity) must evolve to remain consistent with the Paris Agreement. For example:

- The cement sector pathway shows how the clinker-to-cement ratio and process emissions intensity must decline decade by decade to 2050
- The power sector SDA pathway shows how grid emission intensity must fall to near zero by 2050 in high-income economies and by 2045 for absolute electricity companies

Companies use these sector pathways to calibrate their own reduction trajectories. A cement company whose intensity in 2030 is above the sector benchmark has not set a sufficiently ambitious target under the SDA method.

### Sector-Specific Intensity Metrics

General guidance uses economic intensity (revenue-denominated) or physical intensity without specifying the physical unit. Sector guidance defines the exact metric to be used -- removing ambiguity and enabling cross-company comparison. A steel company must use tCO2e per tonne of steel, not tCO2e per dollar of steel revenue, ensuring that intensity improvements reflect genuine operational efficiency rather than simply rising steel prices.

### Tighter Minimum Ambition

Some sectors have available abatement technology that makes the general 4.2% LAR achievable with modest effort. Sector guidance may set tighter requirements reflecting those opportunities. The power sector, for instance, faces a pathway to near-zero electricity generation intensity by 2040 in many markets -- a far more demanding trajectory than the general cross-sector pathway.

<AnalogyBox>
Think of sector-specific guidance as the difference between a general fitness programme for all athletes and a training plan designed specifically for marathon runners. The general programme works, but the marathon-specific plan accounts for the unique demands of the event -- longer distances, pacing strategy, and race-specific nutrition -- leading to a far better outcome.
</AnalogyBox>

## Special Rules for Fossil Fuel-Adjacent Companies

The SBTi has developed specific criteria addressing companies whose business models centre on fossil fuels. Two criteria are particularly important:

### C22: Scope 3 Category 11 for Fossil Fuel Sellers

**Criterion C22** states that companies that sell fossil fuels to end users -- regardless of what share of their total revenue comes from fossil fuel sales -- must set a Scope 3 Category 11 target (use of sold products). This criterion prevents companies from ignoring the dominant source of their value chain emissions simply because they sit upstream of combustion.

<ExampleBox>
**Scenario: A diversified energy company**

A company derives 30% of its revenue from natural gas distribution and 70% from renewable energy development. Under C22, the 30% fossil fuel distribution activity still triggers the requirement to set a Scope 3 Category 11 target covering the emissions from customers combusting that natural gas.

The company cannot argue that because fossil fuels are a minority of its business, it is exempt from the Category 11 requirement. The criterion is triggered by the activity, not the revenue share.
</ExampleBox>

### C23: Who Cannot Currently Set SBTi Targets

**Criterion C23** specifies two categories of company that are currently ineligible to set SBTi targets through the standard process:

1. **Fossil fuel producers** -- companies whose primary business is the extraction and production of coal, oil, or natural gas cannot currently set SBTi targets under the standard framework. The SBTi has not yet published dedicated guidance for fossil fuel producers that would define how such companies could credibly commit to a science-based pathway.

2. **Companies deriving more than 50% of revenue from fossil fuel distribution** -- companies whose majority revenue comes from transporting or distributing fossil fuels (pipelines, fuel retail, etc.) are also currently excluded from the standard SBTi process.

<HighlightBox>
The SBTi's exclusion of fossil fuel producers and majority-fossil-fuel distributors is not permanent. The initiative has signalled its intention to develop guidance for these sectors. Until that guidance is published, these companies cannot obtain SBTi-validated targets, regardless of their ambition level. This is a significant limitation of the current framework.
</HighlightBox>

<DeepDive title="Why are fossil fuel producers excluded from the current SBTi process?">
The exclusion of fossil fuel producers from the SBTi's standard process reflects a genuine scientific and methodological challenge.

A fossil fuel producer's Scope 3 Category 11 emissions -- the combustion of the fossil fuels it extracts and sells -- typically represent 80--95% of its total value chain footprint. For a coal mining company, setting a credible science-based target would logically require committing to stop mining coal, or to do so at a rate consistent with the Paris Agreement's implied fossil fuel phase-out schedule.

The difficulty is that the implied pace of fossil fuel phase-down in 1.5C scenarios is steep and unevenly distributed across geographies. Designing criteria that are technically rigorous, commercially plausible for companies in different parts of the world, and politically defensible requires a level of sectoral specificity the SBTi has not yet finalised.

Some critics argue the SBTi should accept fossil fuel producer targets under a managed phase-out framework. Others argue that accepting such targets would lend unwarranted credibility to companies whose business models are inherently incompatible with a 1.5C pathway. This debate remains unresolved.
</DeepDive>

## Practical Implications for Companies in Covered Sectors

For a company discovering it operates in a sector with dedicated SBTi guidance, the practical steps are:

1. **Download and review the sector-specific guidance document** from the SBTi website. These are publicly available and frequently updated.

2. **Identify which methods are mandated or permitted** for your sector. In some sectors (e.g., power, steel, cement), the SDA is the required method for Scope 1; the general absolute method is not permitted as the primary approach.

3. **Confirm the intensity metric your sector uses** and ensure your data collection systems can report against it consistently.

4. **Check the publication date of the guidance.** Under C24, if the guidance was published more than 6 months ago and your company has not yet aligned with it, you are already out of compliance with the near-term criteria. Immediate action is required.

5. **Review the sector pathway to understand your required trajectory.** The sector pathway tells you not just the minimum reduction for the near-term target but the implied long-term trajectory -- which is relevant when setting net-zero targets.

<HighlightBox>
Companies with approved targets who discover they fall under newly published sector guidance have a 6-month window (C24) to bring their existing targets into alignment. This may require target revision and resubmission, even if the original targets were previously validated as compliant under general criteria.
</HighlightBox>
