Every science-based target begins with a precise answer to the question: what emissions are we actually committing to reduce? Before the SBTi can assess whether a target is ambitious enough, it must first confirm that the target covers the right organizational entity, the right greenhouse gases, and the right scopes. Criteria C1 through C6 establish these foundational boundary rules. Getting the boundary wrong -- too narrow, too fragmented, or missing key scopes -- is one of the most common reasons targets are returned during validation.

## Organizational Boundary: Where the Target Must Be Set (C1)

**C1** states that companies shall submit targets only at the parent or group level, not at the subsidiary level. Parent companies must include the emissions of all subsidiaries in their target submission, in accordance with their chosen GHG inventory consolidation approach.

This means a multinational group cannot submit a target covering only its European operations, or only its manufacturing subsidiary, while leaving other business units out of scope. The target must reflect the whole consolidated entity.

<HighlightBox>
C1 key rule: Targets must be set at the parent or group level. All subsidiaries that fall within the parent company's chosen consolidation approach must be included. Brands, licensees, specific regions, or individual business divisions will not be accepted as separate targets unless they fall outside the parent's consolidation boundary.
</HighlightBox>

A nuance arises when both a parent company and one of its subsidiaries independently submit targets to the SBTi. In that case, the parent company's target must still include the subsidiary's emissions if the subsidiary falls within the parent's consolidation boundary. The subsidiary may have its own validated target, but the parent cannot treat the subsidiary as excluded simply because it has submitted separately.

The consolidation approach itself -- whether equity share, financial control, or operational control -- must be consistently applied. Brands, licensees, and specific regions or business divisions of a company will not be accepted as separate targets unless they genuinely fall outside the parent company's consolidation boundary. Companies also must integrate emissions from structural changes (acquisitions, divestitures) into their GHG inventory within a reasonable timeframe.

**R1 -- Setting organizational boundaries:** The SBTi strongly recommends that a company's organizational boundary, as defined by the GHG Protocol Corporate Standard, be consistent with the organizational boundary used in the company's financial accounting and reporting procedures. Companies should use the same organizational boundary year-on-year. If a company's organizational boundary changes, they should refer to C27 (triggered target recalculation).

<AnalogyBox>
Think of the organizational boundary like the scope of a consolidated financial statement. A parent company cannot publish group-level financials that exclude a profitable subsidiary just because that subsidiary also files its own accounts. The same logic applies to GHG boundaries: if it is in your financial consolidation, it belongs in your emissions target.
</AnalogyBox>

## GHG Coverage: All Seven Gases Required (C2)

**C2** requires that targets cover all relevant emissions of the seven GHGs as required by the GHG Protocol Corporate Standard. The seven gases are:

<ResponsiveTable>
| Gas | Formula | Common Source |
|-----|---------|--------------|
| Carbon dioxide | CO2 | Fuel combustion, industrial processes |
| Methane | CH4 | Livestock, natural gas, landfills |
| Nitrous oxide | N2O | Agriculture, combustion |
| Hydrofluorocarbons | HFCs | Refrigerants, aerosols |
| Perfluorocarbons | PFCs | Aluminum smelting, electronics |
| Sulfur hexafluoride | SF6 | Electrical equipment |
| Nitrogen trifluoride | NF3 | Semiconductor manufacturing |
</ResponsiveTable>

A company cannot limit its target to CO2 alone and ignore fugitive CH4 from its gas operations, or exclude HFC refrigerant leaks from its retail network. All seven gases for which the company has relevant emissions must be included. The GHG Protocol's completeness principle underpins this: emissions perceived to be negligible must still be quantified and reported -- or disclosed as an exclusion -- rather than silently omitted.

## Scope Coverage: Scope 1 and 2 Are Always Required (C3)

**C3** requires that targets cover company-wide scope 1 and scope 2 emissions, as defined by the GHG Protocol Corporate Standard. There is no opt-out for either scope: both must be included in the target boundary.

Scope 1 covers direct emissions from sources owned or controlled by the company -- combustion in boilers and furnaces, company vehicles, fugitive releases, and on-site industrial processes. Scope 2 covers indirect emissions from the generation of purchased electricity, heat, or steam consumed by the company.

GHG accounting that does not adhere to the GHG Protocol accounting standard and the SBTi criteria assessment indicators will not be accepted.

## When Scope 3 Becomes Mandatory (C4)

**C4** introduces a materiality threshold for scope 3. If a company's relevant scope 3 emissions are 40% or more of the company's total scope 1, 2, and 3 emissions combined, then scope 3 must be included in the near-term science-based targets.

<HighlightBox>
C4 threshold: Scope 3 is required when S3 emissions represent 40% or more of total S1 + S2 + S3 emissions. For most companies across most sectors, scope 3 will exceed this threshold -- meaning scope 3 targets are the norm, not the exception.
</HighlightBox>

There is also an unconditional rule for fossil fuel sellers. All companies involved in the sale or distribution of natural gas and/or other fossil fuels must set separate scope 3 targets for the use of sold products (scope 3 category 11), irrespective of what share those emissions represent of the company's total scope 1, 2, and 3 emissions. A gas distribution utility cannot argue that its downstream combustion emissions are below 40% of its total inventory in order to avoid setting a category 11 target -- the obligation is absolute.

<ExampleBox>
**Example -- C4 in practice:**

A consumer goods company reports: S1 = 50,000 tCO2e, S2 = 30,000 tCO2e, S3 = 420,000 tCO2e. Total = 500,000 tCO2e.

S3 share = 420,000 / 500,000 = 84%. Since 84% exceeds 40%, this company must include S3 in its near-term targets.

A cement manufacturer reports: S1 = 900,000, S2 = 100,000, S3 = 200,000. Total = 1,200,000.

S3 share = 200,000 / 1,200,000 = 16.7%. Since 16.7% is below 40%, S3 is not mandatory -- though it is still encouraged.
</ExampleBox>

## Allowable Exclusions: The 5% Rule (C5)

No inventory is perfectly complete, and the SBTi recognizes that some emissions sources may be genuinely difficult to quantify. **C5** sets a maximum exclusion allowance:

- Companies shall not exclude more than **5% of total combined scope 1 and scope 2 emissions** from either the boundary of the GHG inventory or the target boundary.
- Companies shall not exclude more than **5% of emissions from their total scope 3 GHG inventory**.

The total targeted scope 1 and 2 emissions shall be greater than or equal to 95% of total (reported plus excluded) scope 1 and 2 emissions. This means a company cannot exclude 5% from the inventory boundary and then also exclude a further 5% from the target boundary -- the exclusion applies to the combined effect.

Where a company's scope 1 or 2 emissions are deemed immaterial (under 5% of total combined scope 1 and 2), the company may set its science-based target solely on the scope that covers more than 95% of total scope 1 and 2 emissions. However, the company must continue to report on both scopes and adjust targets as needed, per the GHG Protocol's completeness principle and C26 and C27.

The SBTi does not recognize emissions perceived to be negligible as a rationale for not reporting them. Even negligible emissions must be quantified and reported or disclosed as an exclusion.

## Scope 3 Coverage for Near-Term Targets (C6)

When a company does need to set scope 3 targets (because the C4 threshold is met), **C6** governs how comprehensively those targets must cover the scope 3 inventory.

**C6** requires that companies set one or more emission reduction near-term targets and/or supplier or customer engagement targets that collectively cover at least 67% of total reported and excluded scope 3 emissions, considering the minimum boundary of each scope 3 category in conformance with the GHG Protocol Corporate Value Chain (Scope 3) Accounting and Reporting Standard.

<HighlightBox>
C6 key rule: The collective scope 3 target coverage must reach at least 67% of total reported and excluded S3 emissions. This can be achieved through a combination of emission reduction targets on specific categories and supplier or customer engagement targets (see C19). The 67% applies to minimum-boundary scope 3 emissions only.
</HighlightBox>

The 67% coverage requirement is calculated against the minimum required boundary for each scope 3 category as defined in the GHG Protocol Scope 3 Standard (Table 5.4, page 34). Optional emissions within those categories do not count toward either the numerator or denominator for this calculation. Targets on optional scope 3 emissions are encouraged but do not count toward the 67% threshold set in C6.

**R2 -- Targets covering optional scope 3 emissions:** Targets to reduce scope 3 emissions that fall outside the minimum boundary of scope 3 categories are not required but are nevertheless encouraged when these emissions are significant. Companies may cover these emissions with a scope 3 target, but such targets cannot count toward the thresholds defined in C6. GHG accounting not proven to adhere to GHG Protocol minimum boundaries and the SBTi criteria assessment indicators will not be accepted.

<ExampleBox>
**Example -- Applying C6:**

A retail company has total minimum-boundary scope 3 emissions of 1,200,000 tCO2e across all required categories. To meet C6, it must demonstrate that its targets collectively cover at least 800,000 tCO2e (67% of 1,200,000).

It sets:
- An absolute reduction target on category 1 (purchased goods and services): 650,000 tCO2e -- 54% coverage alone.
- A supplier engagement target on category 4 (upstream transportation): 250,000 tCO2e -- adds 21%.

Combined coverage = 75%. C6 is met. The remaining 25% of categories (e.g., employee commuting, business travel) are below the threshold but are still reported and disclosed.
</ExampleBox>

## Summary: The Boundary Criteria at a Glance

<ResponsiveTable>
| Criterion | Rule | Key Number |
|-----------|------|-----------|
| C1 | Target at parent/group level; all subsidiaries in scope | -- |
| C2 | All 7 GHGs covered | 7 gases |
| C3 | S1 and S2 always required | 100% of S1 + S2 |
| C4 | S3 required if S3 &gt;= 40% of total S1+2+3; fossil fuel sellers always set Cat 11 | 40% threshold |
| C5 | Max 5% exclusion from S1+2; max 5% from S3 | 5% cap |
| C6 | Collective S3 targets must cover at least 67% of total S3 | 67% threshold |
| R1 | Boundary should align with financial accounting boundary | Recommendation |
| R2 | Optional S3 targets encouraged but do not count toward C6 | Recommendation |
</ResponsiveTable>

Getting the target boundary right is the foundation on which everything else rests. A target that covers the wrong entity, omits key gases, or falls below the 67% S3 threshold will not pass SBTi validation regardless of how ambitious the percentage reduction claim is. The boundary criteria ensure that the ambition is applied to emissions that genuinely matter at scale.
