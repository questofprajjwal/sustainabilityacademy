Setting a science-based target is not a single event -- it is a structured process with four distinct stages. Each stage has specific obligations, time constraints, and outputs. The four stages are: **Commit, Develop, Submit, and Communicate.**

<AnalogyBox>
Think of it like submitting a planning application. First you declare your intent to build (Commit). Then you engage an architect, survey the site, and draw up detailed plans (Develop). Then you submit the plans to the planning authority for assessment against the building code (Submit). Then, once approved, you display the approved plans publicly and report progress on construction (Communicate). Skipping any stage risks the whole project.
</AnalogyBox>

## Stage 1: Commit

The process begins with a **commitment letter** -- a formal declaration that the company intends to set science-based targets aligned with the SBTi criteria. Submitting a commitment letter is the starting point for large companies following the standard validation route.

The commitment letter serves several purposes. It signals intent to external stakeholders and to the SBTi. It also starts a clock: from the date of the commitment letter, companies have **24 months** to develop and submit their targets for validation. This window ensures that the commitment translates into action within a reasonable timeframe rather than remaining an indefinite aspiration.

It is important to understand what the commitment letter does and does not represent. It is a commitment to *set* targets, not a validated set of targets. Companies should not claim they have science-based targets until their targets have been validated by the SBTi. During the commitment period, a company may say it has "committed to setting science-based targets" but must not represent these as "approved" or "validated."

<HighlightBox>
**The 24-month window:** From the date of committing, companies have 24 months to submit validated targets. If a company fails to submit within this window, it is removed from the SBTi's list of committed companies. The SBTi publishes this list publicly, so removal is visible to investors, customers, and other stakeholders. This creates real accountability for companies that have made public commitments.
</HighlightBox>

## Stage 2: Develop

The Develop stage is where the substantive technical work happens. The SBTi recommends a **five-step approach** to developing science-based targets.

### Step 1: Select a Base Year

Companies need to establish a base year to track emissions performance consistently over the target period. Key requirements include:

- The base year must be no earlier than 2015
- Scope 1 and Scope 2 targets must use the same base year
- The base year should be representative of the company's typical GHG profile (avoid outlier years such as pandemic years)
- The SBTi does not accept multi-year average base years unless specified in sector-specific guidance
- For submissions in 2025, the most recent inventory provided must be no earlier than 2023

### Step 2: Calculate Emissions

A comprehensive GHG emissions inventory is required before targets can be set. The inventory must follow the GHG Protocol Corporate Standard for Scope 1 and 2, and the GHG Protocol Corporate Value Chain (Scope 3) Standard for Scope 3. It must cover all seven GHGs: CO₂, CH₄, N₂O, HFCs, PFCs, SF₆, and NF₃.

Important rules:

- **Carbon credits** must not be included as emission reductions in the inventory -- report them separately
- **Avoided emissions** must be excluded from the inventory and do not count toward targets
- **Bioenergy:** direct CO₂ from biomass combustion must be reported separately
- **Fossil fuel sellers or distributors** must report use-phase emissions in Scope 3 Category 11, regardless of share

### Step 3: Set Target Boundaries

Target boundaries define which portions of the company's emissions are covered by each target.

For **near-term targets:**
- Scope 1 and 2 targets must cover at least 95% of company-wide Scope 1 and 2 emissions
- If Scope 3 emissions are 40% or more of total emissions, Scope 3 targets must cover at least 67% of total Scope 3 emissions

For **long-term targets:**
- Scope 1 and 2 must cover at least 95% of company-wide emissions
- Scope 3 must cover at least 90% of all Scope 3 emissions (mandatory for all companies regardless of the 40% threshold)

<ExampleBox>
**Example: Setting target boundaries for a consumer goods company**

A consumer goods company has the following emissions:
- Scope 1: 10,000 tCO₂e
- Scope 2: 40,000 tCO₂e
- Scope 3: 450,000 tCO₂e (primarily purchased goods, Category 1)
- Total: 500,000 tCO₂e

Scope 3 is 90% of total -- well above the 40% threshold. The company must set Scope 3 targets covering at least 67% of its 450,000 tCO₂e (i.e., at least 301,500 tCO₂e). For the long-term target, Scope 3 coverage must be at least 90% (405,000 tCO₂e).
</ExampleBox>

### Step 4: Choose a Target Year

Near-term targets must have a target year of 2030, or within the 5-10 year window from the date of submission. Long-term targets must have a target year of 2050 or sooner -- some sector pathways (power, maritime) allow an earlier net-zero year.

### Step 5: Choose a Target-Setting Method and Calculate Targets

Available methods under Annex B of the Net-Zero Standard include:

- **Cross-sector absolute contraction (ACA):** reduces absolute emissions at 4.2% per year (1.5C, near-term) or 90% overall (long-term). Applicable to all companies and all scopes.
- **Sector-specific absolute reduction:** for FLAG, iron and steel, cement, power, and buildings sectors.
- **Sector-specific intensity convergence (SDA):** all companies in a sector converge to a shared intensity by 2050. Mandatory for the power sector.
- **Renewable electricity (Scope 2 only):** 80% renewable by 2025, 100% by 2030.
- **Scope 3 economic or physical intensity reduction:** 7% per year minimum for near-term; 97% overall for long-term.
- **Supplier/customer engagement (near-term Scope 3 only):** a target for the percentage of suppliers or customers with SBTi-approved targets.

<DeepDive title="The Forward-Looking Ambition (FLA) adjustment explained">
If a company chooses a base year earlier than the most recent reporting year (e.g., a 2019 base year submitted in 2025), a Forward-Looking Ambition (FLA) adjustment is automatically applied by the Corporate Near-Term Tool. This prevents companies from claiming credit for past reductions and ensures the target continues to drive future action consistent with the remaining carbon budget. The tool handles this calculation automatically given the base year and submission year inputs.
</DeepDive>

## Stage 3: Submit

Once targets have been developed and checked against the criteria, the company submits them to the SBTi through the **SBTi Services Validation Portal**. Submission involves providing the full GHG inventory data, target parameters, tool outputs, and all supporting information required by the Criteria Assessment Indicators. A **validation fee** is payable at submission; the fee structure varies by company size.

SBTi reviews the submission against every criterion. If criteria are not met, the submission is returned with feedback. Companies may revise and resubmit. Upon successful validation, the SBTi adds the company and its approved target wording to the publicly accessible **target dashboard**.

## Stage 4: Communicate

Validation is not the end of the process -- it is the beginning of ongoing obligations.

**Public announcement within 6 months (C28):** Companies must publicly announce their approved targets within six months of receiving validation, using the exact SBTi-approved target wording.

**Annual reporting (C25):** Companies must publicly disclose GHG emissions and progress against all approved targets on an annual basis. The reporting must follow GHG Protocol principles: relevance, completeness, consistency, transparency, and accuracy.

**Mandatory five-year review (C26):** Targets validated five or more years ago must be reviewed against the latest SBTi criteria. If they no longer conform, the company must resubmit for revalidation.

<HighlightBox>
**The four post-validation obligations:**
- **C28:** Publicly announce approved targets within 6 months of validation
- **C25:** Annually disclose GHG emissions and progress against all approved targets
- **C26:** Review targets against latest criteria if validated 5 or more years ago
- Do not claim "net zero" status until the long-term target is achieved and residual emissions are neutralised
</HighlightBox>

## Large Company vs SME Route

**Large companies** must follow the standard route: commit, develop with the full criteria set, submit through the portal with a validation fee.

**SMEs** (as defined by the SBTi) may use the **SME Streamlined Target Validation Route**, which allows them to:
- Bypass the initial commitment letter step
- Immediately set targets by choosing from predefined options
- Avoid the full tool-based calculation and criteria assessment process

The SME route significantly reduces the time, cost, and technical burden, and is appropriate where the full standard route would be disproportionate.

<ExampleBox>
**Two companies, two routes**

**Large company route:** A global manufacturing company with 15,000 employees commits in January 2024. It has 24 months to submit validated targets (by January 2026). It completes a full GHG inventory, runs the Corporate Near-Term and Net-Zero Tools, selects methods, and submits through the portal. Upon approval in June 2025, it must announce publicly by December 2025 and report annually thereafter.

**SME route:** A 45-person professional services firm goes directly to the SME Streamlined Validation Route. It selects from predefined Scope 1+2 near-term targets and commits to Scope 3 reduction, without a commitment letter or tool-based calculations.
</ExampleBox>

## When Targets Must Be Recalculated

After validation, certain business events require a company to recalculate and resubmit its targets under Criterion C27. Triggers include:

- Scope 3 emissions becoming 40% or more of total emissions (for a company that previously lacked Scope 3 targets)
- A change in consolidation approach
- Significant structural changes -- acquisitions, divestitures, mergers
- Data adjustments or methodology changes resulting in a change of 5% or more in total base year emissions

If a recalculation trigger applies and targets no longer meet criteria, the company must resubmit for revalidation.

<DeepDive title="Oil and gas sector: a special case">
The SBTi is currently unable to accept commitments or validate targets for companies in the oil and gas or fossil fuels sectors while sector-specific methods and guidance are being developed. Companies in adjacent sectors -- fossil fuel services, transmission, distribution -- are subject to specific requirements, including mandatory Scope 3 Category 11 targets for use-phase emissions from sold or distributed fossil fuels, regardless of their share of total emissions.
</DeepDive>
