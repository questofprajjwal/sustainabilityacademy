Science-based targets are not derived from what a company finds convenient or commercially palatable. They are derived from what the physical climate system requires. To understand why SBTi targets are set the way they are -- why specific reduction rates and depths are mandated -- you need to understand the underlying climate science and how the SBTi translates global emissions budgets into company-level obligations.

## The IPCC SR15 Findings and the 1.5 Degrees C Carbon Budget

In 2018, the Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change published its Special Report on Global Warming of 1.5 degrees C (SR15). The report was commissioned in response to the Paris Agreement's aspiration to pursue efforts to limit warming to 1.5 degrees C above pre-industrial levels, and its findings reshaped the ambition of corporate climate action.

SR15 found that limiting warming to 1.5 degrees C with no or limited overshoot (defined as overshoot below 0.1 degrees C) requires reaching net-zero CO2 emissions at the global level around 2050, accompanied by rapid and deep reductions in non-CO2 greenhouse gases. The implications for corporate emissions pathways were stark: the pace of reduction required is far steeper than what most existing corporate targets -- including many that had been described as "ambitious" -- were delivering.

The SBTi anchors its pathway methodology to these SR15 findings. All pathways used by the SBTi aim to steer voluntary corporate climate action such that the aggregate effect is consistent with keeping warming at 1.5 degrees C. In aggregate, 1.5 degrees C-aligned pathways used by the SBTi stay within a **500 gigatonne CO2 budget** under the assumption of approximately 20 to 40 gigatonnes of cumulative CO2 removal by 2050.

<HighlightBox>
The 500 Gt CO2 budget is the finite atmospheric "allowance" for all CO2 emissions between now and 2050 that is consistent with a 50% likelihood of limiting warming to 1.5 degrees C. Every tonne emitted today reduces the budget available for future years. This is why near-term reductions have disproportionate value -- they preserve more of the remaining budget.
</HighlightBox>

## The Four Required System Transformations

SR15 identifies four simultaneous and interlocking system transformations that must occur across the global economy to reach net-zero CO2 by 2050. These transformations are not sequential -- they must happen in parallel, which is why the SBTi requires companies to act across all relevant scopes now rather than deferring hard problems.

**1. Energy decarbonisation.** Full or near-full decarbonisation of energy and industrial CO2 emissions, achieving a zero-emissions energy supply system by mid-century. This means phasing out unabated coal, then oil and gas from power generation, and electrifying industrial heat wherever possible.

**2. Land-use change.** Eliminating CO2 emissions associated with agriculture, forestry, and land use (the AFOLU sector). This requires ending deforestation, restoring degraded ecosystems, and shifting agricultural practices toward lower-emission approaches.

**3. Non-CO2 reductions.** Deep reductions in non-CO2 greenhouse gases -- primarily methane (CH4) and nitrous oxide (N2O) -- from all sectors. While non-CO2 gases are not eliminated to zero in most 1.5 degrees C scenarios, their rapid reduction is essential because of their high near-term warming potency.

**4. Carbon dioxide removal (CDR).** Removing CO2 from the atmosphere to neutralise residual emissions and, in some scenarios, to generate net negative emissions that reduce cumulative CO2 concentrations over time. The SR15 pathways assume 20 to 40 Gt CO2 of cumulative removal by 2050 -- a significant but bounded role for CDR that underscores why deep abatement must come first.

<AnalogyBox>
Think of decarbonising the global economy like turning around a very large ship. You cannot steer through individual manoeuvres one at a time -- you need to adjust the rudder, the engine speed, and the trim simultaneously. The four system transformations are like those simultaneous adjustments. Focusing only on energy while ignoring land use or non-CO2 gases is like trying to steer a ship by adjusting only the engine.
</AnalogyBox>

## Near-Term vs Long-Term Pathways: A Fundamental Distinction

Mitigation pathways play two different roles in the SBTi framework depending on whether they inform near-term or long-term targets. This distinction is important because it affects how company targets are structured and what flexibility exists in target-setting.

**For near-term science-based targets**, pathways inform the **rate** of emissions reductions or emissions intensity reductions that are needed. The key variable is the pace of change -- how quickly must the company reduce over its chosen target period (typically 5 to 10 years). Because the rate needed depends on when you start and when you aim to arrive, near-term targets are **target-year dependent**: a company with a 2030 target year will have a different required percentage reduction than one with a 2025 target year, even if both start from the same base year.

**For long-term science-based targets**, pathways inform the **overall level** of emissions reduction or convergence intensity that must be reached. The question is not the pace but the destination: how low must absolute emissions (or emissions intensity) go to be consistent with net-zero at the global or sector level? Because this destination is fixed by the physics of the climate system, long-term targets are **target-year independent**: a company aiming for net-zero in 2040 must reach the same residual level as one aiming for 2050. It just gets there earlier.

<ExampleBox>
**Example: How target-year dependence plays out**

Two companies both have a 2019 base year. Company A sets its near-term target to 2030. Company B sets its near-term target to 2035. To remain on 1.5 degrees C-aligned pathways, Company A needs a larger percentage reduction over its shorter period. Company B has more years but must still achieve the same cumulative reduction over the full period to 2050. If Company B delays and then tries to compress the required reduction into a shorter window post-2035, it will need steeper annual reductions -- and the global carbon budget will have been further consumed in the interim.

For long-term targets, both companies face the same destination: reduce to the residual level (approximately 90% or more from base year) consistent with their applicable pathway. Whether they set their long-term target date at 2045 or 2050, the depth of reduction required at that date is the same.
</ExampleBox>

## The Cross-Sector Pathway

The cross-sector pathway is the SBTi's default method for companies that are not in the power generation or FLAG (forestry, land, agriculture) sectors, or that choose not to use a sector-specific pathway where one is available.

For **scope 1 and scope 2 near-term targets**, the cross-sector pathway requires a minimum linear annual reduction rate that depends on the base year:

- If the base year is on or before 2020, the minimum rate is **4.2% linear annual reduction** in absolute scope 1 and scope 2 emissions over the target period.
- If the base year is after 2020, a higher rate applies. This is because the carbon budget modelled by the SBTi assumed companies would begin reducing from a 2020 base year. Companies starting later must compensate for the years of budget already consumed and achieve the same cumulative reduction overall.

<FormulaBox>
Minimum Reduction (%) = Linear Annual Reduction Rate x (Target Year -- Base Year)

For a base year of 2020 or earlier:
Minimum Reduction = 4.2% x (Target Year -- Base Year)

For a base year after 2020 (Base Year Adjustment):
Minimum Reduction = 4.2% x (Target Year -- 2020)

Example -- Base year 2022, target year 2030:
Minimum Reduction = 4.2% x (2030 -- 2020) = 4.2% x 10 = 42% over the target period
Minimum Annual Rate = 42% / 8 years = 5.25% per year
</FormulaBox>

In the long term, the cross-sector pathway requires at least a **90% absolute reduction** from 2020 levels in scope 1 and 2 emissions by 2050. This 90% figure is also what most sector-specific pathways converge on or exceed, meaning that regardless of which pathway a company uses, its long-term SBT will require near-complete elimination of scope 1 and scope 2 emissions.

## Sector-Specific Pathways: The Sectoral Decarbonization Approach

For companies in certain sectors -- particularly those with emissions that are strongly tied to physical activity (output per unit of product or service) rather than to corporate-level choices -- the SBTi offers sector-specific pathways developed through the **Sectoral Decarbonization Approach (SDA)**.

The SDA allocates the global carbon budget to individual sectors in proportion to each sector's role in the economy and the feasibility of decarbonisation in that sector. This produces sector-specific convergence targets: by a given year, all companies in the sector should converge on the same emissions intensity level per unit of output, regardless of their current starting position.

Sectors currently covered by SDA-based pathways include power generation, iron and steel, cement, residential and service buildings, and transport. For these sectors -- particularly power generation and FLAG -- use of the sector-specific pathway is required rather than optional.

<ExampleBox>
**Example: Sector-specific long-term SBTs from the Standard**

The sector-specific long-term reductions required under SBTi pathways illustrate the scale of transformation expected:

- Cross-sector (most companies): 90% absolute reduction
- Agriculture: 72% absolute reduction
- Power: 97% absolute reduction (convergence to 0.009 kgCO2/kWh)
- Cement: 94% absolute reduction (convergence to 0.03 tCO2/t cement)
- Iron and steel: 91% absolute reduction (convergence to 0.11 tCO2/t steel)
- Service buildings: 98% absolute reduction (convergence to 0.18 kgCO2/m2)
- Residential buildings: 95% absolute reduction (convergence to 0.51 kgCO2/m2)

Power and residential buildings have particularly high reductions because electrification of the grid and buildings is technically achievable and economically feasible.
</ExampleBox>

<DeepDive title="Why the cross-sector pathway uses a linear reduction rate">
The cross-sector pathway's linear annual reduction (LAR) approach is intentionally simple: reduce by the same absolute percentage each year. This has practical advantages. It is easy for companies to model, track, and report. It avoids the problem of "back-loading" reductions to the final years of a target period, which would consume more of the carbon budget in the near term.

In practice, many companies will not follow a perfectly linear path. They may achieve faster reductions early through low-cost measures (energy efficiency, renewable electricity) and face steeper challenges later (industrial heat, long-haul transport, supply chain emissions). The LAR requirement sets a minimum floor of ambition; companies are free -- and encouraged -- to reduce faster.

The SBTi's modelled pathways define minimum ambition. Companies that set targets at the minimum and achieve exactly that level of reduction are meeting the standard, but the standard explicitly notes that going beyond the minimum is desirable and contributes more meaningfully to keeping warming within 1.5 degrees C.
</DeepDive>

## Why Near-Term Reductions Cannot Be Deferred

One of the most important principles embedded in the pathway framework is the irreplaceability of near-term reductions. The Standard states explicitly that near-term emissions reductions are not interchangeable with long-term targets.

The reason is cumulative budget accounting. Every year that a company emits at a higher level than its aligned pathway, it consumes a larger share of the global carbon budget. A company that delays action and then dramatically accelerates in the 2040s will have emitted significantly more cumulative CO2 than one that started reducing steadily from 2020. Those additional early emissions cannot be "made up for" by later reductions, because they have already added to the atmospheric concentration.

This is why the SBTi requires near-term targets in addition to long-term targets. The existence of a 2050 net-zero commitment with no credible near-term milestones is not consistent with the Standard. Both the rate of change (near-term) and the depth of change (long-term) must be addressed simultaneously.

<HighlightBox>
Key takeaways from this lesson:

- The 1.5 degrees C carbon budget is approximately 500 Gt CO2 remaining, assuming 20-40 Gt of cumulative removal by 2050.
- Four system transformations must happen simultaneously: energy decarbonisation, land-use change, non-CO2 reductions, and CDR.
- Near-term pathways define the rate of reduction (target-year dependent). Long-term pathways define the depth of reduction (target-year independent).
- The cross-sector pathway requires a minimum 4.2% linear annual reduction for Scope 1 and 2 from a base year on or before 2020, and at least 90% total reduction by 2050.
- Companies with base years after 2020 face higher annual rates to compensate for the consumed carbon budget.
- Near-term reductions are not interchangeable with long-term reductions -- deferring action consumes irreplaceable carbon budget.
</HighlightBox>
