Before calculating scope 2 emissions, a company must determine which energy uses fall within its scope 2 boundary and which do not. This requires applying both the organisational boundary (which legal entities and operations are included) and the operational boundary (which emission categories apply to those operations) to the specific characteristics of purchased energy flows. Getting the boundary right prevents both the omission of real scope 2 sources and the inclusion of emissions that belong elsewhere.

## Organisational Boundaries

The Corporate Standard allows companies to choose one of three consolidation approaches: equity share, financial control, or operational control. The same approach must be applied consistently across the entire inventory, including scope 2. The choice of consolidation approach determines which facilities and operations a company includes when tallying its purchased energy consumption.

Under the operational control approach, which is the most common, a company reports emissions from operations over which it exercises operational control. This has direct implications for leased assets. Under the Corporate Standard and its supplemental guidance, all leases confer operational control to the lessee or tenant unless otherwise noted. Therefore, if a company is a tenant in a leased space, any energy purchased or acquired from another entity or from the grid must be reported in scope 2.

A common scenario: a tenant in a multi-tenant building may receive heat generated by a basement boiler operated by the building management company. That heat generation typically falls under the operational control of the landlord, so the tenant reports consumption of heat generated on-site as scope 2. If a tenant can demonstrate that it does not exercise operational control in its lease, it must document and justify any exclusion.

## Operational Boundaries: What Counts as Scope 2

Scope 2 is an indirect emission category that includes GHG emissions from the generation of purchased or acquired electricity, steam, heat, or cooling consumed by the reporting company. The key qualifier is "consumed": the energy must have been brought into the organisational boundary and used there.

At least four types of purchased energy are tracked in scope 2:

**Electricity** is used by virtually all companies for machines, lighting, electric vehicle charging, and heat and cooling systems. It is the primary focus of the Scope 2 Guidance, though the accounting methods apply equally to steam, heat, and cooling.

**Steam** is used for mechanical work, heat, or as a process medium in industrial operations. It may be produced in combined heat and power (CHP) facilities that also generate electricity.

**Heat** is used to control interior climates and heat water, and in industrial processes. It may be produced from electricity or through non-electrical processes such as solar thermal or combustion boilers.

**Cooling** is produced from electricity or through the distribution of cooled air or water. It is often measured in ton-hours, where one ton-hour equals 12,000 BTU of cooling capacity.

<HighlightBox>
Scope 2 includes purchased and consumed electricity, steam, heat, and cooling. The critical boundary condition is that the energy must be acquired from an entity outside the reporting company's organisational boundary and consumed within it.
</HighlightBox>

## What Does Not Belong in Scope 2

Understanding exclusions is as important as understanding inclusions.

**On-site generation consumed on-site.** If a company generates electricity in owned or operated equipment and consumes all of that electricity on-site, with no grid connection or exchanges, the emissions from that generation are scope 1 and must not be repeated in scope 2. Including them in both scopes would be double counting within a single inventory.

**Electricity purchased for resale.** Energy purchased and resold to third parties is not "consumed by the reporting company" and should not be included in scope 2 activity data. Including resold energy would inflate the scope 2 figure and misrepresent the company's actual consumption.

**Electricity sold back to the grid from owned generation.** When a company with on-site generation sells excess electricity to the grid, it should use the total (gross) electricity purchased from the grid for its scope 2 calculation rather than the net figure after generation exports. Many markets calculate utility bills on a "net metering" basis, but for GHG accounting, gross purchases from the grid must be used.

## Distinguishing Scope 2 by Energy Production and Distribution Method

The Scope 2 Guidance identifies four scenarios based on how consumed electricity is produced and distributed. These scenarios determine how emissions are accounted for.

**Scenario 1: Energy generated and entirely consumed by the same company.** If no grid connection or exchange occurs, all generation emissions are scope 1 and no scope 2 is reported for that energy.

**Scenario 2: Energy transferred via a direct line.** If energy produced by Company A is fed directly and exclusively to Company B through a direct line connection (without entering the grid), Company A reports scope 1 emissions from generation and Company B reports scope 2 emissions from consumption. This applies to industrial parks where one facility supplies another, on-site solar or fuel cell systems owned by a third party, and central boilers in multi-tenant leased buildings.

**Scenario 3: Energy purchased from the grid.** Most companies purchase the majority of their electricity through the shared electric grid. Generators report their emissions in scope 1; consumers report their purchased consumption in scope 2. Because electricity on the grid is physically indistinguishable by source, use of specified generation can only be determined contractually.

**Scenario 4: Mixed on-site generation and grid purchases.** Some companies own or host on-site generation (such as solar panels or fuel cells) and also purchase from the grid. In this case, the company has scope 1 emissions from generation, scope 2 emissions from grid purchases, and potentially scope 2 from on-site generation if energy attribute certificates from that generation were sold to a third party.

<ExampleBox>
**Example: On-site solar with certificate sales**

A company installs a 500 kW solar array on its warehouse roof. The array generates 700,000 kWh in a year, of which the company consumes 500,000 kWh on-site and exports 200,000 kWh to the grid. The company sells all the RECs from its solar generation to a third-party buyer.

Because the company sold the certificates, it has transferred the environmental attributes of its solar generation to the REC buyer. For its own scope 2 calculation, the company must treat its on-site consumption of solar electricity as though it were purchased from the grid: it uses the location-based emission factor hierarchy (and, for market-based, the residual mix or other available factor) for the 500,000 kWh consumed on-site. The 200,000 kWh exported does not appear in scope 2 activity data.

The company separately reports 0 scope 2 for that solar generation in terms of grid purchases, but it cannot claim a zero emission rate for its own consumption because the certificates have been sold.
</ExampleBox>

## Avoiding Double Counting Between Scope 1 and Scope 2

A specific double-counting risk arises for companies such as electricity utilities or energy suppliers that own generation facilities. Such a company may report scope 1 emissions from its generation assets while also purchasing electricity from the same grid it supplies for its commercial or office buildings. When the company calculates its scope 2, the location-based grid average emission factor for that region will include emissions from the company's own generation facilities, effectively counting those emissions twice in the company's total inventory.

To address this, the guidance recommends that such companies treat their grid consumption as though it were supplied from their own generation facilities, securing a contract or instrument from their own generation unit that meets the Scope 2 Quality Criteria in the market-based method. Any energy consumption not covered by such arrangements should be treated as grid-consumed energy, reported using both location-based and market-based emission factor hierarchies.

<HighlightBox>
The rule against double counting prohibits reporting the same emissions in both scope 1 and scope 2 of the same company's inventory. However, one company's scope 1 (from energy generation) legitimately appears as another company's scope 2 (from purchasing that energy). This cross-company accounting is a feature of the framework, not a problem.
</HighlightBox>

## The Challenge of Grid-Mixed Energy and Traceability

Because electricity on a shared grid is physically indistinguishable by source, a consumer cannot identify which power plant was producing the electrons it consumes at any given moment. Grid operators dispatch generation units throughout the day based on contracts, cost, and stability requirements. This physical reality is precisely why contractual instruments, certificates, and supplier-specific emission factors become necessary: they are the only mechanism by which a specific generation source and its attributes can be reliably attributed to a specific consumer's scope 2 inventory.

The location-based method accepts this physical indistinguishability and uses statistical grid averages. The market-based method resolves it contractually. Both approaches are legitimate and serve different analytical purposes.
