<h2 class="text-2xl font-bold text-gray-800 mb-4">The ISO 14060 Family of GHG Standards</h2>

<HighlightBox>
  <p class="font-semibold text-green-800">Why learn about ISO 14064?</p>
  <p class="text-green-900 mt-1">The GHG Protocol is the most widely used corporate GHG accounting framework. ISO 14064 is the most widely referenced international standard for GHG quantification and verification. They are complementary, not competing - but understanding both is essential for practitioners working across jurisdictions or preparing for third-party verification.</p>
</HighlightBox>

The ISO 14060 family is a suite of international standards for GHG management. Six documents form the core:

## How ISO 14064 Relates to the GHG Protocol

The GHG Protocol and ISO 14064 were developed in parallel and share the same intellectual DNA. They are designed to be compatible, not competing.

<ResponsiveTable>
<table>
  <thead>
    <tr><th>Aspect</th><th>GHG Protocol</th><th>ISO 14064-1</th></tr>
  </thead>
  <tbody>
    <tr><td><strong>Nature</strong></td><td>Voluntary corporate standard</td><td>International standard (ISO)</td></tr>
    <tr><td><strong>Principles</strong></td><td>5 (relevance, completeness, consistency, transparency, accuracy)</td><td>6 (same five plus transparency listed separately from accuracy)</td></tr>
    <tr><td><strong>Boundary terminology</strong></td><td>Organizational boundary + operational boundary (3 scopes)</td><td>Organizational boundary + reporting boundary (6 indirect categories)</td></tr>
    <tr><td><strong>Consolidation</strong></td><td>Equity share or control (financial/operational)</td><td>Same two approaches</td></tr>
    <tr><td><strong>Indirect emissions</strong></td><td>Scope 2 + Scope 3 (15 categories)</td><td>5 indirect categories (b through f)</td></tr>
    <tr><td><strong>Quantification</strong></td><td>Emission factors, mass balance, direct measurement</td><td>Same, with explicit hierarchy favoring lower uncertainty</td></tr>
    <tr><td><strong>Quality management</strong></td><td>Recommended (Chapter 7)</td><td>Required (Section 8) - uncertainty assessment, document retention, internal audit</td></tr>
    <tr><td><strong>Verification</strong></td><td>Encouraged (Chapter 10)</td><td>Separate standard (ISO 14064-3) with detailed process requirements</td></tr>
  </tbody>
</table>
</ResponsiveTable>

<AnalogyBox>
Think of the GHG Protocol as a detailed guidebook that teaches you *how* to build a GHG inventory from scratch, with worked examples and decision trees. ISO 14064-1 is the building code - it specifies the *requirements* your inventory must meet, but with less hand-holding on how to get there. Most practitioners use the GHG Protocol's guidance to build their inventory and ISO 14064 to verify it meets international standards.
</AnalogyBox>

## When Do You Need ISO 14064?

Not every organization needs ISO compliance. Here is when it matters:

- **Regulatory requirements**: The EU CSRD, California climate disclosure rules, and Australia's AASB S2 reference ISO 14064-3 (or the closely aligned ISAE 3410) for mandatory GHG assurance
- **Emissions trading**: Many cap-and-trade programs (EU ETS, California) require verification per ISO 14064-3 or equivalent
- **Carbon offset projects**: ISO 14064-2 is the basis for quantifying project-level emission reductions, used alongside program-specific methodologies (Verra VCS, Gold Standard)
- **Supply chain requests**: Large buyers increasingly require suppliers to have ISO 14064-verified GHG inventories
- **International operations**: ISO standards are recognized globally, making them useful for multinational companies reporting across jurisdictions

<ExampleBox>
**Practical scenario**: A European manufacturer reports its Scope 1 and 2 under the GHG Protocol Corporate Standard. When the CSRD requires third-party assurance of its GHG data from 2025, the verifier conducts the engagement per ISO 14064-3 (or ISAE 3410). The manufacturer does not need to redo its inventory - it just needs to ensure its GHG Protocol-based inventory also meets ISO 14064-1's documentation and quality management requirements. In practice, the main gaps are usually around formal uncertainty assessment and written justification of methodological choices.
</ExampleBox>

## ISO 14064-3: Verification vs. Validation

One of ISO 14064-3's most important contributions is the clear distinction between two types of assurance engagement:

- **Verification** looks backward - "Is this statement of historical emissions materially correct?"
- **Validation** looks forward - "Are the assumptions behind this projected emission reduction reasonable?"

A third engagement type, **agreed-upon procedures (AUP)**, involves the verifier performing specific tests without issuing an overall opinion. AUPs are useful when the client needs targeted checks rather than full assurance.

<HighlightBox>
The GHG Protocol, ISO 14064, and the GHG Protocol Scope 3 Standard are all compatible frameworks that reinforce each other. An inventory built to GHG Protocol standards can be verified under ISO 14064-3 with minimal additional work. The key is to document thoroughly from the start - ISO's requirements for written justification, uncertainty assessment, and record retention are easier to meet when built into the inventory process from day one, rather than retrofitted before a verification engagement.
</HighlightBox>
