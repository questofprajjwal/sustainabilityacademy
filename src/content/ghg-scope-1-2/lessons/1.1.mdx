After setting organisational boundaries (which entities to include), a company sets its **operational boundaries**: which emissions associated with those entities to account for. This involves identifying emissions, categorising them as direct or indirect, and choosing the scope of accounting and reporting for indirect emissions.

## Direct and Indirect Emissions

**Direct GHG emissions** are emissions from sources that are owned or controlled by the reporting company. Examples: combustion in owned boilers, furnaces, and vehicles; chemical production in owned or controlled process equipment.

**Indirect GHG emissions** are emissions that are a consequence of the activities of the company but occur at sources owned or controlled by another company. Examples: emissions from a power station that generates the electricity a company purchases.

What is classified as direct versus indirect depends on the consolidation approach (equity share or control) chosen when setting organisational boundaries. The two types of boundary together constitute the company's **inventory boundary**.

## Introducing the Three Scopes

To delineate direct and indirect emission sources, improve transparency, and provide utility for different types of organisations and different types of climate policies and business goals, the GHG Protocol defines three scopes.

**Scope 1: Direct GHG emissions** occur from sources that are owned or controlled by the company. Direct emissions arise principally from four types of activity:

- **Stationary combustion:** combustion of fuels in stationary equipment such as boilers, furnaces, burners, turbines, heaters, incinerators, engines, and flares
- **Mobile combustion:** combustion of fuels in company-owned or controlled transportation devices: trucks, trains, ships, airplanes, buses, cars
- **Process emissions:** emissions from physical or chemical processes, such as CO₂ from the calcination step in cement manufacturing, CO₂ from catalytic cracking in petrochemical processing, and PFC emissions from aluminium smelting
- **Fugitive emissions:** intentional or unintentional releases, such as equipment leaks from joints, seals, packing, and gaskets; methane emissions from coal mines; HFC emissions during use of refrigeration and air conditioning equipment; and methane leakages from gas transport

**Scope 2: Electricity indirect GHG emissions** accounts for GHG emissions from the generation of purchased electricity consumed by the company. "Purchased electricity" means electricity that is purchased or otherwise brought into the organisational boundary of the company. Scope 2 emissions physically occur at the facility where the electricity is generated, not at the company's own facilities.

**Scope 3: Other indirect GHG emissions** is an optional reporting category that allows for the treatment of all other indirect emissions. Scope 3 emissions are a consequence of the activities of the company but occur from sources not owned or controlled by the company. Examples include the extraction and production of purchased materials, transportation of purchased fuels, and use of sold products and services.

<HighlightBox>
Companies shall separately account for and report on Scopes 1 and 2 at a minimum. Scope 3 reporting is encouraged but voluntary.
</HighlightBox>

## Why the Scope Framework Prevents Double Counting

The scope framework is carefully designed so that two or more companies accounting for emissions consistently will not account for the same physical emissions in the same scope. This makes the scopes useful for GHG trading programs where double counting matters.

Consider a generator that produces 100 MWh of electricity, releasing 20 tonnes of CO₂ in the process. That generator reports 20 tonnes under its Scope 1. An end user that purchases and consumes 95 MWh of that electricity reports the associated indirect emissions (approximately 19 tonnes) under its Scope 2. These are the same physical emissions counted by different entities in different scopes, which is exactly how the system is designed to work: Scope 1 emissions of one company become Scope 2 (or Scope 3) emissions of another. No single physical emission is counted twice within the same scope by two different companies following the standard consistently.

<AnalogyBox>
Think of the three scopes like three concentric rings. The innermost ring (Scope 1) contains emissions physically occurring at your own facilities. The middle ring (Scope 2) contains emissions generated on your behalf by your electricity supplier. The outermost ring (Scope 3) contains all other emissions that your activities cause but that occur at other companies' facilities throughout your value chain, upstream and downstream. Each ring is owned by a different company, preventing double counting within any single ring.
</AnalogyBox>

## Accounting for Purchased Electricity: Key Nuances

**Sale of own-generated electricity.** Emissions associated with the sale of own-generated electricity to another company are NOT deducted from Scope 1. This treatment is consistent with how other GHG-intensive products are accounted for: a cement company does not subtract the Scope 1 emissions from the clinker it sells. Emissions from the sale or transfer of own-generated electricity may be reported in optional information.

**Electricity purchased for resale.** Emissions from the generation of electricity that has been purchased for resale to end users are reported in Scope 3, not Scope 2. This prevents two or more companies from double-counting the same emissions in Scope 2. A utility company that owns a transmission and distribution (T&D) system reports Scope 2 for the electricity it consumes during T&D losses. End consumers of the purchased electricity do not report indirect emissions associated with T&D losses in Scope 2 because they do not own or control the T&D operation where that loss occurs.

## Leased Assets, Outsourced Operations, and Franchises

The selected consolidation approach (equity share or one of the control approaches) is applied consistently to account for and categorise emissions from leased assets, outsourced operations, and franchises.

- **Using equity share or financial control:** the lessee accounts for emissions from leased assets that are treated as wholly owned assets in financial accounting (i.e., finance or capital leases recorded on the balance sheet).
- **Using operational control:** the lessee accounts for emissions from leased assets that it operates (i.e., where the operational control criterion applies).
- Where the selected criterion does not apply to a leased asset or outsourced operation, those emissions are classified as Scope 3.

<ExampleBox>
**DHL Express Nordic: The business case for accounting for outsourced transportation**

DHL Express Nordic found that 98 percent of its emissions in Sweden originated from transport of goods via outsourced partner transportation firms. By requiring each partner to enter data on vehicles used, distance travelled, and fuel efficiency as part of subcontract payment schemes, DHL calculated total emissions via a tailored calculation tool for outsourced transportation. This gave a detailed picture of its Scope 3 emissions. Including Scope 3 increased the relevance of DHL's emissions footprint, expanded opportunities for reducing impacts, and improved its ability to recognise cost-saving opportunities. DHL's reported emissions were: Scope 1: 7,265 tCO₂; Scope 2: 52 tCO₂; Scope 3: 327,634 tCO₂; Total: 334,951 tCO₂. Without Scope 3, DHL would have lacked most of the information needed to effectively manage its emissions.
</ExampleBox>

The GHG Protocol provides an Appendix D that lists GHG sources and activities along the value chain by scope for various industry sectors. This reference can be used as an initial guide to identify major GHG emission sources for a given business, and to understand which scope each source falls into before beginning the detailed inventory process.

<HighlightBox>
The three scopes provide a comprehensive accounting framework for managing and reducing direct and indirect emissions. Together with organisational boundaries, they constitute the complete inventory boundary. Setting both boundaries correctly is the foundation for all subsequent calculation, tracking, and reporting work.
</HighlightBox>

<DeepDive title="Scope 3 categories in detail">
Scope 3 is optional but provides significant strategic value for many companies. The GHG Protocol identifies the following indicative Scope 3 categories: extraction and production of purchased materials and fuels; transport-related activities (transportation of purchased materials or goods, transportation of purchased fuels, employee business travel, employees commuting to and from work, transportation of sold products, transportation of waste); electricity-related activities not included in Scope 2; leased assets, franchises, and outsourced activities (where the selected consolidation approach does not apply); use of sold products and services; waste disposal; and disposal of sold products at end of life. Companies may want to focus on the one or two Scope 3 categories most relevant to their business and for which they have reliable data, rather than attempting a full life-cycle analysis across all categories.
</DeepDive>
