Business operations vary in their legal and organisational structures. They include wholly owned subsidiaries, incorporated and non-incorporated joint ventures, partnerships, franchises, and others. Setting organisational boundaries means selecting a consolidation approach and then consistently applying it to determine which businesses and operations constitute the company for the purpose of accounting and reporting GHG emissions.

The GHG Protocol provides two distinct approaches for this consolidation. Companies **shall** account for and report GHG data according to either the equity share approach or the control approach. The choice matters because the same corporate structure, applied under different approaches, can produce significantly different reported emissions totals.

## The Equity Share Approach

Under the equity share approach, a company accounts for GHG emissions from operations **in proportion to its share of equity** in the operation. Equity share reflects economic interest: the extent of rights a company has to the risks and rewards flowing from an operation.

Typically, equity share is the same as the ownership percentage. Where this is not the case, economic substance takes precedence over legal form. This principle is consistent with international financial reporting standards. Staff preparing the inventory may therefore need to consult the company's accounting or legal staff to confirm the appropriate equity share percentage for each joint operation.

<AnalogyBox>
Think of equity share like owning shares in a company. If you own 40 percent of a joint venture, you receive 40 percent of the profits and bear 40 percent of the losses. Under the equity share approach, you also account for 40 percent of its GHG emissions, because you have taken on 40 percent of the economic risk and reward associated with that operation.
</AnalogyBox>

## The Control Approach

Under the control approach, a company accounts for **100 percent** of GHG emissions from operations over which it has control, and **0 percent** of emissions from operations in which it has an interest but no control.

Control can be defined in either financial or operational terms. When using the control approach, companies must choose between these two criteria and apply the chosen criterion consistently.

**Financial control** exists when the company has the ability to direct the financial and operating policies of the operation with a view to gaining economic benefits from its activities. A company has financial control over an operation for GHG accounting purposes if the operation is considered a group company or subsidiary for financial consolidation purposes. Under this criterion, even a company with less than a 50 percent equity interest may have financial control if the economic substance of the relationship dictates it.

**Operational control** exists when the company or one of its subsidiaries has the full authority to introduce and implement its operating policies at the operation. This criterion is consistent with the practice of many companies that report on emissions from the facilities they operate (i.e., for which they hold the operating licence). Having operational control does not mean that a company necessarily has authority to make all decisions: for example, large capital investments may require approval from all partners who have joint financial control.

Under the operational control approach, a company accounts for 100 percent of emissions from operations over which it or one of its subsidiaries has operational control.

<HighlightBox>
When a company has joint financial control over an operation but not operational control, it must look at the contractual arrangements to determine whether any one of the partners has the authority to introduce and implement its operating policies, and thus has the responsibility to report under operational control. If the operation itself will introduce and implement its own operating policies, the partners with joint financial control will not report any emissions under operational control.
</HighlightBox>

The Holland Industries example demonstrates a key insight: the **same corporate structure produces different reported emissions totals** depending on which consolidation approach is used. The equity share approach captures Holland Industries' proportional economic stake in each operation, including a 56 percent stake in Nallo even though Holland Industries does not control it. The operational control approach captures 100 percent of emissions wherever Holland Industries runs the operating policies, but 0 percent where another party has that authority.

## Handling Special Cases

**Franchises.** In most cases, franchisors do not have equity rights or control over the franchise, so franchises are generally excluded from consolidation. However, if the franchisor does have equity rights or operational/financial control, the same rules as above apply.

**Leased assets.** The selected consolidation approach is also applied to leased assets. If the selected equity or control approach applies to a leased asset, it falls within the organisational boundary (typically Scope 1 or Scope 2). If the selected criterion does not apply, emissions from the leased asset are reported under Scope 3.

**State-owned operations.** The same rules apply to industry joint operations that involve state ownership or a mix of private and state ownership.

## Why the Choice Matters

Different inventory reporting goals may require different consolidation approaches. Government regulatory programs and emissions trading schemes typically require reporting on the basis of operational control, since compliance responsibility falls on the operator. For risk management and financial reporting alignment purposes, the equity share approach may provide a more complete picture of a company's full emissions liability.

<ExampleBox>
**BP and Royal Dutch/Shell: Different approaches, both valid**

BP reports GHG emissions on an equity share basis, including operations where BP has an interest but is not the operator. This approach aligns closely with BP's financial accounting procedures and captures the full economic footprint of BP's activities.

Royal Dutch/Shell, by contrast, reports on the basis of operational control, accounting for 100 percent of GHG emissions from all ventures under its operational control, irrespective of its equity share in those ventures. This approach ensures consistency with Shell's Health, Safety and Environmental Performance Monitoring and Reporting Guidelines, and gives managers clear accountability for operations under their direct control.

Both approaches are valid under the GHG Protocol. The key requirement is consistent, transparent application of the chosen approach across the entire organisational boundary.
</ExampleBox>

<HighlightBox>
Consistency at multiple levels is essential. The consolidation approach chosen at the corporate level must be applied consistently at all lower operational levels: subsidiaries, joint ventures, and individual facilities must all apply the same consolidation policy. Inconsistent application will undermine the comparability and completeness of the resulting inventory.
</HighlightBox>

<DeepDive title="ISO 14064-1 and organisational boundaries">
ISO 14064-1:2018 (Section 5.1) uses the same two consolidation approaches as the GHG Protocol: **control** (financial or operational) and **equity share**. The terminology differs slightly - ISO refers to the result as the "organizational boundary" rather than the "consolidation approach" - but the mechanics are identical. One practical addition in ISO: when a facility is owned or controlled by several organizations, those organizations should adopt the same consolidation approach for that facility. This prevents situations where shared facilities are either double-counted or omitted entirely across different organizations' inventories.
</DeepDive>
