The GHG Protocol Corporate Accounting and Reporting Standard is the world's most widely used greenhouse gas accounting framework for companies and other organisations. It was developed through a multi-stakeholder partnership convened jointly by the **World Resources Institute (WRI)**, a U.S.-based environmental NGO, and the **World Business Council for Sustainable Development (WBCSD)**, a Geneva-based coalition of 170 international companies. Launched in 1998, the initiative's mission is to develop internationally accepted GHG accounting and reporting standards and to promote their broad adoption.

## Two Linked Standards

The GHG Protocol Initiative comprises two separate but complementary standards:

- **GHG Protocol Corporate Accounting and Reporting Standard** (this document): a step-by-step guide for companies to quantify and report their GHG emissions.
- **GHG Protocol Project Quantification Standard**: a guide for quantifying reductions from GHG mitigation projects (used for offset and credit accounting).

These two documents address distinct questions. The Corporate Standard asks: "What are this company's total emissions?" The Project Standard asks: "How much did this project reduce emissions compared to what would have happened otherwise?" Using the Corporate Standard to quantify project-level offsets or credits is explicitly out of scope.

All six gases are reported in a common unit: **tonnes of CO₂ equivalent (tCO₂e)**, using Global Warming Potential (GWP) values published by the Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change (IPCC).

The Corporate Standard covers exactly the six Kyoto Protocol gases. Gases outside this list, such as CFCs and NOx, shall not be included in Scope 1 but may be reported separately as optional information.
</HighlightBox>

## Why Build a GHG Inventory?

A well-designed and maintained corporate GHG inventory can serve five distinct business goals:

1. **Managing GHG risks and identifying reduction opportunities.** A company's GHG exposure is increasingly a management issue given scrutiny from insurers, shareholders, and environmental regulators. Measuring emissions is a prerequisite for managing them. As the standard notes: what gets measured gets managed.

2. **Public reporting and participation in voluntary GHG programs.** NGOs, investors, and other stakeholders are calling for greater corporate disclosure. A GHG inventory prepared under the Corporate Standard is compatible with the Global Reporting Initiative (GRI) guidelines and most voluntary registry requirements.

3. **Participating in mandatory reporting programs.** Governments in Europe, North America, and elsewhere require large emitters to report annually. The Corporate Standard is designed to be compatible with most such programs, though companies should check any specific additional requirements.

4. **Participating in GHG markets.** Cap-and-trade programs and carbon taxes require accurate accounting of direct emissions. Some trading programs, such as the UK Emissions Trading Scheme, also require accounting for electricity-related indirect emissions.

5. **Recognition for early voluntary action.** A credible, independently verified inventory provides documentation of voluntary reductions that may be recognised under future mandatory programs.

<AnalogyBox>
Think of a corporate GHG inventory like a company's financial accounts. Without accurate financial records, you cannot manage costs, attract investors, or comply with tax law. Without accurate GHG records, you cannot manage climate risk, participate in carbon markets, or demonstrate credibility to stakeholders. The analogy runs deeper: the five accounting principles in the next lesson are directly adapted from financial accounting standards.
</AnalogyBox>

## Who Should Use This Standard?

The standard is written primarily from the perspective of a business developing a GHG inventory, but it applies equally to any organisation whose operations give rise to GHG emissions: NGOs, government agencies, universities, and others. The World Resources Institute, for example, uses the Corporate Standard to publicly report its own emissions annually and to participate in the Chicago Climate Exchange.

## Relationship to Other GHG Programs

The Corporate Standard is designed to be **program-neutral** and **policy-neutral**. It does not require companies to report emissions information to WRI or WBCSD. While it does not provide a standard for how verification processes should be conducted, it is designed to produce a verifiable inventory.

Many existing GHG programs use the Corporate Standard as the basis for their own accounting and reporting requirements. These include:

- Voluntary programs: WWF Climate Savers, U.S. EPA Climate Leaders, the Business Leaders Initiative on Climate Change
- GHG registries: California Climate Action Registry, World Economic Forum Global GHG Registry
- Trading programs: UK Emissions Trading Scheme, Chicago Climate Exchange, EU Greenhouse Gas Emissions Allowance Trading Scheme
- Sector-specific protocols developed by the International Aluminium Institute, International Iron and Steel Institute, WBCSD Cement Sustainability Initiative, and others

Since GHG programs often have specific accounting and reporting requirements beyond those in the Corporate Standard, companies should always check with relevant programs for any additional requirements before developing their inventory.

<HighlightBox>
The standard uses the word "shall" in chapters containing mandatory requirements. Guidance sections, by contrast, explain how to implement those requirements in practice. Understanding which is standard and which is guidance helps practitioners identify what is obligatory versus discretionary.
</HighlightBox>

<DeepDive title="Convergence benefits: why a common standard matters">
Before the GHG Protocol, companies used a wide variety of incompatible methods to calculate and report emissions. This made it impossible to compare disclosures across companies, verify claims, or use corporate data in aggregate for policy purposes. By converging on a common standard, businesses reduce costs because a single inventory can meet multiple internal and external reporting requirements simultaneously. For stakeholders, consistency and transparency improve, making it easier to track and compare performance over time. The widespread adoption of the GHG Protocol has also enabled the development of a global market infrastructure for carbon credits and allowances that depends on consistent, verifiable emissions data.
</DeepDive>
