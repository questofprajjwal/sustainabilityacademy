Companies often undergo significant structural changes: acquisitions, divestments, mergers, and outsourcing arrangements. These changes alter a company's historical emission profile, making meaningful comparison over time difficult. To keep comparing "like with like," a consistent approach to tracking emissions over time is required.

## Why a Base Year Is Needed

A meaningful and consistent comparison of emissions over time requires a fixed performance datum: the **base year**. This is the historical reference point against which current emissions are compared. Without a stable base year, it is impossible to assess whether a company's emissions have genuinely risen or fallen, or whether apparent changes simply reflect changes in what is being counted.

Companies may need to track emissions over time for several reasons:

- Public reporting on progress towards GHG reduction targets
- Establishing and tracking GHG targets
- Managing risks and opportunities arising from GHG regulation
- Addressing the needs of investors and other stakeholders

The inventory base year can also serve as the target base year when a company sets a GHG reduction target (see Chapter 11 of the standard).

## Choosing a Base Year

Companies **shall** choose and report a base year for which verifiable emissions data are available, and shall specify their reasons for choosing that particular year.

Most companies select a **single year** as their base year. However, it is also possible to choose an average of annual emissions over several consecutive years. For example, the UK Emissions Trading Scheme specified an average of 1998-2000 emissions as the reference point for tracking reductions. A multi-year average may help smooth out unusual fluctuations in GHG emissions that would make a single year's data unrepresentative of the company's typical emissions profile.

Companies should choose as their base year the earliest relevant point in time for which they have reliable data. Some organisations have adopted 1990 as a base year in order to be consistent with the Kyoto Protocol, but obtaining reliable and verifiable data for historical base years such as 1990 can be very challenging.

<HighlightBox>
The base year should be chosen for verifiability and representativeness, not for strategic advantage. Some GHG trading and registry programs specify the base year externally. If participating in such a program, companies should always check the applicable program rules before selecting a base year.
</HighlightBox>

## When Base Year Emissions Must Be Recalculated

Base year emissions shall be retroactively recalculated when changes in a company's structure would otherwise compromise the consistency and relevance of reported GHG information. Companies **shall** develop a base year emissions recalculation policy, clearly articulating the basis and context for any recalculations.

The following cases **shall** trigger recalculation of base year emissions:

- **Structural changes** in the reporting organisation that have a significant impact on base year emissions. A structural change involves the transfer of ownership or control of emissions-generating activities or operations from one company to another. Structural changes include: mergers, acquisitions, and divestments; and outsourcing and insourcing of emitting activities.
- **Changes in calculation methodology or improvements in the accuracy of emission factors or activity data** that result in a significant impact on the base year emissions data.
- **Discovery of significant errors**, or a number of cumulative errors that are collectively significant.

<ExampleBox>
**ENDESA: Recalculation because of a divestment**

In a deal completed January 2002, the ENDESA Group, a power generation company based in Spain, sold its 87.5 percent holding in Viesgo, a part of its Spanish power generation business, to ENEL, an Italian power company. To account for this structural change, historical emissions from the six power plants included in the sale were no longer accounted for in ENDESA's GHG inventory and were therefore removed from its base year emissions. The recalculation provided ENDESA with a complete and comparable picture of its historical emissions, allowing meaningful tracking of its actual performance over time.
</ExampleBox>

## Structural Changes vs. Operational Changes

This distinction is critical. The recalculation requirement applies only to **structural changes**, not to **operational changes**.

A structural change transfers ownership or control of existing emission sources from one company to another. It moves existing GHG emissions from one company's inventory to another's. An acquisition does not create or destroy GHG emissions at the atmosphere level; it merely transfers their accounting.

An operational change is a change in production output, product mix, the closure or opening of operating units, or other changes at facilities that remain owned or controlled by the same company. Organic growth or decline refers to increases or decreases in production output, changes in product mix, and closures and openings of operating units that are owned or controlled by the company. These changes result in actual changes of emissions to the atmosphere and therefore need to be counted as genuine increases or decreases in the company's emissions profile over time. Base year emissions are **not** recalculated for organic growth or decline.

<AnalogyBox>
Consider two companies. Company A acquires a factory from Company B. The factory's emissions have not physically changed, but they have moved from B's accounts to A's. This is a structural change requiring recalculation: A must add the factory's base year emissions to its own base year, and B must subtract them. Now consider Company C, which builds a new production line at an existing factory it already owns. The factory's emissions genuinely increase. This is organic growth: the higher emissions are recorded as an increase, not as a base year adjustment, because C's actual emissions have changed.
</AnalogyBox>

## The Significance Threshold

Whether base year emissions are recalculated for structural changes depends on the significance of the changes. The GHG Protocol makes no specific recommendations as to what constitutes "significant," but some GHG programs do specify quantitative significance thresholds. For example, the California Climate Action Registry sets the change threshold at 10 percent of the base year emissions, determined on a cumulative basis from the time the base year is established.

The policy should state any significance threshold applied for deciding on historic emissions recalculation. "Significance threshold" is a qualitative and/or quantitative criterion used to define any significant change to data, inventory boundary, methods, or any other relevant factors. Companies must apply their recalculation policy consistently for both increases and decreases in emissions.

## Timing of Recalculations

When significant structural changes occur during the middle of a year, base year emissions should be recalculated for the entire year, rather than only for the remainder of the reporting period after the structural change occurred. Similarly, current year emissions should be recalculated for the entire year to maintain consistency. If it is not possible to make a recalculation in the year of the structural change due to lack of data, the recalculation may be carried out in the following year.

## When No Recalculation Is Required

Base year emissions are **not** recalculated if the company makes an acquisition of (or insources) operations that did not exist in the base year. There may only be a recalculation of historic data back to the year in which the acquired company came into existence.

Outsourcing or insourcing does **not** trigger base year recalculation if the company is already reporting its indirect emissions from relevant outsourced or insourced activities. For example, outsourcing the production of electricity, heat, or steam does not trigger recalculation, since the Corporate Standard requires Scope 2 reporting. However, outsourcing or insourcing that shifts significant emissions between Scope 1 and Scope 3 when Scope 3 is not reported does trigger recalculation.

<HighlightBox>
Base year recalculation and organic growth are fundamentally different concepts. Recalculation maintains comparability by adjusting for structural changes that transfer existing emission sources between companies. Organic growth represents real changes in a company's own emission profile and is reflected as genuine performance change, not a base year adjustment.
</HighlightBox>
