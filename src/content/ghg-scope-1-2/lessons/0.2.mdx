<h2 class="text-2xl font-bold text-gray-800 mb-4">The Five Accounting Principles</h2>

<p class="text-sm mb-4">Every GHG inventory rests on five principles. Think of them as the ground rules that keep your emissions data trustworthy. They come from financial accounting (the same ideas that keep company balance sheets honest) and are adapted for carbon.</p>

<HighlightBox>
  <p class="font-semibold text-green-800">Why do these matter?</p>
  <p class="text-green-900 mt-1">Without these principles, two companies in the same industry could report wildly different numbers - not because their emissions differ, but because they measured and reported differently. These five rules make GHG data comparable, credible, and useful.</p>
</HighlightBox>

<h3 class="text-xl font-bold mt-8 mb-3">1. Relevance - Measure What Actually Matters</h3>
<p class="text-sm mb-3">Your GHG inventory should cover the emission sources that matter for your business and for the people reading your report. It should reflect the real operations of your company, not just what is easy to measure.</p>

<div class="bg-white border border-gray-200 rounded-lg p-4 shadow-sm mb-4">
  <p class="font-semibold text-sm text-emerald-800">Why it matters</p>
  <p class="text-sm text-gray-600 mt-1">If you run a cement company but only report office electricity, your inventory is technically "correct" but completely useless. Relevance means your boundary captures the emissions that actually define your carbon footprint.</p>
</div>

<ExampleBox>
  <p class="font-semibold text-amber-800">Practical Example</p>
  <p class="text-sm mt-2">A food delivery company initially reported only the electricity used at its headquarters. After applying the relevance principle, they expanded the boundary to include fuel consumed by delivery vehicles - which turned out to be 85% of total emissions. The original report gave a misleading picture; the revised one helped management target the right reduction opportunities.</p>
</ExampleBox>

<h3 class="text-xl font-bold mt-8 mb-3">2. Completeness - Don't Leave Anything Out</h3>
<p class="text-sm mb-3">Once you have defined your boundary (using the relevance principle), account for every emission source inside it. If you must leave something out, say so and explain why.</p>

<div class="bg-white border border-gray-200 rounded-lg p-4 shadow-sm mb-4">
  <p class="font-semibold text-sm text-blue-800">Why it matters</p>
  <p class="text-sm text-gray-600 mt-1">An incomplete inventory is like a budget that "forgets" your biggest expense. It makes the total look better than reality. Regulators, investors, and auditors will lose trust the moment they spot a gap.</p>
</div>

<ExampleBox>
  <p class="font-semibold text-amber-800">Practical Example: Volkswagen</p>
  <p class="text-sm mt-2">Volkswagen found that production processes it had classified as "negligible" in 1996 had grown to nearly 20% of plant-level emissions seven years later. New engine testing sites and magnesium die-casting equipment were the culprits. The lesson: what is small today may not stay small. Revisit your inventory regularly to keep it complete.</p>
</ExampleBox>

<AnalogyBox>
  <p class="font-semibold text-blue-800">Think of it like packing for a trip</p>
  <p class="mt-1 text-sm text-blue-900">If you forget to pack socks, the rest of your suitcase does not make up for it. A GHG inventory with a missing emission source has the same problem - the total is simply wrong, no matter how precise the other numbers are.</p>
</AnalogyBox>

<h3 class="text-xl font-bold mt-8 mb-3">3. Consistency - Use the Same Ruler Every Time</h3>
<p class="text-sm mb-3">Use the same methods, emission factors, and boundary definitions from year to year. If you must change something, document it clearly so readers can still compare years fairly.</p>

<div class="bg-white border border-gray-200 rounded-lg p-4 shadow-sm mb-4">
  <p class="font-semibold text-sm text-violet-800">Why it matters</p>
  <p class="text-sm text-gray-600 mt-1">Without consistency, you cannot tell whether a drop in reported emissions is a real reduction or just a change in how you counted. Trend analysis becomes meaningless.</p>
</div>

<ExampleBox>
  <p class="font-semibold text-amber-800">Practical Example</p>
  <p class="text-sm mt-2">A retail chain reported a 12% emissions reduction in 2023. But they had also switched from a location-based to a market-based electricity accounting method that year. Once the auditor recalculated using the original method, actual emissions had only dropped 3%. The "reduction" was mostly an accounting change, not a real improvement. Consistency would have made this obvious from the start.</p>
</ExampleBox>

<h3 class="text-xl font-bold mt-8 mb-3">4. Transparency - Show Your Work</h3>
<p class="text-sm mb-3">Document your methods, assumptions, data sources, and any exclusions clearly enough that someone else could reproduce your numbers. If you estimated something, say so. If you left something out, explain why.</p>

<div class="bg-white border border-gray-200 rounded-lg p-4 shadow-sm mb-4">
  <p class="font-semibold text-sm text-amber-800">Why it matters</p>
  <p class="text-sm text-gray-600 mt-1">A number without context is just a number. Transparency is what turns raw data into credible information. It is also what makes verification possible - an auditor cannot check what is not documented.</p>
</div>

<ExampleBox>
  <p class="font-semibold text-amber-800">Practical Example</p>
  <p class="text-sm mt-2">Two factories both report 50,000 tCO2e. Factory A provides a full breakdown: which emission factors it used, that it estimated refrigerant leakage at 5% of system capacity, and that Scope 2 was calculated using regional grid factors from the national registry. Factory B just reports "50,000 tCO2e." An investor has no way to evaluate Factory B's number. Factory A's report can be checked, challenged, and trusted.</p>
</ExampleBox>

<h3 class="text-xl font-bold mt-8 mb-3">5. Accuracy - Get As Close to Reality As You Can</h3>
<p class="text-sm mb-3">Your numbers should not consistently overstate or understate actual emissions. Where uncertainty exists, reduce it as much as practical and disclose what remains.</p>

<div class="bg-white border border-gray-200 rounded-lg p-4 shadow-sm mb-4">
  <p class="font-semibold text-sm text-rose-800">Why it matters</p>
  <p class="text-sm text-gray-600 mt-1">Overstating emissions wastes resources on reductions that are not real. Understating emissions hides risk and misleads stakeholders. Accuracy means being honest about what you know and do not know.</p>
</div>

<ExampleBox>
  <p class="font-semibold text-amber-800">Practical Example: The Body Shop</p>
  <p class="text-sm mt-2">The Body Shop operates nearly 2,000 stores across 51 countries. Many stores inside shopping centres could not get separate energy meter readings. They solved this with a two-tier approach: stores that could get real data used it directly; stores that could not were given standardised estimation guidelines based on floor area, equipment, and operating hours. This gave them both completeness (all stores included) and reasonable accuracy (estimates were based on real parameters, not guesses).</p>
</ExampleBox>

<HighlightBox>
  <p class="font-semibold text-green-800">The principles work together</p>
  <p class="text-green-900 mt-1">No single principle works in isolation. Completeness without transparency cannot be verified. Accuracy without consistency does not support year-on-year comparison. Relevance sets the scope within which the other four operate. When you face a tough decision about your inventory, test it against all five principles, not just one.</p>
</HighlightBox>

<DeepDive title="How these relate to financial accounting (GAAP)">
These five principles are deliberately modelled on Generally Accepted Accounting Principles (GAAP) from financial reporting. Materiality maps to relevance. Faithful representation maps to accuracy. Comparability maps to consistency. This alignment is intentional: it makes GHG data familiar to financial analysts and auditors, supports integrated reporting, and increases the likelihood that future accounting standards will treat carbon liabilities consistently with how companies already measure emissions.
</DeepDive>

<DeepDive title="ISO 14064-1: A sixth principle?">
The GHG Protocol's five principles - relevance, completeness, consistency, transparency, and accuracy - are mirrored almost exactly in ISO 14064-1:2018 (Section 4). ISO adds a sixth: **transparency** is listed as a standalone principle rather than being grouped with the others. In practice, the two frameworks are fully compatible. ISO 14064-1 defines transparency as "disclose sufficient and appropriate GHG-related information to allow intended users to make decisions with reasonable confidence." If your inventory satisfies the GHG Protocol's five principles, it will also satisfy ISO 14064-1's requirements. The key difference is emphasis: ISO places more weight on documenting, explaining, and justifying every methodological choice - not just disclosing it.
</DeepDive>
