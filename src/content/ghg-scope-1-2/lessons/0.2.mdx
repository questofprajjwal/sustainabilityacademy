GHG accounting and reporting practices are evolving, and they are new to many organisations. The five principles that underpin the GHG Protocol Corporate Standard are derived in part from generally accepted financial accounting and reporting principles, adapted to the specific challenges of quantifying greenhouse gas emissions. Their primary function is to guide implementation, particularly when the application of the standards to specific situations is ambiguous.

GHG accounting and reporting **shall** be based on the following five principles.

## Relevance

The GHG inventory shall appropriately reflect the GHG emissions of the company and serve the decision-making needs of users, both internal and external.

Relevance is primarily about selecting an appropriate inventory boundary. That boundary should reflect the substance and economic reality of the company's business relationships, not merely its legal form. When defining the boundary, practitioners should consider:

- Organisational structures: control (operational and financial), ownership, legal agreements, joint ventures
- Operational boundaries: on-site and off-site activities, processes, services, and impacts
- Business context: the nature of activities, geographic locations, industry sector, purposes of information, and users

A relevant inventory is one that contains the information that users actually need for their decisions. An inventory that technically complies with reporting rules but omits material emission sources is not serving its intended purpose.

## Completeness

All GHG emission sources and sinks within the chosen inventory boundary shall be accounted for and reported. Any specific exclusions shall be disclosed and justified.

In practice, data may be unavailable or costly to gather for some sources. The standard explicitly addresses one common temptation: defining a "materiality threshold" below which sources can be omitted without quantification. The GHG Protocol rejects this approach as the basis for completeness.

<HighlightBox>
A materiality threshold is technically a predefined and accepted negative bias, meaning a systematic underestimate. To apply a threshold, a company would first need to quantify the source to confirm it falls below the threshold. But once quantified, most of the benefit of the threshold is lost anyway. The GHG Protocol requires companies to make a good faith effort to provide a complete inventory. Materiality thresholds are relevant for determining whether an error or omission constitutes a material discrepancy for verification purposes. They are not a licence to exclude sources without quantification.
</HighlightBox>

Where emissions have not been estimated, or have been estimated at an insufficient level of quality, this must be transparently documented and justified. Verifiers can then assess the potential impact of the exclusion on the overall inventory report.

<ExampleBox>
**Volkswagen: Maintaining completeness over time**

Volkswagen discovered that its emission source composition had changed substantially over seven years. Emissions from production processes that were considered irrelevant at a corporate level in 1996 came to constitute almost 20 percent of aggregated GHG emissions at the relevant plant sites. New sites for engine testing and investment in magnesium die-casting equipment had grown from negligible to significant. This case illustrates that emission sources must be regularly reassessed to maintain a complete inventory over time.
</ExampleBox>

## Consistency

Consistent methodologies shall be used to allow for meaningful comparison of emissions over time. Any changes to data, inventory boundary, methods, or any other relevant factors in the time series shall be transparently documented.

Consistency is what enables trend analysis. If a company changes its calculation methodology, updates emission factors, or revises its boundary, reported emissions will change even if actual physical emissions have not. Without consistent, documented methodology, users cannot tell whether a reported reduction reflects genuine performance improvement or simply a change in accounting approach.

The GHG information for all operations within a company's inventory boundary needs to be compiled in a manner that ensures aggregate information is internally consistent and comparable over time.

## Transparency

All relevant issues shall be addressed in a factual and coherent manner, based on a clear audit trail. Relevant assumptions shall be disclosed, and appropriate references shall be made to the accounting and calculation methodologies and data sources used.

Transparency is about the degree to which the processes, procedures, assumptions, and limitations of the GHG inventory are clearly identified and documented. Information must be recorded, compiled, and analysed in a way that enables internal reviewers and external verifiers to attest to its credibility.

A transparent report provides sufficient information that a third party, given access to the same source data, could derive the same results. Specific exclusions or inclusions must be clearly identified and justified. Assumptions must be disclosed. References to methodologies and data sources must be provided.

<AnalogyBox>
Think of transparency like the notes to a company's financial statements. The headline numbers on an income statement are useful, but auditors and investors need the accompanying notes that explain which accounting policies were applied, which estimates were made, and where significant uncertainty exists. A GHG inventory without documented methodology and assumptions is like financial statements without notes: the numbers may be correct, but they cannot be independently verified.
</AnalogyBox>

An independent external verification is a particularly effective way of ensuring that an appropriate audit trail has been established and that the required documentation has been provided.

## Accuracy

The quantification of GHG emissions shall be systematically neither over nor under actual emissions, as far as can be judged. Uncertainties shall be reduced as far as practicable. The quantification process shall be conducted in a manner that minimises uncertainty.

Accuracy is not the same as precision. An accurate inventory is one where errors and biases are identified and minimised, and where remaining uncertainty is disclosed. The standard requires sufficient accuracy to enable users to make decisions with reasonable assurance as to the integrity of the reported information.

<ExampleBox>
**The Body Shop: Solving the trade-off between accuracy and completeness**

The Body Shop operates nearly 2,000 locations across 51 countries. For stores located within shopping centres, energy consumption data were often unavailable and difficult to disaggregate. Inaccurate estimates for these stores created a dilemma: including them added unreliable data, but excluding them created an incomplete inventory.

The Body Shop implemented a two-tier solution. Stores were first encouraged to pursue direct consumption data through disaggregated meter readings or direct monitoring. Where direct data remained unavailable, stores were given standardised guidelines for estimating emissions based on square footage, equipment type, and usage hours. This approach provided greater accuracy and a more complete inventory than the previous fragmentary approach, and the remaining estimation uncertainty was made transparent to report users.
</ExampleBox>

<HighlightBox>
The five principles interact. Completeness without transparency is not verifiable. Accuracy without consistency does not support trend analysis. Relevance sets the scope within which completeness, consistency, transparency, and accuracy all operate. When facing a difficult methodological choice, test the option against all five principles, not just one.
</HighlightBox>

<DeepDive title="How these principles relate to financial accounting">
The five GHG accounting principles are intentionally analogous to the generally accepted accounting principles (GAAP) used in financial reporting. Financial accounting uses concepts of materiality, faithful representation, comparability, and understandability that map closely to the GHG Protocol's relevance, accuracy, consistency, and transparency. This alignment is deliberate: it makes GHG data more familiar to financial analysts and auditors, supports integrated reporting, and increases the likelihood that future financial accounting standards will treat GHG-related liabilities in a way that is consistent with how companies have already been measuring their emissions.
</DeepDive>
