A GHG inventory that is never shared externally has limited strategic value. Chapter 9 of the GHG Protocol Corporate Standard defines what a credible public GHG report must contain, what is optional but recommended, and how reporting requirements differ across different programs and purposes. The guiding principle is straightforward: reported information shall be relevant, complete, consistent, transparent, and accurate.

## Mandatory Reporting Requirements

The GHG Protocol Corporate Standard requires that any public GHG report claiming conformance with the standard must include, at minimum, the following categories of information.

### Company and Inventory Boundary

- An outline of the organisational boundaries chosen, including the consolidation approach used (equity share, financial control, or operational control).
- An outline of the operational boundaries chosen, including which scope 3 categories are covered if scope 3 is reported.
- The reporting period covered.

### Emissions Information

- Total scope 1 and scope 2 emissions, reported independently of any GHG trades such as purchases or sales of allowances, offsets, or credits.
- Emissions data reported separately for each scope.
- Emissions data for all six Kyoto Protocol GHGs separately: CO2, CH4, N2O, HFCs, PFCs, and SF6, expressed both in metric tonnes of each gas and in tonnes of CO2 equivalent.
- The base year chosen, the base year emissions figure, and the emissions profile over time consistent with the chosen base year recalculation policy.
- Appropriate context for any significant emissions changes that triggered a base year recalculation (acquisitions, divestitures, outsourcing, changes in reporting boundaries, or changes in calculation methodologies).
- Direct CO2 emissions from biologically sequestered carbon (for example, CO2 from burning biomass or biofuels), reported separately from the scopes.
- Methodologies used to calculate or measure emissions, including a reference or link to any calculation tools used.
- Any specific exclusions of sources, facilities, or operations, with justification.

<HighlightBox>
The standard requires Scope 1 and Scope 2 totals to be reported **gross** of any GHG trades. Purchased offsets or allowances do not reduce what is reported as physical inventory emissions. These are disclosed separately as optional information.
</HighlightBox>

## Optional but Recommended Reporting

In addition to mandatory categories, a well-structured public report should include the following where applicable:

**Emissions and performance:**
- Emissions data from relevant scope 3 activities for which reliable data can be obtained.
- Emissions further subdivided by business unit, facility, country, source type (stationary combustion, process, fugitive), or activity type.
- Relevant ratio performance indicators, also called intensity ratios (see the section on intensity ratios below).
- An outline of any GHG management or reduction programs.
- Information on inventory quality, including the causes and magnitude of uncertainties, and policies in place to improve quality.

**Offsets:**
- Details of any offsets purchased or developed outside the inventory boundary, specifying the type of project, geographic and organisational origin, how the offsets were quantified, and whether they have been verified or recognised by an external GHG program.

## Intensity Ratios

The GHG Protocol Corporate Standard requires reporting of absolute emissions. Reporting of intensity (or ratio) indicators is optional. However, intensity ratios are widely used because they allow performance comparisons over time and across organisations of different sizes.

There are three main types:

- **Intensity ratios** express GHG impact per unit of physical activity or economic output (for example, tonnes CO2e per tonne of product produced, or per MWh of electricity generated). A declining intensity ratio reflects a performance improvement.
- **Productivity or efficiency ratios** express the value or output of a business divided by its GHG impact (for example, sales per tonne CO2e). An increasing ratio reflects improvement.
- **Percentages** express current GHG emissions as a proportion of base year emissions.

<ExampleBox>
**MidAmerican Energy Holdings Company** adopted a GHG intensity indicator measuring direct emissions in pounds per megawatt-hour generated. In 2001, its Iowa utility business emitted approximately 23 million tonnes of CO2 while generating approximately 21 million megawatt-hours, giving an intensity of approximately 2,177 lbs/MWh. As newer, more efficient combined-cycle gas plants and wind generation are added to its portfolio, total absolute emissions may increase, but the intensity indicator will decline, reflecting genuine performance improvement per unit of electricity produced.
</ExampleBox>

<AnalogyBox>
Intensity ratios are like fuel economy figures for cars. Two vehicles may consume very different absolute quantities of fuel, but comparing litres per 100 km lets you assess efficiency independently of how much each is driven. Similarly, comparing GHG intensity lets you assess emissions performance independently of how much a company produces.
</AnalogyBox>

## How Reporting Requirements Differ by Context

Not every GHG report is a public corporate sustainability report. The appropriate level of detail and the specific requirements depend on the purpose and audience.

**Voluntary programs (CDP, GRI):** The Carbon Disclosure Project (CDP) requests detailed responses aligned closely with the GHG Protocol Corporate Standard, including scope 1, scope 2, and significant scope 3 categories, as well as targets and reduction strategies. GRI Standards reference the GHG Protocol methodology directly and require disclosure of gross scope 1 and scope 2 emissions with references to methodologies used.

**Regulatory frameworks:** Mandatory national or regional programs (such as the EU Emissions Trading System or national greenhouse gas reporting regulations) may specify their own calculation methodologies, reporting formats, and verification requirements. These may differ in boundary definitions, gas coverage, or the treatment of biomass emissions compared to the voluntary standard.

**Internal management reporting:** Management-focused GHG reporting is typically more granular, broken down by facility, business unit, or cost centre, and focused on trend tracking and target performance rather than public communication. Confidential business data (specific facility-level figures, for example) may be included that would not be disclosed publicly.

<HighlightBox>
The standard requires that even a summary or abbreviated public report must provide a link or reference to a publicly available full report containing all required information. Not every document needs to be exhaustive, but the full data must be accessible.
</HighlightBox>

## Avoiding Double Counting

Companies must take care to identify and exclude from reporting any scope 2 or scope 3 emissions that are also accounted for as scope 1 emissions by other facilities, business units, or companies included in the inventory consolidation. When rolling up data from facilities to the corporate level under either a centralised or decentralised approach, staff must verify that emissions are not counted twice across organisational boundaries.

<DeepDive title="What to include when reporting material discrepancies">
Chapter 9 recommends that a public GHG report communicate any material discrepancies identified in prior years' data. If a recalculation of historical emissions was necessary due to a methodology change or a significant structural change such as an acquisition, the report should clearly explain what changed, why, and the quantitative effect on the historical emissions profile. This transparency protects the company's credibility and helps stakeholders interpret year-over-year comparisons correctly.
</DeepDive>
