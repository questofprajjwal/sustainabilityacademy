A scope 2 inventory is most useful when it can be compared over time. This requires a stable baseline, a clearly defined reduction target, and consistent methodology for measuring progress. Chapter 9 of the Scope 2 Guidance covers how to set a base year, when to recalculate it, how to set GHG and energy reduction targets, and how the market-based method enables companies to demonstrate the impact of renewable energy procurement against a target. Chapter 11 addresses how corporate procurement can drive genuine changes in electricity supply over time.

## Setting a Base Year for Scope 2

A base year is a fixed historical reference point against which emission reductions are measured. Without a base year, year-on-year comparisons are meaningless because they conflate changes in the business (acquisitions, divestitures, output changes) with actual emission intensity improvements.

When choosing a base year, companies should select a year in which both market-based and location-based data are available, so that progress can be tracked across both methods. Companies that have already established a base year for scope 2 must specify which method was used to calculate it, in order to allow for clear comparison over time.

If the base year was calculated only using the location-based method and a company now also needs to report a market-based total, the company should recalculate a market-based base-year total if contractual information or residual mix totals are available for that base year. If not, the company should state that location-based data has been used as a proxy for the market-based method in the base year.

## Recalculating Base-Year Emissions

The same triggers for base-year recalculation that apply under the Corporate Standard also apply to scope 2. Recalculation may be necessary when changes to base-year emissions exceed the company's established significance threshold. This can occur when a company:

- Restructures its operations through acquisitions, divestitures, or mergers
- Discovers calculation errors
- Changes its calculation methodology
- Identifies improvements in data accuracy over time

A specific trigger unique to scope 2: the Scope 2 Guidance's introduction of dual reporting (location-based and market-based) constitutes a change in calculation methodology that could require base-year recalculation for companies that previously reported only one scope 2 figure.

Companies must ensure that the base-year inventory includes both a location-based and a market-based scope 2 total, where applicable and feasible. This ensures "like with like" comparison over time.

<HighlightBox>
Base-year recalculation does not occur every time emission factors change. Updates to emission factor values (for example, when the EPA releases a new version of eGRID) represent a change in data, not a methodology change. Prior years' emissions do not need to be adjusted for updated emission factors; only the current year uses the newest available factors.
</HighlightBox>

## Setting GHG Reduction Targets for Scope 2

Companies are not required to set scope 2 reduction targets, but the guidance strongly encourages considering target-setting in the context of business goals, the decision-making value of each method's results, and how procurement can drive supply changes.

If a company sets a scope 2 reduction target, it must specify which method is used for the goal calculation and progress tracking, and it must disclose the method used for the base-year calculation. Several target design choices require consideration:

**Target type.** Companies can set an absolute target (a fixed reduction in total metric tons CO2e) or an intensity target (a reduction in emissions per unit of output, such as per unit of revenue, per square metre, or per product). Intensity targets are useful when significant changes in business scale are expected, but absolute targets are generally more directly linked to global emission reduction requirements.

**Target completion date.** Short-term and long-term targets serve different purposes. Short-term targets drive near-term procurement decisions, while long-term targets (aligned with science-based pathways, for example) require structural changes to energy supply and efficiency.

**Choice of method for goal tracking.** Where certificates or contractual instruments convey legally enforceable claims, the guidance recommends using the market-based method total for goal tracking, because it directly reflects the company's contractual position. This creates coherence between what the company commits to do (make specific procurement choices) and what it measures.

## Energy Targets and Their Link to Emissions

Some companies set energy targets alongside or instead of GHG targets. Energy targets maintain focus on efficiency and isolate the role of consumption from supply changes.

**Energy intensity goals** reduce energy per unit of output (e.g., kWh per square metre of floor space, or kWh per product unit). Reducing energy consumption is the most direct way to reduce scope 2 emissions and is reflected in both location-based and market-based totals through the activity data.

**Renewable energy procurement goals** set a target percentage of total electricity consumption to be supplied by renewable energy. The framework for scope 2 emissions accounting, with its separation by method, applies here as well. A company needs to clarify whether its renewable energy goal is based on a location-based assessment of renewable generation on the grid (showing what percentage of physical grid supply is renewable), or on contractual procurement using instruments that convey a claim about resource identity and use. The 100 percent renewable energy goals adopted by many companies are typically based on the market-based method, requiring sufficient certificates to match total consumption.

## How the Market-Based Method Enables Target Demonstration

The market-based method enables a company to demonstrate the direct impact of its renewable energy procurement decisions against its reduction target. When a company retires certificates equal to its total electricity consumption and those certificates carry a zero emission factor, its market-based scope 2 total becomes zero. This is a direct, verifiable accounting outcome tied to specific procurement actions.

This is why companies setting renewable energy goals typically set market-based targets: the market-based method is the only one that directly and immediately reflects a procurement decision in the reported emission figure.

The location-based result continues to be reported alongside the market-based result. It shows whether the physical grid on which the company operates is decarbonising over time, which is a separate and important indicator. A company achieving a zero market-based scope 2 while operating in a high-carbon grid region still has an incentive to support grid decarbonisation over time, because its location-based total shows the carbon intensity risk associated with its physical presence on that grid.

## How Companies Can Drive Electricity Supply Changes

Chapter 11 of the Scope 2 Guidance addresses a deeper question: can corporate procurement actually change what gets built on the grid, or does it simply reallocate existing attributes among consumers?

The market-based method for scope 2 accounting represents an internationally applicable framework for allowing suppliers and consumers to express demand for specific types of generation. It is based on the same economic theory underlying all other markets: as demand grows for a specific type of product, it pushes up the price of that product's attributes, which in turn can stimulate additional supply. Certificates serve as the demand signal and revenue stream that can make new generation projects economically viable.

<ExampleBox>
**Long-term PPAs and new generation development**

Market research indicates that the effect of voluntary demand on new renewable energy project development is more strongly associated with long-term contracts for energy from projects not yet built than with the price of unbundled certificates on secondary markets. A company signing a 15-year PPA for energy from a new solar facility provides the long-term revenue certainty that project developers and lenders need to finance construction. This is a more direct contribution to new supply development than purchasing vintage certificates from existing facilities.
</ExampleBox>

The guidance does not require instruments to meet "additionality" criteria (that the project would not have been built without the purchase). The market-based method allocates generation attributes among consumers regardless of project age or reason for construction. But companies seeking to maximise their contribution to supply change can take specific steps that go beyond the minimum requirements:

**Supporting new project development.** Companies can sign long-term PPAs with facilities that have not yet been built, providing the financial certainty that enables project financing. This is a more direct contribution to new supply than purchasing certificates from long-established facilities.

**Geographic matching.** Companies can seek certificates from facilities in the same grid region as their consuming operations. This ensures that the generation whose attributes they claim is physically part of the supply mix serving their facilities, strengthening the relationship between procurement and local grid decarbonisation.

**Temporal matching.** Some companies seek hourly or monthly matching between certificate vintage and consumption rather than annual matching. This finer-grained approach ensures that claimed generation corresponds to consumption patterns, including during times when variable renewable generation (wind and solar) may be less available.

**Engaging beyond certificates.** Companies can negotiate with their utility or supplier to supply low-carbon energy, create on-site low-carbon generation projects, or establish contracts that include both energy delivery and certificates from new projects. These actions combine the procurement signal with more direct physical and financial engagement with supply.

<HighlightBox>
The key insight from Chapter 11: substantially changing a grid's resource mix over time generally requires aggregate consumer decisions, often involving large corporate consumers representing a significant percentage of a utility's load. Individual corporate procurement choices matter not just for the company's own scope 2 total, but as part of the collective signal that drives energy system transformation.
</HighlightBox>
