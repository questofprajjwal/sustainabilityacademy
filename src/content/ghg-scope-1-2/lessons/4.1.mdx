Chapter 7 of the Scope 2 Guidance identifies all new accounting and reporting requirements introduced by the guidance. Conformance with these requirements is necessary to prepare an inventory in conformance with the GHG Protocol Corporate Standard. Companies must understand both what they are required to report and what the guidance recommends or permits as additional disclosure.

## Required Information for Scope 2

The guidance creates a fork in reporting requirements based on whether a company operates in markets that provide product or supplier-specific data in the form of contractual instruments.

**Companies with operations only in markets without contractual instruments** are required to report only one scope 2 result, based on the location-based method.

**Companies with any operations in markets providing contractual instruments** (currently including the EU Economic Area, the US, Australia, most Latin American countries, Japan, India, and many others) must account and report scope 2 emissions in two ways, labelling each result according to the method: one based on the location-based method, and one based on the market-based method meeting Scope 2 Quality Criteria.

For multi-regional companies with some operations in markets with contractual instruments and some without: emissions from operations in locations that do not support a market-based approach must be calculated using the location-based method (making those operations' results identical for both methods), and a market-based total must still be calculated for the entire corporate inventory to ensure completeness and consistency.

<HighlightBox>
The dual reporting requirement applies at the corporate inventory level. A company with even one facility in a market where contractual instruments exist must report both a location-based and a market-based scope 2 total for the entire inventory.
</HighlightBox>

## Scope 2 Quality Criteria for Market-Based Instruments

A central contribution of the Scope 2 Guidance is the establishment of Scope 2 Quality Criteria. These criteria define the minimum features that contractual instruments must possess in order to be used in a market-based scope 2 calculation. Without meeting these criteria, an instrument's emission factor cannot be applied.

There are eight criteria:

**Criterion 1: Conveying GHG emission rate claims.** The instrument must convey the direct GHG emission rate attribute associated with the unit of electricity produced. In the US, most states define RECs as conveying "all environmental attributes" associated with MWh of generation, which includes the GHG emission rate. A fully aggregated certificate prevents any other instrument from making a competing GHG emission rate claim for the same MWh.

**Criterion 2: Unique claims.** The instrument must be the only one that carries the GHG emission rate attribute claim associated with that quantity of electricity generation. If multiple instruments can convey attribute claims, the company must ensure that the one being used is the sole valid claim, through pairing or other mechanisms established by the relevant program or jurisdiction.

**Criterion 3: Retirement for claims.** The instrument must be tracked and redeemed, retired, or cancelled by or on behalf of the reporting entity. This can be done through a tracking system, an audit of contracts, third-party certification, or other verifiable means. Retirement ensures that only one consumer can make a claim even as instruments change hands through trading.

**Criterion 4: Vintage matching.** The instrument must be issued and redeemed as close as possible to the period of energy consumption to which it is applied. The vintage is based on the date of energy generation represented by the instrument, not the age of the facility. Instruments should ideally represent energy generated within the reporting period, up to six months prior to the reporting period, or up to three months after.

**Criterion 5: Market boundaries.** The instrument must be sourced from the same market in which the reporting entity's electricity-consuming operations are located and to which the instrument is applied. A market is a geographical area operating under a common regulatory authority with a common system for trading and retiring contractual instruments. The US is considered a single market based on its common regulatory landscape. The EU represents a multi-country market united by common rules.

**Criterion 6: Supplier or utility-specific emission factors.** For utility-specific emission factors, the factor must be calculated based on delivered electricity, incorporating certificates sourced and retired on behalf of customers. Electricity from renewable facilities for which attributes have been sold off must be characterised as having the GHG attributes of the residual mix.

**Criterion 7: Direct contracts or purchasing.** Where energy attribute certificates do not exist, the contract itself should be verified by a third party to convey a unique or sole ownership right to claim a GHG emission rate.

**Criterion 8: Residual mix availability.** To ensure unique claims by all electricity users, an adjusted residual mix characterising the GHG intensity of unclaimed electricity must be available, or its absence must be disclosed by the reporting entity.

## Recommended Disclosure

Beyond required information, the guidance identifies several items companies should disclose.

**Annual electricity consumption.** Companies should report total electricity, steam, heat, and cooling consumed per reporting period separately from the scope totals, in kWh, MWh, BTU, or other relevant units. This should include all scope 2 activity data as well as energy consumed from owned or operated installations (which may be reported only in scope 1, not scope 2).

**Biogenic emissions.** Companies should separately report biogenic CO2 emissions from electricity use (e.g., from biomass combustion in the electricity value chain), while CH4 and N2O emissions from biogenic sources are reported in scope 2.

**Instrument retirement details.** Companies should disclose additional certificate or instrument retirement performed in conjunction with voluntary claims, such as certificate multipliers or pairing with regulatory certificates.

**Methodology disclosure.** For the market-based method, companies must disclose the category or categories of instruments from which emission factors were derived, specifying the energy generation technologies where possible.

**Base-year information.** Companies must disclose the year chosen as base year; the method used to calculate the base-year scope 2 emissions; whether historic location-based data was used as a proxy for a market-based method; and the context for any significant emission changes that trigger base-year recalculation.

**Basis for goal setting.** If a company sets a scope 2-specific reduction goal, it must clarify whether the goal is based on the location-based or market-based total.

## Optional Information

The guidance permits but does not require several additional disclosures, including scope 2 results disaggregated by country, avoided emissions estimations from low-carbon energy generation projects (using project-level methodology and reported separately from the scopes), and advanced grid study estimations for facilities with access to real-time or detailed generation dispatch data.

## Dual Reporting: Why It Matters

Dual reporting allows companies to compare individual purchasing decisions against the overall GHG intensity of the grids on which they operate. It distinguishes changes in procurement choices from changes in grid emissions intensity. It provides transparency for stakeholders, improves comparability across operations that span markets with and without contractual instruments, and facilitates participation in programs with different reporting requirements.

<AnalogyBox>
Dual reporting is similar to a company reporting both its revenue in local currency and its revenue in a common reporting currency for international comparison. Each figure is accurate and useful; they answer different questions. The location-based result answers: "How carbon-intensive is the grid where we operate?" The market-based result answers: "What did our procurement decisions actually do to our reported emissions?"
</AnalogyBox>

The two totals must not be added together or netted. Each represents a separate way of allocating energy generation emissions, and combining them would produce a meaningless hybrid figure.
