The market-based method for scope 2 accounting rests on a specific infrastructure: energy attribute certificates. These instruments exist because grid electricity is physically fungible. Once generated and fed into the grid, electricity from a solar panel is physically indistinguishable from electricity from a coal plant. Energy attribute certificates solve this traceability problem by separating the non-physical attributes of generation from the physical electricity flow, allowing those attributes to be tracked, transferred, and claimed independently.

## Why Energy Attribute Certificates Exist

Consumers of grid-supplied electricity cannot physically direct a specific unit of electricity from a named generation facility to their premises. Grid operators dispatch generation based on contracts, cost, and stability requirements, and the electricity reaching any consumer is the combined output of many generators. This physical indistinguishability means that a consumer who wants to claim that its electricity comes from a specific renewable source needs a contractual mechanism to do so.

Energy attribute certificates provide that mechanism. They are a category of contractual instrument that represents certain information about the energy generated, without representing the energy itself. Once attributes are codified and conveyed in a certificate, the underlying electricity becomes "null power": it has no traceable attributes. Users of null power cannot claim to be buying or using renewable energy in the absence of owning the certificate. Instead, null power is assigned the residual mix emission rate for market-based scope 2 purposes.

<AnalogyBox>
Consider how title deeds work in property markets. The physical land and the legal title to it are separate things. You can stand on a piece of land, but you only own it if you hold the title. Similarly, a company can consume electricity, but it can only claim the generation attributes of a specific power plant if it holds (and retires) the certificate that represents those attributes. The physical electricity and its non-physical attributes travel separately through different systems.
</AnalogyBox>

## Types of Energy Attribute Certificates

Certificate systems have developed differently in different markets, but three main types are most relevant to corporate scope 2 accounting.

**Renewable Energy Certificates (RECs) in the United States and Canada.** In the US, RECs were created primarily to support state Renewable Portfolio Standards (RPS), which require electricity suppliers to source a minimum percentage of their load from eligible renewable resources. Most US states define RECs as conveying "all environmental attributes" associated with one MWh of renewable generation. This fully aggregated approach means a single REC carries the GHG emission rate claim, the resource type claim, and all other environmental attributes. One REC cannot produce multiple competing claims.

RECs may be obtained in several ways: unbundled from energy purchases (purchased separately from electricity delivery), bundled with electricity through a supplier green power product, or through a contract such as a PPA that also conveys the certificates. Voluntary RECs are those above and beyond what suppliers must obtain to comply with RPS requirements.

**Guarantees of Origin (GOs) in Europe.** The EU electricity market liberalisation prompted the development of standardised supplier disclosure about energy supply and its attributes. The Guarantee of Origin system was created to enable suppliers to demonstrate the source of the energy they deliver to customers and to provide consumers with reliable information about their electricity product. In most EU markets, GOs are the primary instrument for voluntary consumer claims about renewable electricity use.

However, the relationship between GOs and regulatory compliance varies by member state. In some countries, renewable energy projects receiving public subsidies must have their GO attributes cancelled or allocated as a public benefit rather than sold to individual consumers. Companies operating in European markets should be aware of these national variations and disclose them as part of instrument feature reporting.

**International RECs (I-RECs).** The International REC Standard creates standardised attribute tracking certificates for voluntary corporate disclosure in markets that do not have developed national certificate systems. I-RECs provide a mechanism for companies with operations in emerging markets or markets without established certificate infrastructure to make voluntary renewable energy claims. The legislative basis for certificate issuance may differ in each country where the standard is active.

## How Certificates Flow from Generation to Consumer

The pathway from certificate creation to consumer claim follows a consistent structure across most markets:

**Step 1: Certificate produced.** A generation facility registers with a tracking system (such as WREGIS, M-RETS, or NEPOOL-GIS in the US, or the Association of Issuing Bodies in Europe). For each MWh of electricity generated and reported, the tracking system issues a certificate with a unique identification number. The certificate carries data about the generation facility: technology type, location, vintage, and emission rate.

**Step 2: Certificate tracked.** The tracking system maintains the certificate in the account of its owner. Each certificate can reside in only one account at a time, preventing double ownership. Certificates may be traded multiple times between the initial generator and the final claimant.

**Step 3: Third-party certification and labelling (optional).** Some programs such as Green-e Energy (US) or EKOenergy (EU) certify certificates based on eligibility criteria specifying what generation technology, facility age, and location conditions must be met. These labels add an additional layer of assurance for consumers.

**Step 4: Purchase and retirement.** The final purchaser or claimant retires the certificate in the tracking system. Retirement is a permanent, irreversible action that removes the certificate from circulation and records the entity on whose behalf it was retired. Only a retired certificate can support a scope 2 claim.

## Certificate Uses

Certificates generally serve four main purposes, which may be handled through a single certificate system or through multiple overlapping systems depending on the jurisdiction:

**Supplier disclosure.** Electricity suppliers may be required to disclose to consumers the fuel mix and related environmental attributes of their delivered supply. In the EU, this fuel mix disclosure requirement has driven the GO system. In Japan, suppliers report their fuel mix and emission factors to the government for publication.

**Supplier quotas.** Many jurisdictions require suppliers to source a minimum share of specified resources (renewable portfolio standards in the US, national renewable targets in EU member states). Certificates are used to demonstrate compliance. Some programs use credit multipliers: when a certificate is redeemed for compliance with supplier quota requirements, it may count as 1.5 certificates toward the quota. Importantly, the multiplier applies only to the compliance function; for scope 2 disclosure purposes, companies use the attributes stated in the certificate per MWh, not the multiplied compliance value.

**Tax or levy exemptions.** In some markets, consumers of renewable electricity may be exempt from certain energy taxes. In the UK, non-residential electricity users are generally taxed through the Climate Change Levy, but electricity generated from renewable sources qualifies for exemption if demonstrated through Levy Exemption Certificates (LECs), which suppliers must acquire and present to HM Revenue and Customs.

**Voluntary consumer programs.** Voluntary programs allow consumers to express demand for specific types of generation. In the US, voluntary RECs can be obtained directly by a consumer (unbundled from energy purchases), or bundled through a supplier green power product or PPA. In the EU, GOs have been used to support voluntary renewable energy purchases and claims. In Australia, the voluntary GreenPower program uses RECs, supported by state governments.

## How Jurisdictional Policies Affect Voluntary Program Impact

The environmental significance of a voluntary certificate purchase depends on the policy context of the jurisdiction in which the generation facility operates. Several policy interactions affect how much voluntary purchases can claim to contribute to new low-carbon supply:

If a jurisdiction excludes voluntarily claimed certificates from counting toward supplier quota compliance, then voluntary demand is truly above and beyond regulatory requirements. If subsidised generation's attributes are treated as a public good rather than available for individual claims, then voluntary programs in that jurisdiction cannot access those attributes. If an emissions cap applies to the power sector, the degree to which voluntary renewable energy purchases reduce overall sector emissions depends on whether allowances are retired alongside the certificates.

These interactions mean that identical certificate instruments in different jurisdictions can carry very different real-world significance. Transparent disclosure of instrument features and policy context, as recommended in Chapter 8 of the Scope 2 Guidance, enables stakeholders to assess these differences.

<HighlightBox>
The Scope 2 Guidance does not require certificates to meet "additionality" criteria (the offset concept that a project would not have been built without the purchase). The market-based method allocates existing generation attributes among consumers. The question of whether voluntary procurement drives new generation development is a supply-and-demand question addressed in Chapter 11 of the guidance, not a criterion for whether a certificate meets the Scope 2 Quality Criteria.
</HighlightBox>
