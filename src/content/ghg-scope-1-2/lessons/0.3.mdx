A GHG inventory is not a neutral technical exercise. The decisions you make about what to include, how to measure, and where to draw boundaries are all shaped by why you are building the inventory in the first place. The GHG Protocol Corporate Standard is designed as a comprehensive accounting framework capable of serving multiple business goals simultaneously, but understanding those goals upfront is essential for making good boundary and design decisions.

## Five Business Goals Served by a GHG Inventory

Companies frequently cite five primary reasons for compiling a GHG inventory:

**1. Managing GHG risks and identifying reduction opportunities.** A comprehensive inventory improves understanding of a company's emissions profile and potential GHG "exposure." In the context of emerging regulations, significant GHG emissions in a company's value chain may result in increased costs upstream or reduced sales downstream, even if the company itself is not directly subject to regulation. A limited focus on direct emissions may miss major GHG risks while leading to a misinterpretation of the company's actual exposure. Accounting for emissions can also help identify where reductions are most cost-effective, driving increased materials and energy efficiency.

**2. Public reporting and participation in voluntary GHG programs.** NGOs, investors, and other stakeholders are increasingly calling for corporate disclosure of GHG information. Companies can improve their standing with customers and the public by being recognised for participating in voluntary programs. A GHG inventory prepared under the Corporate Standard is compatible with the GRI guidelines and most voluntary registry requirements, including the California Climate Action Registry and the World Economic Forum Global GHG Registry.

**3. Participating in mandatory reporting programs.** Some governments require large GHG emitters to report emissions annually. In Europe, facilities covered by the Integrated Pollution Prevention and Control (IPPC) Directive must report emissions exceeding specified thresholds for each of the six GHGs to the European Pollutant Emissions Register (EPER). The Corporate Standard is designed to be compatible with most such requirements.

**4. Participating in GHG markets.** Trading programs determine compliance by comparing emissions against a cap or reduction target. The Corporate Standard's accounting approach is likely to inform the requirements of emerging mandatory trading programs, since it incorporates broad participation and best practices from many companies and programs.

**5. Recognition for early voluntary action.** A credible, documented inventory provides evidence of voluntary reductions that may be recognised under future mandatory programs. Voluntary reductions that have been registered and certified are more likely to be taken into account when regulations go into effect.

<ExampleBox>
**Tata Steel: Building institutional capacity in GHG accounting**

For Tata Steel, Asia's first and India's largest integrated private sector steel company, reducing GHG emissions through energy efficiency is a key element of its primary business goal: market acceptability of its products internationally. Each year, the company introduces new energy efficiency projects and less GHG-intensive processes. To succeed in these efforts and be eligible for emerging trading schemes, Tata Steel must have an accurate GHG inventory that includes all processes and activities. The inventory allows for meaningful benchmarking, measures improvements, and promotes credible reporting. It generates two key strategic performance indicators: specific energy consumption (GCal/tonne of crude steel) and GHG intensity (tonne CO₂e/tonne of crude steel).
</ExampleBox>

## How Business Goals Drive Inventory Design

The GHG Protocol Corporate Standard has been designed as a comprehensive GHG accounting and reporting framework that provides the information building blocks capable of serving most business goals. Inventory data collected in accordance with the standard can be aggregated and disaggregated for various organisational and operational boundaries and for different business geographic scales: state, country, facility, business unit, company, and so on.

This flexibility is only possible if the data are collected and recorded at a sufficiently disaggregated level. Companies should design their data collection processes from the outset to provide information for a variety of different users and uses, both current and future.

Different business goals do, however, influence certain design choices:

- A company seeking to participate in a **mandatory reporting program** must understand that specific government programs may impose additional requirements on organisational boundaries, calculation methodologies, and verification procedures.
- A company focused on **GHG markets** must understand that trading programs often require accounting only for direct emissions, with some exceptions (the UK ETS, for example, also requires electricity-related indirect emissions). Market participation also typically requires an audit trail for GHG information.
- A company seeking **recognition for early action** should document and register reductions as they occur, since retroactive recognition is often harder to obtain once regulatory programs are established.

<AnalogyBox>
Consider two companies in the same industry building a GHG inventory for the first time. Company A's goal is internal cost management: it wants to identify the largest emission sources and prioritise energy efficiency investments. Company B's goal is participation in an emissions trading scheme. Company A might reasonably start with its largest facilities and expand coverage over time. Company B needs a complete, auditable inventory from the start, designed to meet the specific requirements of the trading program it intends to join. Same activity, very different design requirements.
</AnalogyBox>

## The Inventory Design Process

Once business goals are established, the practical process of designing a GHG inventory involves the following steps:

1. Set organisational boundaries: decide which entities and operations to include, using either the equity share approach or a control approach (covered in detail in Lesson 0.4).
2. Set operational boundaries: categorise emissions as direct (Scope 1), indirect from purchased electricity (Scope 2), or other indirect (Scope 3), and decide which Scope 3 categories to report (covered in Lesson 1.1).
3. Select a base year and recalculation policy (covered in Lesson 1.2).
4. Identify emission sources within the boundary and select appropriate calculation approaches (covered in Lesson 1.3).
5. Collect activity data and calculate emissions.
6. Roll up data to the corporate level.
7. Report in accordance with the standard's requirements.

<HighlightBox>
Companies generally want their GHG inventory to be capable of serving multiple goals. It therefore makes sense to design the process from the outset to provide information for a variety of different users and uses, both current and future, rather than narrowly optimising for a single use case.
</HighlightBox>

<DeepDive title="The IBM renewable electricity case">
IBM, a major information technology company and member of WRI's Green Power Market Development Group, systematically accounted for indirect emissions from purchased electricity and identified the significant potential to reduce them. The company implemented strategies to reduce either its demand for purchased energy or the GHG intensity of that energy. One strategy was pursuing the renewable energy market. IBM succeeded in reducing GHG emissions at its Austin, Texas facility while energy use stayed relatively constant, through a contract for renewable electricity with Austin Energy. A five-year contract for 5.25 million kWh of wind power per year lowered the facility's inventory by more than 4,100 tonnes of CO₂ compared to the previous year. Company-wide, IBM's 2002 total renewable energy procurement was 66.2 million kWh, representing 31,550 tonnes of CO₂ reduction. By accounting for indirect emissions and looking for associated reduction opportunities, IBM successfully reduced an important source of its overall GHG emissions.
</DeepDive>
