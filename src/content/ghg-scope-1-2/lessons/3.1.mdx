Scope 2 emissions are the indirect greenhouse gas emissions that occur from the generation of purchased or acquired electricity, steam, heat, or cooling consumed by the reporting company. The emissions themselves take place at the power plant or heat generation facility, owned and operated by another entity. What makes scope 2 "indirect" is precisely this separation: the reporting company causes the emissions through its consumption decisions, but does not own the source that produces them.

The GHG Protocol Scope 2 Guidance, published by WRI and WBCSD, was developed to resolve specific accounting questions that the original Corporate Standard left open. Chief among these were: how to handle renewable energy purchases, which contractual instruments should count toward a company's reported emissions, and how to apply supplier-specific emission factors. The guidance took four years of international consultation with businesses, NGOs, GHG reporting programs, energy utilities, government agencies, and academic institutions.

## Why Purchased Energy Matters at Scale

The electricity and heat generation sector is responsible for at least one third of global GHG emissions, according to IPCC data. Every time a company switches on a light, runs a data center, or heats a factory floor, it draws on a generation system that may be burning coal, natural gas, or a mix of resources. The cumulative demand of millions of commercial and industrial consumers shapes which generation technologies utilities build and dispatch.

This is why scope 2 is not a minor accounting category. For many companies, particularly those in technology, retail, and manufacturing, scope 2 can represent the single largest share of their total GHG inventory. It is also the category most directly responsive to procurement decisions: a company can reduce scope 2 emissions by purchasing from lower-carbon sources without changing a single physical process inside its facilities.

<HighlightBox>
Scope 2 covers purchased and consumed electricity, steam, heat, and cooling. Emissions occur at the generation source, not at the point of consumption. Electricity generated on-site in owned or operated equipment belongs to scope 1, not scope 2.
</HighlightBox>

## Key Accounting Questions the Guidance Resolves

Before the Scope 2 Guidance, companies faced three unresolved problems.

**Renewable energy purchases.** When a company buys electricity from a wind farm, does its scope 2 figure fall to zero for that portion of its consumption? The answer depends on whether the company holds the environmental attributes associated with that generation, and whether those attributes are tracked and retired in a way that prevents anyone else from claiming them.

**Green power programs.** Many utilities offer green tariffs or green power products. The prior Corporate Standard noted these programs but provided no consistent method for determining which instruments could legitimately reduce a reported scope 2 figure. Different companies were making different assumptions, making disclosed numbers incomparable.

**Supplier-specific emission factors.** Where a utility discloses the GHG intensity of its delivered electricity product, should that factor be used instead of a regional grid average? The guidance establishes a clear hierarchy answering this question and sets criteria that any instrument must meet before it can be applied.

<AnalogyBox>
Think of energy attribute certificates like certified-organic labels on food. The certification system ensures that the organic attributes of a batch of produce are tracked from farm to shelf, and that only one buyer can claim them. Without such a system, multiple buyers could claim the same organic origin for the same batch. Energy attribute certificates work the same way: they track the environmental attributes of a unit of electricity generation from the power plant to the consumer, and are retired so no one else can claim the same attributes.
</AnalogyBox>

## Business Goals of Scope 2 Accounting

The Scope 2 Guidance identifies four core business goals that motivate careful accounting for purchased energy emissions.

**Identifying and understanding risks and opportunities.** Electricity is a vital input to most corporate operations, and it increasingly carries GHG-related risks. Climate regulations targeting the energy sector, changing fuel costs, investor scrutiny over energy purchasing decisions, and consumer preferences for low-carbon products all create financial exposure. The location-based method highlights risks tied to the physical grid serving a company's facilities. The market-based method reveals risks and opportunities tied to contractual relationships and procurement choices. Using both methods together provides the most complete risk picture.

Specific risk categories include: regulatory risk from CO2 taxes or carbon pricing applied to electricity; reputational risk from renewable energy claims that are not substantiated by properly tracked instruments; energy cost risks from fuel price volatility correlated with carbon intensity; and legal risk from contractual instruments that convey legally enforceable rights that, if ignored, may cause misrepresentation of environmental performance.

**Setting reduction targets and tracking performance.** Comprehensive scope 2 accounting provides a consistent basis for GHG reduction targets. A company can set targets for absolute emissions, emission intensity, or renewable energy procurement percentage. Both method totals can serve as performance indicators. The market-based method in particular enables a company to directly demonstrate the impact of specific procurement actions, such as signing a power purchase agreement with a new solar facility.

**Engaging energy suppliers and partners.** Reducing emissions from the energy sector requires participation by generators, suppliers, retailers, and consumers. Scope 2 accounting motivates companies to engage their energy suppliers on the GHG intensity of delivered products and to seek out suppliers offering lower-carbon options. On the supply side, consumer demand expressed through procurement can help justify investment in new low-carbon generation capacity.

**Enhancing stakeholder information and corporate reputation.** Transparent scope 2 disclosure helps shareholders, regulators, customers, and communities understand a company's energy strategy and its GHG implications. Reporting both location-based and market-based totals gives stakeholders a fuller view of the company's physical footprint and its procurement intentions.

## Scope 2 and the Broader GHG Framework

Scope 2 sits between scope 1 (direct emissions from owned and controlled sources) and scope 3 (upstream and downstream value chain emissions). One important interaction: when a company calculates scope 3, category 3 (fuel- and energy-related activities not included in scope 1 or 2), it must disclose whether it uses a market-based or location-based scope 2 total as the basis for that calculation. Companies must also specify which scope 2 method they used for goal-setting and for any scope 3 or product-level communication.

Another structural feature: one company's scope 1 emissions from energy generation become another company's scope 2 emissions from purchasing that generation. This is an inherent and intended feature of the GHG accounting framework, not a form of double counting. It allows multiple entities along a value chain to consistently report and act on those emissions where they have the most direct influence.

<HighlightBox>
The Scope 2 Guidance is policy-neutral. It does not require development of certificate markets, express preferences about market design, or promote specific generation technologies. It provides accounting mechanics that function across diverse electricity markets worldwide.
</HighlightBox>
