Beyond the required disclosures covered in Chapter 7, the Scope 2 Guidance recommends that companies provide additional information about the specific features of the contractual instruments they use and the policy context in which those instruments operate. This additional disclosure serves two purposes: it enables stakeholders to make meaningful assessments of a company's procurement strategy, and it surfaces the relationships between voluntary purchases and regulatory policies that can affect the environmental integrity of market-based scope 2 claims.

## Why Instrument Feature Disclosure Matters

Markets currently differ significantly in what types of energy generation facilities can produce certificates, what eligibility criteria define which generators participate in which programs, and what attributes certificates are designed to convey. A Renewable Energy Certificate from a ten-year-old hydropower facility in one country carries very different implications than a certificate from a newly-built offshore wind project in another. A supplier-specific emission rate built from a portfolio that includes nuclear, natural gas, and some wind is different from one representing a 100 percent renewable product.

Without disclosure of instrument features, stakeholders evaluating a company's market-based scope 2 total have no way to assess whether the underlying procurement aligns with the company's stated sustainability goals, how it relates to the company's location-based emissions, or whether the claimed attributes represent a genuine contribution to low-carbon energy development.

The guidance therefore recommends that companies disclose key features of the contractual instruments they claim in the market-based method. These features fall into two categories: generation facility characteristics and policy context.

## Recommended Instrument Feature Disclosures

**Certification or label name.** Companies should note any third-party certification or label associated with the instrument, such as Green-e Energy (US), EcoLogo (Canada), EKOenergy or Naturemade (EU). The certification program typically specifies what it certifies: for example, Green-e Energy certifies against a national standard for eligible renewable energy sources and minimum facility age requirements.

**Energy resource type.** Instruments should clearly identify the resource generating the certificate. For supplier-specific emission rates, the resource type could be "mix" for standard offers, "multiple renewable" for certain green power products, or a specific resource (wind, solar, hydro). Residual mix will typically be a "mix."

**Facility location.** Depending on the information available from the certificate, supplier, or contract, the generation facility's location can be identified at a national or subnational level (either geopolitical, such as a US state, or by grid region, such as a NERC region). This allows stakeholders to assess geographic matching between the facility and the consuming operations.

**Facility age.** Stakeholders often want to know whether a company's procurement consists largely of attributes from older established facilities or from more recently built projects. Companies should note the year the generation facility that created the certificate or contract was first operational or substantially repowered. Newer facilities tend to represent a more incremental contribution to the low-carbon energy supply.

**Additionality considerations.** Companies may note whether the instrument is associated with a certification label or supplier program that contributes incremental funding to new projects, and if so, the quantity of funding included with the contractual purchase. This relates to whether the purchase helps bring new generation capacity online rather than simply claiming attributes from existing facilities.

<ExampleBox>
**Example: Instrument feature disclosure in practice**

A company purchases 50,000 MWh of RECs annually to match its US operations. Useful feature disclosure would include: the certificates are Green-e Energy certified; they represent wind generation from a facility commissioned in 2022; the facility is located in ERCOT (Texas); the purchase is surplus to any state RPS obligation; no allowances have been retired alongside the certificates. This information tells stakeholders far more than simply reporting a zero market-based emission factor.
</ExampleBox>

## Reporting on Policy Context

The relationship between voluntary energy purchases and regulatory policies is complex and varies significantly by jurisdiction. The guidance requires companies to report their scope 2 totals and instrument features without mandating that instruments be "additional to" or "independent from" regulatory requirements. However, for transparency, companies should disclose the relationships between the instruments they claim and any relevant policies.

**Supplier quotas.** Many jurisdictions require electricity suppliers to source a minimum percentage of their supply from eligible renewable energy resources (renewable portfolio standards in the US, national renewable targets in the EU). Companies should note the relationship between their contractual instruments and any supplier quota requirements. The guidance provides a table of six possible reporting options ranging from "no supplier quota in this market" to "claimed instrument is above and beyond supplier quota requirements" to "claimed instrument includes the supplier quota in a blended product."

The significance of this distinction: if a supplier is already required by law to deliver renewable electricity to all its customers, a consumer claiming that electricity product as a voluntary renewable purchase is not creating any incremental demand for renewable energy above what regulation already requires. A voluntary REC purchase that is surplus to any supplier quota, by contrast, represents demand that goes beyond regulatory minimums.

**Relationship to subsidy receipt.** In some countries, renewable energy projects receive public subsidies (such as feed-in tariffs) that may require all attributes from that generation to be either cancelled or treated as a public benefit rather than made available for individual consumer claims. For instance, in Germany, generation facilities receiving feed-in tariff subsidies must have their attributes retired on behalf of all German consumers collectively rather than conveyed to individual voluntary purchasers. In Japan, renewable electricity supported by feed-in tariffs cannot be issued as voluntary energy attribute certificates. Companies operating in such markets should disclose when the subsidy receipt condition applies.

**Relationship to emissions trading programs.** In emissions-capped power sectors such as the EU Emissions Trading Scheme, the presence of a cap does not directly prevent market-based accounting based on contractual instruments, because renewable energy still produces zero emissions at the point of generation. However, whether the overall cap is tightened or the emissions allowances in circulation are reduced in response to voluntary purchases depends on whether allowances are retired alongside certificates. Companies claiming instruments in emissions-capped power sectors should disclose whether an allowance set-aside program is in place and whether any allowances have been retired along with their voluntary certificates. Any allowances retired should be reported in metric tons separately from the scopes.

<HighlightBox>
The guidance does not require contractual instruments to be "in addition to" or independent from regulatory policies such as subsidies, tax exemptions, or supplier quotas. It requires companies to disclose these relationships transparently so stakeholders can assess the procurement strategy in its policy context.
</HighlightBox>

## When Regulatory Requirements Overlap with Voluntary Programs

A practical question arises frequently: if a regulator requires a supplier to deliver renewable electricity to customers, and a company then voluntarily purchases that same product or its associated certificates, is the voluntary purchase meaningful?

The guidance takes a policy-neutral position and does not make eligibility judgments on this basis. Instead, it requires disclosure. Companies and their stakeholders can then assess the relationship. For example:

- If the claimed instrument reflects electricity that was "above and beyond" the supplier's quota obligation, stakeholders can view it as representing genuine additional demand.
- If the claimed instrument includes the quota requirement in a bundled product, stakeholders know that the voluntary claim overlaps with what regulation already required.
- If the claimed instrument is paired with a separately retired compliance certificate from the same generation unit, the environmental claims are strengthened by eliminating potential double use of the same generation attributes.

This framework respects the diversity of market structures globally while maintaining the transparency necessary for stakeholders to assess corporate performance meaningfully.

<DeepDive title="Relationship between offset credits and energy attribute certificates">
Offsets and energy attribute certificates are distinct instruments with different purposes. Offsets convey metric tons of CO2 avoided globally, using project-level accounting. Energy attribute certificates convey information about the direct GHG emission rate at the point of generation. An offset credit does not confer claims about electricity attributes applicable to scope 2 accounting. A single MWh of renewable energy generation can either produce an energy attribute certificate or an offset credit (if additionality criteria are met), but generally cannot produce both, because the same generation cannot simultaneously represent a scope 2 attribute claim and a global avoided-emissions claim. Companies purchasing offsets should report these outside the scopes, separately from scope 2 totals and from energy attribute certificate claims.
</DeepDive>
