Building a GHG inventory is a necessary first step, but it becomes most strategically valuable when it informs action. Chapter 11 of the GHG Protocol Corporate Standard addresses the logical follow-on: setting a corporate GHG reduction target. A target translates inventory data into a commitment, drives internal accountability, and signals to external stakeholders that a company is managing its climate-related risks seriously.

## Why Set a GHG Target?

Setting targets is a routine business practice. GHG targets serve the same function as financial or operational targets: they keep an issue on senior management's agenda and factor it into decisions about products, services, materials, and technologies. The GHG Protocol identifies five principal drivers:

**Minimising and managing GHG risks.** A GHG target is a planning tool that raises internal awareness of climate-related risks and opportunities and ensures the issue is kept on the business agenda. This can serve to more effectively manage the business risks associated with climate change.

**Achieving cost savings and stimulating innovation.** Implementing a GHG target can result in cost savings by driving improvements in process efficiency and resource use. Targets applied to products can drive research and development that creates more emissions-efficient offerings.

**Preparing for future regulations.** Internal accountability mechanisms established to support a target's implementation equip companies to respond more effectively to future GHG regulations, including potential trading programs.

**Demonstrating leadership and corporate responsibility.** A public corporate GHG target demonstrates commitment and can improve a company's standing with customers, employees, investors, and business partners.

**Participating in voluntary programs.** A growing number of voluntary GHG programs assist companies in setting, implementing, and tracking targets, and participation can result in public recognition and enhanced accounting capacity.

## Types of GHG Targets

There are two broad types of corporate GHG targets.

**Absolute targets** specify a reduction in the total quantity of GHG emissions to the atmosphere, expressed in tonnes of CO2e. For example: "Reduce CO2 emissions 25 percent below 1994 levels by 2010." Absolute targets are environmentally robust because they commit to a specific quantity of atmospheric reduction. However, they do not account for changes in production volume: a company that grows organically and increases output will find an absolute target harder to meet even if it improves the emissions efficiency of each unit produced.

**Intensity targets** specify a reduction in the ratio of GHG emissions relative to a business metric, such as tonnes CO2e per tonne of product, per MWh of electricity generated, or per dollar of revenue. For example: "Reduce CO2e per tonne of clinker by 12 percent between 2000 and 2008." Intensity targets reflect GHG performance improvements independently of organic growth or decline, and base year recalculations for structural changes are usually not required. However, they do not guarantee that absolute emissions to the atmosphere will decrease: if output grows faster than intensity improves, total emissions will rise.

## Ten Steps in Setting a GHG Target

The GHG Protocol outlines a ten-step process. While presented sequentially, in practice target-setting involves cycling back and forth between steps as choices in later steps inform earlier ones.

### Step 1: Obtain Senior Management Commitment

As with any corporate-wide target, board- or CEO-level commitment is a prerequisite for success. Implementing a reduction target necessitates changes in behaviour and decision-making throughout the organisation and requires adequate resources. Without senior management commitment, this is not achievable.

### Step 2: Decide on the Target Type

Choose between absolute and intensity targets, or a combination of both. The choice depends on business goals, the structure of the company's operations, and stakeholder expectations.

### Step 3: Decide on the Target Boundary

The target boundary defines which GHGs, geographic operations, sources, and activities are covered. Key questions include:

- **Which gases?** Targets typically cover one or more of the six Kyoto Protocol GHGs. For companies with significant non-CO2 GHG sources, including them increases the range of reduction opportunities.
- **Which geographical operations?** Only operations with reliable GHG inventory data should be included in the target.
- **Which emission sources?** Targets can cover scope 1 only, scopes 1 and 2, or include relevant scope 3 categories.
- **Separate targets by business type?** For companies with diverse operations, separate targets by business unit may be more meaningful, particularly when using an intensity target with a business-unit-specific metric.

### Step 4: Choose the Target Base Year

Most GHG targets are defined as a percentage reduction from a fixed target base year (for example, "25 percent below 1990 levels by 2010"). This provides a stable, transparent reference point. It is generally most efficient to align the target base year with the inventory base year. The base year data must be reliable and verifiable: GHG data from some historical years (such as 1990) is often too unreliable to serve as a verifiable base.

Alternatively, companies may use a rolling target base year, in which the base year rolls forward annually (for example, "reduce emissions by 2 percent compared to the previous year"). This approach eliminates the need for back-casting when a company undergoes structural changes, but it makes long-term like-for-like comparisons more difficult.

### Step 5: Define the Target Completion Date

Long-term targets (ten or more years) facilitate planning for large capital investments with GHG benefits. Short-term targets (five years or fewer) are more practical for companies with shorter planning cycles. Long-term targets carry more uncertainty about future conditions. A five-year target period is often a reasonable balance.

### Step 6: Define the Length of the Commitment Period

The commitment period is the time during which emissions performance is measured against the target. A single-year commitment period means performance is assessed only in the final year. A multi-year commitment period (such as the Kyoto Protocol's five-year period of 2008 to 2012) averages performance over multiple years, reducing the impact of single-year anomalies.

### Step 7: Decide on the Use of GHG Offsets or Credits

A target can be met entirely through internal reductions at sources within the inventory boundary, or partially through purchasing external offsets from GHG reduction projects. When reporting on target performance, companies should specify whether offsets were used and how much of the reduction was achieved through offsets versus internal action. Internal emissions should always be reported separately from any offsets used.

### Step 8: Establish a Target Double Counting Policy

This step applies to companies that engage in GHG trading or whose target boundary overlaps with external programs. A Target Double Counting Policy specifies how reductions and trades related to other targets and programs will be reconciled with the corporate target.

### Step 9: Decide on the Target Level

The target level is the percentage reduction to be achieved. It should be informed by all previous steps and by an analysis of key GHG emission drivers, available reduction strategies, projected future emissions under business-as-usual, and the feasibility and cost of different mitigation options.

### Step 10: Track and Report Progress

Progress tracking requires an annual inventory using the same methodology as the base year, with base year emissions recalculated as needed for structural changes. Performance is reported as both the absolute emissions figure for the tracking year and the percentage change from the (recalculated) base year.

<HighlightBox>
When tracking progress against an absolute target, base year emissions must be recalculated for significant structural changes (acquisitions, divestitures) following the same rules described in the GHG Protocol's base year recalculation chapter. This ensures that like-for-like comparisons are maintained and that structural changes do not artificially inflate or deflate reported progress.
</HighlightBox>

<ExampleBox>
**Royal Dutch/Shell** discovered when implementing its voluntary GHG reduction target that one of the biggest challenges was cascading the corporate target down to the actions of all employees who influence target performance. Shell found that absolute GHG emissions at a plant are a function of three factors: quantity of product manufactured (a corporate-level variable), best process energy use per tonne (a business-level variable), and a plant efficiency index (a facility-level variable). This meant that an absolute corporate target could only be set at the corporate level, while lower levels required intensity or efficiency targets aligned to their decision-making authority.
</ExampleBox>

## How Targets Link Back to Inventory Design

A GHG target is not independent of the inventory. The design of the target directly shapes what the inventory must track. If the target covers scope 1 and scope 2 emissions from all global operations, the inventory must provide reliable, consistent data for all those sources. If the base year is 2005, the inventory must ensure that 2005 data is reliable, verifiable, and consistently comparable with current year data.

Decisions about which gases, boundaries, and base years to include in the target should therefore be made in conjunction with the inventory team, not after the fact. A target that is set without regard for the quality of the underlying inventory data will be neither credible nor achievable.

<DeepDive title="Science-based targets">
Science-based targets (SBTs) are GHG reduction targets that are consistent with the level of decarbonisation required to limit global warming to 1.5°C or well below 2°C above pre-industrial levels, as specified in the Paris Agreement. The Science Based Targets initiative (SBTi) provides methodologies and validation for corporate targets. Unlike targets set solely on business feasibility, science-based targets are set by working backwards from a global carbon budget: the cumulative amount of CO2 that can be emitted while keeping warming within the specified limit. Companies commit to specific absolute or intensity reductions aligned with sectoral decarbonisation pathways. Science-based targets have emerged as the leading framework for ambitious corporate GHG target-setting since the GHG Protocol Corporate Standard was first published.
</DeepDive>
