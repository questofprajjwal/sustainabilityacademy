Verification is the process by which an independent party assesses whether a company's reported GHG information accurately and completely represents its actual emissions. Chapter 10 of the GHG Protocol Corporate Standard defines what verification is, how it differs from internal quality control, what level of assurance it can provide, and what companies need to prepare.

## What Verification Is

The GHG Protocol defines verification as "an objective assessment of the accuracy and completeness of reported GHG information and the conformity of this information to pre-established GHG accounting and reporting principles." In practice, verification involves a risk-based assessment: the verifier prioritises effort towards the data and systems that have the greatest impact on overall data quality and the greatest risk of material discrepancy.

Verification is distinct from internal QA/QC procedures (covered in Lesson 5.1). Internal QA/QC is carried out by the company itself to prevent and detect errors. External verification is conducted by an independent party to provide third-party assurance to external stakeholders. Both are valuable, and strong internal processes make external verification more efficient and less costly.

## Why Verification Matters

Common reasons for commissioning an independent verification include:

- Increased credibility of publicly reported emissions and progress towards GHG targets, leading to enhanced stakeholder trust.
- Increased senior management confidence in reported information on which investment and target-setting decisions are based.
- Improvement of internal accounting and reporting practices by identifying weaknesses in calculation, recording, and internal reporting systems.
- Preparation for mandatory verification requirements in GHG programs.

The more transparent, well-controlled, and well-documented a company's emissions data and systems are, the more efficient its verification will be. An audit trail, as described in Lesson 5.1, is the foundation of a successful verification.

<HighlightBox>
As the GHG Protocol Corporate Standard has become widely adopted, verification practices have shifted from "customised and voluntary" towards "standardised and mandatory." Programs such as the EU Emissions Trading System, the California Climate Action Registry, and the World Economic Forum Global GHG Registry all require some form of emissions verification.
</HighlightBox>

## Types of Verification: Limited vs. Reasonable Assurance

The scope and rigour of a verification engagement determine the level of assurance it provides.

**Limited assurance (negative assurance):** The verifier conducts a less intensive review, typically desk-based, and concludes that nothing has come to their attention that would indicate the inventory is materially misstated. This is sometimes described as a "review" engagement.

**Reasonable assurance (positive assurance):** The verifier conducts a more intensive engagement, including site visits, detailed data testing, and examination of underlying systems and controls, and positively concludes that the inventory is free from material misstatement. This is analogous to a financial audit and provides a higher level of confidence.

<AnalogyBox>
The distinction between limited and reasonable assurance is similar to the difference between a doctor reviewing a patient's file and recommending follow-up tests versus conducting a comprehensive physical examination and confirming a diagnosis. Both are useful, but the depth of evidence gathered differs substantially.
</AnalogyBox>

## The Concept of Materiality

Materiality is central to verification. A material discrepancy is an error, omission, or miscalculation that results in a reported quantity or statement being significantly different from the true value or meaning. As a rule of thumb, a discrepancy is considered material if its value exceeds 5 percent of the total inventory for the part of the organisation being verified.

Materiality thresholds may be specified by a GHG program or a national verification standard. A materiality threshold guides verifiers in concentrating their effort on areas where errors are most likely to lead to materially misleading results. It is not the same as de minimis emissions: a company cannot simply exclude sources from its inventory on the grounds that they fall below the materiality threshold.

Context matters. A 2 percent error that prevents a company from achieving its corporate target would most likely be considered material, even though it is below 5 percent in absolute terms.

## The Verification Process

### Planning and Risk Assessment

Before fieldwork begins, the verifier assesses the risk of material discrepancy across each component of the company's GHG information collection and reporting process. Risk factors considered include:

- The structure of the organisation and how responsibility for monitoring and reporting GHG emissions is assigned.
- The approach and commitment of management to GHG monitoring and reporting.
- The development and implementation of policies and processes for monitoring and reporting, including documented methods.
- Processes used to check and review calculation methodologies.
- Complexity and nature of operations.
- Complexity of the computer information systems used to process the information.
- The state of calibration and maintenance of meters used, and the types of meters used.
- Reliability and availability of input data.
- Aggregation of data from different sources.

### Establishing the Scope of Work

The company and verifier agree in advance on what the verification will cover: which parts of the inventory, the level of scrutiny (desk review or on-site review), and the intended use of the results. This agreement is often called the scope of work. The materiality threshold is a key element of this agreement. Companies should make the scope of work publicly available to enhance transparency.

### Data Testing and Site Visits

Depending on the level of assurance required, verifiers may need to visit a number of sites. Sites are selected based on their contribution to total GHG emissions, the complexity of data collection at each site, and the risk that data from those sites will be materially misstated. At each site, verifiers gather evidence on the completeness, accuracy, and reliability of reported information.

## What Data Must Be Available for External Verification

A verifier cannot issue an opinion on data for which there is no supporting documentation. Companies must ensure that the following are available and retrievable:

- Information about the company's main activities and the types of GHG produced.
- Organisational and ownership structure, including subsidiaries, joint ventures, and outsourcing arrangements.
- Details of any changes to organisational boundaries or processes during the period, with justification for how these changes affect emissions data.
- Energy consumption data (invoices, delivery notes, meter readings for electricity, gas, steam, and hot water).
- Production data (tonnes of material produced, kWh of electricity generated, etc.).
- Emission factors used and their justification.
- Assumptions on which estimations are based.
- Information on measurement accuracy of meters, including calibration records.
- A description of the procedures and systems used to collect, document, and process GHG emissions data at the facility and corporate level.
- A description of internal quality control procedures applied.
- Selected consolidation approach and confirmation of how it aligns with financial reporting.

<HighlightBox>
Companies are responsible for ensuring the existence, quality, and retention of documentation so as to create an audit trail of how the inventory was compiled. If a company issues a specific base year against which it assesses GHG performance, it must retain all relevant historical records to support the base year data.
</HighlightBox>

## Selecting a Verification Body

When choosing a verifier, companies should consider:

- Previous experience and competence in conducting GHG verifications.
- Understanding of GHG issues, including calculation methodologies.
- Understanding of the company's operations and industry sector.
- Objectivity, credibility, and independence.

The knowledge and qualifications of the individual verifiers assigned to the engagement are more important than the size or name of the organisation they come from. Effective GHG verification requires a mix of engineering and industry expertise at the technical level, and verification and business expertise at the managerial level.

<ExampleBox>
**PricewaterhouseCoopers (PwC)** has conducted GHG emissions verifications for over a decade across energy, chemicals, metals, semiconductors, and pulp and paper sectors. PwC's process involves two key steps: (1) evaluating whether the GHG accounting and reporting methodology has been correctly implemented, and (2) identifying any material discrepancies. PwC notes that GHG data for 1990 is often too unreliable to provide a verifiable base year for tracking emissions over time, which illustrates why base year data quality is so critical.
</ExampleBox>

## Timing and Using Verification Findings

Verification can be conducted during a reporting period (allowing deficiencies to be corrected before the final report is published) or after a report has been submitted (as required by some regulatory programs). Some companies establish a semi-permanent internal verification team to maintain GHG data standards on an ongoing basis.

Before issuing a final opinion, verifiers may require the company to correct any material errors identified during the engagement. If agreement on corrections cannot be reached, the verifier may be unable to issue an unqualified opinion. All material errors, considered individually or in aggregate, must be resolved before final sign-off.

Beyond the opinion itself, a verification report typically contains recommendations for future improvements. Verification should be viewed as a valuable input to continual improvement of GHG accounting and reporting systems, not simply as a compliance exercise.

<DeepDive title="Internal assurance as a stepping stone">
Many companies conduct internal verification before commissioning an external verifier. Internal verification is carried out by personnel who are independent of the GHG accounting and reporting process but remain within the company. While external third-party verification provides greater credibility to external stakeholders, internal verification can be a valuable learning experience that identifies weaknesses in data systems and processes before they are exposed to external scrutiny. It also provides useful background material for external verifiers to begin their work.
</DeepDive>
