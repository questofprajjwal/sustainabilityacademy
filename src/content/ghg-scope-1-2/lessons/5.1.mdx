A GHG inventory is only as useful as it is credible. A number that cannot be defended, audited, or compared across years provides little value to management, investors, or regulators. Chapter 7 of the GHG Protocol Corporate Standard addresses this directly: quality management is not an optional add-on but an integral part of the inventory program itself.

## Why Quality Management Matters

Companies prepare GHG inventories for different reasons: voluntary disclosure, regulatory compliance, internal target-setting, or stakeholder reporting. Whatever the motivation, high-quality information has greater value and more uses, while low-quality information may be worthless or even create liability. A quality management system (QMS) provides a systematic process for preventing and correcting errors, and identifies where investments will produce the greatest improvement in overall inventory quality.

The primary objective of any QMS is to ensure the credibility of the reported data. That credibility depends on putting the GHG Protocol's five accounting principles into practice: relevance, completeness, consistency, transparency, and accuracy.

## Implementing the QMS: Seven Steps

The GHG Protocol outlines a seven-step implementation cycle:

1. **Establish an inventory quality team.** This team coordinates interactions between business units, facilities, and external entities such as verifiers and government agencies. A named coordinator is essential for accountability.

2. **Develop a quality management plan.** The plan describes procedures for all organisational levels, from initial data collection through to final reporting. Companies should integrate the QMS with existing quality systems (such as ISO procedures) rather than building a parallel structure from scratch.

3. **Perform generic quality checks.** These apply across the entire inventory and focus on data handling, documentation, and calculation activities. Examples include checking for transcription errors in a sample of input data, verifying that spreadsheet formulas are correct, ensuring unit conversions are consistent throughout, and confirming that version control procedures are in place for electronic files.

4. **Perform source-category-specific quality checks.** These involve more rigorous investigation into the appropriateness of emission factors, recalculation procedures, and adherence to accounting principles for specific source categories. For instance, determining whether electricity bills or meter readings are the better source of consumption data for a given facility.

5. **Review final inventory estimates and reports.** After compilation, an internal technical review focuses on engineering and scientific aspects. A separate managerial review secures corporate approval. A third review by experts external to the program addresses independent verification (covered in Lesson 5.3).

6. **Institutionalise formal feedback loops.** The results of all reviews are fed back to the quality team for correction and improvement. Errors must be corrected and improvements implemented systematically.

7. **Establish reporting, documentation, and archiving procedures.** These specify what information is documented internally, how it is archived, and what is reported to external stakeholders.

<HighlightBox>
The QMS should be treated as an evolving, multi-year effort. It should include steps to ensure that all quality control findings from previous years are adequately addressed in subsequent inventories.
</HighlightBox>

## Managing Activity Data

The collection of high-quality activity data is typically the most significant limitation for corporate GHG inventories. The following practices improve activity data quality:

- Develop data collection procedures that allow the same data to be efficiently re-collected in future years, enabling a consistent time series.
- Convert fuel consumption data to energy units before applying carbon content emission factors, since energy-based factors are generally more accurate than mass- or volume-based factors.
- Compare current year data with historical trends. Changes of more than 10 percent year-over-year warrant investigation to determine whether they reflect real operational changes or data errors.
- Compare activity data against independent reference sources such as government survey data or trade association compilations.
- Check that base year recalculation procedures have been followed consistently and that operational and organisational boundary decisions have been applied correctly.

<ExampleBox>
**Vauxhall Motors and the importance of accuracy checks:** When calculating GHG emissions from staff air travel, Vauxhall's review of its calculation methodology revealed that round-trip distance, not one-way distance, must be used. Failure to catch this error would have resulted in reporting emissions 50 percent lower than the actual value. The lesson: even apparently simple calculations require explicit assumption checks.
</ExampleBox>

## Uncertainty in GHG Inventories

Preparing a GHG inventory is simultaneously an accounting exercise and a scientific one. Unlike financial accounting, which reports single point estimates, scientific practice typically reports values with estimated error bounds. The GHG Protocol acknowledges that point estimates in a corporate inventory have practical uses, but companies should also understand and characterise the uncertainty in their data.

Uncertainties fall into two broad categories:

**Scientific uncertainty** arises when the actual emission or removal process is not completely understood. The GWP values used to convert non-CO2 gases to CO2 equivalents carry significant scientific uncertainty. Quantifying this type of uncertainty is generally beyond the capacity of most company inventory programs.

**Estimation uncertainty** arises whenever GHG emissions are quantified from activity data and emission factors. It has two sub-types:

- *Model uncertainty:* uncertainty in the mathematical relationships used to translate activity data into emissions.
- *Parameter uncertainty:* uncertainty in the input values themselves (activity data quantities and emission factor values). This is the type most accessible for companies to assess, using statistical analysis, instrument calibration records, or expert judgment.

<HighlightBox>
A **material discrepancy** is an error that results in a reported quantity or statement being significantly different from the true value or meaning. As a rule of thumb, an error is considered to be materially misleading if its value exceeds 5 percent of the total inventory for the part of the organisation being assessed.
</HighlightBox>

<AnalogyBox>
Think of uncertainty assessment like a weather forecast. A meteorologist does not simply report "tomorrow's temperature is 18°C" without acknowledging the range of possible outcomes. Similarly, a GHG inventory that reports a single figure without any discussion of its reliability gives users a false sense of precision. Characterising uncertainty does not weaken a report; it strengthens its credibility.
</AnalogyBox>

## Documentation and the Audit Trail

Documentation is the record of methods, data, systems, assumptions, and estimates used to prepare the inventory. Since estimating GHG emissions is inherently technical, high-quality transparent documentation is particularly important to credibility. Records must include:

- Bibliographic references for all primary data sources and emission factors, with copies archived and retrievable
- Assumptions and criteria used to select boundaries, base years, methods, and emission factors
- A log of any changes in data or methodology between reporting years
- Calculation spreadsheets and records, with input data clearly differentiated from calculated values

Without this documentation, an inventory cannot be externally verified. Statements made by management for which there is no supporting documentation cannot be confirmed by a verifier. Companies should therefore design their record-keeping procedures from the outset with future external review in mind.

<DeepDive title="Integrating GHG management with existing business systems">
KPMG found that a key factor in deriving reliable, verifiable GHG data is integrating GHG data management with a company's core operational management and assurance processes. This is more efficient than creating a separate reporting function, and it means that as GHG information becomes increasingly monetised it will already be subject to the same management attention and internal controls as other key performance indicators. Data generation and reporting systems are only as reliable as the people who operate them: adequate training and clear communication of reporting objectives are equally important to the design of the system itself.
