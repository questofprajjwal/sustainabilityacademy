<h2 class="text-2xl font-bold text-gray-800 mb-4">Selecting GRI Indicators: The 200, 300 & 400 Series</h2>

<HighlightBox>
  <p class="font-semibold text-green-800">The core principle</p>
  <p class="text-green-900 mt-1">You do <strong>not</strong> use the full GRI suite for every company. The GRI Standards catalog is comprehensive (deliberately so). Your job is to select the subset that is relevant to this specific company, in this specific industry, for this specific report. This lesson shows you how that selection works in practice.</p>
</HighlightBox>

<h3 class="text-xl font-bold mt-6 mb-3">A Quick Refresher on GRI Structure</h3>

<p class="mb-3">Before we get into selection, you need to understand how GRI is organized. The standards fall into three series, each covering one of the ESG pillars:</p>

<ResponsiveTable>
<table class="mb-6">
  <tr><th>Series</th><th>Pillar</th><th>What It Covers</th><th>Examples</th></tr>
  <tr><td><strong>GRI 200</strong></td><td>Economic</td><td>Financial performance, market presence, procurement, anti-corruption, tax</td><td>201 (Economic Performance), 205 (Anti-corruption), 207 (Tax)</td></tr>
  <tr><td><strong>GRI 300</strong></td><td>Environmental</td><td>Materials, energy, water, biodiversity, emissions, waste</td><td>302 (Energy), 303 (Water), 305 (Emissions), 306 (Waste)</td></tr>
  <tr><td><strong>GRI 400</strong></td><td>Social</td><td>Employment, health & safety, training, diversity, human rights, community</td><td>401 (Employment), 403 (OHS), 404 (Training), 405 (Diversity)</td></tr>
</table>
</ResponsiveTable>

<p class="mb-3">On top of these topic-specific standards, there are the <strong>GRI Universal Standards</strong> (GRI 1, 2, and 3) that apply to every report. GRI 1 sets the foundation, GRI 2 covers general organizational disclosures, and GRI 3 covers the materiality process. These are non-negotiable: every GRI-referenced report includes them.</p>

<p class="mb-4">The topic-specific standards (200, 300, 400 series) are where selection comes in. Each standard has multiple "disclosures" within it: specific data points or qualitative descriptions the company can report on. For example, GRI 305 (Emissions) includes disclosures for Scope 1 emissions (305-1), Scope 2 emissions (305-2), Scope 3 emissions (305-3), GHG emissions intensity (305-4), and reduction of GHG emissions (305-5). You may choose to report on all five, or only the first two if the company does not yet have Scope 3 data.</p>

<h3 class="text-xl font-bold mt-6 mb-3">The Four Inputs That Drive Selection</h3>

<p class="mb-3">Indicator selection is not a guessing game. It is driven by four concrete inputs, each of which you should already have by this point in the process:</p>

<p class="mb-2"><strong>1. Peer benchmarking results.</strong> From Lesson 2.1, you know which indicators your client's peers commonly report on. This is your strongest signal. If every peer reports on GRI 302 (Energy) and GRI 305 (Emissions), those are table stakes: your client should report on them too.</p>

<p class="mb-2"><strong>2. The company's past reports.</strong> If the company has published sustainability reports before, look at what indicators they have already reported against. Dropping indicators without explanation looks bad: it suggests the company is backtracking. If you want to drop something, have a clear rationale (and document it).</p>

<p class="mb-2"><strong>3. Industry relevance.</strong> Some indicators are critical for certain industries and irrelevant for others. A mining company must report on GRI 304 (Biodiversity) and GRI 403 (Occupational Health and Safety). A software company probably does not need 304 at all. Industry relevance is partly covered by peer benchmarking, but you should also apply your own judgment here.</p>

<p class="mb-4"><strong>4. Materiality assessment results.</strong> From Lesson 2.2, you have a prioritized list of material topics. Each material topic maps to one or more GRI standards. If "Climate Change" is a top material topic, GRI 305 is essential. If "Employee Well-being" is high priority, GRI 401, 403, and 404 are in play. The materiality matrix is your justification for including or excluding indicators.</p>

<AnalogyBox>
  <p class="font-semibold text-blue-800">Think of it like building a menu</p>
  <p class="mt-2 text-blue-900">GRI is the full cookbook: every recipe ever written. You are the chef building a menu for a specific restaurant. You pick dishes based on what your customers expect (peers), what you have served before (past reports), what ingredients are available locally (industry relevance and data availability), and what your food critics care about most (materiality). You do not serve every dish in the cookbook. You serve a curated, coherent selection.</p>
</AnalogyBox>

<h3 class="text-xl font-bold mt-6 mb-3">Walking Through the Selection Process</h3>

<p class="mb-3">Let us walk through how this actually works in practice. You sit down with your benchmarking notes, the company's past report (if it exists), and the materiality results. Then you go through each GRI series systematically.</p>

<p class="mb-2"><strong>GRI 200 - Economic Series</strong></p>
<p class="mb-3">Most companies include GRI 201 (Economic Performance) because it covers revenue, operating costs, employee wages, and community investments (data that typically already exists in the annual report). GRI 205 (Anti-corruption) is included by almost everyone because corruption risk is material across industries. GRI 207 (Tax) is increasingly expected, especially for large companies, because tax governance has become a hot topic with investors and regulators. The rest of the 200 series (202 for Market Presence, 203 for Indirect Economic Impacts, 204 for Procurement Practices, and 206 for Anti-competitive Behavior) are included only when relevant. A company with significant local procurement impact might include 203 and 204. Most skip 206 unless there is a specific reason.</p>

<p class="mb-2"><strong>GRI 300 - Environmental Series</strong></p>
<p class="mb-3">This is where selection matters most, because the environmental indicators are the most data-intensive and the most scrutinized. GRI 302 (Energy) and 305 (Emissions) are nearly universal: almost every company reports on them. GRI 303 (Water and Effluents) and 306 (Waste) are standard for manufacturing, FMCG, and heavy industry. GRI 304 (Biodiversity) is relevant for extractives, agriculture, and real estate companies with operations near sensitive areas, and is increasingly expected as biodiversity disclosure regulations emerge. GRI 301 (Materials) is relevant for companies with significant raw material consumption. The key question for each: does the company have data for this, and is it material?</p>

<p class="mb-2"><strong>GRI 400 - Social Series</strong></p>
<p class="mb-4">The social series is broad. GRI 401 (Employment), 403 (Occupational Health and Safety), 404 (Training and Education), and 405 (Diversity and Equal Opportunity) are included in nearly every report. GRI 413 (Local Communities) is common for companies with significant community impact. GRI 414 (Supplier Social Assessment) and 408/409 (Child Labor / Forced Labor) are increasingly expected as supply chain due diligence becomes a regulatory requirement in many jurisdictions. GRI 418 (Customer Privacy) is critical for technology and financial services companies. The rest are situational.</p>

<ExampleBox>
  <p class="font-semibold text-amber-800">Sample indicator selection: mid-size manufacturing company</p>
  <p class="mt-2">A mid-size auto components manufacturer in India, publishing their second sustainability report. Based on benchmarking, past report, and materiality:</p>
  <p class="mt-2"><strong>Economic (200 series):</strong></p>
  <ul class="mt-1 space-y-1">
    <li>GRI 201 (Economic Performance): standard inclusion</li>
    <li>GRI 205 (Anti-corruption): material topic, peers report it</li>
  </ul>
  <p class="mt-2"><strong>Environmental (300 series):</strong></p>
  <ul class="mt-1 space-y-1">
    <li>GRI 301 (Materials): significant raw material use</li>
    <li>GRI 302 (Energy): top material topic, all peers report it</li>
    <li>GRI 303 (Water): manufacturing operations are water-intensive</li>
    <li>GRI 305 (Emissions): top material topic, regulatory expectation</li>
    <li>GRI 306 (Waste): significant waste streams from manufacturing</li>
    <li>GRI 304 (Biodiversity): <strong>excluded</strong>; no operations near sensitive areas, no peer reports on it</li>
  </ul>
  <p class="mt-2"><strong>Social (400 series):</strong></p>
  <ul class="mt-1 space-y-1">
    <li>GRI 401 (Employment): standard inclusion</li>
    <li>GRI 403 (Occupational Health and Safety): critical for manufacturing, high material topic</li>
    <li>GRI 404 (Training and Education): included, though data will be challenging</li>
    <li>GRI 405 (Diversity and Equal Opportunity): included per peer benchmark</li>
    <li>GRI 413 (Local Communities): included due to CSR programs</li>
    <li>GRI 414 (Supplier Social Assessment): <strong>partially included</strong>; company has limited supply chain data</li>
  </ul>
  <p class="mt-2 text-amber-700">Total: 12 topic-specific standards selected out of 30+ available. This is a manageable, defensible scope.</p>
</ExampleBox>

<h3 class="text-xl font-bold mt-6 mb-3">Building the Data Requirement Sheet from Your Selection</h3>

<p class="mb-3">Once you have finalized the indicator list, it becomes the backbone of your data requirement sheet (the Excel template you share with the client). Each selected GRI standard translates into a set of specific data points the client needs to provide.</p>

<p class="mb-3">For example, if you have selected GRI 305 (Emissions), the data requirement sheet will include rows for:</p>

<ul class="list-disc ml-6 space-y-2 mb-4">
  <li>Scope 1 GHG emissions (in tonnes CO2e), broken down by source if possible</li>
  <li>Scope 2 GHG emissions (location-based and market-based, in tonnes CO2e)</li>
  <li>Scope 3 GHG emissions by category (if the company tracks them)</li>
  <li>GHG emissions intensity ratio and the denominator used</li>
  <li>Reduction in GHG emissions achieved during the reporting period</li>
</ul>

<p class="mb-4">You request this data for the current reporting year plus two to three previous years, so the report can show trends. The data requirement sheet is your primary communication tool with the client during the data collection phase: everything the client needs to provide is in one place, organized by GRI standard.</p>

<h3 class="text-xl font-bold mt-6 mb-3">Common Pitfalls in Indicator Selection</h3>

<p class="mb-3">A few mistakes that come up repeatedly:</p>

<ul class="list-disc ml-6 space-y-2 mb-4">
  <li><strong>Selecting too many indicators.</strong> Ambition is good, but if the company cannot provide data for half the indicators you selected, you end up with empty sections or vague narrative filler. Be realistic about data availability.</li>
  <li><strong>Selecting too few indicators.</strong> Going too minimal makes the report look thin and raises questions from rating agencies about what the company is hiding. Find the balance.</li>
  <li><strong>Dropping indicators without explanation.</strong> If the company reported on GRI 303 (Water) last year and you drop it this year, readers will notice. Always explain changes in scope: a brief note in the "About the Report" section is sufficient.</li>
  <li><strong>Ignoring disclosure levels within a standard.</strong> You might include GRI 305 but only report on disclosures 305-1 and 305-2, skipping Scope 3 (305-3). That is fine, but be transparent about it in the GRI Content Index. Partial reporting is better than no reporting, as long as you are honest about the gaps.</li>
  <li><strong>Not aligning with BRSR requirements.</strong> For Indian companies, the BRSR framework has its own set of mandatory disclosures. Many of these overlap with GRI, but not all. If the company files BRSR, make sure your GRI indicator selection covers the overlapping areas so data is consistent across both reports.</li>
</ul>

  <p class="mb-3">While GRI provides the structural backbone, SASB (Sustainability Accounting Standards Board) offers industry-specific materiality maps that can sharpen your indicator selection. SASB identifies the sustainability topics most likely to affect financial performance for 77 industries.</p>
  <p class="mb-3">For example, SASB's standard for the Technology & Communications sector highlights data privacy, energy management in data centers, and employee diversity as financially material. If your client is in this sector, these topics should definitely make your GRI indicator shortlist, even if some peers are not yet reporting on them.</p>
  <p>Using SASB alongside GRI is increasingly common. You reference GRI for structure and comprehensive disclosure, and SASB for investor-relevant focus. Many companies note both frameworks in their "About the Report" section and include a SASB index alongside the GRI Content Index.</p>
</DeepDive>

<h3 class="text-xl font-bold mt-6 mb-3">Getting Client Sign-off</h3>

<p class="mb-3">Before you build the data requirement sheet and start collecting data, get the client to formally agree on the indicator list. This is not a formality: it is a safeguard.</p>

<p class="mb-3">Present the selection as a structured document: each indicator, why it is included (or excluded), and what data will be needed. Walk the client through it. Give them a chance to push back. If they want to add or remove something, discuss the implications.</p>

<p class="mb-4">The reason this matters: if you start collecting data against an indicator list the client has not approved, you risk doing work that gets thrown out later. Worse, the client may complain that you asked their departments for data they "never agreed to report on." Getting sign-off on the indicator list is a two-minute conversation that prevents weeks of rework.</p>
