<h2 class="text-2xl font-bold text-gray-800 mb-4">The Standard Report Structure: What Goes Where</h2>

<HighlightBox>
  <p class="font-semibold text-green-800">Why structure matters</p>
  <p class="text-green-900 mt-1">Every ESG report follows a broadly similar structure. The market has settled on an order that works - readers (investors, rating agencies, regulators) know where to look for what. Deviate too much and you create friction. Follow it well and the report feels intuitive before anyone reads a single word.</p>
</HighlightBox>

<h3 class="text-xl font-bold mt-6 mb-3">The Typical Table of Contents</h3>

<p class="mb-3">If you look at 20 sustainability reports across industries, you will notice that about 80% of the structure is the same. The remaining 20% is where companies add their own flavor - a special section on innovation, a deep dive into a flagship program, or a standalone climate chapter. But the bones are consistent.</p>

<p class="mb-4">Here is the standard structure that the market follows, and that clients will expect you to know:</p>

<ResponsiveTable>
<table class="mb-6">
  <tr><th>#</th><th>Section</th><th>What It Covers</th></tr>
  <tr><td>1</td><td><strong>About the Report</strong></td><td>Boundary, scope, reporting period, standards referenced, contact details</td></tr>
  <tr><td>2</td><td><strong>Chairman's / CEO's Message</strong></td><td>Vision, performance highlights, forward-looking statements, sustainability commitment</td></tr>
  <tr><td>3</td><td><strong>Company Overview</strong></td><td>Business model, operations, markets served, key financial highlights</td></tr>
  <tr><td>4</td><td><strong>Materiality</strong></td><td>Material topics, stakeholder engagement process, materiality matrix</td></tr>
  <tr><td>5</td><td><strong>Environmental</strong></td><td>Energy, emissions, water, waste, biodiversity, targets and progress</td></tr>
  <tr><td>6</td><td><strong>Social</strong></td><td>Workforce, diversity, health and safety, training, community, supply chain</td></tr>
  <tr><td>7</td><td><strong>Governance</strong></td><td>Board composition, ethics, risk oversight, compliance, data governance</td></tr>
  <tr><td>8</td><td><strong>Risk</strong></td><td>ESG risk identification, mitigation strategies, climate risk (TCFD-aligned if applicable)</td></tr>
  <tr><td>9</td><td><strong>Business Performance</strong></td><td>Economic value generated, R&D investment, sustainable products/services</td></tr>
  <tr><td>10</td><td><strong>GRI Content Index</strong></td><td>Maps each GRI indicator to the page or section where it is disclosed</td></tr>
  <tr><td>11</td><td><strong>Glossary / Abbreviations</strong></td><td>Definitions of technical terms and acronyms used in the report</td></tr>
</table>
</ResponsiveTable>

<h3 class="text-xl font-bold mt-6 mb-3">Why This Order Works</h3>

<p class="mb-3">The structure follows a natural logic. You open by telling the reader what they are looking at (About the Report), who is speaking (CEO Message), and what the company does (Company Overview). Then you establish what matters most (Materiality) before going into the three pillars - E, S, and G. Risk and business performance provide the broader context. The GRI Content Index at the end serves as a reference map.</p>

<p class="mb-4">Think of it like a conversation. You would not walk into a meeting and start talking about your water consumption data. You would introduce yourself, set the context, explain what you think is important, and then get into the details. The report structure mirrors that.</p>

<h3 class="text-xl font-bold mt-6 mb-3">When to Deviate</h3>

<p class="mb-3">There are legitimate reasons to change the order or add sections. Here are the common ones:</p>

<ul class="list-disc ml-6 space-y-2 mb-4">
  <li><strong>Regulatory requirements:</strong> If the company is reporting under CSRD or another regulation, the structure may need to follow a prescribed format. The standard structure adapts, but the regulatory requirements take priority.</li>
  <li><strong>Theme-driven reports:</strong> Some companies organize by theme rather than by pillar. For instance, a company with a "Circular Economy" theme might have chapters organized around materials, products, operations, and people - weaving E, S, and G into each. This is sophisticated and requires strong writing to pull off.</li>
  <li><strong>Industry-specific sections:</strong> A mining company might add a dedicated chapter on tailings management. A pharma company might add one on access to medicine. These sit alongside the standard structure, not instead of it.</li>
  <li><strong>Client preference:</strong> Some companies want the Governance section before Environment. Some want Materiality woven into the pillar sections rather than standing alone. If the client has a strong preference and it makes logical sense, go with it.</li>
</ul>

<AnalogyBox>
  <p class="font-semibold text-blue-800">Think of it like a house</p>
  <p class="mt-2 text-blue-900">The standard structure is the floor plan that works - kitchen near the dining room, bedrooms away from the street. You can customize the finishes, add a room, or move a wall. But if you put the kitchen on the roof, people will be confused. The same applies to report structure: innovate within reason, but respect what readers expect.</p>
</AnalogyBox>

<h3 class="text-xl font-bold mt-6 mb-3">A Closer Look at the Opening Sections</h3>

<p class="mb-3"><strong>"About the Report"</strong> is deceptively simple. It sets the boundary for everything that follows. Whatever boundary you define here - standalone entity, consolidated group, specific geographies - you must follow that same boundary for ALL data across the entire report. If you say "this report covers our India operations," then every number in the report must be for India operations only. No mixing in global figures in one section and India figures in another.</p>

<p class="mb-4">This is where many reports run into trouble later. The boundary was vague at the start, or it was set without checking whether all departments could actually provide data at that level. Be crystal clear with the client on this from day one. If subsidiaries do not have the data, exclude them from the boundary upfront and note it. It is far better to have a tight, accurate boundary than a broad one full of gaps.</p>

<HighlightBox>
  <p class="font-semibold text-green-800">The boundary rule</p>
  <p class="text-green-900 mt-1">The boundary you set in "About the Report" is the boundary for the entire report. If you include an entity, you need its data everywhere. If you cannot get that data, exclude the entity and be transparent about it. This single decision prevents dozens of problems down the line.</p>
</HighlightBox>

<h3 class="text-xl font-bold mt-6 mb-3">The Chairman's / CEO's Message</h3>

<p class="mb-3">This section gets read more than you think. Investors often start here. Rating agencies glance at it to understand the company's posture. The message is written by either the consultant team or the client's internal communications team, but it always gets the Chairman's or CEO's personal approval.</p>

<p class="mb-3">The content is a blend of:</p>
<ul class="list-disc ml-6 space-y-2 mb-4">
  <li>Vision for the company's sustainability journey</li>
  <li>How the company has performed in the reporting period</li>
  <li>Where the company is headed - targets, commitments, strategy</li>
  <li>A flavor of business and sustainability together - not just one or the other</li>
</ul>

<p class="mb-4">A common mistake is making the CEO Message either entirely about financial performance (that belongs in the annual report) or entirely about sustainability aspirations without substance. It needs to balance both. It should also reference specific targets and how the company is tracking against them. Vague language like "we are committed to a sustainable future" does not impress anyone.</p>

<ExampleBox>
  <p class="font-semibold text-amber-800">What a strong CEO Message includes</p>
  <p class="mt-2">Compare these two opening lines:</p>
  <p class="mt-2"><strong>Weak:</strong> "We are deeply committed to sustainability and believe in creating a better world for future generations."</p>
  <p class="mt-2"><strong>Strong:</strong> "In FY2025, we reduced Scope 1 and 2 emissions by 14% against our 2030 baseline while growing revenue by 8%. This report details how we did it and where we fell short."</p>
  <p class="mt-2 text-amber-700 text-sm">The strong version is specific, honest about shortcomings, and gives the reader a reason to keep reading. The weak version could appear in any report by any company.</p>
</ExampleBox>

<h3 class="text-xl font-bold mt-6 mb-3">Company Overview: Setting the Business Context</h3>

<p class="mb-3">The Company Overview section gives the reader enough business context to understand the ESG disclosures that follow. If you are writing about water consumption, the reader needs to know what industry the company operates in and where. This section provides that.</p>

<p class="mb-3">It typically includes:</p>
<ul class="list-disc ml-6 space-y-2 mb-4">
  <li>Nature of business and key products/services</li>
  <li>Geographic presence and operational footprint</li>
  <li>Key financial highlights (revenue, employees, sites)</li>
  <li>Value chain overview - where the company sits in its industry</li>
</ul>

<p class="mb-4">Do not turn this into a 15-page company brochure. Two to four pages is sufficient. The annual report already covers the business in detail - the sustainability report just needs enough context to anchor the ESG narrative. Keep it concise, visual (a map of operations, an infographic of the value chain), and purposeful.</p>

<DeepDive title="How Materiality Fits Into the Structure">
  <p class="mb-3">The Materiality section is the bridge between the business context and the pillar sections. It answers the question: "Of all the ESG topics we could talk about, which ones matter most to this company and its stakeholders?"</p>
  <p class="mb-3">If the company has already done a materiality assessment, they typically reuse it for two to three years. You refer to it and structure the pillar sections accordingly - giving more space to highly material topics and less to lower-priority ones.</p>
  <p class="mb-3">If they have not done one, this is where you will need to have a conversation about whether to conduct one. Many companies are now moving toward double materiality assessments (even when CSRD does not apply to them) because it has become the industry expectation. Double materiality combines impact materiality (how the business affects the world) with financial materiality (how the world affects the business).</p>
  <p>Either way, the material topics identified here should visibly drive the depth and emphasis of the E, S, and G chapters that follow. If water is a top material topic, the Environmental section should devote meaningful space to it - not just a paragraph.</p>
</DeepDive>

<h3 class="text-xl font-bold mt-6 mb-3">The GRI Content Index: Last In, First to Be Checked</h3>

<p class="mb-3">The GRI Content Index is created at the very end, once everything is finalized. It is a mechanical exercise - you map each GRI indicator to the page number or section where it is addressed. But it is also one of the first things an assurance provider or rating agency checks. If your index says GRI 305-1 (Direct GHG Emissions) is on page 47 and it is actually on page 52, that looks sloppy.</p>

<p class="mb-4">Wait until the design is done and page numbers are locked before creating the index. Doing it too early means redoing it when content shifts during design. This is always the last thing you do.</p>

<div class="bg-green-50 border border-green-200 rounded p-4">
  <p class="font-semibold text-green-800">Key Takeaway</p>
  <p class="text-green-900 mt-1">The standard ESG report structure is well-established and readers expect it. Start with context (About, CEO Message, Company Overview), establish what matters (Materiality), go deep on the three pillars (E, S, G), add risk and business context, and close with the GRI Content Index. The boundary you set in "About the Report" governs every data point in the document - get it right early.</p>
</div>
