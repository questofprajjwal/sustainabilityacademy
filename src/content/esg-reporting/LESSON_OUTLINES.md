# Sustainability / ESG Reporting in Practice - Lesson Outlines

## Module 0: Why Companies Report & How to Begin

### 0.1 - Why Do Companies Write ESG Reports?
- The five real reasons: investor demand, communication, regulation, peer pressure, ratings improvement
- The "why" problem: most companies don't actually know why they want a report
- How the "why" shapes the entire engagement - following intent gets you faster approvals
- Different audiences (investors, rating agencies, regulators, public) want different nuances from the same report
- HighlightBox: The "why" determines the "what" - a report written for ratings improvement emphasizes different things than one written for regulatory compliance, even though the content is 80% the same

### 0.2 - Who Is Involved? Stakeholders, Teams & Roles
- The sustainability team as your primary contact
- Supporting departments and what they provide:
  - HR: diversity, training, benefits, health & safety data
  - Operations: energy, water, waste, emissions data
  - Finance: economic performance, tax governance, investment data
  - Health & Safety: incident rates, safety metrics
  - CSR: community projects, spend, impact data
  - IT: data governance, cybersecurity, privacy
- The consultant's role vs. the in-house team's role
- Who signs off: the board approval chain

### 0.3 - The First Conversation: Discovery & Setting Expectations
- Two client scenarios: the fresh starter vs. the "mature" company wanting innovation
- What to ask in the discovery call: why now? who asked for it? what's the deadline? who's the audience?
- What clients won't tell you voluntarily: the real motivation and the real deadline
- Recognizing red flags early: stakeholders who assume they know more than you, teams that haven't thought about this at all
- HighlightBox: "Always push back on timelines. They will always want it done early, it will always be because of them that it takes long, and they will put it on you."

### 0.4 - Timelines, Scope & Boundary Decisions
- Ideal timeline (2.5-3 months) vs. reality (as long as it takes)
- The boundary decision: standalone vs. consolidated
- Why this matters: whatever boundary you set, you follow it for ALL data across the entire report
- If subsidiaries don't have data, exclude them from the boundary upfront
- Aligning boundary with BRSR and annual report to avoid data mismatches
- The scope conversation: what reporting period, what geographies, what entities

## Module 1: The Standards Landscape

### 1.1 - GRI: The Backbone of Sustainability Reporting
- Why GRI is the structural base regardless of what else companies reference
- GRI's strengths: most comprehensive, most interoperable, most widely used KPI set
- The structure: Universal Standards, 200 (Economic), 300 (Environmental), 400 (Social) series
- How GRI is used in practice: not every standard applies; you select what's relevant
- GRI as the data requirement sheet template backbone

### 1.2 - Other Frameworks at a Glance: SASB, TCFD, IFRS, CDP, BRSR
- SASB: industry-specific materiality maps
- TCFD: climate risk disclosure (now absorbed into ISSB/IFRS)
- IFRS S1/S2: the new global baseline for sustainability and climate disclosure
- CDP: questionnaire-based disclosure for climate, water, forests
- BRSR: India's mandatory Business Responsibility & Sustainability Reporting
- UNPRI: investor-focused indexing
- How these relate to each other - most reference or align with GRI
- Cross-link to platform courses: IFRS S2, Double Materiality, ESG Benchmarking

### 1.3 - How Standards Interoperate & Which to Choose
- GRI as the base layer; other standards as lenses or add-ons
- How to choose: depends on audience (investors want IFRS/CDP, European stakeholders want CSRD, Indian companies need BRSR)
- The practical reality: companies pick GRI + 1-2 others to index against
- Don't overwhelm a first-time reporter with every standard

## Module 2: Peer Benchmarking & Materiality

### 2.1 - Conducting a Peer Benchmarking Exercise
- Why you start here: understanding what peers report tells you what the market expects
- How to select peers: same industry, same geography, similar size, publicly reporting
- What to look for: standards they reference, topics they cover, how they structure the report, design and length
- The practical insight: everyone reports roughly the same things; only design and language change
- Using peer benchmarking to narrow down which GRI indicators to include

### 2.2 - Materiality Assessment in Practice
- If the company already has one: they reuse it for 2-3 years; you refer to it
- If they don't: the rise of double materiality (even when CSRD doesn't apply - it's the buzzword)
- Double materiality explained simply: impact materiality (how business affects the world) + financial materiality (how the world affects the business)
- Stakeholder identification: internal and external groups
- How surveys/discussions actually happen
- Does materiality actually influence the report? Yes, somewhat - it helps choose focus topics
- Indian context: materiality is often not taken rigorously
- Cross-link to platform's Double Materiality course for deep-dive

### 2.3 - Selecting GRI Indicators: The 200, 300 & 400 Series
- You do NOT use the full GRI suite for every company
- Selection process: peer benchmarking results + past reports + industry relevance + materiality
- Walking through examples: why a manufacturing company picks 302 (Energy), 305 (Emissions), 306 (Waste) but may skip 304 (Biodiversity)
- The data requirement sheet is built from this selection

## Module 3: Data Collection & The Data Requirement Sheet

### 3.1 - Building & Sharing the Data Requirement Template
- The template is based on GRI structure - consulting firms generally have it as an Excel workbook
- Request 2-3 years of historical data + current year
- Providing guidance: explain every field, what units, what format
- The template as a communication tool - it tells the client exactly what you need
- Sharing the selected subset (not the entire GRI universe)

### 3.2 - Working with Departments: HR, Operations, Finance & Beyond
- Each department has different data readiness levels
- Finance and governance: usually well-sorted (annual report already tracks this)
- Environmental: scattered across operations, facilities management, procurement
- Social/HR: training data and supply chain data are the hardest
- People hearing about this data request for the first time - they're not prepared
- Repeated walkthroughs of the same template - you'll explain it many times

### 3.3 - When Data Doesn't Come: Escalation & the Force of Hierarchy
- First approach: explain the need and why you need it
- Second approach: the force of hierarchy - escalate to senior leadership who can mandate it
- This is the #1 reason engagements take longer than planned
- Managing your own frustration while staying professional
- HighlightBox: Data collection is the bottleneck. Not frameworks. Not writing. Getting data out of people is the hardest part of the entire process.

### 3.4 - Data Sanity Checks: The Practitioner's Checklist
- Check for extremes: 0% and 100% need proof
- "The absence of evidence is not the evidence of absence" - missing data does NOT mean zero
- Internal consistency: same metric, same number everywhere in the report
- Cross-report consistency: data must match between sustainability report, BRSR, annual report
- Year-on-year changes: explain significant swings; check for methodology changes
- Decimal places: generally no more than two
- Currency consistency across years and geographies
- GHG inventory definitions and breakups must be clear and consistent
- Numbers must add up: totals = sum of parts. Always.
- CalculationExercise: Given a sample data table with deliberate errors, identify what's wrong

### 3.5 - Version Control & Avoiding the Data Disaster
- The nightmare: data coming in 10 different times, losing track of which version is which
- Version naming conventions: date-stamped, clearly labeled
- The golden rule: prefer getting final data AFTER assurance is complete
- If assurance happens after your draft: expect many changes
- Maintaining a single source of truth for data

## Module 4: Structuring the Report

### 4.1 - The Standard Report Structure: What Goes Where
- The typical structure that the market follows:
  1. About the Report (boundary, scope, period, standards)
  2. Chairman's / CEO's Message
  3. Company Overview (business, operations, markets)
  4. Materiality section
  5. Environmental section
  6. Social section
  7. Governance section
  8. Risk section
  9. Business performance elements
  10. GRI Content Index
  11. Glossary / Abbreviations
- Why this order works and when to deviate
- CaseStudy/Exercise: Order these shuffled chapters into a logical table of contents

### 4.2 - Building a Blueprint Alongside the ToC
- Don't just share a ToC - share a blueprint showing sub-components of each chapter
- This acclimatizes the client AND yourself to what will be written
- Blueprint = chapter title + 4-6 sub-sections + 1-line description of each
- Getting client buy-in on the blueprint prevents surprises later
- ExampleBox: Sample blueprint for an Environmental chapter

### 4.3 - Setting a Theme: The Character Arc of Your Report
- The analogy: a theme is like a character arc in a movie or series - consistency matters
- Where themes come from: the company's focus for the year, annual report direction, sustainability strategy
- Add a sustainability lens to their business focus - that's the theme
- If the client doesn't know: suggest 2-3 themes with a brief intro and how the report would be structured around each
- Choosing agnostic themes over specific ones: "AI & Sustainability" is too narrow (forced in S & G sections); broader themes flow naturally across all pillars
- HighlightBox: A good theme is broad enough to touch every chapter naturally. If you have to force it, it's too specific.

## Module 5: Writing the Report

### 5.1 - The Writing Method: Questions First, Answers Second
- The technique: write the questions you want to answer in each section
- Then write answers based on what you want to communicate
- Remove the questions - only answers remain
- Edit, connect paragraphs, polish flow
- Why this works: it gives structure to your thinking before you write prose
- This works especially well for sections where you're unclear on what to write
- HighlightBox: "Just go ahead and write it. Don't wait for the client to give you narratives. Dig through their past reports, policies, annual reports - write it yourself, share it, let them correct."

### 5.2 - About the Report, Boundary & Chairman's Message
- "About the Report": mostly straightforward - boundary, scope, reporting period, standards followed
- The boundary you set here = the boundary for ALL data. Be crystal clear with the client.
- Chairman's Message: written by consultant or internal team, approved by Chairman
- Content: vision + past performance + future direction + sustainability flavor + targets
- Heavily influenced by the annual report's similar section
- Must blend business and sustainability - not just one or the other

### 5.3 - Writing the Environmental Section
- Start with the business-environment interplay: how business impacts environment AND how environment impacts business
- Identify the core environmental resource for that specific business (energy for some, water for others, waste for others)
- Key topics: energy & emissions, water management, waste management, biodiversity (if relevant), resource efficiency
- Targets are critical - investors and rating agencies look at progress toward targets
- Practitioner tip: convert savings to relatable equivalents ("equivalent to X cars off the road", "equivalent to planting X trees")
- Data precision: environmental data must be exact, units consistent, methodology noted
- Cross-link to platform courses: Scope 1 & 2, Scope 3

### 5.4 - Writing the Social Section
- Two main pillars: HR/Human Capital and CSR
- HR section: diversity, worker benefits, health & safety, training, community engagement
- Supply chain: human rights due diligence, grievance mechanisms, labor laws (POSH, etc.)
- CSR: spend, projects, impact metrics
- Hardest data: supply chain and training (most companies don't track it)
- Watch out: definitions of social concepts (like diversity) vary across geographies
- Writing for a global company vs. a domestic company - different standards apply

### 5.5 - Writing the Governance Section
- The easiest section to write (data comes from annual report and policies - already well-documented)
- Start with a clear governance structure - where many companies fall short
- Key topics: board composition, board meeting attendance, ethics & anti-corruption, risk management, executive compensation, whistleblower mechanisms
- Emerging areas: tax governance, IT governance & data governance, board skill sets (IFRS asks for this)
- How to draw from annual report without copy-pasting - add sustainability governance lens

### 5.6 - Flow, Language & Narrative-Data Balance
- Flow: chapters should not feel siloed; one section leads into the next
- Within a chapter: sections link logically, paragraphs connect (one ends where the next begins)
- Think of it as a story you're telling in a professional setting
- Language consistency: first person or third person - pick one and stick to it
- Active vs. passive voice: don't switch within paragraphs (mix across report is fine)
- Narrative-data balance: aim for roughly 50/50
- Too much narrative = purposeless to read. Too much data = no story.
- No repetition: don't repeat policies in every chapter; don't copy last year's content
- AnalogyBox: Writing an ESG report is like writing a professional story - one paragraph ends and the next picks up from there

### 5.7 - Using AI to Write (Without Producing AI Slop)
- AI (ChatGPT etc.) is a legitimate tool - most consultants use it
- The problem: unedited AI output sounds robotic and generic
- Big Fours have been penalized for AI-generated content without review
- The rule: AI writes the first draft, YOU edit it to be human and purposeful
- What to check: tone, specificity (AI generalizes - you need to make it about THIS company), accuracy of any claims, consistency with the rest of the report
- Never send AI output without reading it yourself. Ever.

## Module 6: Revisions, QA & the GRI Content Index

### 6.1 - Surviving the Revision Cycle
- The reality: revisions are never-ending until someone decides it's done
- By the end you'll forget what you wrote, what the client asked, and the client will forget where you started
- Managing revisions: track changes, version numbers, clear comment threads
- Setting revision limits or milestones upfront helps
- How to handle conflicting feedback from different stakeholders

### 6.2 - The Final QA Checklist: What to Check Before It's Too Late
- Index page matching: ToC page numbers match actual pages
- Data matching: same number everywhere in the report (no contradictions)
- Cross-report matching: sustainability report data = BRSR data = annual report data (same boundary)
- Factual corrections: all claims and facts verified
- Spell check: obvious but often missed under time pressure
- Spacing and formatting: clean, consistent
- Full read-through: read the entire report end-to-end
- Team review: get someone from your team to review
- Fresh eyes: have someone who was NOT involved in writing read it for the first time
- HighlightBox: The fresh-eyes review is non-negotiable. You've been staring at the report for weeks - you will miss things. A new person catches them instantly.

### 6.3 - Creating the GRI Content Index
- Created at the very end, once everything is sorted and finalized
- It's the last thing you do
- Maps each GRI indicator to the page number / section where it's addressed
- A mechanical exercise but important for credibility and reference

### 6.4 - Assurance & Verification: What You Need to Know
- Assurance is the company's choice - not mandatory for all (depends on regulation)
- If they want it: get it done
- Placement: beginning or end of report, wherever client prefers
- Key advice: try to get final data ONLY after assurance is complete
- If you draft before assurance, expect data changes and re-work
- Assurance increases reliance on data quality - good for everyone

## Module 7: Design, Delivery & What Comes After

### 7.1 - Design Principles: Brand Language, Visuals & Common Mistakes
- Best practice: use the company's existing design agency (they know the brand)
- Same agency that does annual report, investor materials - keeps visual consistency
- The report should look in sync with the annual report, not jarringly different
- Biggest mistake: hiring a new designer unfamiliar with the brand - they'll create something original but the company won't like it
- Balance of visuals, narrative, and data is what makes a report stand out
- Keep it short - too long is useless (a lot of companies overdo length)

### 7.2 - Deliverable Formats: PDF, Microsite & Beyond
- Clients want all formats: print-ready PDF, digital PDF, interactive version
- A microsite is generally advised and adds value
- Microsite benefits: searchability, shareability, accessibility, analytics
- Consider: executive summary as a standalone deliverable

### 7.3 - BRSR & Annual Report Alignment
- BRSR (India) is done alongside the annual report, not the sustainability report
- If the boundary is the same across BRSR and sustainability report: DATA MUST MATCH
- Common and serious error: data inconsistency between the two
- Check alignment early in the process, not at the end
- Cross-link to platform: this is about consistency, not about BRSR standards themselves

### 7.4 - Lessons from the Field: What I Wish I Knew on Day One
- "Just go ahead and write it" - don't wait for the client to hand you narratives and data
- Dig through past reports, annual reports, policies, everything publicly available - and write it yourself
- Share your draft with the client - they'll fine-tune and correct
- You will barely get narratives from them; that's how it goes
- The reports that stand out: balance between visuals, narrative, and data; kept concise
- The landscape in 2026: AI slop is everywhere; the differentiator is human editing and intentionality
- Consistency is everything: boundary, data, language, theme, design
- People management IS the job - the technical work is the easy part
