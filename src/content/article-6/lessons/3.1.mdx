Not every form of international climate cooperation involves the transfer of carbon credits. Article 6.8 of the Paris Agreement recognises this explicitly: it creates a framework for **non-market cooperative approaches** -- forms of cooperation that help countries achieve their NDCs without generating tradable units, without requiring corresponding adjustments, and without the complex accounting infrastructure that Article 6.2 and 6.4 demand.

For many countries -- particularly the least developed, the most remote, and those with the fewest technical resources -- Article 6.8 is not a fallback option but the primary realistic pathway for international climate cooperation.

## What Article 6.8 Actually Says

The Paris Agreement's Article 6.8 is brief. It states that Parties "recognize the importance of integrated, holistic and balanced non-market approaches available to Parties to assist in the implementation of their nationally determined contributions." It then specifies several aims:

- To promote mitigation and adaptation ambition
- To enhance public and private sector participation in NDC implementation
- To enable opportunities for coordination across instruments and relevant institutional arrangements

The key phrase is **non-market approaches**. Unlike Articles 6.2 and 6.4, there is no mechanism for generating tradable units, no corresponding adjustment requirement, and no UN-supervised registry. Article 6.8 is a framework for cooperation that delivers climate benefits through means other than carbon trading.

<HighlightBox>
Article 6.8 is the "everything else" track of Article 6. Where 6.2 governs bilateral credit transfers and 6.4 governs UN-supervised credits, 6.8 covers cooperation that does not involve credit generation at all -- capacity building, technology transfer, policy support, and finance provided without an expectation of carbon credits in return.
</HighlightBox>

## Why Non-Market Approaches Matter

The premise behind Article 6.8 is that carbon markets, despite their power and efficiency, cannot and should not be the only tool of international climate cooperation. Several important categories of climate action are poorly suited to market mechanisms:

**Adaptation actions** -- building sea walls, improving drought-resilient agriculture, establishing early warning systems -- produce climate benefits that are real and valuable, but they do not reduce atmospheric CO2 in a way that can be credibly quantified, verified, and traded. Carbon markets are fundamentally about mitigation. Adaptation finance requires non-market mechanisms.

**Systemic policy change** -- reforming energy subsidies, strengthening building codes, improving land use regulations -- produces mitigation benefits at scale, but those benefits are diffuse, long-term, and resistant to the project-level quantification that carbon crediting requires. Technical assistance and policy capacity building are more appropriate tools.

**Institutional capacity building** -- training regulators, establishing MRV systems, developing national registries -- is the precondition for participation in carbon markets, not a product of it. Countries that lack basic MRV infrastructure cannot participate in Article 6.2 or 6.4 without support that does not depend on generating credits.

**Finance for the poorest countries** -- many LDCs and SIDS lack the regulatory frameworks, technical expertise, and institutional infrastructure to participate effectively in carbon markets. Non-market approaches allow them to receive climate finance and technical support without first building all the systems that carbon market participation requires.

<AnalogyBox>
Think of international climate cooperation as a construction project. Carbon markets are the specialist contractors -- highly efficient at specific tasks, but they need a stable site, clear plans, and working infrastructure before they can begin. Article 6.8 is the site preparation and infrastructure work: building roads, laying foundations, training the local workforce. Without it, the specialist contractors have nowhere effective to operate. For the most capacity-constrained countries, site preparation is what they need most.
</AnalogyBox>

## Examples of Non-Market Cooperation in Practice

Article 6.8 encompasses a wide range of cooperative activities. The following examples illustrate the breadth of what non-market cooperation can include:

**Technology transfer:** A developed country provides a developing country with access to renewable energy technology -- solar panel manufacturing equipment, wind turbine design specifications, grid management software -- without charging for credits generated by the resulting deployment. The technology flows; the climate benefits accrue to the developing country's NDC.

**Capacity building:** The European Union funds a multi-year programme to help several West African nations establish national GHG inventory systems, train statisticians in Tier 2 emissions factor calculation, and build MRV capacity across key sectors. This does not generate credits; it builds the foundation for future participation in both Article 6.4 and domestic carbon pricing.

**Policy support:** A group of donor countries funds technical assistance to help a Pacific island nation develop a national climate adaptation plan, incorporating vulnerability assessments, community consultations, and budget integration. The result is a stronger NDC and more resilient communities -- achieved through knowledge and expertise transfer, not credit trading.

**Results-based finance without credit transfer:** A development bank provides payments to a country for verified emissions reductions -- but the payments are structured as grants or concessional loans, and the country retains the climate benefit in its NDC accounts. The bank does not receive A6.4 mitigation contribution credits or ITMOs. The finance flows; the credit stays with the host country.

**Joint research and development:** Two countries collaborate on developing lower-cost carbon capture technology, sharing research costs and results. Neither generates tradable credits from the collaboration; both benefit from the technological progress.

## The Decision 4/CMA.3 Work Programme

The operational framework for Article 6.8 was established at COP26 in Glasgow through **Decision 4/CMA.3**, which created a **work programme** to develop and operationalise non-market approaches. This work programme:

- Is coordinated by the UNFCCC Secretariat
- Reports annually to the CMA
- Identifies and promotes best practices in non-market cooperation
- Develops guidance on how non-market approaches can be integrated into NDC implementation
- Facilitates information sharing between Parties about available non-market cooperation opportunities

Unlike the Article 6.2 and 6.4 frameworks, which have detailed rulebooks with specific accounting requirements, the Article 6.8 work programme is deliberately flexible. Non-market approaches are diverse by nature, and a rigid rule structure would undermine their usefulness.

## The Katowice Digital Platform

A key infrastructure element for Article 6.8 is the **Glasgow -- Sharm el-Sheikh work programme digital platform**, often informally called the **Katowice Platform** after earlier UNFCCC discussions that first proposed a similar system. This is an online platform maintained by the UNFCCC Secretariat that:

- Allows countries with climate action needs to post their requirements for non-market support (capacity building needs, technology requirements, policy assistance requests)
- Allows potential providers -- developed country governments, development banks, international organisations, private sector actors -- to browse needs and identify opportunities for partnership
- Facilitates matching between those who need cooperation and those who can provide it
- Maintains a record of non-market cooperative activities undertaken, enabling tracking of progress

The platform is essentially a matchmaking system for climate cooperation. A small island nation might post a need for technical assistance in designing a community-based coastal adaptation programme. A Scandinavian development agency with relevant expertise sees the posting and initiates a cooperation agreement. No credits are generated; the cooperation flows through the platform.

<ExampleBox>
**Article 6.8 in action -- Pacific Islands:** Samoa and Tuvalu have extremely limited technical capacity for participation in carbon markets. Both countries face existential threats from sea-level rise. Through the Article 6.8 platform, they identify a capacity-building offer from Japan's international cooperation agency (JICA) covering climate vulnerability assessment and coastal resilience planning.

JICA sends technical experts to work with both governments. They help build national vulnerability maps, train local officials in adaptation planning, and support the integration of adaptation measures into each country's NDC. No credits are generated. No corresponding adjustments are required. The cooperation delivers adaptation benefits to two of the world's most climate-vulnerable nations without the transaction costs and infrastructure requirements of carbon market participation.
</ExampleBox>

## Who Uses Article 6.8

The primary users of Article 6.8 are countries that face structural barriers to carbon market participation:

**LDCs and SIDS** are the most frequent beneficiaries. They typically lack the MRV systems, regulatory frameworks, and institutional capacity that carbon market participation requires. Non-market cooperation allows them to receive climate support immediately, while building toward eventual carbon market participation over time.

**Countries with complex political situations** may prefer non-market cooperation because it does not require the authorisation of credit transfers, the establishment of national registries, or the disclosure of detailed NDC accounting information.

**Countries with NDC targets that leave little room for credit exports** may still benefit from technology transfer and policy support that helps them meet their own NDC ambitions more effectively, without needing to sell credits.

**Donors and providers** -- developed countries, international organisations, and development banks -- use Article 6.8 to provide climate finance and technical support in situations where the credit market infrastructure is not in place or where the cooperation is explicitly non-commercial in character.

## Article 6.8 and Its Relationship to 6.2 and 6.4

Article 6.8 does not compete with Articles 6.2 and 6.4 -- it complements them. The three tracks serve different purposes, operate at different levels of complexity, and are suited to different types of countries and cooperation:

- **Article 6.2** is for countries with functioning carbon markets and the technical capacity for bilateral credit transfers and corresponding adjustments
- **Article 6.4** is for countries and project developers that want access to a UN-supervised, internationally recognised credit market
- **Article 6.8** is for cooperation that does not involve credit generation -- either because the recipient country cannot yet participate in credit markets, or because the nature of the cooperation is inherently non-market

In practice, Article 6.8 is often the first step. A developing country receives capacity-building support through Article 6.8 -- building its MRV system, training its registry staff, strengthening its institutional capacity. Once that foundation is in place, the same country may begin participating in Article 6.4 project registration or entering Article 6.2 bilateral agreements. Article 6.8 is the on-ramp to the broader Article 6 ecosystem for the countries that need it most.

<HighlightBox>
Article 6.8 is not a lesser form of climate cooperation -- it is a necessary one. For many of the world's most climate-vulnerable countries, non-market approaches are the realistic path to climate support, adaptation finance, and the capacity building needed to eventually participate in carbon markets. The Article 6 framework is only complete when all three tracks are functioning.
</HighlightBox>
