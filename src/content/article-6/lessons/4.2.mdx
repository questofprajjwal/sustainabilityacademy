The 29th Conference of the Parties to the UNFCCC (COP29) took place in Baku, Azerbaijan from 11 to 22 November 2024. It was the most consequential COP for Article 6 since Glasgow, and it arrived after years of frustrated expectations. For Article 6.4 in particular, Baku delivered what Dubai had failed to deliver twelve months earlier: the operational standards needed to begin the mechanism's practical work.

## The Backdrop: Why COP29 Mattered

To understand what COP29 achieved, it helps to understand the impasse it resolved.

At COP26 in Glasgow (2021), the Paris Rulebook for Article 6 was finally agreed -- six years after the Paris Agreement itself. The decisions adopted at Glasgow (Decisions 2/CMA.3 for 6.2 and 3/CMA.3 for 6.4) established the governance framework, the Supervisory Body, the accounting rules, and the mandate for the mechanism to begin operations.

But the Glasgow decisions delegated the technical operational details to the Supervisory Body -- the standards, the methodology rules, the activity cycle procedures. The Supervisory Body spent 2022 and 2023 developing these standards and presented them at COP28 in Dubai for adoption by the CMA.

At COP28, Parties **rejected** both the carbon dioxide removal (CDR) methodology standard and the activity cycle standard. The standards were sent back with instructions to revise them. The concerns raised included:

- Inadequate treatment of permanence risk for carbon removals
- Ambiguity about whether the Supervisory Body had stayed within its mandate or had made decisions that required CMA-level political approval
- Questions about the transparency of the methodology development process
- Concerns from civil society and some Parties about the rigour of additionality requirements in the proposed standards

The rejection at Dubai meant that the A6.4 mechanism could not begin registering projects. It had a governance body and accounting rules but no operational procedures. The mechanism was legally established but functionally dormant.

## What COP29 Adopted

After twelve months of intensive revision, consultation, and negotiation, **both standards were adopted by the CMA at COP29 in Baku**.

### The Carbon Dioxide Removal (CDR) Methodology Standard

The CDR standard establishes how carbon removal activities are defined and accounted under Article 6.4. Key elements of the adopted standard include:

**Definitions and scope:** The standard distinguishes clearly between emission reductions (activities that prevent CO2 from entering the atmosphere), carbon dioxide removals (activities that extract CO2 already in the atmosphere), and avoided emissions (activities that prevent the release of stored carbon). Each is treated differently in the accounting framework.

**Permanence requirements:** The standard sets minimum permanence periods for different removal categories and requires buffer pool contributions to insure against reversal risk. Biological removals (forests, soils) face more stringent permanence rules than geological removals (direct air capture with geological storage), reflecting their higher reversal risk.

**Baseline methodology for removals:** Setting a baseline for removal activities is conceptually different from setting a baseline for emission reductions. The CDR standard provides specific guidance on how to establish the "without-project" removal scenario -- the amount of carbon sequestration that would have occurred anyway -- and how to calculate additional removal above that baseline.

**Reversal liability:** If removed carbon is subsequently released (e.g., a credited forest burns), the standard specifies how the reversal must be accounted for and what remediation is required.

### The Activity Cycle Standard

The activity cycle standard is the operational manual for all A6.4 activities. It specifies:

**Activity Design Document (ADD) requirements:** What information a project developer must include in their ADD, including project description, boundary, baseline scenario, additionality demonstration, monitoring plan, and stakeholder consultation documentation.

**Validation requirements:** How an Approved Designated Operating Entity (ADOE) must conduct a validation audit of the ADD before project registration, what documents must be reviewed, and what site visits are required.

**Registration procedures:** How the Supervisory Body processes registration requests, what review steps apply, and what timelines are expected.

**Monitoring and verification requirements:** How the monitoring plan must be implemented, what data records must be kept, and how verification audits must be conducted for each crediting period.

**Credit issuance procedures:** How the Supervisory Body authorises credit issuance, how OMGE deductions and SOP levies are applied, and how credits are recorded in the central registry.

**Public disclosure requirements:** What information must be publicly available for each registered activity, including the ADD, validation reports, and monitoring reports.

<HighlightBox>
The two COP29 standards together give the A6.4 mechanism everything it needs to begin registering projects and issuing credits. The CDR standard answers "what types of activities qualify and how are they measured?" The activity cycle standard answers "what is the step-by-step process for registering and crediting an activity?" Both were necessary before any project could move forward.
</HighlightBox>

## First-Transfer Rules and Irrevocability

COP29 also clarified important issues about the timing and irrevocability of credit transfers under both Article 6.2 and Article 6.4.

**First-transfer timing** refers to when a corresponding adjustment must be applied. The question had been ambiguous: does the CA apply when the credit is first issued, when it is first transferred between parties, or when it is finally used for NDC purposes? COP29 confirmed that the CA applies **at the point of first international transfer** -- when the credit moves from the registry of the authorising country to an international registry or another country's registry.

**Irrevocability** means that once a credit has been authorised and transferred, the authorisation cannot be withdrawn. This is critical for buyer confidence. If a host country could retroactively revoke the authorisation of credits it had already sold, buyers would face the risk that their purchased credits might lose their corresponding adjustment -- and therefore their value for NDC purposes -- after the fact. The irrevocability principle, confirmed at COP29, prevents this.

These clarifications were particularly important for the Article 6.2 bilateral market, where first-transfer and irrevocability rules govern all ITMO transactions. But they were confirmed consistent across both 6.2 and 6.4, providing a unified principle across the Article 6 framework.

## What Remained Unresolved After Baku

Despite the significant progress at COP29, several issues remained unresolved or under continuing negotiation as of early 2026:

**CORSIA eligibility criteria:** The International Civil Aviation Organization (ICAO) must formally approve Article 6.4 credits for use in CORSIA (the aviation offsetting scheme). The criteria for CORSIA eligibility are being developed separately from the CMA decisions, and the pace of that process has been slower than market participants would like.

**Registry interoperability:** The technical architecture for connecting the central A6.4 registry with national registries under Article 6.2 and with the ITL remains under development. Full interoperability -- the ability to track credits seamlessly across all registry systems -- is essential for confidence in the double-counting protections.

**Article 6.2 transparency rules:** Several Parties continued to contest the granularity of reporting required under Article 6.2, particularly regarding bilateral agreements that host countries consider commercially sensitive. The tension between transparency (needed for environmental integrity verification) and confidentiality (sought by some Parties) remained unresolved.

**Treatment of pre-2020 CDM activities:** A significant number of CDM project developers sought to transition their activities to the A6.4 mechanism. The rules for which CDM activities could transition, and under what conditions, were still being finalised.

## The Status as of Early 2026

By early 2026, the Article 6.4 mechanism was in active operationalisation. The Supervisory Body was:

- Reviewing methodology proposals submitted by project developers and standards bodies
- Processing early-stage activity registration requests
- Finalising the IT architecture for the central registry
- Completing the accreditation of Approved Designated Operating Entities

The first project registrations under A6.4 were expected in 2025, with the first credit issuances expected to follow in 2025-2026 once registered projects completed their first crediting periods and verification audits.

Under Article 6.2, bilateral agreements between countries were already operational. Singapore had signed bilateral Article 6.2 agreements with multiple countries (including Papua New Guinea, Ghana, and others). Switzerland had transacted under its bilateral agreements. The pipeline of bilateral ITMO transactions was growing, though volumes remained modest by global carbon market standards.

## The Demand Side: Why Scale Matters

One significant demand driver for Article 6 credits is the growth of corporate climate commitments. As of early 2026, more than **10,000 companies** had validated targets through the Science Based Targets initiative (SBTi). Many of these companies are committed to achieving net-zero emissions by 2050 or earlier, and their pathway to net zero will require high-quality carbon credits for residual emissions that cannot be eliminated.

SBTi and other credible corporate net-zero frameworks are increasingly specifying that the credits used for residual emissions must come from **high-integrity sources** -- a category that Article 6 credits, with their CA backing and Supervisory Body oversight, are well positioned to fill. This corporate demand creates a structural pull for Article 6 credit supply, supplementing the sovereign demand from countries seeking cost-effective NDC achievement.

<ExampleBox>
**Corporate demand in practice:** A major technology company has committed to net-zero by 2040. By 2039, it has eliminated 90% of its scope 1, 2, and 3 emissions through efficiency improvements, renewable energy procurement, and supply chain transformation. The remaining 10% -- primarily from some manufacturing processes and long-haul logistics -- cannot be eliminated with current technology. To achieve net zero, the company needs high-quality credits for these residual emissions. It turns to the Article 6.4 market, purchasing authorised A6.4ERs that carry a corresponding adjustment and Supervisory Body verification. These credits are credible under the emerging corporate net-zero standards that require CA-backed credits for residual emission claims.
</ExampleBox>

## The Road Ahead

The trajectory for Article 6 after COP29 is one of gradual scaling. The framework is in place; the question is execution. Several milestones mark the path:

**2025-2026:** First A6.4 project registrations and initial credit issuances. Growth of bilateral Article 6.2 ITMO transactions. Development of registry infrastructure.

**2026-2028:** Scaling of A6.4 project pipeline as more methodologies are approved and project developers gain confidence in the process. Expansion of ITMO markets as more bilateral agreements are signed and first transactions completed.

**2028:** Scheduled review of avoidance-only activities under A6.4. This review could significantly expand the scope of eligible projects -- particularly REDD+ and other avoided deforestation activities -- if adequate methodological standards can be agreed.

**2030 and beyond:** As NDC ambition increases with successive five-year cycles, demand for carbon credits as cost-effective mitigation tools should grow. Article 6 is intended to be a permanent feature of the global climate architecture, scaling with the ambition of the Paris Agreement itself.

The Baku breakthrough was necessary but not sufficient. The mechanism's ultimate success will be measured not by the standards adopted at COP but by the quality of projects registered, the rigour of verification conducted, the pace of credit issuances, and the confidence of buyers that Article 6 credits genuinely represent what they claim.
