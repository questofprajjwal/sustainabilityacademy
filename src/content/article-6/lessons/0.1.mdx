The rules governing international carbon markets did not emerge in a vacuum. They are the product of nearly three decades of negotiation, experimentation, and hard-won lessons about what happens when countries try to cooperate on emissions reductions across borders. Article 6 of the Paris Agreement represents the most sophisticated attempt yet to build a framework that is both flexible enough for global adoption and rigorous enough to prevent the accounting failures that undermined its predecessors.

## The Paris Agreement: Foundations and Goals

The **Paris Agreement** was adopted at COP21 in Paris in December 2015 and entered into force the following year. It replaced the Kyoto Protocol as the central instrument of international climate policy and introduced a fundamentally different architecture for how countries coordinate on emissions reductions.

Where Kyoto imposed binding reduction targets on developed countries only, Paris operates on a **universal, bottom-up** model. Every country -- developed, developing, and emerging alike -- submits its own **Nationally Determined Contribution (NDC)**: a national climate plan setting out what each country intends to do to cut emissions and build resilience. These NDCs are not assigned by an external body; each country determines them for itself.

The Agreement's temperature goals are explicit:

- Limit global average temperature rise to **well below 2 degrees Celsius** above pre-industrial levels
- Pursue efforts to limit warming to **1.5 degrees Celsius**
- Achieve net-zero CO2 emissions globally by around **2050**

The 1.5C target is not merely aspirational language. The IPCC's 2018 Special Report on 1.5C found that the difference between 1.5C and 2C of warming carries significant consequences -- for sea-level rise, extreme weather events, ecosystem loss, and human displacement. That science gave the 1.5C target renewed political weight and has since shaped the ambition of many national climate plans.

<HighlightBox>
The Paris Agreement operates on a universal, bottom-up architecture. Unlike Kyoto, it does not assign targets to countries. Instead, every country sets its own NDC -- its climate plan -- and submits it to the UNFCCC. The system's strength lies in its reach: nearly every country on Earth has ratified it.
</HighlightBox>

## NDCs: The Engine of Paris

**Nationally Determined Contributions** are each country's formal commitment under the Paris Agreement. They are submitted to the UNFCCC, updated every five years, and are expected to become progressively more ambitious over time -- this is the so-called **ratchet mechanism**, enshrined in Article 4.

An NDC typically includes:

- An emissions reduction target (e.g. "reduce GHG emissions 45% by 2030 relative to 2005 levels")
- Sector-specific policies and measures
- Adaptation commitments
- Finance, technology, and capacity-building needs

The critical phrase is "progressively more ambitious." The Paris Agreement does not merely ask countries to set targets once; it requires each successive NDC to represent a step forward from the previous one. This is intended to create a virtuous cycle of escalating ambition that eventually closes the gap between current policies and the temperature goals.

As of 2025, however, the aggregate of all submitted NDCs still falls far short of the emissions pathway needed for 1.5C. This is precisely why cost-effective mechanisms for achieving NDC targets -- including carbon markets -- carry such importance. Countries that can meet their NDCs at lower cost have more political headroom to commit to greater ambition in future rounds.

## A Brief History: From Kyoto to Paris

To understand why Article 6 was designed the way it was, it helps to understand what came before.

The **Kyoto Protocol** (1997) established the first binding international carbon markets. Two mechanisms are directly relevant:

**The Clean Development Mechanism (CDM)** allowed developed countries (listed in Annex I of the UNFCCC) to fund emission-reduction projects in developing countries and receive **Certified Emission Reductions (CERs)** that could be used against their Kyoto targets. At its peak, the CDM had over 7,000 registered projects in more than 100 countries. It pioneered project-based carbon crediting at scale and developed many of the methodological foundations still in use today.

**Joint Implementation (JI)** was a similar mechanism, but operated between two developed (Annex I) countries. It produced **Emission Reduction Units (ERUs)**.

<ExampleBox>
**CDM in practice:** A Chinese wind farm developer registers a CDM project. An independent auditor verifies that the project reduces emissions beyond what would have occurred without CDM funding. CERs are issued and transferred to a European government or company, which uses them to offset a portion of its Kyoto compliance obligation. The Chinese government retains the rest of the emissions reduction for its national inventory.
</ExampleBox>

The CDM was a genuine innovation. It channelled significant finance to low-carbon projects in developing countries, built institutional capacity, and demonstrated that international carbon trading could work. But it also accumulated well-documented problems:

- **Additionality failures**: Many credits were issued for projects that would have happened anyway -- particularly large hydro and industrial gas projects -- meaning no real emissions reduction occurred.
- **Perverse incentives**: The HFC-23 scandal is the most egregious example. Factories received enormous CDM payments for destroying a potent refrigerant byproduct (HFC-23), but the payment structure created incentives to produce *more* of the parent chemical (HCFC-22) in order to destroy more byproduct. Credits were generated that represented no net climate benefit.
- **Geographic concentration**: The majority of CDM projects flowed to a handful of large emerging economies (China, India, Brazil, Mexico), leaving most of the developing world -- particularly Africa -- with minimal participation.

<AnalogyBox>
Think of the CDM like a grant scheme with inadequate oversight. The intention was sound: pay for verified emissions reductions anywhere in the world. But when the verification standards were too weak and the financial rewards too large relative to the underlying activity, the system attracted gaming. This is the core challenge that Article 6 was designed to address: how do you build a carbon market that is both liquid and trustworthy?
</AnalogyBox>

## The Double Counting Problem

Of all the flaws in the Kyoto-era carbon markets, **double counting** is the one that Article 6 was most directly designed to solve.

Double counting arises when the same tonne of CO2 reduction is counted more than once -- most commonly, when both the country that produced the reduction and the country that purchased the credit each claim it toward their own emissions target. Under the Kyoto framework, this was structurally possible because developing countries did not have binding national targets. A Brazilian wind farm could generate CERs that a European country counted against its Kyoto obligation, while Brazil simultaneously counted the same emission reduction in its national inventory. The carbon was, in effect, subtracted twice from global accounts -- but it only physically stopped existing once.

In a Paris Agreement world, where virtually every country has an NDC, this problem becomes far more acute. If Country A sells carbon credits to Country B, and both countries count those reductions in their NDC progress, global accounting is corrupted and the temperature goals are undermined.

<HighlightBox>
The double counting problem is not a technicality -- it goes to the heart of whether carbon markets deliver real climate outcomes. If the same tonne of CO2 is counted as a reduction twice, then the global accounting shows progress that does not exist in the atmosphere. Article 6 addresses this through a mechanism called the **corresponding adjustment**.
</HighlightBox>

## Why Article 6 Was Needed

The Paris Agreement's bottom-up architecture created a new problem that Kyoto never had to fully confront: in a world where every country has its own emissions target, what rules govern the transfer of emission reductions across borders?

Without clear rules, several things can go wrong:

1. **Double counting** -- the problem described above
2. **Accounting inconsistency** -- different countries using different baselines, methodologies, and accounting periods, making it impossible to compare or aggregate progress
3. **Lack of environmental integrity** -- credits issued without robust additionality, permanence, or verification standards undermine the market's climate credibility
4. **Market fragmentation** -- without common rules, multiple incompatible bilateral markets emerge, reducing liquidity and driving up transaction costs

Article 6 was negotiated as Part IV of the Paris Agreement's rulebook to address precisely these issues. It creates a framework -- not a single mechanism, but a set of interconnected rules -- within which countries and the private sector can trade emission reductions in a manner that is consistent with achieving the Agreement's temperature goals.

The framework has three distinct tracks, each suited to different types of cooperation, different levels of governance involvement, and different trade-offs between flexibility and oversight. These are covered in detail in the next lesson.

<HighlightBox>
Article 6 does not replace or override the voluntary carbon market. It creates a parallel international framework for sovereign-level carbon trading and establishes rules -- particularly the corresponding adjustment -- that affect how voluntary market credits interact with national NDC accounting.
</HighlightBox>

## The Cost-Effectiveness Case for Carbon Markets

Why allow carbon markets at all, rather than simply requiring every country to reduce its own emissions domestically?

The answer is cost-effectiveness. The marginal cost of reducing one additional tonne of CO2 varies enormously across countries, sectors, and technologies. Reducing a tonne of emissions through solar energy deployment in a high-irradiance emerging economy may cost a fraction of what it costs to achieve the same reduction through industrial fuel switching in a cold-climate developed economy. Carbon markets allow reductions to be made where they are cheapest, and the financial benefit to be shared between buyer and seller.

<FormulaBox>
Global least-cost abatement:

Tonne abated cheapest (Host Country) + Finance transferred (Buyer Country)
= Same net atmospheric outcome at lower total cost

Without markets: each country must meet its NDC entirely through domestic action.
With Article 6 markets: countries can trade, directing finance to lowest-cost reductions globally.
</FormulaBox>

Economic models consistently estimate that Article 6 cooperation could reduce the cost of meeting the Paris Agreement's collective goals by **hundreds of billions of dollars per year**. The UNFCCC itself has published estimates suggesting that full implementation of Article 6 could roughly halve the cost of NDC implementation. This cost saving could either be returned to taxpayers, reinvested in more ambitious climate action, or used to fund adaptation in vulnerable countries -- all without increasing the total emissions reduction achieved.

The architecture of Article 6 -- its three tracks, its accounting rules, and its governance mechanisms -- is the subject of the lessons that follow.
