The credits issued under the Article 6.4 mechanism -- formally called **A6.4 Emission Reductions (A6.4ERs)** -- are distinct from any carbon credit that existed before. Understanding what they are, how they are produced, and what the COP29 breakthrough at Baku in November 2024 means for the mechanism's operationalisation requires examining both their technical architecture and the political negotiations that shaped it.

## The Two Types of A6.4ERs in Detail

As established in the previous lesson, the A6.4 mechanism produces two categories of credits. The distinction between them is not administrative -- it has fundamental implications for how they can be used, how they are priced, and what accounting obligations they trigger.

### Authorised A6.4ERs

An authorised A6.4ER is a credit that the **host country government has formally authorised** for transfer to another Party for use toward that Party's NDC achievement (or another international mitigation purpose). The authorisation must be explicit -- it is recorded in the host country's Letter of Authorisation submitted to the Supervisory Body and reflected in the central registry.

When an authorised A6.4ER is transferred internationally, the host country must apply a **corresponding adjustment (CA)**:

- The host country's NDC accounting adds back the transferred emission reduction (i.e., it increases reported emissions by the equivalent amount, or reduces claimed reductions)
- The acquiring country or entity subtracts the same amount from its own NDC or reporting

This is the mechanism that prevents double counting. The emission reduction can only be credited once, either to the host country or to the acquiring party -- never both.

Authorised A6.4ERs may also be used for **CORSIA** (the Carbon Offsetting and Reduction Scheme for International Aviation), provided they meet CORSIA eligibility criteria set by the International Civil Aviation Organization (ICAO).

### Mitigation Contribution A6.4ERs

A mitigation contribution A6.4ER is a credit that has been issued by the Supervisory Body but **not authorised** by the host country for international NDC transfer. These credits:

- Do not trigger a corresponding adjustment
- Cannot be used by an acquiring party to claim NDC achievement under the Paris Agreement
- Can be used for results-based climate finance (i.e., payments to project developers based on verified reductions, without an international claim on the reductions themselves)
- Can be used in domestic carbon pricing mechanisms in the host country
- Cannot be used for CORSIA unless separately authorised

The distinction matters for buyers. A government purchasing authorised A6.4ERs can count them toward its NDC. A development bank or philanthropic fund disbursing results-based payments for reductions that stay within the host country's own NDC accounting uses mitigation contribution credits. The host country retains the climate benefit; the funder receives the verified proof of outcome.

<HighlightBox>
Authorised A6.4ERs = corresponding adjustment required + usable for NDC achievement internationally. Mitigation contribution A6.4ERs = no CA, no international NDC use, host country retains the climate benefit. Both are issued by the Supervisory Body with the same verification rigour -- the difference is purely in what the host country has agreed to allow.
</HighlightBox>

## How Methodologies Are Developed and Approved

A **methodology** is the technical rulebook for a specific type of emission-reduction project. It specifies:

- The eligible project types and boundaries
- How to establish the baseline scenario (what emissions would have occurred without the project)
- How to demonstrate additionality (that the project would not have occurred without carbon finance)
- How to monitor, measure, and calculate actual emission reductions
- What data, records, and monitoring equipment are required
- How to apply uncertainty discounts and conservative estimates

Under the CDM, methodologies were developed by project developers and approved by the CDM Executive Board. The A6.4 mechanism follows a broadly similar process, but with higher standards and greater Supervisory Body involvement.

**The approval process works as follows:**

1. A project developer (or a methodology developer -- this can be a government, a standards body, or a technical consultancy) prepares a methodology proposal following the Supervisory Body's **methodology standards**.
2. The proposal is submitted to the Supervisory Body's methodology expert panel for technical review.
3. The panel publishes its assessment. There is a public comment period during which Parties, experts, and civil society can submit observations.
4. The Supervisory Body considers the panel's recommendation and public comments, then approves, conditionally approves, or rejects the methodology.
5. Approved methodologies are published in the methodology registry and made available for use by project developers.

Approved methodologies may be:

- **Entirely new A6.4 methodologies** developed specifically for the mechanism
- **Transition methodologies** -- existing CDM methodologies that have been reviewed and updated to meet A6.4 standards. The CDM legacy library is large; rather than starting from scratch, the Supervisory Body has established a transition pathway for methodologies that remain scientifically sound.

<ExampleBox>
**Methodology in practice:** A developer in Kenya wants to register a cookstove project that distributes improved biomass cookstoves to households. The project reduces fuel wood consumption and therefore reduces CO2 emissions from combustion. The developer identifies an applicable A6.4 methodology for clean cooking projects. The methodology specifies how to survey participating households, measure fuel consumption before and after, establish the baseline (what fuels households would otherwise use), and apply appropriate emission factors. The developer follows the methodology exactly, and their monitoring reports use its formulas to calculate tonnes of CO2 avoided.
</ExampleBox>

## A6.4ERs vs Kyoto CERs

The differences between A6.4ERs and the CDM's Certified Emission Reductions (CERs) are more than cosmetic:

| Dimension | CERs (CDM) | A6.4ERs |
|---|---|---|
| Governing body | CDM Executive Board | Article 6.4 Supervisory Body |
| Accounting framework | Kyoto Protocol targets (Annex I only) | Paris Agreement NDCs (all Parties) |
| Corresponding adjustment | Not required | Required for authorised credits |
| OMGE cancellation | None | 2% of issuance cancelled |
| SOP to Adaptation Fund | Yes (2% volume + 0.1% administrative) | Yes (5% volume + 3% issuance fees) |
| Credit type distinction | Single type (CER) | Two types (authorised vs mitigation contribution) |
| Avoidance vs removal | Both eligible | Removals and reductions eligible; avoidance-only reassessed 2028 |

The CER was a simpler instrument for a simpler world. In the Kyoto framework, only developed countries had binding targets, so double counting was not a systemic risk. In the Paris framework, where every country has an NDC, the corresponding adjustment becomes indispensable -- and the A6.4ER's two-track architecture reflects that complexity.

## The COP29 Breakthrough: What Changed at Baku

The COP29 conference in Baku, Azerbaijan (November 2024) was a pivotal moment for the A6.4 mechanism. To understand why, it helps to know what had gone wrong in Dubai.

At COP28 in Dubai (December 2023), the Supervisory Body presented two critical standards for CMA adoption:

1. A **carbon dioxide removal (CDR) methodology standard** -- the technical rules for how removal activities (such as reforestation, soil carbon, direct air capture, and enhanced weathering) can be quantified and credited under A6.4
2. An **activity cycle standard** -- the procedural rules governing the entire project lifecycle, from design document submission through validation, registration, monitoring, verification, and credit issuance

Both standards were **sent back** by the CMA. Parties raised concerns about the adequacy of the CDR standard -- particularly around permanence of removals, the risk of reversal, and how removals should be treated differently from emission reductions. There were also procedural concerns about whether the Supervisory Body had stayed within its mandate.

The result was an additional year of technical work and negotiation. The Supervisory Body revised both standards, engaging extensively with Parties, technical experts, and civil society.

At **COP29 in Baku (November 2024)**, the CMA adopted both standards. This was the major Article 6.4 breakthrough:

**The CDR methodology standard** establishes how carbon dioxide removal activities are defined, quantified, and accounted under A6.4. It addresses:

- The distinction between removal and avoidance (removal actively extracts CO2 from the atmosphere; avoidance prevents emissions from occurring)
- Permanence requirements -- how long removed carbon must be stored, and what buffer pools or insurance mechanisms must be maintained against reversal risk
- Baseline setting for removals, which is conceptually different from baseline setting for emission reductions
- Specific requirements for different removal categories (biotic removals such as forests and soils; abiotic removals such as direct air capture and mineralisation)

**The activity cycle standard** establishes the step-by-step procedural requirements for all A6.4 activities. It is essentially the mechanism's operational manual, covering:

- Requirements for Activity Design Documents
- Validation and verification procedures and standards for Approved Designated Operating Entities
- Monitoring plan requirements
- Credit issuance procedures and timelines
- Requirements for public disclosure and stakeholder consultation

Together, these two standards give the mechanism the technical and procedural foundation it needs to begin registering projects and issuing credits. Without them, the mechanism had a legal mandate but no operational rules.

<AnalogyBox>
Think of the CDR standard and activity cycle standard as the building code and planning permission process for a new city. The Paris Agreement gave the mandate to build the city. The Glasgow Rulebook laid out the broad zoning plan. But until COP29 adopted the building code and planning process, no construction could legally begin. The Baku breakthrough was the moment the first builders could finally break ground.
</AnalogyBox>

## The 2028 Avoidance Credit Review

One important limitation of the current A6.4 framework is the **treatment of avoidance-only credits**. Avoidance refers to activities that prevent emissions from occurring -- avoided deforestation, avoided methane from landfills, avoided emissions from fossil fuel combustion -- as distinct from activities that reduce emissions relative to a baseline through a technology switch or efficiency improvement.

The Supervisory Body determined that the existing methodology standards are sufficient for:

- **Emission reductions** (e.g., switching from coal to solar, improving industrial efficiency)
- **Carbon dioxide removals** (e.g., reforestation, soil carbon sequestration, direct air capture)

But for **avoidance-only** activities -- particularly avoided deforestation (REDD+) -- the methodological and permanence challenges are sufficiently distinct that the CMA mandated a **dedicated review by 2028**. Until that review concludes and the CMA adopts appropriate standards, avoidance-only activities cannot be registered under A6.4.

This is a significant constraint. Avoided deforestation represents one of the largest potential sources of low-cost carbon credits globally. Its exclusion from A6.4's initial scope reflects the lessons of the voluntary market, where many REDD+ credits have been criticised for poor additionality, reversal risk, and weak permanence. The 2028 review will determine whether these methodological challenges can be adequately addressed within the A6.4 framework.

<DeepDive title="Why avoidance credits are methodologically harder">
Emission reduction and removal projects both involve measuring a physical change that can be observed: the amount of fuel no longer burned, the amount of carbon newly stored in biomass or soil. Avoidance projects require measuring something that did not happen -- the deforestation that would have occurred without the project. This requires constructing a credible counterfactual baseline, which is inherently uncertain and susceptible to manipulation. How do you prove that a forest would have been cut down if you had not intervened? The answer requires historical deforestation rates, economic modelling of land use pressures, and spatial analysis of where deforestation is likely -- all of which involve significant assumptions. The A6.4 mechanism's caution about avoidance-only credits reflects hard-won experience from the voluntary market, where overcrediting of REDD+ projects has been extensively documented.
</DeepDive>

## After COP29: What Comes Next

With the two critical standards adopted at Baku, the A6.4 mechanism entered a new phase. The Supervisory Body began operationalising project registration in 2025, with the first project registrations and credit issuances expected to follow in 2025 and 2026.

The mechanism still faces practical challenges:

- Building the IT infrastructure for the central registry
- Accrediting a sufficient number of Approved Designated Operating Entities to handle project validation and verification
- Transitioning CDM methodologies to A6.4 standards to give developers a broader library to draw from
- Attracting project developers who have grown accustomed to the faster, less regulated voluntary market

But the COP29 breakthrough removed the most fundamental obstacle: the absence of operational standards. The mechanism now has the legal mandate, the governance body, the credit accounting rules, and the methodology and procedural standards it needs to function. The question shifts from "can it work?" to "how quickly will it scale?"
