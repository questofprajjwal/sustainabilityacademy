Article 6.2 of the Paris Agreement creates the legal basis for countries to work together to achieve their climate targets through voluntary bilateral agreements. At the heart of these agreements is a new type of unit: the **Internationally Transferred Mitigation Outcome**, or ITMO. Understanding what ITMOs are, who can create them, and how they can be used is the foundation for everything else in Article 6.

## What Is an ITMO?

An ITMO is a unit representing a verified reduction or removal of greenhouse gas emissions that one country transfers to another for use in meeting climate targets. The term was coined in the Paris Agreement itself, which states in Article 6.2 that parties may "use internationally transferred mitigation outcomes towards nationally determined contributions."

Several characteristics define ITMOs:

- They represent a **mitigation outcome**: a measurable reduction in emissions or an enhancement of removals compared to what would have happened otherwise.
- They are **transferred internationally**: the country where the reduction occurs (the host country) transfers it to another country (the acquiring country).
- They are measured primarily in **metric tonnes of CO2-equivalent (tCO2e)**, though the Paris Agreement also allows for non-GHG metrics in specific cases, such as kilowatt-hours of renewable energy generated or hectares of forest protected.
- They carry a **vintage**: the year or period in which the underlying emission reduction occurred. Vintage matters because it determines which NDC cycle the reduction falls within.
- They can be attributed to a **sector**: energy, transport, forestry, agriculture, and so on. Sector attribution helps the acquiring country demonstrate that the ITMO supports NDC targets, which are often sector-specific.

<HighlightBox>
ITMOs are not a physical commodity -- they are accounting units. What is actually transferred between countries is the right to count a verified emission reduction or removal toward a national climate target. The underlying project or activity remains in the host country.
</HighlightBox>

## Legal Basis in the Paris Agreement

The Paris Agreement (adopted at COP21 in Paris in December 2015) established the framework for ITMOs in just three paragraphs of Article 6.2. The language is deliberately flexible, allowing countries to design bilateral arrangements without needing a central UN bureaucracy to approve each one.

The Agreement requires only that:

1. Participating countries shall promote **sustainable development** and ensure **environmental integrity**.
2. Countries shall apply **robust accounting** to avoid double counting, through corresponding adjustments (covered in detail in Lesson 1.2).
3. Countries shall report on their use of ITMOs in their periodic **transparency reports** under the Agreement.

This flexibility is a double-edged sword. On the one hand, it allows countries to tailor deals to their specific circumstances. On the other hand, it creates a risk that different countries apply different standards, potentially undermining the environmental credibility of the system. The Glasgow Rulebook (discussed in Lesson 1.3) was the international community's attempt to add rigour without sacrificing flexibility.

## Authorised vs. Non-Authorised ITMOs

Not all ITMOs are equal in terms of what they can be used for. The authorisation status of an ITMO -- whether the host country's government has formally approved its export -- determines its permissible uses.

**Authorised ITMOs** are those for which the host country has issued an explicit government authorisation. This authorisation is the key that unlocks the ITMO's most valuable uses:

- Achievement of the acquiring country's **Nationally Determined Contribution (NDC)**
- Use in the **CORSIA** aviation offsetting scheme under ICAO
- Use in **compliance carbon markets**

When an ITMO is authorised, the host country is also legally required to apply a **corresponding adjustment** (CA) to its own national accounting -- it must add the transferred emission reduction back to its reported emissions total, as if the reduction had not occurred domestically. This prevents the same tonne from being counted twice, once by the host country and once by the acquiring country.

**Non-authorised ITMOs** are emission reductions that have been internationally transferred but without a formal government authorisation. Non-authorised ITMOs:

- Do **not** require corresponding adjustments from the host country.
- **Cannot** be used for NDC achievement by the acquiring country.
- Can only be used in the voluntary carbon market for claims that do not assert national-level accounting credit -- for example, certain results-based climate finance arrangements, or legacy voluntary market transactions where the buyer is not claiming a national accounting benefit.

<AnalogyBox>
Think of authorisation like a customs declaration. An authorised ITMO has been formally declared at the border: both countries acknowledge the transfer, and the host country adjusts its books accordingly. A non-authorised ITMO crosses the border informally -- it still represents a real emission reduction, but neither country has formally claimed or disclaimed it in their national accounts. For many purposes that matters enormously.
</AnalogyBox>

## Who Can Generate and Trade ITMOs?

A critical feature of Article 6.2 is that ITMOs are **government-to-government instruments**. The parties to an ITMO transfer are countries, not companies. This distinguishes the Article 6.2 track from the voluntary carbon market, where private buyers and sellers can transact directly.

In practice, however, the underlying emission reductions are almost always generated by **private sector projects** -- a solar farm, a cookstove programme, a reforestation initiative. What happens is:

1. A project generates verified emission reductions (often using methodologies from existing standards such as Verra's VCS, Gold Standard, or new Article 6.4 methodologies).
2. The host country's government decides to **authorise** those reductions for export as ITMOs, bringing them under the Article 6.2 framework.
3. The host country transfers the authorised ITMOs to the acquiring country through a bilateral agreement.
4. The acquiring country may use them toward its NDC, pass them to its aviation industry for CORSIA compliance, or sell them onward.

Private entities can be involved as project developers, intermediaries, or buyers of the credits that represent the ITMOs, but the formal transfer is always between sovereign governments.

## The Role of Designated National Authorities

Every country participating in Article 6.2 must establish a **Designated National Authority (DNA)** -- a government body with the legal authority to authorise ITMO exports. The DNA is the gatekeeper:

- It reviews proposed ITMO transactions and decides whether to issue an authorisation letter.
- It ensures the underlying emission reductions meet the country's own standards for environmental integrity.
- It maintains records of all authorised transfers and reports them to the UNFCCC.
- It applies corresponding adjustments to the country's national GHG inventory.

The quality and capacity of DNAs varies enormously across countries. In developed nations with established carbon market experience, a DNA can be a sophisticated regulatory agency. In less-developed countries that are still building their environmental governance systems, a DNA might be a small team within a ministry. Building DNA capacity has become a major focus of international technical assistance programmes.

<HighlightBox>
Without a functioning DNA, a country cannot participate in Article 6.2 as a host. Projects may still operate, but their emission reductions cannot be formally authorised as ITMOs. This is why institutional capacity-building is treated as a prerequisite for meaningful Article 6.2 participation by lower-income countries.
</HighlightBox>

## Uses of ITMOs

Authorised ITMOs can be used for three distinct purposes:

**1. NDC Achievement**
The primary purpose envisioned by the Paris Agreement. An acquiring country that falls short of its domestic emission reduction target can use ITMOs to close the gap. From a climate perspective, this is supposed to be equivalent to achieving the reduction domestically -- the corresponding adjustment mechanism ensures the same tonne is not claimed twice.

**2. CORSIA (Carbon Offsetting and Reduction Scheme for International Aviation)**
CORSIA is the ICAO scheme requiring international airlines to offset the growth in their CO2 emissions above 2019 levels. Certain authorised ITMOs can qualify as CORSIA Eligible Emissions Units, allowing airlines to meet their offsetting obligations. This creates a significant additional demand channel for ITMOs, particularly for countries that host high-quality, low-cost projects.

**3. Voluntary Carbon Market**
Non-authorised ITMOs can still be sold in the voluntary carbon market, where companies purchase credits to support their corporate sustainability claims. However, the key limitation is that without authorisation and a corresponding adjustment, the buyer cannot credibly claim that the reduction contributes to any nationally-reported climate target -- it is simply a private transaction. As corporate buyers become more sophisticated, demand for authorised ITMOs (with CA) over non-authorised ones is growing.

## Real-World Examples

**Switzerland and Thailand -- the first bilateral ITMO deal (2024)**

The most prominent early example of an Article 6.2 agreement is the deal between Switzerland and Thailand, which became fully operational in 2024. Switzerland, which has ambitious climate targets but high domestic abatement costs, agreed to purchase ITMOs generated by clean cooking projects in Thailand. The Thai government authorised the ITMOs for export, applied the necessary corresponding adjustments, and transferred the units to Switzerland to help it meet its NDC. This deal demonstrated that the Article 6.2 framework could work in practice, not just in theory.

**Ghana -- first country to authorise ITMO exports (COP27)**

At COP27 in Sharm el-Sheikh in November 2022, Ghana became the first country to formally authorise ITMO exports under the new Glasgow Rulebook framework. Ghana had established its DNA early, built a national carbon registry, and developed a pipeline of eligible projects, particularly in the energy and land-use sectors. Ghana's early mover status positioned it as a model for other African nations considering participation in Article 6.2.

<ExampleBox>
**Example: How a cookstove project becomes an ITMO**

A non-governmental organisation operates a programme distributing efficient cookstoves in rural Thailand. Each stove burns less wood and charcoal than a traditional stove, reducing CO2 and methane emissions.

An independent auditor verifies the emission reductions -- say, 50,000 tCO2e per year across the programme. Thailand's DNA reviews the verification and issues an authorisation letter, formally designating 50,000 ITMOs for export to Switzerland for a given year.

Switzerland's registry records the receipt of 50,000 ITMOs. Thailand's national GHG inventory is adjusted upward by 50,000 tCO2e for that year (the corresponding adjustment). Switzerland's inventory is adjusted downward by 50,000 tCO2e.

Net result: one tonne is counted once. Switzerland counts it toward its NDC. Thailand cannot also count it toward its own NDC.
</ExampleBox>

## Metrics, Vintage, and Sector Attribution

Article 6.2 allows ITMOs to be expressed in different metrics depending on what the underlying project measures. The default metric is **tCO2e**, which makes ITMOs fungible with other international carbon units and compatible with NDC accounting, which is almost universally expressed in GHG terms.

However, the Agreement also permits non-GHG metrics for cases where the mitigation outcome is better measured in other units. A renewable energy programme might generate ITMOs expressed in **kilowatt-hours of clean electricity**, which can then be converted to tCO2e for NDC accounting purposes using agreed emission factors. The flexibility is designed to accommodate a wide range of project types and sectoral circumstances.

**Vintage** refers to the year in which the emission reduction occurred. It matters because:

- NDC cycles run for defined periods (the first NDC period is roughly 2020--2030 for most countries).
- An ITMO generated in 2025 can only be used toward an NDC commitment covering 2025.
- Using old-vintage credits to meet current targets -- a practice known as "temporal mismatch" -- is a recognised risk to environmental integrity.

**Sector attribution** helps both host and acquiring countries manage their sectoral accounting. If an acquiring country has a sector-specific NDC target (say, a 40% reduction in power sector emissions), it needs ITMOs that can be credibly attributed to the power sector to demonstrate progress against that specific target.

<DeepDive title="Can ITMOs be traded more than once?">
The Paris Agreement does not explicitly prohibit secondary trading of ITMOs -- in theory, an acquiring country could transfer ITMOs it has received onward to a third country. However, the Glasgow Rulebook introduced the concept of "first transfer," which is the point at which corresponding adjustments are triggered and the ITMO's accounting status is locked. Once an ITMO has been first transferred and corresponding adjustments applied, revocations by the original host country cannot affect it. This provides security for acquiring countries but also means that the chain of custody must be carefully tracked. The UNFCCC International Registry is designed to provide this chain-of-custody tracking at the international level.
</DeepDive>

ITMOs represent a genuinely new instrument in international climate finance -- one that blends the accounting rigour of national GHG inventories with the market mechanisms of carbon trading. Their value depends entirely on the quality of the authorisation and verification processes behind them, which is why the governance architecture discussed in Lessons 1.2 and 1.3 is so important.
