Two distinct carbon market systems operate in parallel today. One is the Article 6 framework established by the Paris Agreement: a sovereign-level architecture in which governments authorise credit transfers between nations, apply corresponding adjustments to their national accounting, and take on formal UNFCCC obligations. The other is the voluntary carbon market (VCM): a private, decentralised ecosystem in which independent standards bodies certify emission reductions, corporate buyers purchase credits to support sustainability claims, and no national accounting adjustments are required.

Understanding how these two systems relate -- where they overlap, where they diverge, and why the distinction matters -- is essential for any organisation active in climate action.

## The Voluntary Carbon Market: An Overview

The VCM is not a single market. It is a collection of markets organised around competing private standards, each with its own rules, registries, and certification criteria.

The dominant players are:

**Verra (VCS -- Verified Carbon Standard)** is the world's largest voluntary carbon standard, responsible for the majority of all voluntary carbon credits issued globally. Verra's REDD+ (avoided deforestation) credits have been the subject of significant controversy, though Verra has introduced reforms to its methodology standards in recent years.

**Gold Standard** emphasises sustainable development co-benefits alongside emission reductions. It applies stricter additionality requirements than VCS in some areas and focuses heavily on energy access and clean cooking projects in developing countries.

**American Carbon Registry (ACR) and Climate Action Reserve (CAR)** are North American standards used primarily for projects in the United States and Latin America, often feeding into compliance markets such as California's cap-and-trade programme.

Credits in the VCM are issued through a project-level process: a developer registers a project with a standard body, submits a project design document, obtains third-party validation, monitors emissions reductions, obtains third-party verification, and receives credits (Verified Carbon Units, Gold Standard Credits, etc.) into the standard body's registry. Buyers purchase these credits, retire them in the registry, and use the retirement as evidence for their sustainability claims.

## Key Structural Differences

The Article 6 and VCM systems differ across four fundamental dimensions:

**Governance:** Article 6 is intergovernmental -- governed ultimately by the CMA (the 196 Parties to the Paris Agreement) through their UNFCCC negotiation processes. The PACM (A6.4 mechanism) is overseen by the Supervisory Body, a UN committee. Article 6.2 is governed by bilateral agreements between sovereign states. The VCM, by contrast, is governed by private organisations -- Verra, Gold Standard, ACR, and their boards -- with no intergovernmental mandate or international legal basis.

**Purpose:** Article 6 is designed to support NDC achievement -- national climate targets under the Paris Agreement. Credits authorised under Article 6 carry formal accounting value in national GHG inventories. VCM credits are used for corporate sustainability claims -- offsetting scope 1/2/3 emissions, achieving "carbon neutral" product certifications, supporting "net zero" pledges. These are private claims, not sovereign accounting entries.

**Accounting (corresponding adjustment):** For authorised Article 6 credits, the host country applies a corresponding adjustment when credits are transferred internationally. This ensures the reduction is counted exactly once in global NDC accounting. VCM credits carry no such requirement. A company purchasing a Verra VCS credit from a project in Brazil does not trigger any adjustment to Brazil's national GHG inventory. Brazil may still count that same emission reduction toward its own NDC.

**Access:** Article 6.2 is technically government-to-government -- only sovereign states are formal parties to ITMO transfers. Private sector participation is indirect, through projects that governments authorise. Article 6.4 allows private project developers to participate directly, but still requires host-country authorisation for the highest-value credit type. The VCM is entirely open to private actors -- project developers, brokers, buyers -- without sovereign intermediation.

<HighlightBox>
The single most consequential structural difference between Article 6 and the VCM is the corresponding adjustment. When a credit is authorised under Article 6, the host country cannot also count that reduction toward its own NDC. When a VCM credit is purchased, the host country retains the right to count the same reduction in its national inventory. Without a CA, the same tonne can be claimed by both the corporate buyer (in its sustainability report) and the host government (in its NDC progress report).
</HighlightBox>

## The Interaction and Overlap

Despite their structural differences, the two systems interact extensively. Many projects operate in both simultaneously.

A reforestation project in Kenya might be registered under Verra VCS and generating voluntary carbon units sold to European corporations. If Kenya's government decides to authorise some of those reductions as ITMOs under a bilateral Article 6.2 agreement, the Verra registry must deregister those units -- or flag them as CA-affected -- to prevent the same reduction being sold twice. This deregistration requirement is technically and operationally complex, and the procedures for managing it are still being standardised.

Similarly, Article 6.4 methodologies may be based on, or derived from, existing Verra or Gold Standard methodologies. Projects transitioning from the VCM to A6.4 bring existing methodology frameworks with them.

The **corresponding adjustment is the dividing line** between these two systems. Credits without a CA are VCM credits, regardless of whether the underlying project also has an Article 6 authorisation. Credits with a CA are Article 6 credits, with all the accounting obligations and rights that implies. The same physical emission reduction -- the same avoided tonne of CO2 from the same forest -- can be a VCM credit or an Article 6 credit depending entirely on whether the host government has applied a CA.

## The "Article 6 Premium"

Authorised credits with corresponding adjustments trade at a meaningful premium over comparable VCM credits. This premium reflects the additional value that the CA provides:

- **Accounting exclusivity:** Buyers can claim the reduction without risk that the host country has also claimed it. This eliminates a key reputational and legal risk for corporate buyers.
- **NDC transferability:** Acquiring countries can use the credit toward their own national climate targets, a use that VCM credits cannot support.
- **CORSIA eligibility:** CORSIA (the aviation offsetting scheme) has strict eligibility criteria, and CA-backed Article 6 credits are the primary vehicle expected to qualify.
- **Regulatory durability:** As corporate climate disclosure regulations tighten, CA-backed credits are expected to withstand scrutiny that standard VCM credits may not.

<AnalogyBox>
The Article 6 premium is like the difference between a property title recorded at the land registry versus an informal agreement. Both might describe the same piece of land, but the formally recorded title carries exclusive legal recognition -- no one else can simultaneously hold a valid claim to the same property. The informal agreement might be genuine, but it lacks the institutional guarantee of exclusivity. Corporate buyers paying a premium for Article 6 credits are paying for that institutional guarantee.
</AnalogyBox>

## Corporate Use Cases: What Can Be Claimed?

The appropriate use of carbon credits depends critically on what accounting claim the buyer intends to make.

**With VCM credits only (no CA):** A company can make claims about supporting climate action globally, contributing to emission reductions in the host country, and achieving voluntary carbon neutrality. However, it cannot credibly claim that the reduction is not being simultaneously counted by the host country. Emerging corporate disclosure standards are increasingly scrutinising these claims.

**With authorised Article 6 credits (CA-backed):** A company can make stronger claims -- that the reduction is exclusive (not double-counted), that it has formal accounting standing in the national GHG inventory framework, and that it is consistent with the host country's NDC. Under emerging standards from the Voluntary Carbon Markets Integrity Initiative (VCMI) and Science Based Targets initiative (SBTi), CA-backed credits are positioned as the highest-quality category for corporate residual emissions claims.

The VCMI's **Claims Code of Practice** establishes a hierarchy of claims that companies can make based on the quality of credits purchased. At the top of the hierarchy sit claims that require CA-backed credits -- "Beyond Value Chain Mitigation" at the highest integrity level. Lower-tier claims can be supported by VCM credits meeting the ICVCM's Core Carbon Principles, but without the full exclusivity that a CA provides.

## The ICVCM Core Carbon Principles

The Integrity Council for the Voluntary Carbon Market (ICVCM) developed the **Core Carbon Principles (CCPs)** as an attempt to establish a minimum quality floor for voluntary carbon credits. The CCPs cover:

- Effective governance of the standard body
- Transparent tracking and reporting
- Robust independent third-party validation and verification
- Additionality
- Permanence
- Robust quantification of emission reductions
- No double counting (within the VCM -- not equivalent to a CA)
- Sustainable development safeguards
- No net harm to communities or ecosystems

A credit from a carbon programme assessed as meeting the CCPs receives a "CCP-label," distinguishing it from uncertified VCM credits. CCP-labelled credits represent a meaningful quality improvement and are increasingly sought by sophisticated corporate buyers. However, the CCP "no double counting" criterion applies within the voluntary market's own systems -- it does not address the deeper double-counting issue that arises when host countries also count the same reductions in their NDCs. Only a CA resolves that problem.

<ExampleBox>
**A company's credit purchasing decision in practice**

A German automotive manufacturer has committed to net-zero by 2045. In 2030, after exhausting all feasible emission reduction measures, it has 500,000 tCO2e of residual scope 3 emissions from steel supply chain processes that cannot yet be eliminated. It needs high-integrity credits for a credible net-zero claim.

Option A: Purchase Verra VCS REDD+ credits at €8/tCO2e (not CCP-labelled). Brazil, where the projects are located, is also likely counting these reductions toward its own NDC. Cost: €4 million.

Option B: Purchase CCP-labelled Gold Standard clean energy credits at €15/tCO2e. Higher quality, but still no CA. Cost: €7.5 million.

Option C: Purchase authorised A6.4ERs from a solar project in Morocco, with CA confirmed by Morocco's DNA. Price: €28/tCO2e. Cost: €14 million.

Under the company's guidelines aligned with SBTi's net-zero standard, only Option C provides the accounting exclusivity needed for a credible residual emissions claim. The €10 million premium over Option B is the price of environmental integrity.
</ExampleBox>

## Market Fragmentation Risk

One consequence of the quality divergence between Article 6 credits and standard VCM credits is the risk of **market fragmentation**. As sophisticated buyers -- large corporations, institutional investors, compliance entities -- migrate toward CA-backed Article 6 credits, the VCM may be left serving only buyers with lower quality requirements or smaller budgets.

This fragmentation creates problems:

- **Stranded supply:** Projects that cannot obtain host-country authorisation -- because the host country lacks a functioning DNA, or because the government is unwilling to apply CAs -- are left selling into a market segment that is declining in credibility and value.
- **Price divergence:** The gap between Article 6 premium credits and standard VCM credits may widen to the point where the two are effectively different products serving different markets, rather than a quality spectrum within a single market.
- **Access inequality:** Host countries with less institutional capacity cannot authorise credits and therefore cannot access the premium market, concentrating the financial benefits of carbon markets in countries that already have strong governance.

## The "Waterfall" Theory

Some analysts propose a **waterfall model** for the long-term relationship between VCM and Article 6: over time, the Article 6 system expands its scope and capacity, the CA requirement becomes universal, and the VCM as a separate system gradually dissolves -- its projects, methodologies, and infrastructure absorbed into Article 6.4 or bilateral 6.2 agreements.

Under this theory:

- Projects currently in the VCM without CAs represent a transitional stock that will diminish as host countries build the capacity to authorise credits.
- As NDC ambitions tighten with each successive Paris Agreement cycle, host countries have less headroom to authorise large volumes of credits without compromising their own targets, naturally constraining VCM-to-Article-6 conversion.
- The long-term equilibrium is a fully integrated international carbon market operating under Article 6 rules, with the VCM as a purely domestic or sub-national complement.

This remains a theory, not a certainty. The VCM has deep institutional roots, large registries, established buyer-seller relationships, and strong advocacy from standard bodies reluctant to cede their authority to a UN body. The coexistence of Article 6 and VCM systems may persist for decades.

## Practical Guidance for Organisations

For organisations navigating this landscape, several practical principles apply:

**Match credit quality to claim quality.** If you are making a net-zero claim that requires accounting exclusivity, purchase CA-backed Article 6 credits. If you are making a voluntary "contribution to climate action" claim without asserting accounting exclusivity, CCP-labelled VCM credits may suffice -- but understand precisely what you are and are not claiming.

**Ask about the CA.** Before purchasing any credit described as "Article 6," request evidence of the corresponding adjustment. Not all credits marketed as "Article 6" or "Paris-aligned" actually carry a CA -- they may be from projects in countries that have signed Article 6 bilateral agreements without yet completing the authorisation and CA application for specific credit batches.

**Check host country NDC headroom.** Even with a CA, if the host country's NDC ambition is very low -- a target it would meet without taking any additional action -- the transferred reduction may represent hot air rather than a genuine decrease in global emissions.

**Understand the vintage.** Credits should be vintage-matched to the period in which you are making your claim. Old-vintage credits do not represent current-period climate action.

**Monitor evolving standards.** Corporate disclosure frameworks -- SBTi, VCMI, GHG Protocol -- are actively revising their guidance on credit use. Requirements for credible claims are becoming more stringent, and credit strategies calibrated to today's standards may not satisfy tomorrow's.

The parallel existence of Article 6 and VCM systems is not a flaw -- it reflects the reality that different buyers have different needs and different capacities to pay for quality. But the gap between what the two systems deliver is significant, and it is widening. For organisations that take their climate commitments seriously, understanding that gap is not optional.
