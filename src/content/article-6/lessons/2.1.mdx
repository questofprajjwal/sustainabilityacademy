The Article 6.4 mechanism is the multilateral centrepiece of the Paris Agreement's carbon market architecture. Where Article 6.2 governs bilateral deals between countries, Article 6.4 establishes a centralised, UN-supervised crediting mechanism open to all Parties. It creates a new class of internationally recognised carbon credits, a new governance body, and a new set of rules designed to ensure that credits issued under the mechanism represent genuine, additional, and verified emissions reductions.

## Legal Basis and Political Mandate

Article 6, paragraph 4 of the Paris Agreement mandates the establishment of "a mechanism to contribute to the mitigation of greenhouse gas emissions and support sustainable development." This language is deliberately broad. It does not prescribe in detail how the mechanism must operate -- that was left for subsequent negotiations. What it does establish is the political mandate for a UN-supervised carbon market that goes beyond anything in the Kyoto Protocol.

The mechanism is formally known as the **Paris Agreement Crediting Mechanism (PACM)**, though it is commonly referred to as the **Article 6.4 mechanism** or simply the **A6.4 mechanism**. Its operational rules were agreed at COP26 in Glasgow (Decision 3/CMA.3) and subsequently refined at COP28 in Dubai and COP29 in Baku.

<HighlightBox>
The Article 6.4 mechanism -- also called the PACM -- is a centralised, UN-supervised carbon market open to all Paris Agreement Parties. It produces A6.4ERs (Article 6.4 Emission Reductions), a new class of internationally recognised carbon credit with distinct rules on accounting, cancellation, and transfer.
</HighlightBox>

## The Supervisory Body: Composition and Mandate

The mechanism is overseen by the **Article 6.4 Supervisory Body (SB)**, a UN committee of twelve members elected by the Conference of the Parties serving as the meeting of the Parties to the Paris Agreement (CMA). Its composition reflects the UNFCCC's commitment to geographic equity and the inclusion of vulnerable nations:

- Representatives from each of the five UN regional groups (Africa, Asia-Pacific, Eastern Europe, Latin America and the Caribbean, and Western Europe and Others)
- One representative from Least Developed Countries (LDCs)
- One representative from Small Island Developing States (SIDS)
- Additional members to reach the total of twelve, drawn on a rotating basis

Membership is rotating, with staggered terms to maintain continuity. Members serve in their personal capacity as experts, not as government delegates, which is intended to insulate the body from direct political pressure.

The Supervisory Body's mandate covers:

1. **Methodology approval** -- reviewing and approving methodologies for quantifying emissions reductions and removals
2. **Activity registration** -- reviewing and approving project (activity) registration requests
3. **Validation and verification oversight** -- accrediting and overseeing third-party auditors
4. **Credit issuance** -- authorising the issuance of A6.4ERs upon receipt of verified monitoring reports
5. **Registry management** -- operating the central UN registry for A6.4ERs
6. **Transparency and reporting** -- publishing decisions, standards, and annual reports

<AnalogyBox>
Think of the Supervisory Body as the governing council of a regulated stock exchange. It does not trade credits itself, but it sets and enforces the rules under which all participants must operate. Methodology approvals are like listing requirements -- only projects meeting the standard can issue credits on the exchange. Registry oversight ensures that every credit in circulation has a verified chain of custody.
</AnalogyBox>

## How the PACM Differs from the Kyoto CDM

The A6.4 mechanism was consciously designed to learn from the Clean Development Mechanism (CDM). The two systems share surface similarities -- both allow project-based carbon crediting, both involve UN oversight, both enable finance to flow from richer to poorer countries -- but the architectural differences are substantial.

**Universality:** The CDM was asymmetric: only developed (Annex I) countries could buy credits, and only developing countries could host projects. The A6.4 mechanism is open to all Parties. Any country can host an activity, and any country (or private actor) can acquire A6.4ERs. This reflects the Paris Agreement's universal architecture.

**Corresponding adjustments:** Credits transferred across borders under A6.4 require a corresponding adjustment (CA) in the host country's NDC accounting. This did not exist under the CDM and is the primary mechanism preventing double counting. The seller country adds the transferred emissions back to its national inventory; the buyer country subtracts them from theirs.

**Overall Mitigation of Global Emissions (OMGE):** 2% of all issued A6.4ERs are automatically cancelled and never used. This mandatory cancellation is designed to ensure that the mechanism produces a net benefit for the global atmosphere, not merely a transfer of mitigation between countries. There was no equivalent in the CDM.

**Share of Proceeds (SOP):** A levy on transactions and issuances flows to the Adaptation Fund to finance adaptation projects in developing countries. The CDM had a SOP, but the A6.4 mechanism formalises and extends it.

**Stricter methodology standards:** The Supervisory Body applies rigorous standards for additionality, baseline setting, and permanence. Methodologies must be approved at the international level, not merely at the national level, reducing the risk of weak standards in host countries.

<ExampleBox>
**CDM vs A6.4 in practice:** Under the CDM, a solar energy project in Vietnam generates CERs. A German company buys them to offset its Kyoto obligations. Vietnam has no binding target, so it also counts the emission reduction in its own national inventory. The same tonne is subtracted twice -- once by Germany, once by Vietnam -- without any atmospheric double benefit.

Under A6.4, the same Vietnamese solar project generates A6.4ERs. When Germany acquires them, Vietnam must apply a corresponding adjustment: it adds the transferred reductions back to its NDC accounts. The reduction is counted exactly once globally. Germany's use is real. Vietnam's NDC progress reflects only what it retains.
</ExampleBox>

## The Project Cycle

An A6.4 activity follows a structured cycle from inception to credit issuance:

**1. Methodology selection or development.** The project developer identifies a Supervisory Body-approved methodology applicable to the project type. If no approved methodology exists, the developer may propose a new one for SB review.

**2. Activity Design Document (ADD) preparation.** The developer prepares an ADD -- the core technical document describing the project, its baseline scenario, additionality demonstration, monitoring plan, and expected emission reductions.

**3. Validation.** An accredited third-party auditor (an **Approved Designated Operating Entity**, or ADOE) reviews the ADD and confirms that the project meets all applicable requirements.

**4. Activity registration.** The validated ADD is submitted to the Supervisory Body. Upon approval, the activity is registered in the central A6.4 registry and assigned a unique identifier.

**5. Implementation and monitoring.** The project developer implements the project and monitors actual emissions reductions according to the approved monitoring plan.

**6. Verification.** At the end of each monitoring period, an ADOE independently verifies the monitored data and confirms the quantity of emission reductions achieved.

**7. Credit issuance.** Based on the verified monitoring report, the Supervisory Body authorises the issuance of A6.4ERs into the registry. The OMGE deduction (2%) and any administrative fees are applied at this stage.

**8. Transfer.** The host country (or project proponent) may then transfer A6.4ERs to acquiring parties. Authorised A6.4ERs transferred for NDC use trigger a corresponding adjustment.

## The Central UN Registry

All A6.4ERs are recorded in the **central A6.4 registry** operated by the Supervisory Body. This is a key governance innovation: unlike many voluntary market registries operated by private standards bodies, the A6.4 registry is a UN-administered system with mandatory participation.

The registry tracks:

- Each A6.4ER from issuance through transfer to final use or cancellation
- Whether credits are authorised (for NDC use) or mitigation contribution credits
- Corresponding adjustments applied
- OMGE cancellations
- SOP remittances to the Adaptation Fund

The registry is designed to interoperate with national registries under Article 6.2 and with the International Transaction Log (ITL), providing a unified ledger of international carbon transfers under the Paris Agreement.

## The Two Credit Types

The A6.4 mechanism issues two distinct types of credits, serving different purposes:

**Authorised A6.4ERs** are credits that the host country government has formally authorised for international transfer and use toward another party's NDC (or other international mitigation purpose, such as CORSIA). These credits:

- Require a corresponding adjustment in the host country's NDC accounts
- Can be transferred internationally and used by the acquiring country or entity for NDC achievement
- Carry a premium over voluntary market credits precisely because they represent an internationally verified, CA-backed unit

**Mitigation Contribution A6.4ERs** are credits that are not authorised for international NDC use. These credits:

- Do not require a corresponding adjustment
- Can be used for results-based climate finance, domestic carbon pricing schemes, or other purposes that do not involve international NDC claims
- Cannot be used by an acquiring party to claim NDC achievement

The distinction matters enormously for buyers. A corporate buyer seeking to make credible climate claims under the Paris accounting framework needs authorised A6.4ERs. A domestic carbon pricing scheme in a host country, or a results-based finance programme providing payments for verified reductions, may use mitigation contribution credits without needing the full CA infrastructure.

<HighlightBox>
The two-track credit structure is a deliberate design choice. It allows host countries to participate in the mechanism even when they are not ready to authorise all credits for international transfer. Countries can use mitigation contribution credits domestically while reserving authorised credits for transactions that command a higher price and carry greater accounting obligations.
</HighlightBox>

## Developing Country Access

The A6.4 mechanism includes several provisions designed to make it accessible to developing countries, particularly LDCs and SIDS, which lack the technical capacity and financial resources of larger economies.

**LDC/SIDS exemptions from SOP:** Least Developed Countries and Small Island Developing States are exempt from the Share of Proceeds levy, reducing the financial burden of participation. This recognises that these countries are among the least responsible for climate change and most in need of finance.

**Capacity building obligations:** Developed country Parties have committed to support developing countries in building the technical and institutional capacity needed to participate in the A6.4 mechanism, including national registry infrastructure, MRV systems, and expertise in methodology development.

**Supervisory Body representation:** The guaranteed seats for LDC and SIDS representatives on the Supervisory Body ensure that the mechanism's rules are shaped by the needs and perspectives of the most vulnerable nations, not just by the largest market participants.

The Article 6.4 mechanism thus attempts to balance three competing objectives: environmental integrity (ensuring credits represent real reductions), market efficiency (enabling cost-effective mitigation at scale), and equity (ensuring the mechanism works for all countries, not just the largest and most technically sophisticated).
