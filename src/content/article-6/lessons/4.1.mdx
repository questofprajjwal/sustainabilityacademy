The central promise of carbon markets is that they deliver real reductions in atmospheric greenhouse gas concentrations. Every credit traded is supposed to represent one tonne of CO2 equivalent that was genuinely reduced, removed, or avoided -- a tonne that would have reached the atmosphere if the carbon market had not existed. When this promise is kept, carbon markets are a powerful tool for cost-effective climate action. When it is broken, carbon markets become at best an accounting fiction and at worst a mechanism that allows emissions to continue under a veneer of climate responsibility.

**Environmental integrity** is the term used to describe whether carbon credits keep this promise. It is the single most important concept in evaluating any carbon crediting system.

## The Five Pillars of High-Quality Carbon Credits

The international carbon market community -- including the UNFCCC, the Integrity Council for the Voluntary Carbon Market (ICVCM), and the major standards bodies -- has converged on five core criteria that a high-quality carbon credit must satisfy:

### 1. Additionality

A credit is **additional** if the emission reduction or removal would not have occurred without the carbon market incentive. This is perhaps the most important and most difficult criterion to verify. If a solar power plant would have been built anyway because it was commercially viable, the carbon credits it generates are not additional -- they represent reductions that would have happened regardless of the market.

Additionality is tested through a **counterfactual analysis**: what is the most plausible scenario of what would have happened without the project? If the project outcome (reduced emissions) would have occurred under the baseline scenario, there is no additionality.

<HighlightBox>
Additionality asks: "Did the carbon market actually cause this emission reduction?" If the answer is no -- if the reduction would have happened anyway -- the credit has no climate value. It represents a payment for something that was going to happen regardless.
</HighlightBox>

### 2. Permanence

A credit is **permanent** if the emission reduction or removal it represents is durable. For emission reductions (e.g., switching from coal to solar), permanence is relatively straightforward -- once a coal plant is shut and replaced, the emissions it would have produced do not re-enter the atmosphere. For carbon removals stored in biological systems (forests, soils), permanence is far more complex.

A forest that sequesters carbon can burn, be cut down, or die from disease. If it does, the stored carbon is released back to the atmosphere, and the removal is reversed. Crediting systems address permanence risk through **buffer pools** (setting aside a portion of issued credits as an insurance reserve), **time-limited crediting** (issuing credits that expire and must be renewed by demonstrating the removal is still intact), and **monitoring requirements** (tracking the status of the carbon store over time).

### 3. Real and Measurable

Credits must represent emissions reductions or removals that actually occurred, can be measured with reasonable precision, and are based on verifiable data. This requires a credible **monitoring plan**, appropriate **measurement methods** (direct measurement, modelling, or default factors), and data that can be independently reviewed.

"Measurable" does not mean perfect precision -- all measurement involves uncertainty. But the uncertainty must be quantified and handled conservatively: where measurement is imprecise, credits should be discounted to ensure that issued credits represent a conservative estimate of actual reductions, not an optimistic one.

### 4. Independently Verified

Credits must be verified by an **independent, accredited third-party auditor** who reviews the monitoring data and confirms that the reported reductions meet the applicable methodology's requirements. Self-reporting by project developers is not sufficient -- independent verification is the mechanism by which the system identifies errors, misrepresentations, or fraud.

The quality of verification matters enormously. A verification body that rubber-stamps developer reports does not provide genuine assurance. Effective verification requires auditors with technical expertise in the project type, the authority to request additional information, and the independence to issue qualified or negative opinions when data does not support the claimed reductions.

### 5. No Double Counting

As discussed extensively in earlier lessons, **double counting** occurs when the same emission reduction is credited more than once. Double counting takes three main forms:

- **Double issuance**: the same project generates credits under two different registries or standards simultaneously
- **Double claiming**: the same credit is used by two different entities to make separate climate claims
- **Double counting with NDC**: the host country counts the reduction in its own NDC progress while the buyer also counts it toward its own targets

Article 6 addresses the third form -- the most systemically significant -- through the **corresponding adjustment** requirement. The first two forms are addressed through registry-level controls (unique serial numbers, retirement tracking, and cross-registry communication protocols).

<AnalogyBox>
Think of double counting like photocopying a banknote. One physical banknote has one face value. If you photocopy it and spend both the original and the copy, you have taken value twice from a system that only holds it once. A carbon credit that is counted twice -- by both the seller and the buyer -- is like the photocopy. The atmospheric benefit exists once, in the real world, but it has been spent twice in the accounting world.
</AnalogyBox>

## The Voluntary Market's Quality Problem

The scale of the environmental integrity problem in the voluntary carbon market (VCM) is significant. Multiple independent investigations -- by academic researchers, journalists, and NGOs -- have examined large samples of voluntary carbon credits and found that the fraction representing genuine, additional emission reductions is far smaller than the market assumes.

The most widely cited finding: **fewer than 16% of investigated voluntary carbon credits** have been found to represent real, additional emission reductions. The majority appear to have been issued for projects where the emission reduction was overstated, the additionality was questionable, or the baseline was poorly constructed.

The most heavily criticised category has been **REDD+ (avoided deforestation)** credits under the Verra VCS standard. Investigative journalism and academic analyses published from 2022 onwards found that many REDD+ projects used inflated baseline scenarios -- projecting implausibly high deforestation rates that the project would prevent -- resulting in credits for reductions that never occurred.

The implications are serious. If companies purchasing voluntary credits to offset their emissions are acquiring credits that represent no real reduction, then:

- The company's "net zero" or "carbon neutral" claim is false
- The atmosphere receives no benefit from the transaction
- Real emission reductions are delayed or avoided
- Consumer and investor trust in carbon markets is damaged

This is not an argument against carbon markets per se -- it is an argument for higher standards, more rigorous verification, and stronger regulatory oversight. It is precisely the problem that Article 6 was designed, at least partially, to address.

<DeepDive title="The Verra REDD+ controversy in detail">
In January 2023, a joint investigation by The Guardian, Die Zeit, and SourceMaterial published an analysis of Verra's REDD+ credits. Working with a team of academic researchers, the journalists found that approximately 90% of Verra's rainforest offset credits -- representing tens of millions of credits sold to major corporations -- were "phantom credits" that did not represent genuine carbon reductions. The methodology used inflated baseline deforestation rates by comparing project areas to unsuitable reference regions, generating massive credit quantities that bore little relationship to actual deforestation prevented. Verra disputed the findings and published rebuttal analysis. But the episode crystallised years of academic criticism of the VCM's additionality standards and accelerated demand for a higher-integrity alternative -- which Article 6.4 is intended to provide.
</DeepDive>

## What Article 6 Does Differently

Article 6's mandatory requirements address the voluntary market's integrity problems through several mechanisms:

**Corresponding adjustments for authorised credits** eliminate the double-counting-with-NDC problem for authorised A6.4ERs and ITMOs. This is the most significant structural improvement. A corporate buyer purchasing authorised credits from a country can be confident that the reduction has not also been claimed by the host country in its NDC.

**Supervisory Body methodology oversight** applies centralised, internationally agreed standards to project methodologies. Methodologies must be approved by the 12-member Supervisory Body, which is accountable to the CMA, not to private standards organisations. This does not guarantee perfect methodologies, but it introduces a level of international governance that the voluntary market lacks.

**OMGE mandatory cancellation** ensures that the mechanism produces a net atmospheric benefit, not just a transfer. This addresses the concern that carbon markets merely shift mitigation between actors without increasing total ambition.

**Stricter additionality standards** in A6.4 methodology standards are designed to be more conservative than comparable voluntary market standards, with more rigorous baseline setting requirements and stronger financial additionality tests.

**Independent accreditation of verifiers** by the Supervisory Body, rather than self-accreditation or standards-body accreditation, introduces a further layer of quality control on who can conduct verification.

## MRV: The Technical Backbone of Integrity

**MRV** -- **Monitoring, Reporting, and Verification** -- is the technical process by which emission reductions are quantified and credentialed. It is the backbone of any carbon crediting system's environmental integrity.

**Monitoring** involves the ongoing collection of data during project implementation. This includes fuel consumption data, energy production records, soil sample analysis, deforestation satellite imagery, or whatever data the methodology specifies. Monitoring must follow the approved monitoring plan and use calibrated instruments where measurement is required.

**Reporting** involves compiling monitoring data into a structured monitoring report at the end of each crediting period. The report documents the baseline scenario, the actual emissions during the period, the calculated reductions, any uncertainties and how they were handled, and any deviations from the monitoring plan.

**Verification** is the independent audit of the monitoring report. An accredited third-party auditor reviews the data, visits the project site where appropriate, interviews project personnel, and confirms (or disputes) the reported reductions. The verification opinion -- a statement of whether the reported reductions are fairly presented and meet methodology requirements -- is the basis for credit issuance.

The quality of MRV is not uniform across the market. In the voluntary market, under-resourced auditors reviewing complex projects in challenging environments sometimes fail to catch errors or misrepresentations. Article 6.4's Supervisory Body oversight of verifiers, combined with the international political stakes of NDC accounting, creates stronger incentives for rigorous verification.

## "Hot Air" and the Ambition Ratchet

One final integrity risk deserves attention: **hot air**. This occurs when a country sets an NDC target that is less ambitious than its business-as-usual emissions trajectory -- i.e., a target it could meet without taking any additional action. If such a country then sells carbon credits under Article 6, it is selling "reductions" relative to a baseline that overstates what would have occurred, generating credits that represent no actual atmospheric benefit.

Hot air was a serious problem under the Kyoto Protocol. Russia and Ukraine, for example, had Kyoto targets set at 1990 emission levels -- levels far above their actual early-2000s emissions following economic contraction. They could sell enormous quantities of emissions allowances while actually increasing emissions, because their targets were so easy to meet.

The Paris Agreement's **ambition ratchet** -- the requirement that each successive NDC be more ambitious than the previous one -- is intended to reduce hot air risk over time. As NDC targets tighten with each five-year cycle, the gap between targets and business-as-usual scenarios should narrow, making it harder for countries to sell large volumes of easy credits.

Additionally, Article 6.2's requirement that ITMOs be authorised by the host country, and Article 6.4's Supervisory Body oversight of project baselines, create mechanisms to scrutinise whether claimed reductions are genuine relative to realistic baselines.

<HighlightBox>
Environmental integrity is not a binary quality -- credits are not simply "real" or "fake." It is a spectrum determined by the rigour of additionality testing, baseline setting, permanence management, MRV quality, and double-counting prevention. Article 6 raises standards across all five dimensions, but it is not a guarantee. The quality of implementation -- by host countries, project developers, verifiers, and the Supervisory Body -- will determine whether the promise of environmental integrity is kept.
</HighlightBox>
