Two features of the Article 6.4 mechanism distinguish it most sharply from its predecessors and from the voluntary carbon market: the mandatory cancellation of a portion of all credits issued (the OMGE requirement), and the levy on transactions and issuances that flows to the Adaptation Fund (the Share of Proceeds). Together, these mechanisms ensure that the A6.4 mechanism serves two goals simultaneously -- contributing to overall global mitigation and channelling finance to climate-vulnerable nations -- rather than simply facilitating the transfer of mitigation from one country to another.

## OMGE: The Logic of Mandatory Cancellation

**OMGE** stands for **Overall Mitigation of Global Emissions**. The concept is enshrined in Article 6.4, paragraph 4 of the Paris Agreement, which states that the mechanism shall "deliver an overall mitigation in global emissions."

This phrase has a precise operational meaning: **2% of all A6.4ERs issued are automatically cancelled**. These cancelled credits are retired into the central registry and can never be used by any party for any purpose -- not for NDC achievement, not for voluntary claims, not for compliance schemes. They simply cease to exist as usable units.

Why does this matter? Carbon markets, in their basic form, are transfer mechanisms. A tonne of CO2 avoided in Country A is transferred to Country B and counted against Country B's targets. From a global atmospheric perspective, the net result is zero additional benefit: the same reduction is achieved, just accounted for differently. The OMGE requirement changes this.

By cancelling 2% of every issuance, the A6.4 mechanism ensures that for every 100 tonnes verified and issued:

- 98 tonnes are available for transfer and use
- 2 tonnes are cancelled, representing a permanent global benefit that no one claims

This 2% cancellation means that the cumulative operation of the mechanism reduces global atmospheric CO2 beyond what would occur if credits were simply transferred between parties. The mechanism does not just redistribute mitigation -- it generates net mitigation above the level implied by NDC targets alone.

<HighlightBox>
OMGE in numbers: For every 100 A6.4ERs issued, 2 are automatically cancelled and never used. This ensures the mechanism produces a net benefit for the global atmosphere, beyond what NDC targets would deliver without it. The cancellation happens at the point of issuance -- before any credits are transferred.
</HighlightBox>

<AnalogyBox>
Imagine a shared water fund where every village contributes its water savings. Under a simple transfer scheme, Village A's savings go to Village B -- the total water in the system stays the same. But if the fund rules require that 2% of all savings is poured into a permanent reserve that nobody can access, the total water in accessible circulation decreases. Over time, the reserve grows, and the system as a whole has more water saved than any individual transaction would suggest. OMGE is that 2% reserve, but for atmospheric carbon.
</AnalogyBox>

## Share of Proceeds: Structure and Rates

The **Share of Proceeds (SOP)** is a levy on Article 6.4 activity that flows to the **Adaptation Fund** -- a multilateral fund that finances concrete adaptation and resilience projects in developing countries. The SOP has two components:

**Component 1 -- Volume levy:** **5% of the A6.4ERs transferred** in each transaction are cancelled and the equivalent value remitted to the Adaptation Fund. This is applied to transfers, not issuances.

**Component 2 -- Issuance fee:** **3% of the fees collected** at the point of credit issuance go to the Adaptation Fund. This fee is assessed on the project developer at the time the Supervisory Body processes the credit issuance request.

These rates are higher than those that applied under the CDM (which had a 2% volume levy and a smaller administrative fee). The increase reflects both the Adaptation Fund's ongoing need for finance and the Paris Agreement's ambition to ensure that carbon markets actively support climate resilience, not merely mitigation.

<ExampleBox>
**SOP calculation:** A project in Ghana generates 500,000 A6.4ERs in a verification period. At the point of issuance:
- OMGE: 2% x 500,000 = 10,000 credits cancelled (never used)
- Net issuable credits: 490,000

When the project developer later transfers 300,000 of these credits to an acquiring country:
- SOP volume levy: 5% x 300,000 = 15,000 credits cancelled, value equivalent remitted to Adaptation Fund
- Credits actually delivered to acquirer: 285,000

The project developer and acquiring country must factor both the OMGE deduction (at issuance) and the SOP volume levy (at transfer) into their economic planning. For a project generating 1 million credits per year, the combined effect of OMGE and SOP means that only around 930,500 credits ultimately reach acquirers after all levies -- a material difference that affects project revenue projections.
</ExampleBox>

## The LDC and SIDS Exemption

Least Developed Countries (LDCs) and Small Island Developing States (SIDS) are **exempt from the Share of Proceeds requirement**. This exemption applies to activities hosted in LDC or SIDS territories. The rationale is straightforward: these countries bear the least historical responsibility for climate change and face the greatest barriers to participating in carbon markets. Imposing a financial levy on their participation would further disadvantage the most vulnerable nations.

The LDC/SIDS exemption does not extend to the OMGE cancellation. The 2% mandatory cancellation applies universally to all A6.4ERs regardless of where the project is hosted. This reflects the different purpose of the two mechanisms: OMGE is about global atmospheric benefit (universal by definition), while SOP is about financing adaptation (where the equity case for exemption is strongest).

<HighlightBox>
LDC and SIDS exemption: host countries classified as Least Developed Countries or Small Island Developing States pay no Share of Proceeds levy on their A6.4 activities. The OMGE 2% cancellation still applies. This exemption makes the mechanism more economically viable for the world's most vulnerable countries.
</HighlightBox>

## The Adaptation Fund: What the Proceeds Finance

The **Adaptation Fund** was established under the Kyoto Protocol to finance adaptation projects in developing countries. It is governed by a Board with majority representation from developing countries and operates independently of the World Bank, making it one of the few multilateral climate funds directly accountable to developing nations.

Projects financed by the Adaptation Fund include:

- Coastal protection infrastructure in SIDS at risk from sea-level rise
- Drought-resistant agriculture programmes in sub-Saharan Africa
- Early warning systems for extreme weather events in vulnerable regions
- Water security projects in climate-stressed river basins

The SOP flow from Article 6.4 is intended to be a predictable, market-linked source of adaptation finance. As the A6.4 mechanism scales up and credit volumes grow, the SOP revenue to the Adaptation Fund should grow proportionally. This linkage between carbon market activity and adaptation finance is a deliberate design feature of the Paris Agreement -- carbon markets must contribute to resilience, not only to mitigation.

## How These Levies Affect Project Economics

Both OMGE and SOP impose real costs on project developers and credit acquirers. For a project developer, the effective yield of a project -- the proportion of verified reductions that translate into transferable credits -- is reduced by both mechanisms.

For a project selling all of its verified reductions:

- OMGE reduces issuable credits by 2% (applied at issuance)
- SOP volume levy reduces transferable credits by a further 5% (applied at transfer)
- Combined effect: approximately 7% of verified reductions never reach the acquirer as usable credits

For acquirers, this means that for every tonne of CO2 they need to account for, they must purchase slightly more than one A6.4ER to receive one net tonne after levies. This is typically factored into pricing -- A6.4ERs are priced to include the cost of OMGE and SOP, meaning that projects must generate sufficient revenue per tonne to cover both the levy deductions and project costs.

In practice, higher-quality projects in high-demand sectors can command premiums that more than offset the OMGE and SOP costs. The levies are constraints on marginal projects, not prohibitive barriers to well-designed activities.

## Policy Rationale: Beyond Mere Transfer

The deeper purpose of OMGE and SOP is to change what carbon markets are for. A pure carbon market -- one that simply transfers credits between parties with no mandatory cancellation and no adaptation levy -- is a cost-reduction tool. It minimises the expense of meeting existing targets, but it does not inherently raise global ambition or support vulnerable nations.

The OMGE and SOP requirements transform the A6.4 mechanism from a cost-reduction tool into a mechanism that:

1. **Raises global mitigation beyond NDC floors** (via the 2% cancellation)
2. **Channels finance to adaptation** in the most vulnerable nations (via the SOP to the Adaptation Fund)
3. **Creates a market incentive for host countries** to set ambitious NDC targets (because a more ambitious NDC can accommodate more authorised credit transfers without undermining national goals)

This combination -- market efficiency, environmental integrity, and development equity -- is what the Paris Agreement intended when it mandated an "overall mitigation of global emissions" and a mechanism that "promotes sustainable development." OMGE and SOP are the operational expression of those political commitments.
