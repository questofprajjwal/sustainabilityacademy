Article 6 of the Paris Agreement is not a single mechanism. It is a framework with three distinct tracks, each reflecting a different philosophy about how international climate cooperation should work -- how much oversight is needed, how much flexibility countries should retain, and whether monetary carbon credits are the right instrument at all. Understanding the three tracks, their differences, and how they interact is essential to navigating the landscape of Paris Agreement carbon markets.

## Article 6.2: Bilateral and Multilateral Cooperative Approaches

Article 6.2 is the most flexible of the three tracks. It creates a framework for countries to engage in **cooperative approaches** -- essentially bilateral or multilateral agreements to trade emission reductions -- while leaving most of the design choices to the participating countries themselves.

### What Is an ITMO?

The unit traded under Article 6.2 is called an **Internationally Transferred Mitigation Outcome (ITMO)**. The term is deliberately broad. Unlike the CDM's Certified Emission Reductions, which were defined in units of CO2-equivalent tonnes, ITMOs can represent mitigation outcomes measured in a variety of ways -- including tonnes of CO2e, but potentially also other metrics such as kilowatt-hours of renewable electricity or square kilometres of forest protected, depending on the sectors covered by the participating countries' NDCs.

This flexibility is intentional. Countries with NDCs expressed in non-greenhouse-gas terms (for example, an NDC targeting a specific renewable energy share rather than an absolute emissions cap) may find it more natural to trade in units that correspond to their own accounting frameworks.

### How Article 6.2 Works in Practice

Under Article 6.2, two or more countries negotiate and formalise a **cooperative approach**. The host country -- the one where the mitigation activity takes place -- authorises the issuance of ITMOs. The acquiring country receives those ITMOs and applies them toward achieving its own NDC, with a corresponding adjustment applied to both countries' national inventories.

The rules require:

- **Authorisation**: The host country must formally authorise each ITMO for transfer
- **Corresponding adjustment**: The host country's national GHG inventory must be adjusted upward (showing more emissions) by the amount of the ITMO transferred, so that the reduction is only counted once globally
- **Transparency and reporting**: Countries must report their cooperative approaches through biennial transparency reports submitted to the UNFCCC

The corresponding adjustment is the accounting mechanism that prevents double counting. It works by ensuring that when Country A sells an ITMO to Country B, Country A's official emissions account is increased by the equivalent amount -- effectively returning the emission reduction to Country A's ledger, where it is then subtracted again only by Country B.

<AnalogyBox>
Think of ITMOs like transferring a voucher between two organisations' expense accounts. If Organisation A earns a cost-reduction voucher and hands it to Organisation B to use, Organisation A's account must be debited by the same amount Organisation B credits. The total savings in the combined system do not change -- they are simply attributed to Organisation B, not Organisation A.
</AnalogyBox>

### Real-World Deployment of Article 6.2

Article 6.2 has been the most active track in the years since the Paris rulebook was agreed. As of March 2025, **97 bilateral agreements** have been signed between **59 countries**, and approximately **155 pilot projects** are underway. The most significant milestone came in 2024 when Switzerland and Thailand completed the **first formal ITMO transfer** under Article 6.2 -- a landmark that demonstrated the mechanism can function in practice, not just in theory.

Switzerland, which has a federal law allowing domestic emissions targets to be partially met through offshore carbon credits, has been the most active Article 6.2 buyer. Its agreements with countries including Ghana, Peru, and Thailand have established some of the first real precedents for how ITMOs are authorised, verified, and transferred under the Paris rules.

<ExampleBox>
**Switzerland-Thailand (2024):** Switzerland agreed to fund emission-reduction projects in Thailand -- including electric buses and solar installations -- and receive ITMOs in return. Thailand applied corresponding adjustments to its national inventory, confirming that the reductions count only for Switzerland's NDC. This was the first completed ITMO transfer under Article 6.2, establishing a live template for how bilateral carbon cooperation works under the Paris Agreement.
</ExampleBox>

### Strengths and Limitations

The primary strength of Article 6.2 is its flexibility. Countries can design cooperative approaches that fit their NDC structures, sector priorities, and administrative capacities without waiting for a centralised international body to approve each project. This makes Article 6.2 particularly suited to large-scale, government-to-government transactions and to sectors that are difficult to address through project-by-project crediting.

The limitation is that Article 6.2 provides minimal standardisation. The quality of the mitigation outcome depends entirely on the rules the participating countries set for themselves. There is no international body reviewing whether the emission reductions are real, additional, or permanent. Buyers and sellers bear the responsibility for environmental integrity, which can create significant variability in credit quality across different bilateral agreements.

## Article 6.4: The Paris Agreement Crediting Mechanism (PACM)

Article 6.4 creates what is often called the **Paris Agreement Crediting Mechanism (PACM)** -- a centralised, UN-supervised carbon market intended to serve as the successor to the Kyoto CDM. Where Article 6.2 leaves design to participating countries, Article 6.4 places oversight with an international institution.

### The Supervisory Body

The Article 6.4 mechanism is governed by a **12-member Supervisory Body** established under the authority of the Conference of the Parties serving as the meeting of the Parties to the Paris Agreement (CMA). The Supervisory Body is responsible for:

- Approving and updating methodologies for calculating emission reductions
- Accrediting the independent third-party auditors (Designated Operational Entities) that verify projects
- Reviewing and registering project activities
- Overseeing the issuance of credits
- Maintaining the international registry

This centralised oversight is intended to address the additionality and methodological failures of the CDM by applying more rigorous and standardised rules across all projects, regardless of which countries are involved.

### The A6.4ER: Two Types of Credit

The unit produced under Article 6.4 is the **A6.4ER** (Article 6.4 Emission Reduction). However, not all A6.4ERs are alike. The rulebook distinguishes two categories based on how they will be used:

**Authorised A6.4ERs** are credits that the host country has formally authorised for use toward another country's NDC or for international purposes such as CORSIA (the Carbon Offsetting and Reduction Scheme for International Aviation). When a host country issues an authorisation, it must apply a **corresponding adjustment** to its own national inventory -- the same mechanism as under Article 6.2. These credits carry the strongest climate guarantee: the emission reduction is formally attributed to one and only one entity.

**Mitigation Contribution (MC) Units** are A6.4ERs that have not been authorised for NDC use. They can be used for results-based finance (where project finance is contingent on verified outcomes), domestic offsetting programmes, or purchases by private buyers who do not need internationally recognised NDC credits. Because no authorisation is granted, no corresponding adjustment is applied. These credits contribute to the host country's climate efforts and to global mitigation, but they do not transfer the NDC accounting right to the buyer.

<HighlightBox>
The distinction between authorised A6.4ERs and mitigation contribution units matters enormously for buyers. An authorised credit, with a corresponding adjustment, represents a genuine transfer of a nationally recognised emission reduction. A mitigation contribution unit supports real mitigation activity but does not carry the same NDC accounting guarantee. Buyers need to understand which type they are purchasing and why it matters for their own climate claims.
</HighlightBox>

### Mandatory Levies: OMGE and SOP

Article 6.4 imposes two mandatory levies on all issued A6.4ERs:

**Overall Mitigation in Global Emissions (OMGE):** 2% of all issued A6.4ERs are automatically cancelled -- that is, they are retired from the registry without being used by any country or entity. This "share of cancellation" ensures that the Article 6.4 mechanism contributes to a net reduction in global emissions beyond what would be achieved by NDC accounting alone. It is a structural mechanism for ensuring that international carbon trading does not merely shuffle reductions around, but also contributes net additional mitigation.

**Share of Proceeds (SOP) for Adaptation:** 5% of proceeds from all Article 6.4 transactions are directed to the **Adaptation Fund**. This levy ensures that the mechanism channels finance to developing countries' adaptation needs, not merely to mitigation projects. The Adaptation Fund supports concrete projects helping vulnerable communities manage the impacts of climate change that are already occurring.

<FormulaBox>
Article 6.4 mandatory levies on every credit issuance:

OMGE cancellation: 2% of issued A6.4ERs automatically retired (net global mitigation)
SOP to Adaptation Fund: 5% of transaction proceeds directed to adaptation finance

Example: 1,000 A6.4ERs issued from a project
-- 20 ERs are immediately cancelled (OMGE)
-- 5% of the sale price of all 1,000 ERs flows to the Adaptation Fund
-- 980 ERs remain available for sale and use
</FormulaBox>

<DeepDive title="Why does OMGE matter for environmental integrity?">
The OMGE mechanism addresses a philosophical concern at the heart of carbon markets: if Country A reduces emissions and sells those reductions to Country B, the net effect on the atmosphere is zero -- one country emits more (relatively speaking) and another emits less by the same amount. The temperature goal is met through the aggregate of NDCs, but no additional mitigation occurs.

The 2% OMGE cancellation changes this. By retiring 2% of every credit batch, the mechanism ensures that for every 100 tonnes of mitigation generated under Article 6.4, only 98 tonnes are transferred to buyers and 2 tonnes are permanently removed from global accounts. Over time, at scale, this builds in a structural net climate benefit -- the mechanism does not just redistribute reductions but generates genuine net mitigation beyond what NDC trading would otherwise achieve.
</DeepDive>

## Article 6.8: Non-Market Cooperative Approaches

Article 6.8 is the least visible of the three tracks but addresses a genuinely different need. It creates a framework for **non-market cooperative approaches** -- climate cooperation between countries that does not involve the trading of carbon credits at all.

### What Non-Market Cooperation Looks Like

Under Article 6.8, countries can cooperate through:

- Direct financial transfers (grants, concessional loans, development finance)
- Technology transfer (sharing clean energy or adaptation technologies)
- Capacity building (training, institutional strengthening, regulatory reform)
- Policy support and technical assistance

The defining feature is that no carbon credit is created and no corresponding adjustment is required. There is no tradeable instrument. A developed country might fund a renewable energy programme in a developing country under Article 6.8 simply because both parties agree it is the right thing to do -- without needing to claim any carbon credit in return.

### The Digital Platform

To facilitate Article 6.8 cooperation, the Paris Agreement established a **digital platform** managed by the UNFCCC Secretariat. This platform is intended to connect countries that have mitigation or adaptation projects seeking funding with countries and institutions that have finance available. It functions as a matchmaking and transparency tool, making it easier for project developers in smaller or less well-connected countries to find partners.

<HighlightBox>
Article 6.8 recognises that not all international climate cooperation needs to be transactional. Some of the most important climate action -- particularly in the world's most vulnerable and least-developed countries -- may be better served by grants, technology transfers, and technical assistance than by carbon markets. The Article 6.8 framework gives this kind of cooperation a formal home within the Paris Agreement architecture.
</HighlightBox>

## How the Three Tracks Interact

The three tracks are not mutually exclusive. A single country may participate in Article 6.2 bilateral agreements with some partners, host Article 6.4 projects generating A6.4ERs, and benefit from Article 6.8 financial and technical assistance -- all simultaneously.

In practice, the key interaction to understand is between Article 6.2 and Article 6.4. An A6.4ER that is authorised by its host country for international transfer becomes, in effect, an ITMO under Article 6.2 rules. The centralised mechanism of 6.4 feeds into the bilateral trading framework of 6.2. This means that the same corresponding adjustment requirement applies: whichever mechanism generates the credit, the accounting rules ensure it is counted once and once only.

Article 6.8 sits apart. Because no credit is transferred, there is no interaction with the corresponding adjustment system. It is a separate lane for a different kind of international cooperation.

<AnalogyBox>
Think of the three tracks like different ways a hospital system could share resources between hospitals. Article 6.2 is a direct negotiated transfer between two hospitals -- they agree on what to share and manage the paperwork themselves. Article 6.4 is a centralised system managed by a hospital authority that standardises what can be transferred, how it is verified, and how records are kept. Article 6.8 is a charitable foundation that provides grants and medical equipment to underfunded hospitals with no expectation of receiving anything in return. All three serve the broader goal of improving health outcomes, but through different mechanisms with different levels of standardisation and oversight.
</AnalogyBox>
