Carbon markets under Article 6 exist to serve one primary purpose: helping countries achieve their **Nationally Determined Contributions** more cost-effectively. But to understand how that works -- and where it can go wrong -- it is necessary to understand what NDCs actually are, how they function within the Paris Agreement architecture, and why the interaction between NDC accounting and carbon credit transfers is so technically demanding.

## What Is an NDC?

**Nationally Determined Contributions** are defined under **Article 4** of the Paris Agreement. Each Party to the Agreement is required to prepare, communicate, and maintain successive NDCs that represent its highest possible ambition in light of its national circumstances.

An NDC is, at its core, a national emissions target packaged with the policies and measures a country intends to use to achieve it. Most NDCs include:

- A headline emissions reduction target, typically expressed as a percentage reduction against a base year or a deviation from a business-as-usual emissions trajectory
- A target year, almost universally 2030 for current-round NDCs
- Sector-specific commitments (energy, transport, land use, industry, waste)
- Adaptation commitments
- Conditional elements that depend on international support

The word "nationally determined" is deliberately chosen and reflects the Paris Agreement's fundamental political bargain: no international body imposes targets on countries. Each country determines its own level of ambition. The Paris Agreement then creates a process -- the global stocktake, held every five years -- to assess the collective adequacy of all NDCs against the Agreement's temperature goals, and to create political pressure for greater ambition in the next round.

<HighlightBox>
NDCs are not legally binding emissions targets in the traditional sense. The Paris Agreement requires countries to submit NDCs and to pursue them in good faith, but the specific targets within each NDC are determined by each country for itself. What is legally binding is the obligation to submit, communicate, and update NDCs -- not the obligation to achieve any particular number.
</HighlightBox>

## The Five-Year Ratchet Mechanism

The ratchet mechanism is the engine of progressive ambition within the Paris Agreement. Under Article 4.9, each Party must submit a new NDC every **five years**, with the expectation -- and the explicit requirement -- that each successive NDC will go further than the last.

The sequence runs:

- 2020: First NDCs submitted (many were updates to earlier INDCs -- Intended NDCs)
- 2025: Second NDCs due
- 2030: Third NDCs due
- 2035: Fourth NDCs due

Each round is supposed to incorporate the findings of the most recent global stocktake (the first formal stocktake was concluded at COP28 in Dubai in 2023) and reflect increased domestic ambition where possible. The 2025 NDC round was therefore especially significant: it came immediately after the first stocktake, which found that current policies and targets fall far short of the 1.5C pathway.

<AnalogyBox>
The ratchet mechanism works like a physical ratchet tool: you can only turn forward, never backward. Each NDC must be at least as ambitious as the previous one, and ideally more so. There is no formal mechanism to enforce this -- the Paris Agreement relies on transparency and reputational pressure -- but the obligation to submit progressively more ambitious NDCs is unambiguous in the text.
</AnalogyBox>

## What It Means to "Achieve" an NDC

When a country claims that it has achieved its NDC, it means that its reported national GHG emissions over the target period fell at or below the level specified in that NDC. But how those emissions are measured, reported, and verified matters enormously.

Countries report their emissions through **National Greenhouse Gas Inventories**, prepared according to IPCC methodological guidelines and submitted to the UNFCCC. The inventory covers all sectors of the economy and all greenhouse gases, each converted to CO2-equivalent using standardised **Global Warming Potential (GWP)** factors.

**Achieving an NDC does not require domestic emissions alone to hit the target.** Under Article 6, countries may use internationally transferred mitigation outcomes (ITMOs) to contribute toward NDC achievement. When Country B purchases ITMOs from Country A and applies a corresponding adjustment, Country B's effective emissions -- for NDC accounting purposes -- are reduced by the amount of the ITMO. This is economically equivalent to reducing domestic emissions, but achieved through international cooperation rather than domestic policy.

This creates a critical distinction:

- **Domestic mitigation action** = actual reductions in the country's own GHG inventory
- **ITMO use** = accounting adjustment that effectively lowers the country's NDC "bill" without requiring the same domestic emissions change

Both are legitimate under the Paris Agreement. But they have different implications for long-term decarbonisation trajectories, for domestic industry, and for the credibility of a country's climate ambition.

## Additionality in the NDC Context

The concept of **additionality** in carbon markets asks whether an emission reduction would have occurred anyway, without the carbon market incentive. In the context of NDCs, this question takes on a more nuanced dimension.

Consider the following scenario: a host country has committed in its NDC to achieving 20GW of solar power by 2030. Suppose a foreign buyer purchases ITMOs from a solar project in that country. Is that project additional? In a narrow technical sense, perhaps -- the project may not have proceeded without foreign finance. But from a national accounting perspective, the emission reduction from the solar project was already part of what the host country intended to achieve under its own NDC.

This is why Article 6.2 rules require not just that ITMOs are real and verified, but that the corresponding adjustment is applied correctly. If a country sells ITMOs derived from activities that were already part of its NDC pathway, it must either:

- Accept that it will need to find additional domestic reductions to compensate for what it has sold, or
- Revise its NDC ambition upward to account for the sold reductions

Without this discipline, a country could sell its NDC "slack" -- the easiest and cheapest reductions already planned -- to foreign buyers, pocket the carbon finance, and end up with a weakened domestic climate trajectory while claiming NDC achievement through accounting.

<ExampleBox>
**Scenario -- NDC integrity:** Country X has an NDC target of reducing emissions by 30% by 2030. Its domestic analysis suggests it can comfortably achieve 35% with planned policies. The 5% difference represents "headroom" -- reductions beyond the NDC target.

Country X decides to sell ITMOs representing 3% of that headroom to Country Y. Country X applies a corresponding adjustment: its effective NDC ledger now shows that it has transferred 3% of its planned reductions to Country Y. Country X now needs to achieve 33% domestically (its NDC target of 30% plus the 3% sold). Country Y can count the 3% against its own NDC.

This is the intended operation. The integrity depends on Country X's domestic projections being accurate and honest, and on the corresponding adjustments being applied rigorously.
</ExampleBox>

## Why 78% of Countries Plan to Use Article 6

The scale of interest in Article 6 is striking. As of the time of writing, **78% of all countries that submitted NDCs** indicated an intention to use at least one Article 6 mechanism. This near-universal interest reflects a straightforward economic reality: meeting NDC targets entirely through domestic action is expensive, and for many countries -- particularly those with significant but costly abatement potential, or those that lack the domestic capital markets to finance it -- international carbon cooperation represents an attractive pathway.

The reasons vary by country type:

**Developed buyer countries** (Switzerland, Japan, South Korea, various EU member states operating through bilateral agreements) are interested in Article 6 because domestic abatement is expensive. Purchasing ITMOs from countries with lower-cost mitigation opportunities allows them to achieve their NDC targets at lower fiscal cost, freeing budget for other priorities including adaptation, technology investment, and the next round of more ambitious NDC commitments.

**Developing host countries** see Article 6 as a mechanism to attract international climate finance. A country that has significant renewable energy potential but lacks domestic investment capital can use Article 6 to attract foreign buyers willing to pay for the resulting ITMOs. The project finance flows to the host country, infrastructure gets built, emissions are reduced, and the corresponding adjustment ensures the reduction is counted only once.

**Countries with ambitious conditional NDCs** often set two targets: an unconditional target achievable with domestic resources, and a more ambitious conditional target achievable only with international support. Article 6 finance is exactly the kind of international support these conditional NDCs contemplate. Achieving the conditional target typically requires a country to receive not just grant finance, but structured cooperation that provides ongoing incentives -- which is what Article 6 agreements can offer.

<HighlightBox>
78% of countries' NDCs express intent to use Article 6. This is not merely symbolic. It reflects a structural feature of the Paris Agreement's design: the Agreement deliberately allows countries to cooperate on emissions reductions because the global cost of meeting the temperature goals is much lower when reductions are made where they are cheapest, regardless of where the finance comes from.
</HighlightBox>

## Country Use vs Corporate Use: A Critical Distinction

It is important to be precise about who is doing what with carbon credits under Article 6. The framework is designed for **sovereign cooperation** between countries. A company purchasing carbon credits -- even those generated under Article 6.4 -- is doing something categorically different from a country using ITMOs toward its NDC.

**When a country uses Article 6 ITMOs toward its NDC:**
- The ITMO is formally authorised by both host and acquiring countries
- A corresponding adjustment is applied to both national inventories
- The reduction is formally counted as part of the acquiring country's NDC progress
- The accounting is tracked through official UNFCCC reporting channels

**When a company purchases voluntary carbon credits:**
- The company retires the credit in a voluntary registry (e.g. Verra, Gold Standard) and claims a "carbon offset" for its own reporting purposes
- No corresponding adjustment is automatically required (unless the underlying credit was authorised under Article 6 for international use)
- The credit does not contribute to the host country's NDC -- or, if the host country has applied no corresponding adjustment, the emission reduction remains in the host country's national inventory
- The company's claim is a private, voluntary commitment, not a nationally recognised accounting adjustment

This distinction has significant practical implications. A company that purchases **mitigation contribution units** (non-authorised A6.4ERs) supports real mitigation activity but cannot claim that its purchase reduces the burden on any country's NDC. A company that purchases **authorised A6.4ERs** -- which carry a corresponding adjustment -- is purchasing a credit that the host country has explicitly agreed to remove from its own NDC accounting. In the latter case, the host country must find other reductions to replace what it has sold.

<DeepDive title="Does buying carbon credits help a country meet its NDC?">
Not automatically. Whether a corporate carbon credit purchase helps or hinders NDC achievement depends on the type of credit and the corresponding adjustment rules.

If a company buys an authorised A6.4ER or an authorised ITMO under Article 6.2, the host country has applied a corresponding adjustment. The emission reduction has been formally transferred to the international accounting system. The host country must find additional domestic reductions to replace it. In this case, the corporate purchase does not "help" the host country's NDC -- it actually creates an additional accounting obligation.

If a company buys a mitigation contribution unit (no corresponding adjustment), the emission reduction remains in the host country's inventory and contributes to its NDC progress. The company gets a voluntary offset claim; the host country gets NDC benefit. However, the company cannot claim it is "using an Article 6 credit" in a fully accounting-rigorous sense.

This is not a flaw -- it is a deliberate design feature. Article 6 was designed to ensure that climate accounting at the international level is coherent and double-counting-free, even as it allows different uses for credits at different stages of the authorisation chain.
</DeepDive>

The distinction between sovereign NDC use and corporate voluntary use of carbon credits runs through all the practical questions that arise in Article 6 implementation: who can authorise what, which credits carry corresponding adjustments, and what claims buyers can legitimately make. These are the questions that subsequent modules of this course address in depth.
