## The Long Road to Glasgow

Article 6 of the Paris Agreement was adopted in December 2015, but it contained only the broadest outlines of how international carbon cooperation would actually work. The specific rules -- the operating manual for Article 6.2 -- needed to be negotiated separately. It took six years, two failed COP attempts, and a global pandemic to get them done.

**2015 (COP21, Paris):** The Paris Agreement is adopted. Article 6.2 establishes the concept of ITMOs and cooperative approaches, but leaves all operational details to future decisions. Parties simply agree to "pursue voluntary cooperation" and "promote sustainable development" and "ensure environmental integrity" -- aspirations without mechanics.

**2016--2018:** Technical work begins in earnest. The UNFCCC's Subsidiary Body for Scientific and Technological Advice (SBSTA) leads negotiations. Key fault lines emerge immediately: How should corresponding adjustments be structured? Should they apply to non-authorised ITMOs? What level of reporting detail is required? Can countries use pre-2020 credits? How are carryover Kyoto units to be handled?

**2018 (COP24, Katowice):** The "Katowice Climate Package" is adopted, completing the Paris Rulebook -- except for Article 6. Negotiators could not agree. The text is bracketed and deferred to COP25.

**2019 (COP25, Madrid):** Another failure. The central disagreement is between countries (led by Brazil) that want to allow the "carry-over" of surplus Kyoto-era units into the new Article 6 system, and those (led by the EU and small island states) that argue this would flood the market with cheap, low-ambition credits and undermine the Paris Agreement's goals. The session ends without agreement, becoming one of the most acrimonious COP negotiations in recent memory.

**2020:** The COVID-19 pandemic postpones COP26 by a year.

**2021 (COP26, Glasgow):** Under intense pressure after six years of failure, negotiators finally reach agreement. Decision 2/CMA.3 -- the **Glasgow Rulebook** for Article 6.2 -- is adopted, along with companion decisions for Article 6.4 and 6.8. The Kyoto carry-over question is resolved by prohibiting the use of legacy credits from the CDM in the new system (with limited exceptions for credits issued after January 2013 under tightly constrained conditions).

<HighlightBox>
The Glasgow Rulebook was not perfect -- negotiators explicitly acknowledged that some questions remained unresolved. But it was the first time countries had agreed on a comprehensive operational framework for international carbon markets under the Paris Agreement, and that agreement mattered enormously for market confidence.
</HighlightBox>

## What the Glasgow Rulebook Requires

Decision 2/CMA.3, along with its annexes and accompanying guidance documents, established a comprehensive set of requirements for countries participating in Article 6.2 cooperative approaches. The main pillars are:

### 1. Authorization

Countries wishing to export ITMOs must issue a formal **authorisation** for each transfer. The authorisation is the government's official declaration that:

- The specified ITMOs are approved for use toward the acquiring country's NDC (or other specified purpose such as CORSIA).
- The host country will apply a corresponding adjustment.
- The underlying emission reductions meet the host country's own quality criteria.

Authorisations must be recorded and reported. They cannot be made verbally or informally -- the Rulebook requires written documentation that can be reviewed and verified. Countries are free to design their own authorisation procedures, but the output -- the authorisation letter or equivalent -- must contain specific information: the quantity of ITMOs, the vintage year(s), the metric, the sector, the authorized use, and the identity of the acquiring party.

The Rulebook also introduced a key protection for private sector participants: once an authorisation is issued, the host country can modify or revoke it only before the first transfer. After first transfer, the ITMO and its authorisation are irrevocable (a principle further clarified at COP29, discussed below).

### 2. Corresponding Adjustment Requirements

The Glasgow Rulebook codified the corresponding adjustment mechanism that Article 6.2 had described in principle but left undefined. The key rules:

- CAs are mandatory for all authorised ITMOs used toward NDC achievement or CORSIA.
- CAs are not required for non-authorised ITMOs or for uses that do not constitute NDC achievement.
- CAs must be reported in the host country's biennial transparency report (BTR).
- CAs must be consistent with the host country's national GHG inventory methodology.

The Rulebook also addressed a subtle but important question: what if an ITMO is used for **voluntary cancellation** (a private company retiring a credit without any country-level NDC claim)? The Glasgow decision left this somewhat ambiguous, noting that corresponding adjustments are not required in such cases -- but this created ongoing debate about whether purely voluntary market retirements without CAs are environmentally credible. COP27 and COP28 added further guidance but did not fully resolve all edge cases.

### 3. Reporting Obligations

Transparency is a cornerstone of the Article 6.2 framework. The Glasgow Rulebook established a three-tier reporting system:

**Initial Report:** A country's first submission when it begins participating in Article 6.2 cooperative approaches. The initial report establishes the baseline: the country's current GHG inventory, its NDC targets, and the accounting framework it will use for tracking ITMOs. It must be submitted before any ITMO transfers begin.

**Annual Information:** Countries must submit annual reports covering all ITMO transactions during the year -- quantities transferred in and out, corresponding adjustments applied, and any changes to authorisations. The annual information must be submitted to the UNFCCC within a specified deadline, typically by April 15 of the following year.

**Biennial Transparency Report (BTR):** Every two years, countries submit a comprehensive transparency report covering all their Paris Agreement obligations, including a detailed accounting table for ITMOs and CAs. The BTR is the primary vehicle through which the international community can assess whether a country's Article 6.2 activities are consistent with its NDC commitments. The first BTRs were due in December 2024.

<ExampleBox>
**Example: Switzerland's reporting obligations**

Switzerland has entered into an Article 6.2 agreement with Thailand. Under the Glasgow Rulebook, Switzerland must:

1. Submit an initial report confirming its NDC baseline and Article 6.2 accounting methodology before the agreement takes effect.
2. Submit annual information each year reporting the quantity of ITMOs received from Thailand, the vintage years, and the corresponding adjustments Switzerland has recorded.
3. Include a full Article 6.2 accounting table in its Biennial Transparency Report every two years, showing cumulative ITMO receipts and how they contribute to NDC achievement.

Thailand, as the host country, must similarly report the ITMOs it has authorised and transferred, the CAs it has applied to its own inventory, and confirmation that the underlying projects continue to meet quality standards.
</ExampleBox>

### 4. Review Process

The Glasgow Rulebook established a technical expert review (TER) process for Article 6.2 reporting. Expert teams -- drawn from the UNFCCC's roster of trained reviewers -- examine each country's annual information and BTR to assess whether:

- The accounting is consistent with the Rulebook requirements.
- Corresponding adjustments have been correctly applied.
- The reported ITMO quantities are consistent with registry records.
- The underlying authorisations are properly documented.

If reviewers identify inconsistencies, they flag them in a review report. Countries are then given an opportunity to explain or correct the discrepancy. Persistent non-compliance can result in a country's ITMOs being flagged as potentially not meeting the Rulebook standards -- a reputational and market consequence that is intended to incentivise good practice.

### 5. Registry Requirements

The Glasgow Rulebook does not require a single centralised registry for all ITMOs. Countries have flexibility to use:

- Their own **national registry** (many larger countries operate one).
- A **third-party registry** (such as those operated by Verra, Gold Standard, or other standards bodies, adapted for Article 6.2 purposes).
- The **UNFCCC International Registry** (an ITL-style registry operated by the UNFCCC Secretariat for countries that do not have their own).

Whatever registry a country uses, it must be capable of:
- Issuing, holding, transferring, and retiring ITMOs.
- Recording the authorisation status of each unit.
- Connecting to the UNFCCC's centralized reporting infrastructure.
- Preventing the same unit from appearing in more than one account simultaneously.

The UNFCCC International Registry plays a coordination role -- it receives reports from all national and third-party registries and provides a consolidated view of all Article 6.2 transactions. This allows the technical review teams to cross-check national reports against registry data.

## What Glasgow Left Unresolved

Despite its landmark status, the Glasgow Rulebook explicitly deferred several important questions:

**The voluntary cancellation question:** Whether carbon credits retired by private companies in the voluntary market (without a national NDC claim) require corresponding adjustments was not definitively settled. The Rulebook implies no CA is needed, but critics argued this creates a two-tier market where privately-retired credits lack the integrity guarantee of NDC-directed transfers.

**Metrics beyond tCO2e:** The Rulebook confirmed that non-GHG metrics are permissible but left the conversion and accounting guidance for future elaboration.

**Cooperative approaches involving multiple countries or programmes:** Most negotiations assumed bilateral deals between two countries. How to account for multilateral programmes -- where one project spans multiple host countries, or where one acquiring country aggregates ITMOs from many hosts -- was left for future guidance.

**Capacity of developing country DNAs:** The Rulebook acknowledged that many lower-income countries lack the institutional infrastructure to participate on equal terms, and called for enhanced support, but provided limited specifics.

## Post-Glasgow Developments: Sharm el-Sheikh and Dubai

**COP27 (Sharm el-Sheikh, 2022) -- Decision 6/CMA.4:**

At COP27, countries adopted additional guidance on Article 6.2, primarily focused on the voluntary cancellation question and the treatment of ITMOs used for non-NDC purposes. The Sharm el-Sheikh decision confirmed that:

- ITMOs used solely for "voluntary cancellation" by non-state actors do not require CAs -- but this use must be clearly specified in the authorisation, and countries cannot later try to claim the reduction toward their NDC.
- Ghana became the first country to formally authorise ITMO exports under the new framework, demonstrating that the Rulebook was operational.

**COP28 (Dubai, 2023):**

COP28 focused primarily on Article 6.4 (the UN-supervised carbon market), where significant disputes arose over the Supervisory Body's proposed standards for carbon removals and activity methodologies. Article 6.2 negotiations at COP28 were relatively procedural, primarily addressing registry connectivity and reporting templates.

## COP29 (Baku, November 2024) -- The First-Transfer Clarification

COP29 produced one of the most practically significant clarifications to the Glasgow Rulebook since its adoption. The Baku decisions resolved the ambiguity around the timing of corresponding adjustments and introduced the irrevocability principle in clearer terms.

**First-transfer timing:** Countries must apply the corresponding adjustment no later than the time of **first transfer**. The Baku decisions defined first transfer precisely: it is the moment the ITMO moves from the host country's registry or account to any other party's account. Countries may choose to apply the CA at an earlier point (such as at authorisation or at issuance of the underlying credit), but they cannot delay it beyond first transfer.

**Irrevocability:** Once the CA has been applied and the first transfer has occurred, the host country cannot revoke the authorisation or reclaim the accounting benefit. The ITMO is irrevocably the acquiring party's asset from an accounting perspective. The host country's inventory permanently reflects the CA out, regardless of any subsequent changes in domestic policy.

**Why this matters for markets:** Before COP29, there was genuine uncertainty about whether a country could authorise ITMOs, receive payment, and then revoke the authorisation -- effectively claiming the accounting benefit back while keeping the financial consideration. The irrevocability rule removes this uncertainty and was widely welcomed by private sector participants as a necessary condition for scaling up investment in Article 6.2 projects.

<HighlightBox>
COP29 did not create new rules so much as it hardened and clarified the existing ones. The first-transfer and irrevocability clarifications are particularly important for financial institutions and project developers who need legal certainty before committing capital to Article 6.2 projects.
</HighlightBox>

## The Glasgow Rulebook in Context

It is worth stepping back to consider what the Glasgow Rulebook actually achieved. After six years of failed negotiations, it delivered:

- A legally agreed framework for authorisation, CAs, reporting, review, and registries.
- A resolution of the Kyoto carryover question that had blocked agreement since 2018.
- An operational system that early movers like Switzerland and Ghana could immediately begin using.
- A foundation that subsequent COPs have been progressively elaborating and strengthening.

What it did not deliver was a perfect system. The Rulebook relies heavily on national self-reporting and national inventory quality. Countries with weak GHG inventories, limited DNA capacity, or political incentives to game the system can still do so without the international community being able to easily detect it. The technical review process helps but is not a substitute for robust domestic systems.

The years since Glasgow have seen a gradual but real operationalisation of Article 6.2. Bilateral agreements have been signed between Switzerland and several Asian countries, Japan has been particularly active in pursuing deals through its Joint Crediting Mechanism framework, and a growing number of developing countries have established or are establishing their DNAs and national registries. The framework is working -- imperfectly, as all international systems do -- but working.

<DeepDive title="How does the Glasgow Rulebook relate to the Joint Crediting Mechanism?">
Japan's Joint Crediting Mechanism (JCM) pre-dates the Paris Agreement. Established around 2013, the JCM is a bilateral programme through which Japan co-finances low-carbon projects in partner countries and receives a share of the resulting emission reductions. As of 2024, Japan had JCM agreements with 29 countries.

After Glasgow, Japan moved to bring the JCM into alignment with the Article 6.2 Rulebook -- applying corresponding adjustments to JCM credits used toward Japan's NDC. This transition illustrates how the Glasgow framework is reshaping existing bilateral programmes: activities that pre-date the Rulebook are being restructured to meet the new accounting standards. Countries that do not make this transition face increasing questions about the credibility of their climate accounting.
</DeepDive>

The Glasgow Rulebook for Article 6.2 represents the international community's best current answer to the question of how countries can cooperate on emissions reductions without creating accounting fictions. Whether that answer proves sufficient to support the scale of international climate finance needed -- trillions of dollars annually -- remains the defining challenge of the Article 6 system as it enters its operational phase.
