# Ecodesign for Sustainable Products Regulation (EU) 2024/1781

## Overview
Regulation (EU) 2024/1781, adopted June 13, 2024, establishes a comprehensive framework for setting ecodesign requirements applicable to physical goods placed on the EU market. It replaces Directive 2009/125/EC and extends ecodesign principles beyond energy-related products to cover broader sustainability aspects.

## Core Scope

The regulation applies to "all physical goods placed on the market or put into service," including components and intermediate products. Digital content integral to physical products falls within scope.

Key exclusions:
- Food and feed
- Medicinal and veterinary products
- Living organisms
- Vehicles covered under existing sector-specific regulations

## Primary Regulatory Tools

Performance Requirements establish minimum/maximum performance levels or prohibit specific technical solutions. Examples include:
- Energy consumption limits
- Recycled content minimums
- Restrictions on substances hindering circulability

Information Requirements mandate disclosure of environmental performance data. These drive market transformation by enabling informed purchasing decisions and comparative product assessment.

## Digital Product Passport (DPP)

The regulation mandates digital product passports as a key information delivery mechanism. Essential features include:
- Decentralized management by economic operators
- Unique product identifiers linked to a centralized registry
- Differentiated access rights for various stakeholders
- Data carrier integration (QR codes, watermarks) on products
- Backup copies maintained by independent service providers
- Registry managed by the European Commission for enforcement purposes

## Product Durability and Repairability

Requirements address product longevity through:
- Durability performance standards
- Repairability scoring using harmonized methodologies
- Spare parts availability obligations
- Disassembly accessibility requirements
- Repair information disclosure

These measures combat "premature obsolescence practices" contributing to excessive waste generation.

## Hazardous Substances Management

The regulation enables restrictions on substances that hinder product reusability or recyclability. The Commission identifies problematic substances during impact assessments, considering:
- Complications for product reuse or recycling
- Negative effects on recycled material properties
- Cross-product applicability patterns

## Unsold Product Destruction Prevention

The regulation prohibits economic operators from destroying unsold consumer apparel, clothing accessories, and footwear.
- Medium-sized enterprises face six-year transition periods
- Transparency obligations require disclosure of destroyed product quantities
- Derogations may apply for health and safety concerns
- Micro and small enterprises receive exemptions

## Implementation Framework

Working Plans: The Commission adopts multi-year working plans prioritizing product groups based on:
- Climate impact potential
- Environmental benefits
- Cost proportionality

Priority categories for initial implementation:
- Iron, steel, aluminum
- Textiles
- Furniture
- Tires
- Detergents, paints, chemicals
- Information technology products
- Cement (delegated acts: December 31, 2028 to January 1, 2030)

## Ecodesign Forum

A stakeholder consultation body including:
- Member State experts
- Industry representatives (including SMEs)
- Trade unions
- Consumer organizations
- Researchers

## Enforcement and Governance

- Customs authorities access DPP registry to verify product compliance at borders
- Technical specifications through delegated acts
- Alignment with international standards for interoperability
- Member States provide SME support through guidance, training, and technical assistance

## Relationship to Existing Law

The regulation complements rather than replaces existing EU frameworks on:
- Chemicals (REACH, CLP)
- Food safety
- Waste management
- Energy labeling

## Key Innovation: From Energy to Sustainability

The original Ecodesign Directive 2009/125/EC focused only on energy-related products. ESPR 2024 transforms this into a broad sustainability framework covering:
- Durability and reliability
- Reusability
- Upgradability
- Repairability
- Maintenance and refurbishment possibilities
- Presence of hazardous substances
- Energy and resource efficiency
- Recycled content
- Remanufacturing and recycling possibilities
- Carbon and environmental footprints

This shift represents the EU's commitment to making sustainable products the norm in the EU market.
