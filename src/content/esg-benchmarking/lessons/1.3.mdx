With individual key issue scores calculated for each pillar, MSCI then combines these scores into a single company-level number, adjusts it relative to the industry peer group, and maps it to a letter rating. This lesson walks through that final calculation: the Weighted Average Key Issue Score, the industry adjustment, and how the result maps to the AAA-CCC scale.

## The Weighted Average Key Issue Score (WAKIS)

The Weighted Average Key Issue Score, referred to as the WAKIS, is the weighted average of all the individual scores that contribute to a company's overall rating. Specifically, it is calculated from:

- All the individual Environmental and Social Key Issue Scores contributing to the rating of the company, and
- The Governance Pillar Score.

Each Environmental and Social Key Issue typically comprises 5% to 30% of the total ESG Rating. The weights for each key issue are determined for each GICS sub-industry based on two factors: the sub-industry's contribution to the negative externality associated with the key issue, and the expected time horizon for the key issue to materialise. A key issue with a high impact that is expected to materialise in the short term (under two years) receives the highest weight. A key issue with a low impact expected to materialise in the long term (five or more years) receives the lowest weight. The framework means that a High-Impact, Short-Term key issue would be weighted three times higher than a Low-Impact, Long-Term key issue.

Key Issue weights undergo a formal review and feedback process at the end of each calendar year. Company-specific key issues and weights are permitted, subject to committee approval, for companies with diversified business models, companies facing controversies, or companies where predefined rules apply.

## How the Governance Pillar Score works differently

The Governance Pillar Score is calculated differently from the Environmental and Social Pillar Scores. Rather than using an exposure-management framework, the Governance Pillar uses a deduction-based model.

The Governance Pillar Score starts at 10. Deductions are then applied based on the sum of deductions derived from Key Metrics included in the Corporate Governance Theme (comprising Ownership and Control, Board, Pay, and Accounting) and the Corporate Behavior Theme (comprising Business Ethics and Tax Transparency).

The Governance Pillar weight in the WAKIS calculation is determined differently from Environmental and Social weights. For Governance, weight-setting is undertaken at the Pillar level, not at the individual key issue level. This is because the deduction-based scoring model in the Governance Pillar means that Key Issue weights within it are not published separately. The Governance Pillar weight is floored at a minimum of 33%. This means that no matter how heavily weighted the Environmental and Social issues are for a given sub-industry, Governance always accounts for at least one-third of the overall WAKIS.

<HighlightBox>
The 33% floor on the Governance weight means that a client with weak governance practices will face a ceiling on their overall rating, regardless of how well they perform on Environmental and Social issues. Strong governance is a prerequisite for a top-tier MSCI rating.
</HighlightBox>

## The Industry-Adjusted Company Score

The WAKIS is not the final score. It is adjusted relative to the industry peer group to produce the Industry-Adjusted Company Score, which sits on a 0-10 scale.

The adjustment works by normalising the WAKIS relative to the ESG Rating industry peer group, based on score ranges set by the benchmark values in the peer set. The benchmark values are determined by the performance of companies at the 5th and 95th percentiles of the peer group. This means the adjustment translates a company's raw WAKIS into a position within the peer group's distribution.

<ExampleBox>
Consider a company with a WAKIS of 6.2, operating in a peer group where the 5th percentile score is 5.0 and the 95th percentile score is 8.0. The peer group range spans 3.0 points (from 5.0 to 8.0). The company sits 1.2 points above the 5th percentile, which is 40% of the way through the range. When this is normalised to the 0-10 scale, the company would receive an Industry-Adjusted Company Score of approximately 4.0. This places the company in the BBB band (Average). The same WAKIS of 6.2 in a peer group with a wider distribution would produce a different Industry-Adjusted Company Score, which is why the adjustment step matters.
</ExampleBox>

The industry minimum and maximum scores used for normalisation are updated according to a defined schedule. There are also defined exceptions to the 95th and 5th percentile boundaries to handle cases where extreme outliers would distort the normalisation.

## Mapping the score to a letter rating

The final Industry-Adjusted Company Score is mapped to a seven-band letter rating using the scale shown in Exhibit 2 of the MSCI methodology. The 0-10 scale is divided into seven equal parts, each corresponding to a letter rating.

<ResponsiveTable>

| Letter Rating | Category | Final Industry-Adjusted Company Score |
|---|---|---|
| AAA | Leader | 8.571 to 10.0 |
| AA | Leader | 7.143 to 8.571 |
| A | Average | 5.714 to 7.143 |
| BBB | Average | 4.286 to 5.714 |
| BB | Average | 2.857 to 4.286 |
| B | Laggard | 1.429 to 2.857 |
| CCC | Laggard | 0.0 to 1.429 |

</ResponsiveTable>

The three categories are:

- **Leaders** (AAA and AA): companies that are leading their industry in managing the most significant ESG risks and opportunities.
- **Average** (A, BBB, and BB): companies with a mixed or satisfactory track record of managing the most significant ESG risks and opportunities relative to peers.
- **Laggards** (B and CCC): companies that are lagging their industry in managing the most significant ESG risks and opportunities.

## The committee review process

The MSCI methodology includes a committee review process for certain cases. After the WAKIS is calculated and the industry adjustment is applied, the result is reviewed in predefined cases before the final letter rating is issued.

This committee review applies in certain predefined circumstances and is intended to ensure that the quantitative output reflects the most current information available at the last ESG Rating action date. The Company ESG Rating is derived from the final Industry-Adjusted Company Score, based on an assessment of the underlying data available at that date.

<HighlightBox>
From a consulting perspective, the committee review process is relevant when a client has recently undergone a major change in governance, operations, or controversy status. A change that occurred after the last data collection cycle may not yet be reflected in the rating. Understanding the timing of rating updates and when a review might be triggered is important for managing client expectations about when improvements will appear in their published rating.
</HighlightBox>

<DeepDive title="How are supplemental pillar and theme scores calculated?">
MSCI also calculates supplemental scores that do not directly contribute to the overall ESG Rating but provide additional insight. Pillar Scores for the Environmental and Social Pillars are calculated as the weighted average of all Key Issue Scores under each pillar, normalised by the total sum of weights underlying each pillar. Theme Scores for the eight Environmental and Social themes are calculated as the weighted average of Key Issue Scores underlying each theme, normalised by the total sum of weights underlying each theme. Theme Scores range from 0 to 10, with lower scores indicating more severe risk. For the Governance Pillar, a Theme Score is calculated for both the Corporate Governance and Corporate Behavior themes. Percentile rankings are also published for each Governance Theme and Key Issue, both relative to the company's Home Market and relative to the global coverage universe.
</DeepDive>
