The two agencies whose methodologies are most widely used in institutional ESG analysis are MSCI ESG Research and Morningstar Sustainalytics. Both produce ratings that draw on company disclosures and external data, but they are built around fundamentally different questions. Understanding this distinction is essential before you can interpret ratings for a client or help them improve their position.

## The core difference: relative vs. absolute

MSCI asks: how well is this company managing its ESG risks compared to the other companies in its industry?

Sustainalytics asks: how much unmanaged ESG risk does this company carry, expressed in absolute terms?

The MSCI ESG Rating is a seven-band letter rating from AAA to CCC. It is an industry-relative measure. A company's rating reflects its performance relative to its industry peers, not against some universal standard.

The Sustainalytics ESG Risk Rating is a numerical score representing units of unmanaged ESG risk. It is an absolute measure. Companies are placed into one of five risk categories: negligible, low, medium, high, and severe. A "high" rating means a comparable degree of unmanaged ESG risk regardless of which sub-industry the company belongs to.

<HighlightBox>
The practical implication: MSCI is most useful when you need to help a client understand their standing within their competitive peer group. Sustainalytics is most useful when you need to compare ESG risk across different industries, or when a client's investors use an absolute risk lens rather than a relative ranking.
</HighlightBox>

## The MSCI approach: hierarchy of pillars, themes, and key issues

The core building blocks of the MSCI ESG Ratings methodology are organised in a three-level hierarchy.

At the top are three pillars: Environment, Social, and Governance.

Each pillar is divided into themes. There are 10 themes in total across the three pillars.

Each theme contains specific Key Issues. There are 33 ESG Key Issues in total.

Each company is evaluated on a selection of two to seven Environmental and Social Key Issues, chosen based on the company's industry. All companies are evaluated on the six Governance Key Issues, which are universal. The Environmental and Social Key Issues that apply to a company are determined by which of the 163 GICS sub-industries it belongs to.

For Environmental and Social Key Issues, the evaluation looks at the company's exposure to risks or opportunities and its ability to manage that exposure. These are calculated using a Key Issue Exposure Score and a Key Issue Management Score.

The Governance Pillar works differently. Rather than using an exposure-management framework, it uses a deduction-based model starting from a score of 10. Deductions are applied based on the gap between best practice and the company's actual governance practices.

<AnalogyBox>
Think of the MSCI model as a company report card. The report card is divided into subjects (pillars), then topics within each subject (themes), then individual questions within each topic (key issues). Each student takes a different subset of questions depending on their course, but every student answers the same compulsory governance questions.
</AnalogyBox>

## The Sustainalytics approach: three building blocks

Sustainalytics organises its methodology around three building blocks that together determine a company's overall ESG Risk Rating.

**Building Block 1: Material ESG Issues (MEIs).** These are the core of the rating. MEIs are focused on a topic, or set of related topics, that require a common set of management initiatives or a similar type of oversight. For example, the topics of employee recruitment, development, diversity, engagement, and labor relations are all encompassed by the material ESG issue of Human Capital, because they all require Human Resources initiatives and oversight. There are 22 material ESG issues used for new company assessments across all sub-industries.

**Building Block 2: Corporate Governance and Stakeholder Governance.** These are treated as baseline issues that apply to all companies. Corporate Governance is a system of rules and practices that help ensure the interests of owners and managers are aligned and promote transparency and accountability amongst management and shareholders. Stakeholder Governance considers perspectives and interests of various stakeholders to identify and mitigate potential reputational and financial risks. Both baseline issues are considered material because poor governance poses material risks for companies.

**Building Block 3: Systemic ESG Issues and Idiosyncratic Issues.** Systemic ESG Issues capture the material risk of unpredictable, large-scale events affecting many companies across a multitude of ESG issues. They are assessed through Systemic Event Indicators (SEIs) and are largely outside a company's control. Idiosyncratic Issues are triggered by company-specific events that are atypical for the company's business model and therefore not part of its standard material ESG issues.

## Side-by-side comparison

<ResponsiveTable>

| Feature | MSCI ESG Ratings | Sustainalytics ESG Risk Ratings |
|---|---|---|
| Output type | Letter rating (AAA to CCC) | Numerical score (unmanaged risk) |
| Scale | Seven-band, industry-relative | Open-ended, absolute (five risk categories) |
| What it measures | How well a company manages its ESG risks vs. peers | How much unmanaged ESG risk a company carries |
| Peer comparison | Relative: always compared within industry | Absolute: comparable across industries |
| Key structural elements | 3 pillars, 10 themes, 33 key issues | 3 building blocks: MEIs, Corporate/Stakeholder Governance, Systemic/Idiosyncratic Issues |
| Governance treatment | Universal key issues (deduction-based from 10) | Baseline material issues (Corporate Governance and Stakeholder Governance) |
| Industry classification | GICS sub-industry (163 sub-industries) | Sub-industry level |
| Key issue selection | 2-7 E/S key issues per company, set by GICS sub-industry | MEIs assessed at sub-industry level, can be removed at company level |

</ResponsiveTable>

## Which approach to use in a consulting context

In practice, your choice depends on the client's situation and audience.

If a client's institutional shareholders are using MSCI ratings for portfolio screening or engagement, you need to work within the MSCI framework. The MSCI rating will show how the company ranks within its industry, and your recommendations will focus on improving management scores on the key issues that are most heavily weighted for that sub-industry.

If a client is seeking to attract capital from investors who compare ESG risk across a diversified portfolio spanning multiple industries, or if the client operates in a conglomerate structure spanning several industries, the Sustainalytics absolute risk score is more relevant. It allows for cross-industry comparability.

In many engagements you will need to work with both. Understanding both methodologies allows you to identify whether a performance gap shows up in both systems or only one, which tells you something about whether the gap is driven by industry-relative factors or absolute risk levels.

<HighlightBox>
Start every benchmarking engagement by confirming which agency's data the client's key investors and lenders actually use. Do not assume both are equally relevant in every situation.
</HighlightBox>
