When you benchmark a client company on environmental performance, you are comparing its emissions, energy use, water management, waste practices, and land use against a defined group of industry peers. Each rating framework assigns specific key issues and material ESG issues (MEIs) to measure these areas. This lesson maps the core environmental KPIs, explains what each measures, and shows how MSCI and Sustainalytics use them in their assessments.

## Carbon and Climate

### What the KPIs measure

The most widely used carbon KPIs in peer benchmarking fall into two categories: absolute figures and intensity ratios.

**Absolute GHG emissions** measure the total volume of greenhouse gases a company releases, typically in metric tonnes of CO2 equivalent (tCO2e). Scope 1 covers direct emissions from owned or controlled sources. Scope 2 covers indirect emissions from purchased electricity, heat, or steam. Together, Scope 1 and Scope 2 give a picture of what a company emits directly from its operations.

**Carbon intensity** expresses emissions relative to a business output, most commonly revenue or units of production. Intensity normalises for company size, making it possible to compare a large and a small company in the same industry on an equal footing. A company may have rising absolute emissions while its intensity is falling if it is growing revenue faster than its emissions.

**Energy consumption** covers total energy used across operations, usually in gigajoules (GJ) or megawatt-hours (MWh). The share of that total coming from renewable sources is tracked separately and signals the pace of transition away from fossil fuels.

**Carbon reduction targets** show whether a company has committed to reducing emissions over a defined time horizon and whether those targets are aligned with science-based pathways.

### How MSCI uses these KPIs

In the MSCI ESG Ratings model, the Environmental Pillar is organised into four Themes: Climate Change, Natural Capital, Pollution and Waste, and Environmental Opportunities. Carbon and energy metrics feed primarily into the Climate Change Theme.

The **Carbon Emissions** key issue sits within the Climate Change Theme. MSCI assesses each GICS sub-industry on the carbon intensity of its typical business activities. A company's Business Segment Exposure Score on this key issue is derived from the carbon emissions generated per unit of revenue by its business segments, using data such as direct GHG emissions intensity from sources including the Comprehensive Environmental Data Archive (CEDA), Eurostat, IEA projections, and company disclosures (as shown in Appendix 2 of the MSCI methodology). Industries with high average Carbon Emissions Business Exposure Scores, such as construction materials, integrated oil and gas, and independent power producers, receive Carbon Emissions as a weighted key issue. Industries where the average exposure score falls below the threshold do not carry this key issue in their rating.

Under MSCI's risk management assessment, carbon-related performance indicators are evaluated under Strategy and Governance (policy and target commitments), Initiatives and Programs (scope and strength of emissions-reduction programs), and Performance (track record on actual emissions and intensity trends benchmarked against industry peers).

### How Sustainalytics uses these KPIs

Sustainalytics identifies **Carbon - Own Operations** as a Material ESG Issue (MEI) covering GHG emissions from a company's direct operations. The Sustainalytics model assesses this through management indicators (policies, management systems, certifications scored 0 to 100) and outcome-focused indicators measuring performance in quantitative terms, for example CO2 emissions or CO2 intensity. The management score for a company is derived from these indicators, and the unmanaged risk score reflects how much of the company's carbon exposure remains unaddressed.

Sustainalytics also identifies **Carbon - Products and Services** as a separate MEI. This covers emissions associated with the use of a company's products, which is most relevant for sectors such as automotive manufacturers, oil and gas producers, and electric utilities.

<HighlightBox>
For a benchmarking engagement, request Scope 1 and Scope 2 absolute emissions data alongside carbon intensity per unit of revenue from the client and each peer. Check whether each company's carbon targets have been independently verified. Non-disclosure on emissions should be treated as a data gap, not zero emissions.
</HighlightBox>

## Water

### What the KPIs measure

**Water withdrawal** measures the total volume of fresh water drawn from all sources, including surface water, groundwater, and municipal supplies, typically in megalitres (ML) or cubic metres (m3).

**Water consumption** is the portion of water withdrawn that is not returned to the source, either because it evaporates, is incorporated into a product, or is discharged to a different watershed.

**Water intensity** expresses water use relative to a unit of output such as revenue or production volume, enabling peer comparison regardless of company size.

**Water stress exposure** reflects the geographic dimension: a company operating in a highly water-stressed basin faces greater risk from the same volume of withdrawals than a company in a water-abundant region.

### How MSCI uses these KPIs

The **Water Stress** key issue sits within the Natural Capital Theme of the MSCI Environmental Pillar. MSCI evaluates exposure to this key issue partly through geographic analysis: the Geographic Exposure Score adjusts upward for companies with significant operations or revenue in highly water-stressed regions. MSCI's methodology notes that water stress is added as a company-specific key issue in certain cases, such as casinos operating hotels in water-stressed regions where more than 20% of revenue comes from hotel operations in highly water-stressed areas. For these companies, the Water Stress key issue carries weight in their MSCI rating regardless of the sub-industry default.

Risk management on Water Stress is assessed across strategy (water reduction commitments and policies), programs (water recycling and efficiency initiatives), and performance (actual water intensity trends relative to peers). MSCI uses targets that companies commit to on reducing water consumption as an example of where ESG analytical personnel compare the strength of companies' commitments.

### How Sustainalytics uses these KPIs

Sustainalytics identifies **Water Use - Own Operations** as an MEI. The risk drivers for this issue include the volume and nature of water use in a company's production process and the water intensity of operations. Subindustry exposure scores on this MEI reflect how dependent a given subindustry's business model is on large volumes of water. Companies in agriculture, beverages, semiconductors, and mining tend to carry higher exposure scores on this MEI.

Management indicators for Water Use - Own Operations assess whether the company has a water policy, water management systems, reduction targets, and whether its performance on water intensity is improving relative to its peers.

<AnalogyBox>
Think of water stress exposure as a multiplier. Two companies with identical water intensity scores can face very different actual risk depending on where they operate. A semiconductor fabrication plant in a water-abundant region and one in an arid region may look identical in a simple water intensity table but face very different risks. The geographic layer in MSCI and the beta indicators in Sustainalytics both exist to capture this difference.
</AnalogyBox>

## Waste and Pollution

### What the KPIs measure

**Hazardous waste generated** measures the volume of waste classified as hazardous under applicable regulations, in metric tonnes. This is distinct from total waste, which includes all waste streams.

**Total waste** captures all waste generated by operations, including materials sent to landfill, incinerated, or recovered.

**Recycling and recovery rate** expresses the share of total waste that is recycled, composted, or otherwise diverted from disposal.

**Toxic emissions** cover releases of regulated pollutants to air, water, and land, including substances regulated under emissions inventories.

### How MSCI uses these KPIs

The **Toxic Emissions and Waste** key issue sits within the Pollution and Waste Theme of the MSCI Environmental Pillar. MSCI assesses this key issue for industries where operations generate significant volumes of toxic substances or hazardous waste, including chemicals, metals and mining, oil and gas, and utilities. Business Exposure Scores on this key issue are derived from the average levels of toxic emissions and hazardous waste generation associated with a company's business activities.

MSCI also maintains a separate **Electronic Waste** key issue within the Pollution and Waste Theme. This is relevant to industries such as consumer electronics, IT hardware, and household appliances, where the end-of-life disposal of products creates material environmental liability.

The **Packaging Material and Waste** key issue covers industries where product packaging represents a significant share of the material waste generated.

### How Sustainalytics uses these KPIs

Sustainalytics identifies **Emissions, Effluents and Waste** as an MEI. This issue encompasses releases of pollutants to air, water, and land, as well as the generation and disposal of waste streams. The common management thread is ensuring that emissions are controlled and waste is managed through proper disposal, recycling, and reduction initiatives. For industries with high pollution intensity, such as chemicals, metals, and paper production, this MEI typically carries a high subindustry exposure score.

## Biodiversity

### What the KPIs measure

**Land use** measures the area of land directly disturbed, converted, or occupied by a company's operations, typically in hectares.

**Biodiversity impacts** are assessed qualitatively and quantitatively, including whether operations overlap with protected areas, critical habitats, or areas recognised by bodies such as UNESCO.

### How MSCI uses these KPIs

The **Biodiversity and Land Use** key issue sits within the Natural Capital Theme. MSCI evaluates this key issue for industries with significant land footprints, particularly mining, oil and gas, forestry, and utilities with hydroelectric operations. The methodology notes that utilities with significant hydro operations can receive Biodiversity and Land Use as a company-specific key issue if they have a high absolute capacity (above a defined threshold of megawatts) and operate in biodiversity hotspots. MSCI's example is a Brazilian utility with total hydro capacity above 35,000 MW, representing approximately 85% of capacity, operating in biodiversity hotspots.

For companies assessed on this key issue, risk management evaluation covers whether the company has policies on biodiversity protection, what programs it has to minimise disturbance from operations, and what its track record is. A specific metric cited by MSCI for the Biodiversity and Land Use key issue is: "To what extent do companies minimize disturbances from operations?"

<DeepDive title="How MSCI determines whether Carbon Emissions is a key issue for an industry">
MSCI ESG Research ranks all 163 GICS sub-industries by the average Carbon Emissions Business Exposure Score of the companies in each sub-industry. An industry is proposed for addition of the Carbon Emissions key issue when the size of the externality (measured by the average Business Exposure Score) is at or above the 80th percentile of all sub-industries and the average Business Exposure Segment Risk Exposure score is greater than or equal to 5.0. An industry is proposed for removal when the average score falls to or below the 70th percentile and the score is less than or equal to 3.3. Changes go through a 30-day client consultation period in Q4 of each calendar year before taking effect.
</DeepDive>
