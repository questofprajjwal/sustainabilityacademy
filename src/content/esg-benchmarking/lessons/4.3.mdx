The governance pillar differs from the environmental and social pillars in a fundamental way. Environmental and social risks are industry-specific: certain key issues only matter for certain types of companies. Governance risks, by contrast, apply to all companies. MSCI applies the Governance Pillar universally: all companies in all industries are evaluated on the six governance key issues across two Themes. Sustainalytics similarly treats Corporate Governance and Stakeholder Governance as baseline issues assessed for all companies.

This lesson maps the governance KPIs you will use in a benchmarking engagement, explains how MSCI and Sustainalytics assess them, and identifies the specific key issues and scoring approaches that drive the results.

## MSCI Governance Structure

In the MSCI model, the Governance Pillar is divided into two Themes: Corporate Governance and Corporate Behavior.

The **Corporate Governance** Theme contains four Key Issues: Ownership and Control, Board, Pay, and Accounting.

The **Corporate Behavior** Theme contains two Key Issues: Business Ethics and Tax Transparency.

A deduction-based scoring model is applied across all governance key issues. Each company starts from a perfect score of 10. Points are deducted based on the triggering of Key Metrics. A Key Metric is a pass/fail evaluation of a specific aspect of a company's governance risk profile. When a metric result is a failure, the Key Metric is "flagged" and a score deduction is applied.

The maximum points value for governance is 128, split across the two Themes: Corporate Governance has a maximum of 100 (Ownership and Control: 29, Board: 58, Pay: 22, Accounting: 17) and Corporate Behavior has a maximum of 50 (Business Ethics: 49, Tax Transparency: 10).

## Board Structure

### What the KPIs measure

**Board independence percentage** measures the share of board members who are classified as independent from management and controlling shareholders. MSCI's methodology states that the independence of directors is assessed based on a standardised set of evaluation criteria applied globally, offering more comparability across boards than using local market definitions alone.

**Board diversity** is measured along two dimensions: gender (percentage of women on the board) and skills or expertise relevant to the company's business.

**Board size** is reported as the total number of directors. Very large or very small boards can signal governance risk.

**Separation of CEO and Chair roles** is a binary KPI: are the chief executive and the chairperson of the board two different people?

**Audit committee independence** measures whether all members of the audit committee meet the independence criteria applied in the governance assessment.

### How MSCI uses these KPIs

The **Board** key issue sits within the Corporate Governance Theme. It is the most heavily weighted individual key issue in the governance model, with a maximum points value of 58 out of the Corporate Governance maximum of 100. Key Metrics under this key issue include assessments of director independence, board size, the separation of the CEO and Chair roles, audit committee composition, and other specific board practices. Where board members are not disclosed by the company, no Corporate Governance Theme Score is published, which reflects the central importance of the board assessment.

GRI 2: General Disclosures 2021 requires organisations to describe the composition of the highest governance body by executive and non-executive members, independence, tenure, gender, under-represented social groups, and competencies relevant to the impacts of the organisation (Disclosure 2-9). This disclosure provides the underlying data that both MSCI and Sustainalytics use to assess board composition.

### How Sustainalytics uses these KPIs

Sustainalytics assesses **Corporate Governance** as a baseline Material ESG Issue for all publicly traded companies. Corporate Governance is described as a system of rules and practices that help ensure the interests of owners and managers are aligned and promote transparency and accountability among management and shareholders. For publicly traded companies, Sustainalytics assigns a relatively higher Corporate Governance exposure score of 7 (on a scale of 2 to 10) due to the typically more complex management structures, greater regulatory scrutiny, and higher cost of operation compared with privately owned companies.

## Executive Pay

### What the KPIs measure

**CEO pay ratio** compares the total annual compensation of the chief executive to the median total annual compensation of all employees. GRI 2: General Disclosures 2021 requires disclosure of the annual total compensation ratio at Disclosure 2-21, which defines it as the ratio of the annual total compensation of the highest-paid individual to the median annual total compensation of all employees.

**Ratio of fixed to variable pay** describes the structure of executive remuneration. A very high proportion of variable pay relative to fixed salary can encourage excessive short-term risk-taking.

**ESG-linked remuneration** measures the percentage of executive pay that is explicitly tied to ESG performance targets, for example emissions reductions, safety rates, or diversity metrics.

### How MSCI uses these KPIs

The **Pay** key issue sits within the Corporate Governance Theme, with a maximum points value of 22. MSCI's methodology notes that the evaluation of CEO Pay uses a standardised methodology for calculating total pay figures and a standardised set of rules for peer group construction, both set out in the individual Pay key issue methodology document. Peer-based Key Metrics use standardised data to generate rules-based peer groups for comparison and evaluation.

MSCI treats non-disclosure of executive pay information as an absence of the relevant practice. For example, if a company has not disclosed whether it has adopted a clawback policy that applies to its short- and long-term incentive pay, this non-disclosure is interpreted as the absence of a clawback policy, and the company is flagged under the relevant Key Metric.

<HighlightBox>
When benchmarking executive pay, request total compensation data in a consistent format. Pay can be structured very differently across geographies. MSCI standardises total pay figures using a defined methodology to make comparisons valid. Flag to your client if their pay disclosure is less granular than best-in-class peers, as this itself signals lower transparency.
</HighlightBox>

## Ownership and Accounting

### What the KPIs measure

**Ownership concentration** measures the percentage of shares held by the largest shareholder or by all shareholders holding more than a defined threshold. High concentration can reduce the independence of the board and limit minority shareholder influence.

**Shareholder rights** are assessed qualitatively: does the company's governance structure allow one share, one vote, or do structures such as dual-class shares reduce minority investor rights?

**Accounting quality** is assessed through audit-related indicators, including auditor tenure and the independence of the audit process.

### How MSCI uses these KPIs

The **Ownership and Control** key issue (maximum points value: 29) covers the risks arising from concentrated ownership and ownership structures that may not adequately protect minority shareholders. The **Accounting** key issue (maximum points value: 17) covers audit quality and the risk of accounting misstatement. MSCI's methodology notes that some Key Metrics are relevant only to companies with specific governance characteristics stemming from ownership type, management structure, or other factors.

For the Auditor Tenure Key Metric, where the auditor's tenure is not disclosed, a default non-disclosure deduction is applied at 50% of the deduction that would apply for an auditor having served longer than 20 years.

## Ethics and Compliance

### What the KPIs measure

**Anti-bribery policies** are binary: does the company have a formal policy prohibiting bribery and corruption that applies to employees and agents?

**Code of conduct** covers the existence and scope of a company-wide code of ethical business conduct.

**Whistleblower mechanisms** measure whether the company provides a confidential channel for employees and third parties to report concerns about misconduct.

**Number of corruption controversies** counts instances where the company has been investigated, fined, or prosecuted for bribery or corruption.

### How MSCI uses these KPIs

The **Business Ethics** key issue sits within the Corporate Behavior Theme and has the highest maximum points value of any individual governance key issue at 49. It covers policies, practices, and the track record of a company on anti-bribery and anti-corruption, conflicts of interest, and other ethical conduct matters.

MSCI's Exhibit 18 in the methodology provides a worked example using the "Oversight for Ethics Issues" Key Metric within the Corporate Behavior Theme. A company with no board-level committee and no C-suite oversight of ethics issues, but with only minimum domestic industry norms in place, receives a deduction of -1.0 from its Theme Score. A company with no evidence of any oversight structure receives a deduction of -1.4. This illustrates how the deduction model penalises gaps in governance practice.

GRI 2: General Disclosures 2021 requires organisations to report their policy commitments (Disclosure 2-23), including their commitment to responsible business conduct and how those commitments are made publicly available. Disclosure 2-26 covers mechanisms for seeking advice and raising concerns, which directly maps to whistleblower provisions.

<AnalogyBox>
Think of the MSCI governance deduction model as a credit report that starts at a perfect score. Governance risks are added up as deductions. A company with well-disclosed, clean governance practices ends up close to 10. A company with concentrated ownership, no clawback policies, an audit committee with non-independent members, and past bribery controversies accumulates multiple deductions and ends up with a much lower score. The scoring is transparent in the sense that each Key Metric deduction can be identified.
</AnalogyBox>

## Tax Transparency

### What the KPIs measure

**Effective tax rate** is the actual rate of tax paid by a company as a percentage of pre-tax profit, compared with the statutory rate in its home market.

**Tax haven exposure** measures the extent to which a company's profits, revenues, or assets are located in jurisdictions with very low or zero corporate tax rates.

**Country-by-country reporting** is a disclosure practice in which a company publishes tax payments, revenues, profits, and number of employees for each country in which it operates. It is required for large multinationals in some jurisdictions and is considered a best practice by transparency advocates.

### How MSCI uses these KPIs

The **Tax Transparency** key issue sits within the Corporate Behavior Theme with a maximum points value of 10. It covers the risk that a company's tax practices represent an aggressive or non-compliant approach to tax that could result in reputational damage, regulatory action, or financial liabilities.

### How Sustainalytics uses these KPIs

Sustainalytics assesses **Stakeholder Governance** as a baseline Material ESG Issue for all publicly traded companies. Stakeholder Governance considers the perspectives and interests of various stakeholders to identify and mitigate potential reputational and financial risks. This issue is assessed for all companies at an exposure score of 2 for publicly traded firms, increasing to 5 for privately owned companies where effective management of stakeholder relationships is considered more critical. Tax transparency and responsible tax practices fall within the scope of Stakeholder Governance because aggressive tax practices can trigger reputational and financial risks from regulators, civil society, and customers.

<DeepDive title="Why MSCI floors the Governance Pillar weight at 33%">
MSCI's methodology states that all companies in all industries are evaluated on the key issues under the Governance Pillar, and that governance is universally important and should be evaluated in an integrated way, regardless of industry. The Governance Pillar weight is floored at a minimum value of 33% for every GICS sub-industry. This means that even for a company in an industry where the Environmental and Social Pillars carry heavy weight due to high exposure to those risks, governance always contributes at least one third of the overall ESG Rating. The floor reflects the view that governance failures can affect any company in any industry and should never be treated as immaterial.
</DeepDive>
