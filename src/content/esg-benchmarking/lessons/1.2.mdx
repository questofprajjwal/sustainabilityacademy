Once you know which key issues apply to a client's company, the next step is understanding how each of those issues is actually scored. For Environmental and Social Key Issues, each issue score is built from two separate components: an Exposure Score and a Management Score. Understanding how these work is essential for identifying where a client's score is strong or weak, and what can realistically be done about it.

## The two components of a key issue score

Every Environmental and Social Key Issue is assessed using two components:

**The Key Issue Exposure Score** reflects how exposed the company is to the ESG risk associated with that issue.

**The Key Issue Management Score** reflects how well the company is managing that exposure.

The relationship between these two components is important. Companies facing higher risk exposure must have stronger management practices in place to achieve the same key issue score. Conversely, the model does not penalise companies with minimal risk management strategies if they face low or minimal exposure to the specific risk. A company with very low exposure to a risk does not need to demonstrate extensive management of that risk to score well.

<AnalogyBox>
Exposure is like the size of the target you present. If you operate in a high-hazard industry or geography, you are a large target. Management is like the quality of your shield. Even a strong shield matters less if you are a small target. But if you are a large target, you need a much better shield to achieve the same level of protection. The MSCI model captures this relationship directly.
</AnalogyBox>

## What the Exposure Score reflects

MSCI ESG Research's assessment of risk exposure may draw on three areas of analysis, depending on the key issue:

**Business Risk Exposure** analyses the breakdown of a company's business in terms of revenue, assets, or operations. Business Exposure is a weighted average of the Business Segment Exposure Scores of a company's business segments, adjusted for the proportion of total assets or revenue for which each segment accounts. The Business Segment Exposure Score measures the extent of the externality generated by each business activity. For example, the carbon emissions generated per dollar of revenue by cement production contributes to the Business Segment Exposure Score for the Carbon Emissions Key Issue.

**Geographic Risk Exposure** analyses the breakdown of a company's geographic segments in terms of revenue, assets, or operations. This captures situations where the same business activity carries different risks depending on where it is conducted.

**Company-Level Risk Exposure factors** cover indicators such as number of employees, size, reliance on government contracts, or reliance on outsourced production.

Each company's overall Business Exposure Score is the weighted average of the Segment Exposure Scores of the company's business segments, weighted by the percentage of sales, percentage of assets, or percentage of operations, depending on which measure is most appropriate for that key issue.

<HighlightBox>
A client's Exposure Score is largely determined by what they do and where they do it. It is not something that can be changed quickly. However, a client can influence how their business segments are presented and categorised. Accurate and detailed segment disclosure can ensure the Exposure Score correctly reflects the actual business mix.
</HighlightBox>

## What the Management Score reflects

The Management Score is assessed across four types of indicators:

**Strategy and programs** cover whether the company has policies, management systems, and targets in place to address the key issue.

**Initiatives** cover specific programs or actions the company has taken, such as certifications, audits, or supplier engagement programs.

**Performance metrics** cover quantitative data on outcomes, such as actual emissions levels, injury rates, or water consumption figures, reported relative to industry peers.

**Controversies** cover any negative incidents or events associated with the key issue, which can reduce the Management Score.

<ExampleBox>
For the Carbon Emissions Key Issue, a company might receive a high management score if it has: a board-level climate policy (strategy), a third-party verified GHG emissions reduction target (initiative), reported scope 1 and 2 emissions intensity data that compares favourably against industry peers (performance metric), and no major controversies related to emissions misreporting or significant pollution incidents. A company that discloses none of this would receive a low management score even if its exposure level is moderate.
</ExampleBox>

## How controversies affect management scores

Controversies are relevant events or incidents that suggest a company's management systems may not be adequate. They are drawn from media monitoring, regulatory filings, and other external sources. When a controversy is associated with a particular key issue, it can reduce the Management Score for that issue.

The MSCI methodology is explicit that management measures are generally evaluated through companies' governance structures, policies and targets, quantitative performance metrics, and relevant controversies. Controversies function as evidence against the effectiveness of management systems: if a company claims to have strong policies but has experienced significant incidents, the controversy record limits how highly the management system can be rated.

## Standardisation and benchmarking within the peer group

Both the Exposure Score and Management Score are standardised relative to the industry peer group. This means that what counts as a high or low score is determined by the distribution of scores within the peer group, not against a universal scale.

For management, the standardisation means that a company is judged not just on whether it has management systems, but on how good those systems are relative to its peers. A company in a sector where all peers have strong disclosure and robust policies will need to perform at a high standard just to achieve an average Management Score.

<HighlightBox>
This peer-relative standardisation is why a client cannot simply point to the existence of a policy and expect a good score. The question is whether their management practices are stronger or weaker than what their peers in the same sub-industry are doing and disclosing.
</HighlightBox>

## Variations in disclosure

Disclosure gaps directly affect Management Scores. MSCI's methodology accounts for variations in disclosure, meaning that a company's score can reflect how much it discloses, not just what it does.

A company that does not report data on a relevant performance metric will typically be assessed based on what can be inferred or estimated from other sources, or will receive a lower score on indicators where no evidence of management is available. Companies that disclose more detailed and verifiable information give analysts more to work with and typically score better on management indicators, all else equal.

This is one of the most actionable findings in a benchmarking engagement. Improving disclosure on the indicators that feed into heavily weighted key issues can have a direct and measurable effect on a client's management scores, even before any operational changes are made.
