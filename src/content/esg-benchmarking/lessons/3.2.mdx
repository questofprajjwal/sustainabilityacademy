GRI 2: General Disclosures 2021 is a Universal Standard that applies to all organisations reporting under the GRI Standards, regardless of sector or size. It contains the disclosures that every GRI-reporting organisation must provide. Because these disclosures are common across all companies, they form a standardised baseline that is particularly useful for peer benchmarking.

## What GRI 2 Is

GRI 2: General Disclosures 2021 contains disclosures for organisations to provide information about their reporting practices, activities and workers, governance, strategy, policies and practices, and stakeholder engagement. This information gives insight into the profile and scale of organisations and provides a context for understanding their impacts.

The Standard is effective for reports or other materials published on or after 1 January 2023. It is issued by the Global Sustainability Standards Board (GSSB) and is part of the system of GRI Standards, which is structured into three series:

- **GRI Universal Standards (GRI 1, GRI 2, GRI 3):** Apply to all reporting organisations.
- **GRI Sector Standards:** Provide information about likely material topics for specific sectors.
- **GRI Topic Standards:** Contain disclosures for reporting on specific topics such as emissions, water, or human rights.

An organisation reporting in accordance with the GRI Standards is required to report all disclosures in GRI 2. Reasons for omission are permitted for all disclosures except Disclosures 2-1, 2-2, 2-3, 2-4, and 2-5.

## The Five Sections of GRI 2

GRI 2 is structured into five sections, each containing a different category of disclosures:

**Section 1: The organization and its reporting practices** (Disclosures 2-1 to 2-5): Five disclosures covering organizational details, entities included in sustainability reporting, reporting period and frequency, restatements, and external assurance.

**Section 2: Activities and workers** (Disclosures 2-6 to 2-8): Three disclosures covering activities, value chain and other business relationships, employees, and workers who are not employees.

**Section 3: Governance** (Disclosures 2-9 to 2-21): Thirteen disclosures covering governance structure, composition, roles, and remuneration.

**Section 4: Strategy, policies and practices** (Disclosures 2-22 to 2-28): Seven disclosures covering the organisation's sustainable development strategy and its policies and practices for responsible business conduct.

**Section 5: Stakeholder engagement** (Disclosures 2-29 to 2-30): Two disclosures covering the organisation's stakeholder engagement practices and collective bargaining.

## The Governance Disclosures in Detail

Section 3 of GRI 2 contains thirteen disclosures on governance. These are among the most useful for peer benchmarking because they require every reporting company to disclose comparable information about how its board and senior management are structured, what oversight they exercise, and how they are remunerated.

**Disclosure 2-9: Governance structure and composition**
The organisation must describe its governance structure, including committees of the highest governance body. It must list committees responsible for decision-making on and overseeing the management of the organisation's impacts on the economy, environment, and people. It must also describe the composition of the highest governance body and its committees by: executive and non-executive members, independence, tenure, other significant positions and commitments, gender, under-represented social groups, competencies relevant to the organisation's impacts, and stakeholder representation.

**Disclosure 2-10: Nomination and selection of the highest governance body**
The organisation must describe nomination and selection processes for the highest governance body and its committees. It must describe the criteria used, including whether and how stakeholder views, diversity, independence, and competencies relevant to the organisation's impacts are taken into consideration.

**Disclosure 2-11: Chair of the highest governance body**
The organisation must report whether the chair of the highest governance body is also a senior executive. If so, it must explain their function, the reasons for the arrangement, and how conflicts of interest are prevented and mitigated.

**Disclosure 2-12: Role of the highest governance body in overseeing the management of impacts**
The organisation must describe the role of the highest governance body and senior executives in developing, approving, and updating the organisation's purpose, value or mission statements, strategies, policies, and goals related to sustainable development. It must also describe the role of the highest governance body in overseeing the organisation's due diligence and other processes to identify and manage impacts, including whether and how the highest governance body engages with stakeholders to support these processes and how it considers the outcomes.

**Disclosure 2-13: Delegation of responsibility for managing impacts**
The organisation must describe how the highest governance body delegates responsibility for managing impacts, including whether senior executives have been appointed with responsibility for managing impacts and whether responsibility has been delegated to other employees. It must also describe the process and frequency for senior executives or other employees to report back to the highest governance body.

**Disclosure 2-14: Role of the highest governance body in sustainability reporting**
The organisation must report whether the highest governance body is responsible for reviewing and approving the reported information, including material topics, and if so, describe the process.

**Disclosure 2-15: Conflicts of interest**
The organisation must describe processes for the highest governance body to ensure conflicts of interest are prevented and mitigated. It must report whether conflicts of interest are disclosed to stakeholders, covering cross-board membership, cross-shareholding with suppliers and other stakeholders, existence of controlling shareholders, and related party transactions.

**Disclosure 2-16: Communication of critical concerns**
The organisation must describe whether and how critical concerns are communicated to the highest governance body, and report the total number and nature of critical concerns communicated during the reporting period.

**Disclosure 2-17: Collective knowledge of the highest governance body**
The organisation must report measures taken to advance the collective knowledge, skills, and experience of the highest governance body on sustainable development.

**Disclosure 2-18: Evaluation of the performance of the highest governance body**
The organisation must describe processes for evaluating the performance of the highest governance body in overseeing the management of impacts, report whether evaluations are independent and their frequency, and describe actions taken in response to evaluations.

**Disclosures 2-19, 2-20, and 2-21: Remuneration**
These three disclosures address how board members and senior executives are paid. Disclosure 2-19 requires a description of remuneration policies, including fixed and variable pay, sign-on bonuses, termination payments, clawbacks, and retirement benefits. Critically, it also requires a description of how remuneration policies relate to objectives and performance in relation to the management of the organisation's impacts on the economy, environment, and people. Disclosure 2-20 covers the process to determine remuneration, including whether independent committees oversee this. Disclosure 2-21 requires the organisation to report the annual total compensation ratio: the ratio of the highest-paid individual's compensation to the median compensation for all employees.

<HighlightBox>
For peer benchmarking, Disclosures 2-12 (oversight of impacts), 2-14 (role in sustainability reporting), 2-17 (collective knowledge on sustainability), and 2-19 (remuneration linked to sustainability performance) are the most informative. They reveal whether governance of sustainability is substantive or merely nominal.
</HighlightBox>

## Strategy, Policies and Practices

Section 4 of GRI 2 covers seven disclosures on strategy, policies, and practices. These provide information about an organisation's approach to responsible business conduct.

**Disclosure 2-22: Statement on sustainable development strategy**
A statement from the highest governance body or most senior executive about the relevance of sustainable development to the organisation and its strategy for contributing to sustainable development.

**Disclosure 2-23: Policy commitments**
A description of policy commitments for responsible business conduct, including commitments on human rights, whether the commitments reference authoritative intergovernmental instruments, and how these commitments are communicated to workers, business partners, and other relevant parties.

**Disclosure 2-24: Embedding policy commitments**
A description of how the organisation embeds each of its policy commitments throughout its activities and business relationships, including how it allocates responsibility across different levels of the organisation, integrates commitments into strategies and operational procedures, and provides training.

**Disclosures 2-25 and 2-26:** Cover processes to remediate negative impacts and mechanisms for seeking advice and raising concerns.

**Disclosure 2-27: Compliance with laws and regulations**
The organisation must report the total number of significant instances of non-compliance with laws and regulations during the reporting period.

**Disclosure 2-28: Membership associations**
A list of industry associations, other membership associations, and national or international advocacy organisations in which the organisation participates in a significant role.

## Stakeholder Engagement

Section 5 contains two disclosures.

**Disclosure 2-29: Approach to stakeholder engagement**
The organisation must describe its approach to engaging with stakeholders, including: the categories of stakeholders engaged, the purpose of the engagement, how the organisation ensures the engagement is meaningful, and how the outcomes of engagement inform decisions and activities.

**Disclosure 2-30: Collective bargaining agreements**
The organisation must report the percentage of total employees covered by collective bargaining agreements, broken down by region.

## How GRI 2 Supports Peer Comparison

Because GRI 2 is a universal standard applied consistently across all GRI-reporting companies, it creates a common reporting structure that enables meaningful comparison. When comparing peers, a consultant can use GRI 2 disclosures to assess:

- **Governance quality:** Does the company have a sustainability committee at board level (2-9)? Does the board have relevant sustainability competencies (2-17)? Is the highest governance body responsible for reviewing sustainability reporting (2-14)?

- **Management accountability:** Has the company appointed senior executives with explicit responsibility for managing sustainability impacts (2-13)? How frequently do they report to the board?

- **Pay alignment:** Is executive remuneration linked to sustainability performance (2-19)? This is a particularly strong indicator of whether sustainability is treated as a core strategic priority or as a reporting exercise.

- **Policy substance:** Does the company have publicly available policy commitments (2-23)? Are those commitments embedded throughout the organisation and its supply chain (2-24)?

- **Stakeholder engagement:** How structured is the company's approach to engaging with affected stakeholders (2-29)?

<ExampleBox>
**Using GRI 2 to compare two peers:**

Company A discloses under 2-14 that its highest governance body is responsible for reviewing and approving the sustainability report and all material topics. Under 2-17, it reports that all board members completed sustainability training in the reporting period. Under 2-19, it discloses that 20% of executive variable pay is linked to progress against sustainability targets.

Company B reports under 2-14 that sustainability reporting is the responsibility of the CFO and is not reviewed by the board. Under 2-17, it reports no formal sustainability training for board members. Under 2-19, executive pay has no link to sustainability performance.

Using only GRI 2 disclosures, a consultant can clearly identify that Company A has substantively integrated sustainability into its governance, while Company B has not. This analysis requires no additional data beyond what GRI 2 mandates.
</ExampleBox>

<HighlightBox>
GRI 2 data is only as useful as the quality of what companies disclose. GRI requirements are set out using the word "shall", meaning companies are required to comply. However, the level of detail provided varies significantly in practice. When benchmarking, assess not just whether a company has responded to a disclosure, but the quality and specificity of the response.
</HighlightBox>
