The Sustainalytics ESG Risk Ratings assess materiality through two dimensions: Exposure and Management. This lesson focuses on the first dimension: Exposure. Understanding exposure is essential because it determines how much risk a company faces before accounting for any management response.

## What Exposure Means

Exposure can be considered as a set of ESG-related factors that pose potential economic risks for companies. Another way to think of exposure is as a company's **sensitivity or vulnerability to ESG risks**.

Material ESG Issues and their exposure scores are assessed at the subindustry level and then refined at the company level through **Beta Indicators**. Exposure informs investors about what material ESG risks a company is facing.

## Subindustry Exposure Assessment

The Subindustry Exposure Score assessment begins with identifying the most relevant risk drivers based on an MEI's content, as well as associated data points that best allow Sustainalytics to estimate the materiality of each risk driver at the subindustry level.

Examples of risk drivers include labor and skill intensity for Human Capital, or data intensity and data sensitivity for Data Privacy and Cybersecurity. Individual risk driver assessments are combined to produce a MEI-level quantitative materiality assessment, which forms the basis of the subindustry exposure score assessments.

This process follows three steps, as shown in Exhibit 3 of the methodology:

1. **Quantitative Materiality Assessment:** MEI-level risk drivers and data points to evaluate materiality.
2. **Expert Review:** Review and validation of quantitative outcomes, identification of subindustry-specific risk drivers and adjustments.
3. **Subindustry Exposure Calibration:** Subindustry-level calibration and cross-subindustry checks.

The outcomes are reviewed and calibrated at the subindustry level by sector experts who may adjust outputs based on unique subindustry factors. The final subindustry exposure score ranges from **2 (low exposure) to 10 (high exposure)**, with scores below 2 deemed immaterial.

<HighlightBox>
A subindustry exposure score below 2 means the MEI is considered immaterial for that subindustry and will not appear in the rating at all. Scores of 2 to 10 indicate increasing degrees of exposure, with 10 representing the highest possible exposure to that issue at the subindustry level.
</HighlightBox>

### Corporate Governance and Stakeholder Governance Exposure

Corporate Governance and Stakeholder Governance deviate slightly from the approach taken for MEIs on the exposure dimension. Both issues are assessed for all publicly traded companies and cannot be disabled. Their exposure score across all subindustries is evaluated at 7 for Corporate Governance and 2 for Stakeholder Governance.

For privately owned companies, a typical assessment of Corporate Governance is not suitable as factors like ownership, shareholder rights, and financial reporting are either irrelevant or significantly less important. However, it is crucial for private companies to effectively manage their relationships with stakeholders. To reflect this, the exposure score of Stakeholder Governance is increased to 5 through the application of Betas.

## The Beta Concept: Making the Rating Company-Specific

Betas are a key part of what makes the ESG Risk Ratings company-specific. They reflect the degree to which a company's exposure to a material ESG issue deviates from the average exposure to that issue within its subindustry.

To arrive at a company's exposure score for a particular ESG issue, the subindustry exposure score is multiplied by the company's **Issue Beta**.

<ExampleBox>
**From Exhibit 4 of the Sustainalytics methodology:**

A subindustry exposure score of 6 multiplied by a company's Issue Beta of 1.35 produces a company-level exposure score of 8.1 for that issue.

Company Exposure = Subindustry Exposure x Issue Beta
Company Exposure = 6 x 1.35 = **8.1**

This means the specific company faces higher exposure to this MEI than the average company in its subindustry. A beta below 1.0 would indicate lower-than-average exposure; a beta above 1.0 indicates higher-than-average exposure.
</ExampleBox>

Exhibit 4 in the methodology illustrates this across multiple companies and issues. Companies with the same subindustry exposure score can end up with very different company-level exposure scores depending on their individual beta values.

## Beta Indicators and Beta Signals

Beta indicators have been created for **five distinct thematic areas**:

1. **Product and Production:** Derived Beta indicators and build-for-purpose Beta indicators related to what the company produces and how it produces it.
2. **Financials:** Assessment of relative financial strength.
3. **Events:** Events (relative frequency and severity) and Exceptional Event Adjustment (categories 4 and 5).
4. **Geographic:** Country risk, headquarters, revenues, and assets.
5. **Governance:** Largest shareholder voting power and ownership structure.

<HighlightBox>
The Governance components within Beta Indicators are only applied to Corporate Governance or Stakeholder Governance MEIs. The Subindustry Correction Factor is not applicable to Governance components.
</HighlightBox>

## The Three-Step Beta Calculation

The beta for a company relative to an ESG issue is calculated in a three-stage process, as shown in Exhibit 5 of the methodology.

**Step 1: Beta Signals**
The core of the model is a list of subindustry and MEI-specific Beta indicators. Their assessment constitutes the first step in the process. The outcomes generate the Beta Signals, which are added to the subindustry default beta value of 1.

**Step 2: Subindustry Correction Factor**
A technical correction factor, the Subindustry Correction Factor, is applied to ensure that the average beta within a subindustry is one. This preserves the integrity of the subindustry exposure baseline.

**Step 3: Qualitative Overlay**
A Qualitative Overlay may be applied by analysts when updating a company profile to reflect company-specific factors that are not reflected in the standard model. This step also adjusts for factor biases.

<AnalogyBox>
Think of the beta like an adjustment dial that sits on top of the subindustry average. The subindustry average is the starting point (beta = 1.0). If a company has particular characteristics that make it more exposed to a risk than its peers, the dial turns up, producing a beta above 1.0. If the company is less exposed than its peers, the dial turns down. The result is an exposure score that reflects the company's actual situation, not just the average for its sector.
</AnalogyBox>

The Beta framework is what allows investors and consultants to compare companies within the same subindustry at a granular level, because two companies in the same subindustry can have meaningfully different company-level exposure scores if their business models, geographies, financial positions, or event histories differ.
