When a client asks you to assess their ESG performance relative to their industry, the first question you need to answer is: relative to what, and measured by whom? ESG peer benchmarking is the process of comparing a company's ESG performance against a defined set of comparable companies, using a consistent and structured framework. This lesson explains what that means in practice, why it matters, and what you need to understand before you begin.

## What ESG peer benchmarking is

ESG peer benchmarking uses ESG ratings and scores to compare companies on their environmental, social, and governance risks and performance. Rather than assessing a company in isolation, benchmarking places it within a reference group, typically companies in the same industry or sub-industry, and asks: how does this company perform relative to those peers?

The ratings produced by agencies such as MSCI ESG Research and Morningstar Sustainalytics provide a structured, standardised way to make those comparisons. Each agency has its own methodology, but both produce scores that allow consultants and investors to place a company on a spectrum relative to others.

<HighlightBox>
MSCI ESG Ratings are explicitly described as industry-relative measures. They are determined at the company level on a global seven-band scale from AAA (the highest rating) to CCC (the lowest). These assessments are not absolute. They are intended to be interpreted relative to a company's industry peers.
</HighlightBox>

This is an important starting point. When a client's investor receives an MSCI ESG Rating, that rating does not say the company is good or bad in some universal sense. It says the company is managing its ESG risks better or worse than the other companies in its industry.

## Why consultants do this work

Three types of pressure typically drive a client to commission ESG benchmarking work.

**Investor pressure.** Institutional investors increasingly use ESG ratings when making allocation decisions and engaging with portfolio companies. A client that receives a low rating relative to peers may face investor questions or find themselves excluded from certain funds or indices. Understanding where you stand relative to peers, and why, is the starting point for any response.

**Regulatory requirements.** Disclosure frameworks and reporting regulations increasingly require companies to demonstrate awareness of their ESG risks and how they are managing them. A benchmarking exercise can help a client identify gaps in their management practices relative to what peers are disclosing and doing.

**Operational improvement.** Beyond external pressure, benchmarking reveals where a company's management systems, policies, and performance are weaker than those of competitors. This gives a concrete basis for internal improvement priorities.

## The concept of unmanaged risk

Morningstar Sustainalytics introduces a concept that is especially useful as a frame for benchmarking work: the idea of ESG risk as something that can be measured in a single, comparable unit.

<HighlightBox>
Sustainalytics describes its approach as providing "a single currency for ESG risk." Their ESG Risk Ratings measure the degree to which a company's economic value, specifically its enterprise value, is at risk driven by ESG factors. For each company, unmanaged risk is measured by evaluating a set of material ESG issues based on both the company's exposure to and management of those issues.
</HighlightBox>

The resulting unmanaged risk scores for each issue are summed to provide one score representing the company's overall ESG risk. Because these risk categories are absolute rather than relative, a high-risk assessment reflects a comparable degree of unmanaged ESG risk across all sub-industries covered. This means, for example, that a bank can be directly compared with an oil company on an apples-to-apples basis.

This is a different approach from MSCI's, where the rating is always relative to industry peers. Understanding which frame is more useful depends on what question the client is trying to answer.

## Why industry context matters

MSCI's approach reflects a key insight about ESG risk: not all companies face the same risks. The Environmental and Social Key Issues relevant to a given company are selected based on the company's exposure to potentially material ESG risks, which are driven by industry-specific and market-specific factors.

<AnalogyBox>
Think of it like comparing driving records. If you compare a delivery driver and an office worker, it would be unfair to judge them on the same absolute standard. The delivery driver faces structurally more driving risk simply because of their job. Industry-relative benchmarking adjusts for this. It asks: given the risks your industry faces, how well are you managing them?
</AnalogyBox>

This is why ESG benchmarking is always grounded in industry classification. MSCI uses the Global Industry Classification Standard (GICS) to assign companies to one of 163 sub-industries, and the key issues evaluated for each company depend on that classification. A company in the gold mining sub-industry faces different key issues than a company in the software sub-industry.

## Where to start as a consultant

Before beginning any benchmarking engagement, you need to establish three things.

First, which agencies' ratings are most relevant to your client's investors and stakeholders. If your client's primary institutional shareholders use MSCI data for portfolio decisions, then an MSCI rating gap is the most pressing issue to address. If a client is being reviewed by ESG-focused lenders or bond investors who use Sustainalytics scores, that becomes the priority. The starting point is always understanding whose ratings your client's investors actually look at.

Second, what peer group is being used. Different agencies define peer groups differently, and the composition of the peer group affects where a company lands in the distribution. You need to understand which sub-industry classification applies to your client.

Third, what data is feeding the ratings. Both MSCI and Sustainalytics draw on company financial and sustainability disclosures, government and academic data sets, media searches, and other sources. A company that discloses less will typically receive lower management scores, because analysts cannot assess what is not publicly available. Identifying disclosure gaps is often the most actionable finding from a benchmarking exercise.

<HighlightBox>
A benchmarking project does not start with the scores. It starts with understanding which agency matters to which stakeholder, what industry peer group applies, and where the client's current disclosures fall short.
</HighlightBox>
