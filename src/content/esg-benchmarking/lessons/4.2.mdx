Social KPIs cover how a company manages the people who work for it, the people in its supply chain, and the people who use its products. In a peer benchmarking engagement, the social pillar is often the hardest to standardise because companies define and calculate workforce metrics differently. This lesson maps the core social KPIs, explains how each is measured, and shows how MSCI and Sustainalytics use them.

## Workforce and Human Capital

### What the KPIs measure

**Employee turnover rate** is the percentage of the workforce that voluntarily leaves in a given year. High turnover relative to peers signals difficulty retaining talent, which drives recruitment and training costs.

**Gender diversity in senior roles** measures the percentage of women in executive or senior management positions. It is a common proxy for broader workplace inclusion.

**Pay gap** compares average or median pay across gender or other demographic groups. GRI 2: General Disclosures 2021 (Disclosure 2-7) requires companies to report total employee numbers by gender and by region, which provides a foundation for pay gap analysis.

**Training hours per employee** measures the average number of hours each employee spends on formal training in a year. It signals investment in workforce development.

**Employee engagement score** is derived from internal or third-party surveys and measures how committed and motivated employees are. Not all companies publish this metric.

### How MSCI uses these KPIs

The Social Pillar in the MSCI ESG Ratings model covers four Themes: Human Capital, Product Liability, Stakeholder Opposition, and Social Opportunities. Workforce and human capital KPIs feed into the Human Capital Theme.

The **Labor Management** key issue assesses the relationship between a company and its workforce, including labour practices, union relations, and the risk of strikes or stoppages. MSCI notes that the time horizon for Labor Management can vary by industry: it is treated as a short-term issue in industries where labour disruptions and stoppages are very common, and as a medium-term issue when the relevance is to general worker productivity.

The **Human Capital Development** key issue covers investment in training, skills development, and employee retention. MSCI calculates Key Issue Scores for Carbon Emissions, Water Stress, and Human Capital Development for all rated companies even if these key issues carry no weight in a particular industry's ESG rating, so that the scores can contribute to Theme Scores.

### How Sustainalytics uses these KPIs

Sustainalytics identifies **Human Capital** as a Material ESG Issue. The common management thread behind Human Capital topics is attracting and retaining qualified employees. It encompasses employee recruitment, development, diversity, engagement, and labour relations. Management indicators for this MEI assess policies on equal opportunity and non-discrimination, human resources management systems, training programs, and the company's track record on turnover and diversity metrics.

<HighlightBox>
When benchmarking a client's human capital performance, check whether peer companies are reporting employee data on a consistent basis. GRI 2: General Disclosures 2021 requires organisations to report total employee numbers by gender and by region, the breakdown of permanent, temporary, non-guaranteed hours, full-time, and part-time employees, and to specify whether figures are in head count or full-time equivalent. Inconsistent scope (for example, one company including contractors and another excluding them) makes direct comparison unreliable. Flag these differences clearly in your benchmarking output.
</HighlightBox>

## Health and Safety

### What the KPIs measure

**Total Recordable Incident Rate (TRIR)** measures the number of work-related injuries and illnesses per 200,000 hours worked (the equivalent of 100 full-time workers for one year). It is the standard metric used across most industries to compare safety performance.

**Lost Time Injury Rate (LTIR)** counts only those incidents that result in at least one day away from work, per 200,000 hours worked. It is a subset of TRIR and indicates more serious injuries.

**Fatality rate** measures the number of work-related deaths per a defined number of employees or hours worked. MSCI cites industry fatality statistics from the International Labour Organization (ILO) and health and safety authorities such as OSHA in its key issue exposure scoring.

### How MSCI uses these KPIs

The **Health and Safety** key issue sits within the Human Capital Theme of the Social Pillar. MSCI measures the externality for this key issue based on the extent to which companies' business segments are prone to injuries and fatalities. MSCI's methodology gives the example that for underground coal mines (SIC 1222) the average fatality rate per 1,000 employees is 0.45, compared with 0.13 for surface coal mines (SIC 1221). These figures are converted to 0-10 scores based on the relative ranking of industry intensities. A company's overall Business Exposure Score on Health and Safety is the weighted average of its segment exposure scores, weighted by the percentage of sales, assets, or operations in each segment.

Risk management on Health and Safety is assessed through strategy (safety policy and management systems), programs (safety initiatives and targets), and performance (injury and fatality rates compared with industry peers). MSCI's methodology notes that where lack of disclosure on injury or fatality rates prevents a rigorous performance analysis, the model assigns a score of 3 out of 10 rather than the worst possible score.

### How Sustainalytics uses these KPIs

Sustainalytics identifies **Occupational Health and Safety** as a Material ESG Issue. This MEI is distinct from Human Capital because the common management thread is ensuring the health and safety of employees at their workplace, and the business risks associated with it are different from general Human Capital risks and are managed through a different set of activities. Management indicators for this MEI assess the existence and strength of an occupational health and safety policy, management systems such as ISO 45001 certification, injury rate trends, and involvement in health and safety controversies.

<ExampleBox>
**Example: TRIR calculation**

A company employs 5,000 workers who collectively work 10 million hours in a year. During that year, 25 recordable incidents occur.

TRIR = (25 incidents x 200,000) / 10,000,000 hours = **0.5**

A peer company with a TRIR of 1.2 has more than twice the incident frequency per hour worked. In a benchmarking report, you would present these figures alongside the industry median TRIR so the client can see where it sits relative to the full peer group.
</ExampleBox>

## Supply Chain Labor

### What the KPIs measure

**Percentage of suppliers audited** measures the share of the supply base that has been assessed against labour standards, either directly or through a recognised audit scheme.

**Child labour and forced labour policies** are assessed as policy commitments: does the company have a supplier code of conduct that explicitly prohibits these practices, and does it have a mechanism to verify compliance?

### How MSCI uses these KPIs

The **Supply Chain Labor Standards** key issue sits within the Human Capital Theme of the Social Pillar. MSCI applies this key issue to industries where outsourcing of production creates material risk of labour abuses in the supply chain. The methodology gives the example that Samsung Electronics, which outsources most of its production and has significant brand recognition, receives Supply Chain Labor Standards as a company-specific key issue. MSCI also notes that semiconductor companies reliant on outsourced manufacturing are assessed on this key issue based on the ratio of outsourced to in-house production.

Risk management on Supply Chain Labor Standards is assessed through the existence and scope of a supplier code of conduct, audit programs, remediation procedures, and the company's track record on supply chain labour controversies.

### How Sustainalytics uses these KPIs

Sustainalytics identifies **Human Rights - Supply Chain** as a Material ESG Issue covering the risk of human rights abuses, including forced labour and child labour, occurring through a company's supply chain relationships. This is distinct from the **Human Rights** MEI, which covers the company's own operations. The common management thread behind Human Rights - Supply Chain is ensuring that suppliers meet minimum human rights standards and that the company has due diligence processes to identify and address violations.

## Product and Customer

### What the KPIs measure

**Product safety incidents** count reported cases of product defects, recalls, or regulatory actions related to product safety.

**Data breaches** count confirmed incidents where customer or employee personal data was accessed without authorisation.

**Customer satisfaction** is typically measured through net promoter scores or satisfaction surveys and signals reputational risk from poor product or service quality.

### How MSCI uses these KPIs

Three key issues in the Product Liability Theme are relevant here.

The **Chemical Safety** key issue covers companies whose products contain chemicals that pose health or environmental risks, including consumer products, agricultural chemicals, and pharmaceuticals.

The **Privacy and Data Security** key issue covers companies that collect, store, or process significant volumes of sensitive personal data. MSCI's example is that data intensity and data sensitivity are among the risk drivers for this key issue at the subindustry level, as referenced in Sustainalytics' similar treatment of Data Privacy and Cybersecurity.

The **Product Safety and Quality** key issue covers companies where defective products could cause significant harm to consumers. MSCI's methodology notes that Herbalife (personal products), which sells weight supplements regulated by food safety bodies, was assessed with an added Product Safety and Quality key issue as a company-specific addition.

<DeepDive title="Why MSCI's Health and Safety exposure scoring uses industry fatality data from ILO and OSHA">
MSCI measures the externality for the Health and Safety key issue based on the extent to which companies' business segments are prone to injuries and fatalities. This is not self-reported by companies: it is based on industry statistics from the International Labour Organization and health and safety authorities such as the Occupational Safety and Health Administration. By grounding the exposure score in independent industry-level data, MSCI avoids penalising companies that disclose more injury data than their peers. The performance component of the score then evaluates each company's actual track record relative to those industry benchmarks.
</DeepDive>
