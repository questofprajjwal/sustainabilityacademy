The Morningstar Sustainalytics ESG Risk Ratings are built on a single central idea: measuring the portion of a company's material ESG risk that has not yet been addressed by its management. This lesson introduces the building blocks of that framework and explains why they matter for peer benchmarking work.

## What "Unmanaged Risk" Means

The ESG Risk Ratings measure the degree to which a company's economic value, specifically its enterprise value, is at risk driven by ESG factors. More precisely, the ratings measure the magnitude of a company's **unmanaged ESG risk**.

Unmanaged risk is defined as the portion of material ESG risk that is not yet managed by a company. For each material ESG issue, Sustainalytics evaluates both how exposed a company is to that risk and how well the company is managing it. The residual, the part of exposure that management has not yet addressed, is the unmanaged risk for that issue. These issue-level unmanaged risk scores are then summed to produce one overall score representing the company's total unmanaged ESG risk.

<HighlightBox>
Unmanaged risk is not total ESG risk. It is the slice of material ESG risk that remains after accounting for what the company is already doing to manage it. A company with strong management practices will have a lower unmanaged risk score than a peer with equivalent exposure but weaker practices.
</HighlightBox>

The quantitative score represents units of unmanaged ESG risk. Lower scores mean less unmanaged risk. The scale starts at zero, which means no unmanaged risk. For 95% of companies, the maximum score is below 50. Based on their scores, companies are grouped into five risk categories: negligible, low, medium, high, and severe.

These categories are absolute, meaning that a "high risk" assessment reflects a comparable degree of unmanaged ESG risk across all subindustries covered. A bank can therefore be compared directly with an oil company or any other type of company. This is what Sustainalytics describes as introducing a "single currency" for ESG risk.

<AnalogyBox>
Think of unmanaged risk like the unplugged portion of a leaking pipe. The total leak is the company's exposure to the ESG issue. How much of the pipe the company has plugged is its management response. What is still leaking is the unmanaged risk. Two companies with the same-sized leak but very different repair efforts will have very different unmanaged risk scores.
</AnalogyBox>

## The Three Building Blocks

The ESG Risk Ratings are composed of three building blocks that contribute to a company's overall rating score.

**Building Block 1: Material ESG Issues**

**Building Block 2: Corporate Governance and Stakeholder Governance (baseline issues)**

**Building Block 3: Systemic ESG Issues and Idiosyncratic Issues**

Each building block captures a different dimension of ESG risk. Together, they are intended to cover the full range of ESG risks that can affect a company's enterprise value.

## Building Block 1: Material ESG Issues

The core building block of the ESG Risk Ratings is the **Material ESG Issues (MEIs)**. MEIs are focused on a topic, or set of related topics, that require a common set of management initiatives or a similar type of oversight.

For example, the topics of employee recruitment, development, diversity, engagement, and labor relations are all encompassed by the MEI of Human Capital because they are all employee-related and require Human Resources initiatives and oversight. The common thread behind all Human Capital topics is attracting and retaining qualified employees.

Occupational Health and Safety also concerns employees, but the common thread there is to ensure the health and safety of employees at their workplace. The business risks associated with this are different from general Human Capital risks and are managed through a different set of activities. This is why they are treated as separate MEIs.

The assessment of material ESG issues occurs at the **subindustry level** and is reviewed annually through a comprehensive and structured process. At a company level, Material ESG Issues can be removed from the rating if they are not relevant to the company's business model.

The MEI building block forms the essential core of the methodology. It assumes that ESG issues can influence the economic value of a company in a given subindustry in a fairly predictable manner. The rating is forward-looking in the sense that it identifies these issues based on the typical business model and business environment a company is operating in.

## Building Block 2: Corporate Governance and Stakeholder Governance

The second building block consists of two baseline Material ESG Issues: **Corporate Governance** and **Stakeholder Governance**. These are the foundational element of the ESG Risk Ratings.

**Corporate Governance** is a system of rules and practices that help to ensure the interests of owners and managers are aligned and promote transparency and accountability amongst management and shareholders.

**Stakeholder Governance** considers perspectives and interests of various stakeholders to identify and mitigate potential reputational and financial risks.

Both baseline issues are considered material, reflecting the conviction that poor governance poses material risks for companies. However, in contrast to other MEIs, these risks are not determined by a company's business model. Their materiality assessment is not affected by a company's subindustry affiliation.

Instead, the exposure and therefore relative importance of Corporate Governance and Stakeholder Governance is largely dependent on whether the company is traded publicly on exchanges or if it is privately owned. For companies that are publicly traded, Sustainalytics assigns relatively higher exposure scores due to the typically more complex management structures, greater degree of regulatory scrutiny, and higher cost of operation compared to privately owned corporations.

Their exposure scores across all subindustries are evaluated at 7 for Corporate Governance and 2 for Stakeholder Governance for publicly traded companies.

<HighlightBox>
The key distinction is this: MEIs apply to companies based on their subindustry, and their materiality varies by sector. Corporate Governance and Stakeholder Governance are baseline issues that apply to all companies regardless of subindustry. They cannot be disabled in the rating for publicly traded companies.
</HighlightBox>

## Building Block 3: Systemic ESG Issues and Idiosyncratic Issues

**Systemic ESG Issues** capture the material risk of sea-change events: unpredictable in nature, and affecting a large set of companies across a multitude of ESG issues. Systemic events are assessed through the Systemic Event Indicator (SEI). This type of issue is not caused by any company action and their management is almost completely out of a company's control.

For example, the SEI "Business Resilience Risk Due to Ukraine Conflict" assesses the level of business resilience risk relating to the Russia-Ukraine war that companies face. It is based on the percentage of assets held in Ukraine. This is an external shock that falls entirely outside the logic of normal subindustry-specific material ESG issues.

**Idiosyncratic Issues** are triggered by company-specific events which are atypical for a company's business model, and therefore not part of its material ESG issues. For example, a human rights scandal is atypical for many subindustries like Heavy Machinery and Trucks or Electronics Equipment. Idiosyncratic issues become material only for the specific company in question, not for the entire subindustry that the company is part of. The issue represents a failure in a company's management.

<DeepDive title="How do Systemic and Idiosyncratic Issues affect the exposure score?">
Both Systemic ESG Issues and Idiosyncratic Issues are built upon the Events concept used in the rating. When a Systemic ESG Issue or Idiosyncratic Issue passes a significance threshold of high impacts and risks (category 4) or severe impacts and risks (category 5), a company's exposure increases by 6 or 8 points respectively. These scores can still be adjusted by analysts depending on the unique case.
</DeepDive>
