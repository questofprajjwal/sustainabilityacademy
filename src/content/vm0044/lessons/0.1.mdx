Biochar is a solid, carbon-rich material made by heating biomass in a low-oxygen environment. That process is called thermochemical conversion. The result is a stable material that holds carbon in a form that resists decay for a very long time.

## How Biochar is Made

There are two thermochemical processes recognized in VM0044.

**Pyrolysis** heats biomass in an inert, oxygen-deficient environment. No oxidants are present. The biomass breaks down into biochar, bio-oil, and a combustible gas called syngas.

**Gasification** converts carbon-based material into fuel gas at temperatures greater than 700 degrees C. A very small amount of oxidant is introduced, but not enough to cause combustion. The main output is syngas, with biochar as a solid by-product.

VM0044 treats both processes the same way for carbon accounting purposes.

<AnalogyBox>
Think about how charcoal for a barbecue is made. Wood is placed in a kiln with very little air and heated until the water and volatile gases drive off, leaving behind mostly carbon. Biochar works on the same principle, but at industrial scale. Instead of burning that charcoal to cook food, the carbon is measured and tracked, then placed permanently into soil or a long-lived product. The carbon that would have rotted away in the forest or field is now locked in a stable solid.
</AnalogyBox>

## Why Biochar Stores Carbon

In nature, dead plant matter decays. Microbes break it down and release carbon dioxide (CO2) and methane (CH4) back into the atmosphere. When biomass is converted to biochar, the thermochemical process rearranges the carbon atoms into stable, aromatic ring structures. These structures resist microbial attack.

The result is that carbon which would have re-entered the atmosphere within a few years or decades can now remain stored in biochar for up to 100 years or more.

<HighlightBox>
Key rule: GHG benefits are credited ONLY for biochar that reaches an eligible soil or non-soil application. Biochar that is produced but never applied does not generate carbon credits under VM0044.
</HighlightBox>

## The Carbon Sink Concept

A carbon sink is any place that absorbs and holds more carbon than it releases. When biochar is applied to soil or incorporated into a long-lasting material like concrete or asphalt, it acts as a carbon sink.

VM0044 is designed around this idea. The methodology measures how much stable organic carbon enters an eligible end-use application. That quantity, adjusted for permanence and production emissions, is what determines the number of carbon credits issued.

## The Three-Part Project Activity

Every VM0044 project involves three stages:

1. **Sourcing waste biomass as feedstock.** The project collects by-products, residues, or waste streams from agriculture, forestry, food processing, or other industries. This biomass must meet specific eligibility criteria.

2. **Producing biochar via thermochemical conversion.** The feedstock is processed at a greenfield production facility using pyrolysis or gasification. The biochar is tested and characterized.

3. **Utilizing the biochar in an eligible application.** The biochar is applied to agricultural or other land (soil application) or incorporated into manufactured products such as concrete or asphalt (non-soil application).

All three stages are inside the project boundary and are accounted for in the GHG calculations.

## Key Definitions from VM0044 Section 3

Understanding the terminology makes the methodology much easier to follow. Here are the most important definitions.

**Biochar:** A solid and stabilized carbon material produced by the thermochemical processing of biomass in an oxygen-limited environment. It is distinguished from charcoal by being intended for application to soil or non-soil uses, not for combustion.

**Pyrolysis:** The thermochemical decomposition of organic material in inert, oxygen-deficient conditions. No oxidants are added. It produces biochar, bio-oil, and syngas.

**Gasification:** The conversion of carbon-based material into fuel gas at temperatures greater than 700 degrees C. A very low quantity of oxidant is present. The main output is syngas; biochar is a co-product.

**Waste biomass:** Biomass by-products, residues, and waste streams from agriculture, forestry, recycling industries, animal manure, and related industries. It does not include biomass grown specifically to make biochar.

**Organic carbon content (OC%):** The amount of organic carbon permanently stored in biochar, measured as a mass proportion on a dry weight basis. This is a core measurement for calculating the carbon stored.

**Anaerobic decomposition:** The microbial breakdown of organic matter in the absence of oxygen. It produces mainly methane (CH4) and some CO2. When biomass such as manure or wet agricultural waste decomposes anaerobically, it releases potent greenhouse gases. Converting that biomass to biochar prevents those emissions.

**Feedstock:** The biogenic materials that undergo thermochemical processes to create biochar. Feedstock must be waste biomass to be eligible under VM0044.

**Biogenic:** A material produced by or originating from a living organism. Biogenic carbon is part of the natural carbon cycle. VM0044 uses only biogenic feedstocks.

<DeepDive title="Why does the distinction between pyrolysis and gasification matter for measurement?">
Both processes produce biochar with stable carbon, but they operate differently. Pyrolysis at lower temperatures tends to produce more biochar with higher carbon content per unit of feedstock. Gasification at very high temperatures produces more syngas and less biochar. The organic carbon content of the resulting biochar, measured by laboratory analysis, determines how many credits can be issued. So while VM0044 treats both processes the same in principle, the actual quantity of credits depends on measured OC% for each production batch. High-temperature gasification biochar may have very high carbon stability, but lower biochar yield per tonne of feedstock.
</DeepDive>

## Project Start Date and Crediting

The **project start date** under VM0044 is defined as the first instance of biochar production at the facility. This is the moment the project clock starts.

For additionality, VM0044 uses the **Activity Method**. This means the project demonstrates that biochar production for carbon storage would not happen without the carbon finance incentive, based on the type of activity rather than a project-by-project financial analysis.

For the crediting baseline, VM0044 uses the **Project Method**. This means the baseline emissions are calculated based on what would have happened to the specific feedstock and project infrastructure in the absence of the project, not a sectoral average.

<ExampleBox>
A rice farm in Southeast Asia generates large amounts of rice straw after harvest. In the baseline scenario, the farmer burns the straw in the field. This releases CO2, CH4, and nitrous oxide (N2O) into the atmosphere. Under a VM0044 project, the straw is collected, transported to a pyrolysis facility (built new for this purpose), and converted to biochar. The biochar is then applied to nearby rice paddies. The project start date is the day the first batch of biochar is produced. From that date forward, the project can earn carbon credits for the carbon stored in biochar that is confirmed to reach the soil application site.
</ExampleBox>
