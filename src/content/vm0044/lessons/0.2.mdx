A VM0044 project does not happen in one place. It spans multiple locations and activities. To account for all the greenhouse gas flows accurately, the methodology defines a clear project boundary that covers the entire journey of biomass from source to final application.

## The Three Stages of the Biochar Value Chain

Every VM0044 project moves through three distinct stages. All three must be inside the project boundary.

**Stage 1: Sourcing.** Waste biomass is identified, collected, and transported to the production facility. Some feedstocks require pre-treatment such as drying, shredding, or pelletizing before they can be used.

**Stage 2: Production.** The prepared biomass is fed into a thermochemical conversion unit (pyrolysis or gasification) at a greenfield facility. The output is biochar, which is then sampled and tested.

**Stage 3: Application.** The biochar is transported to its final destination and applied to soil (cropland, grassland, forest) or incorporated into non-soil products (concrete, asphalt).

<AnalogyBox>
Think of this like a factory supply chain where every input and output is logged. A car manufacturer tracks steel from the mine to the foundry to the assembly plant. In the same way, VM0044 tracks carbon from the biomass residue pile, through the kiln, to the field or construction site. At each step, emissions and carbon flows are recorded. If any step is missing from the accounting, the overall calculation is incomplete.
</AnalogyBox>

<HighlightBox>
The project boundary must cover all three stages: sourcing, production, and application. If any stage is left out of the accounting, the GHG calculation is incomplete and the project does not meet methodology requirements.
</HighlightBox>

## The Greenfield Requirement

VM0044 requires that biochar production takes place at a new, greenfield facility. A greenfield facility is one built specifically for this project, not an existing facility that is repurposed.

The **project start date** is the first instance of biochar production at the greenfield facility. This is when the crediting period begins.

## The Spatial Project Boundary

The project boundary has three spatial components, reflecting the three stages of the value chain:

- The **sourcing location**: the farm, forest, processing plant, or other site where waste biomass originates.
- The **production facility**: the site where biomass is converted into biochar. This must be a greenfield facility.
- The **application location**: the field, urban green space, concrete plant, or asphalt facility where biochar is ultimately used.

These locations may be in different places, sometimes far apart. The methodology requires that GHG flows at each location are included in the accounting.

## Why Baseline Emissions at the Sourcing Stage are Set to Zero

This is one of the more counterintuitive aspects of VM0044. You might expect that the baseline scenario would include the emissions from burning or decaying feedstock, because preventing those emissions is part of the project's climate benefit.

The methodology does include some baseline emissions from feedstock decay or combustion in certain cases. However, for the sourcing stage overall, the conservative default is zero.

The reason is practical: waste biomass feedstocks are diverse. Rice straw decays differently from sawmill offcuts, and decay rates vary by region, moisture content, and climate. Rather than use uncertain or variable emission factors that could inflate the credit calculation, VM0044 takes the most conservative approach. It sets baseline sourcing emissions to zero.

This is conservative because it means the project does not claim credit for emissions it avoids at the sourcing stage unless there is strong evidence to support a non-zero value. In practice, the carbon credit calculation focuses primarily on the stable carbon stored in biochar, not on baseline decay emissions.

<HighlightBox>
Setting baseline sourcing emissions to zero is a conservative approach. It ensures that credits are not over-issued due to uncertain or variable decay rates across different feedstock types and regions.
</HighlightBox>

## The 200 km Transportation Threshold

Transportation emissions are only included in the accounting when the distance exceeds 200 km. This threshold applies to both feedstock transport (sourcing to production) and biochar transport (production to application site).

For distances up to 200 km, transportation emissions are considered too small to be worth measuring and are excluded. For distances over 200 km, only road transport is allowed, and emissions are calculated using the CDM TOOL12 methodology.

This rule simplifies accounting for the many local projects where all stages are within a reasonable geographic radius.

## Chain of Custody

The methodology requires documentation that tracks biochar from the point of waste biomass origin through production to the final application site. This is called the chain of custody.

Chain of custody records provide assurance to auditors and buyers that the biochar credited was actually produced from eligible feedstocks and applied in an eligible end use. Without this documentation trail, credits cannot be issued.

<DeepDive title="What does a chain of custody document look like in practice?">
A chain of custody system for a VM0044 project would typically include: (1) records of feedstock origin (farm name, crop type, quantity, date of collection), (2) transport logs (vehicle, distance, date), (3) production records (batch number, feedstock input, biochar output, date of production, test results for OC% and H:Corg), and (4) application records (location GPS coordinates, quantity applied, date of application, landowner or facility confirmation). Third-party auditors review these records during verification to confirm that every tonne of biochar credited can be traced back to eligible feedstock and forward to an eligible application.
</DeepDive>
