Not all biomass can be used as feedstock under VM0044. The methodology sets strict criteria to ensure that the biomass used is genuinely waste material and that its conversion to biochar represents a real climate benefit. This lesson covers who can supply feedstock, what types of biomass qualify, and what sustainability standards apply.

## The Four Core Feedstock Conditions (Condition 4)

Every feedstock used in a VM0044 project must meet all four of these conditions simultaneously.

**Condition 4a: Purely biogenic waste biomass, not purpose-grown.**
The biomass must be a by-product, residue, or waste stream. It must not have been grown specifically to make biochar. Planting trees or crops with the primary intention of converting them to biochar would be considered purpose-grown and is not eligible.

**Condition 4b: Would have otherwise decayed or been combusted without energy recovery.**
In the baseline scenario (what would have happened without the project), the biomass must have been left to decompose or burned without producing useful energy. Biomass that was being productively used for energy or other purposes before the project cannot be diverted into biochar production and claimed as a climate benefit.

**Condition 4c: Not imported from other countries.**
The feedstock must be sourced domestically. Cross-border biomass imports are not eligible. This rule prevents projects from drawing on biomass that could affect land use or emissions in other countries.

**Condition 4d: Must meet the sustainability criteria in Table 1.**
Each feedstock type has specific sustainability requirements that go beyond the four general conditions. These are described in the next section.

<HighlightBox>
Feedstock must NOT be purpose-grown. It must be waste or residue material that would have otherwise decayed or been burned without producing useful energy. If any of the four core conditions is not met, the feedstock is ineligible regardless of type.
</HighlightBox>

### A. Agricultural Waste Biomass

Agricultural residues are among the most common feedstocks for biochar projects. They come from two types of sources.

**From fields directly (not from centralized processing):** The project must demonstrate that removing residues will not reduce soil organic carbon stocks or reduce crop productivity. In many farming systems, a portion of straw or stalks is left on the field to maintain soil quality. If there is no documentation proving that removal is safe, the project can collect no more than 50% of total available residues. This cap protects soil health.

**From centralized biomass processing:** Examples include rice mills or grain processing facilities. Here, the biomass is already separated from the land. The sustainability requirement is to show that in the baseline scenario, the residue was burned without producing energy, for example as open field burning of stubble after milling.

### B. Food Processing Residues

These come from facilities that process food for human or animal consumption. The key sustainability check is that the amount of residue generated per unit of facility output has not increased specifically because of the biochar project. This prevents a food processor from artificially generating more waste to sell as feedstock.

Examples include the liquid and solid waste from washing, cleaning, and peeling fruits and vegetables; expired or off-specification food products; and processing residues from coffee, tea, cocoa, and tobacco industries.

### C. Forestry and Other Wood Processing

Wood from forests raises additional concerns about deforestation and unsustainable harvesting. VM0044 requires that forest-sourced wood be proven to come from sustainably managed forests. Acceptable evidence includes:

- A certified sustainable forest management plan.
- PEFC (Programme for the Endorsement of Forest Certification) or FSC (Forest Stewardship Council) certification.
- Compliance with CDM Executive Board document EB23, Annex 18, which defines renewable biomass.

When processed timber is used in soil applications, it must be free from paint residues, solvents, or other toxic contaminants that could harm soil or crops.

### D. Recycling Economy

This category covers biomass from urban and industrial waste streams. Because these streams can contain heavy metals, plastics, or other contaminants, the methodology requires compliance with heavy metal and contaminant thresholds set by the International Biochar Initiative (IBI) Biochar Testing Guidelines or the European Biochar Certificate (EBC) Production Guidelines.

Only the non-fossil fraction of the waste can be counted. Mixed waste streams that contain plastics or petroleum-derived materials cannot have those components included in the feedstock calculation.

### E. Aquaculture Plants

Seaweed, algae, and aquatic plant residues from fish farms or marine cultivation operations qualify under this category. One important rule is that invasive aquatic plant species (such as water hyacinth) must not have been deliberately introduced to a water body in order to qualify as feedstock. The species must be from the plant kingdom (Plantae), not animals or microorganisms.

### F. Animal Manure

Manure from livestock operations is a particularly valuable feedstock because it would otherwise decompose anaerobically and release methane, a potent greenhouse gas. Converting manure to biochar prevents those methane emissions and stores the carbon. The feedstock must be a genuine by-product of animal husbandry, not sourced from livestock raised specifically for manure production.

### G. High-Carbon Fly Ash from Biomass (HCFA)

This is a specialized feedstock category. When biomass burns in a cogeneration facility, not all carbon is fully oxidized. Some partially converted carbon exits as fly ash. If an affirmative technology (such as a carbon trap or separator) is used to capture this partially converted biochar before full combustion, that material may be eligible.

Two strict limits apply. First, HCFA must represent no more than 5% of the total annual waste biomass throughput from the bioenergy facility. Second, the project must demonstrate that no fossil fuel was added to replace the caloric value of the biomass that was diverted before combustion.

## Additional Applicability Conditions (Conditions 5-8)

**Condition 5, Biochar quality standards:** Biochar produced from single or mixed feedstocks must comply with either the IBI Biochar Testing Guidelines or the EBC Production Guidelines. These international standards specify acceptable levels of heavy metals, polycyclic aromatic hydrocarbons (PAHs), and other contaminants.

**Condition 6, Transportation distance and mode:** Feedstock and biochar can be transported up to 200 km by any vehicle type without triggering additional emissions accounting. Beyond 200 km, only road transport is permitted, and emissions must be calculated using CDM TOOL12.

**Condition 7, Mineral additives:** Biochar products may include mineral additives such as lime, rock minerals, or ash to improve soil performance. If these additives are 10% or less of the total mass, no additional testing is required. If they exceed 10% of total mass, laboratory tests are required to characterize the product.

**Condition 8, Recognized certification schemes:** Compliance with the sustainability criteria in Table 1 can be demonstrated through recognized certification schemes. Examples include the Roundtable on Sustainable Biomaterials (RSB), the International Sustainability and Carbon Certification (ISCC), or any scheme approved by the European Union for renewable energy directives. Holding such a certificate provides evidence of sustainability compliance without requiring project-specific documentation for each criterion.

<DeepDive title="Appendix 2: Demonstrating Feedstock as Waste Biomass">
Appendix 2 of VM0044 provides practical guidance on how to prove that a feedstock is genuinely waste biomass and not purpose-grown. There are three approaches.

**Approach 1: Identified source with no baseline use.** The project proponent can identify the specific source of the biomass (for example, a named farm or processing facility) and demonstrate that in the baseline, the biomass had no productive use. It was simply left to decay, burned in the open, or disposed of as waste. Documentation could include photographs, contracts, or waste management records.

**Approach 2: Identified source with partial baseline use.** Some feedstock may have had a partial baseline use, such as being sold as low-grade fuel or animal feed, but in quantities less than the total available. The project can use the excess quantity that was previously unused. The project proponent identifies the source and demonstrates what portion had no baseline productive use.

**Approach 3: Unidentifiable source from a residue market.** In some cases, biomass is purchased from a commodity market for residues (such as a regional straw market). The individual sources cannot all be identified. In this case, the project proponent must provide market-level evidence that the material traded in that market is genuinely residue or waste, with no significant baseline productive use across the market as a whole.

All three approaches require documentation and are subject to third-party verification.
</DeepDive>

<ExampleBox>
A coffee roasting company generates significant quantities of coffee chaff (the papery skin removed during roasting) and spent coffee grounds from its operations. In the baseline, both materials were sent to landfill. A VM0044 project collects these food processing residues (Category B), converts them to biochar at a nearby greenfield pyrolysis facility (less than 200 km away), and applies the biochar to coffee plantation soils. The residue generation rate at the roasting facility is documented to ensure it has not increased purely to generate feedstock. The biochar is tested to confirm it meets IBI standards before application.
</ExampleBox>
